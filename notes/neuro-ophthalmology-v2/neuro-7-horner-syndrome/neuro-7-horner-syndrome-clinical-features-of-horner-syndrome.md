# Clinical Features of Horner Syndrome: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Horner syndrome results from disruption of the sympathetic pathway to the eye and face. Recognition of this syndrome is crucial as it may indicate serious underlying pathology.

## Classic Triad

### 1. Ptosis
- Mild (1-2 mm) upper lid ptosis
- Due to paralysis of Müller's muscle
- Lower lid may be slightly elevated ("inverse ptosis")
- Apparent enophthalmos due to narrowed palpebral fissure

### 2. Miosis
- Affected pupil smaller
- More noticeable in dim light
- Dilation lag: slow pupil dilation in dark (takes 15-20 seconds)
- Normal constriction to light and near

### 3. Anhidrosis
- Loss of sweating on ipsilateral face
- Present in first and second-order lesions
- Absent in third-order lesions
- May be difficult to detect clinically

## Additional Features

### Iris Heterochromia
- Congenital Horner: lighter iris on affected side
- Due to sympathetic role in iris pigmentation
- Does not develop after 2 years of age

### Conjunctival Injection
- May be present acutely
- Due to loss of sympathetic vasoconstriction

## Anatomical Pathway

### Three-Neuron Pathway
1. **First-order:** Hypothalamus → Ciliospinal center (C8-T2)
2. **Second-order:** Synapse at T1 → Superior cervical ganglion
3. **Third-order:** Superior cervical ganglion → Eye (along ICA)

## Pharmacologic Testing

### Cocaine Test (Confirmation)
- Cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake
- Normal pupil dilates
- Horner pupil fails to dilate
- Diagnosis confirmed if anisocoria >1mm after 45 minutes

### Apraclonidine Test (Confirmation)
- Alpha-1 and Alpha-2 agonist
- Causes dilation of Horner pupil (denervation supersensitivity)
- Normal pupil may constrict slightly
- Reversal of anisocoria confirms Horner

### Hydroxyamphetamine Test (Localization)
- Releases norepinephrine from presynaptic terminals
- Dilates first and second-order Horner
- No effect in third-order Horner
- **Note:** Must wait 48 hours after cocaine test

## Etiology by Order

### First-Order Lesions
- Stroke (Wallenberg syndrome)
- Tumors (pontine, hypothalamic)
- Syringomyelia
- Multiple sclerosis

### Second-Order Lesions
- Pancoast tumor (lung apex)
- Thyroid surgery
- Neck trauma
- Central venous catheterization

### Third-Order Lesions
- Carotid artery dissection (EMERGENCY)
- Cavernous sinus pathology
- Cluster headache
- Carotid artery aneurysm

## Carotid Artery Dissection

### Warning Signs
- Neck pain
- Headache
- Recent trauma
- Horner syndrome

### Action
- Urgent imaging (CTA/MRA)
- Anticoagulation may be indicated
- Risk of stroke

## Examination Points

**Key Clinical Features:**
1. Mild ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis
2. Dilation lag in dark
3. Pharmacologic testing confirms diagnosis
4. Third-order: NO anhidrosis
5. Carotid dissection: urgent evaluation needed

**Pharmacology Summary:**
- Cocaine: confirms Horner (no dilation)
- Apraclonidine: confirms Horner (dilates)
- Hydroxyamphetamine: localizes (third-order = no dilation)
