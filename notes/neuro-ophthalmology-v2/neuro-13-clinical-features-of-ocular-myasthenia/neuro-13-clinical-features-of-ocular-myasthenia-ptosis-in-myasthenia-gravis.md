# Ptosis in Myasthenia Gravis: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Ptosis is one of the most common presenting signs of ocular myasthenia gravis. Understanding its characteristics and differentiation from other causes of ptosis is essential for optometric practice.

## Characteristics of Myasthenic Ptosis

### Key Features
1. **Variable severity** throughout the day
2. **Fatigability** - worsens with use
3. **May be unilateral or bilateral**
4. **Often asymmetric**
5. **No pain**

### Temporal Patterns
- Worse at end of day
- Worse after prolonged use
- May improve with rest
- Can fluctuate day to day

## Clinical Testing

### Fatigue Test
1. Have patient look up for 1-2 minutes
2. Observe for worsening ptosis
3. Positive if ptosis increases significantly

### Sleep Test (Rest Test)
1. Have patient close eyes for 15-30 minutes
2. Observe improvement after rest
3. Myasthenic ptosis typically improves

### Ice Test
1. Apply ice pack to closed eyelid for 2-5 minutes
2. Improvement in ptosis suggests myasthenia
3. Sensitivity ~80%, specificity ~95%

### Mechanism
- Cold improves neuromuscular transmission
- Acetylcholinesterase works better at lower temperatures
- More ACh available for receptors

## Differentiation from Other Ptosis

### Myasthenic vs Aponeurotic Ptosis

| Feature | Myasthenic | Aponeurotic |
|---------|------------|-------------|
| Variability | Marked | Minimal |
| Fatigue | Present | Absent |
| Lid crease | Normal | High |
| Ice test | Positive | Negative |
| Rest | Improves | No change |

### Myasthenic vs CN III Ptosis

| Feature | Myasthenic | CN III |
|---------|------------|--------|
| Pupil | Normal | May be dilated |
| Eye movements | Variable weakness | Specific pattern |
| Fatigue | Present | Absent |
| Variability | Marked | Fixed |

## Associated Ocular Signs

### Other Ocular Myasthenia Features
- Diplopia (variable)
- Cogan's lid twitch
- Quiver eye movements
- Variable strabismus
- Orbicularis weakness

### Cogan's Lid Twitch
- Patient looks down, then quickly up
- Eyelid overshoots, then settles
- Due to recovery of NMJ during rest
- Characteristic of myasthenia

## Ocular vs Generalized Myasthenia

### Ocular Myasthenia
- Ptosis and/or diplopia only
- 50% remain purely ocular
- May progress to generalized

### Conversion Risk
- 50-60% convert to generalized within 2 years
- Higher risk if AChR antibody positive
- Monitor for systemic symptoms

## Diagnosis

### Antibody Testing
- **AChR antibodies:** 50-60% of ocular MG
- **MuSK antibodies:** Rare in ocular MG
- Negative antibodies does not exclude MG

### Electrophysiology
- Repetitive nerve stimulation
- Single-fiber EMG (most sensitive)
- May be normal in ocular MG

### Imaging
- Chest CT for thymoma
- 10-15% of MG patients have thymoma

## Treatment

### Medical
- Pyridostigmine (Mestinon)
- Corticosteroids
- Immunosuppressants

### Symptomatic
- Ptosis crutches (glasses with lid props)
- Lid tape
- Ptosis surgery (only after stabilization)

## Examination Points

**Key Clinical Features:**
1. Variable, fatigable ptosis
2. Ice test positive
3. Cogan's lid twitch
4. Normal pupil
5. May progress to generalized MG

**Diagnostic Algorithm:**
1. Suspect from clinical presentation
2. Perform ice test or fatigue test
3. Check for AChR antibodies
4. Consider single-fiber EMG
5. CT chest for thymoma
