# Arteritic Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (A-AION): Emergency Guide

## Introduction

A-AION is an ophthalmic emergency caused by giant cell arteritis (GCA). Prompt recognition and immediate treatment is essential to prevent bilateral blindness.

## Giant Cell Arteritis Overview

### Demographics
- Age >50 years (mean 75 years)
- Female predominance (3:1)
- Associated with polymyalgia rheumatica (PMR)

### Pathophysiology
- Granulomatous inflammation of medium and large arteries
- Involves internal carotid artery branches
- Giant cells and epithelioid cells in vessel wall

## Clinical Presentation

### Visual Symptoms
- Severe sudden vision loss
- Often profound (counting fingers or worse)
- May be preceded by transient visual loss
- Frequently bilateral (if untreated)

### Systemic Symptoms of GCA
- Headache (new onset)
- Scalp tenderness
- Jaw claudication (highly specific)
- Polymyalgia rheumatica symptoms
- Weight loss, fatigue, fever

### Fundus Findings
- Chalky white disc edema
- More pallid than NAION
- May have cilioretinal artery occlusion
- Cotton wool spots

## Diagnosis

### Laboratory Tests
- **ESR:** Elevated, often >50 mm/hr
- **CRP:** Elevated (more sensitive)
- **Platelet count:** Often elevated
- **Hemoglobin:** May show anemia of chronic disease

### Temporal Artery Biopsy
- Gold standard for diagnosis
- Skip lesions possible
- Should not delay treatment
- Positive in 85-95% of cases

### Imaging
- Color Doppler ultrasound
- MRI/MRA of temporal arteries
- PET-CT in select cases

## Treatment Protocol

### Emergency Treatment
- **IV methylprednisolone 1g/day** for 3-5 days
- Followed by high-dose oral prednisone (1 mg/kg)
- Gradual taper over 12-18 months

### Critical Points
- Do NOT wait for biopsy results
- Biopsy can be done within 1-2 weeks of starting steroids
- Steroids prevent second eye involvement
- Visual recovery limited once ischemia occurs

## Prognosis

### Visual Outcome
- Poor if treatment delayed
- Vision loss usually permanent
- Second eye at risk without treatment

## Examination Points

**Emergency Recognition:**
1. Age >50 with sudden severe vision loss
2. Ask about headache, jaw claudication, scalp tenderness
3. ESR and CRP immediately
4. Start high-dose steroids before confirming diagnosis
5. Temporal artery biopsy within 1-2 weeks

**Key Differences from NAION:**
- Severe vision loss (often NLP)
- Chalky white disc
- Systemic symptoms
- Elevated ESR/CRP
- Requires emergency treatment
