# Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia in MS: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Internuclear ophthalmoplegia (INO) is a disorder of conjugate horizontal gaze caused by a lesion in the medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF). It is a highly localizing sign and is one of the most common eye movement disorders in multiple sclerosis.

## Anatomical Basis

### MLF Function
- Connects PPRF (paramedian pontine reticular formation) to CN III nucleus
- Coordinates horizontal gaze
- Allows adduction of one eye with abduction of the other
- Lesion disrupts this coordination

### Pathway
1. PPRF activates CN VI nucleus (abduction)
2. Simultaneous signal through MLF to contralateral CN III (adduction)
3. MLF lesion = abduction works, adduction impaired

## Clinical Features

### Classic Signs
1. **Impaired adduction** of the affected eye
2. **Abduction nystagmus** of the contralateral eye
3. **Normal convergence** (differentiates from CN III palsy)

### Additional Features
- Slow adducting saccades
- Skew deviation may be present
- Vertical nystagmus
- May be bilateral

## Etiology

### Multiple Sclerosis (Young Patients)
- Most common cause in young
- Bilateral INO typical
- Demyelination of MLF
- May be presenting sign of MS

### Stroke (Older Patients)
- Unilateral INO more common
- Small pontine infarct
- Vascular risk factors present

### Other Causes
- Brainstem tumor
- Trauma
- Infection
- Wernicke encephalopathy

## Examination Technique

### Assessing INO
1. Have patient look to one side
2. Observe adduction lag in one eye
3. Watch for abducting nystagmus in other eye
4. Test convergence (should be preserved)

### Localization
- **Right INO:** Right MLF lesion, cannot adduct right eye
- **Left INO:** Left MLF lesion, cannot adduct left eye
- **Bilateral:** Both MLF affected (suggests MS)

## INO vs Other Conditions

### Differentiation from CN III Palsy
- INO: Normal convergence, ptosis absent, pupil normal
- CN III: Poor convergence, ptosis present, pupil may be involved

### Differentiation from Myasthenia
- Myasthenia: Fatigable, variable
- INO: Consistent, nystagmus present

## One-and-a-Half Syndrome

### Definition
- INO + ipsilateral PPRF lesion
- Only one eye can move horizontally (abduct)
- "One" eye moves, "half" of the other

### Clinical Picture
- Complete gaze palsy to one side
- INO to the other side
- Only abduction of one eye preserved

## Treatment

### For MS-related INO
- Disease-modifying therapy for MS
- Usually improves with MS treatment
- Gabapentin may help nystagmus

### Symptomatic Treatment
- Prisms (limited benefit)
- Botulinum toxin (controversial)
- Strabismus surgery (rare)

## Examination Points

**Key Clinical Features:**
1. Impaired adduction + abducting nystagmus
2. Normal convergence distinguishes from CN III palsy
3. Bilateral INO in young = suspect MS
4. MLF lesion in brainstem
5. One-and-a-half syndrome = PPRF + MLF lesion

**MS Examination Pearls:**
- INO is a common presenting sign
- Bilateral INO highly suggestive of MS
- MRI shows white matter lesions
- Part of diagnostic criteria for MS
