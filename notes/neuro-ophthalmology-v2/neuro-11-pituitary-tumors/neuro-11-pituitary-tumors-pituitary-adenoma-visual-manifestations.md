# Pituitary Adenoma Visual Manifestations: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Pituitary adenomas are the most common cause of chiasmal compression and produce characteristic visual field defects. Early recognition and treatment can prevent permanent vision loss.

## Classification

### By Size
- **Microadenoma:** <10 mm
- **Macroadenoma:** ≥10 mm
- Visual symptoms usually only with macroadenomas

### By Function
- **Functioning:** Hormone-secreting (prolactinoma, GH, ACTH)
- **Non-functioning:** No hormone secretion

## Visual Field Defects

### Mechanism
- Superior growth of tumor compresses optic chiasm
- Chiasm typically 10 mm above pituitary
- Central chiasmal fibers affected first

### Typical Progression
1. **Superior bitemporal defect** (early)
2. **Complete bitemporal hemianopia** (advanced)
3. **Postfixation blindness** (severe)

### Field Defect Patterns

**Bitemporal Hemianopia:**
- Classic defect
- Midline respects vertical meridian
- May be complete or partial

**Junctional Scotoma:**
- Central scotoma one eye
- Superior temporal defect fellow eye
- Anterior chiasmal involvement

**Bitemporal Hemianopic Scotoma:**
- Central temporal defects
- Macular fibers affected
- Peripheral fields intact

## Fundus Findings

### Chronic Compression
- Optic disc pallor
- Bow-tie atrophy (band atrophy)
- Nerve fiber layer thinning

### Bow-tie Atrophy
- Horizontal band of pallor
- Corresponds to nasal retinal fibers
- Pathognomonic of chiasmal lesion

## Associated Symptoms

### Hormonal Manifestations
- **Prolactinoma:** Galactorrhea, amenorrhea, impotence
- **GH-secreting:** Acromegaly features
- **ACTH-secreting:** Cushingoid features
- **Non-functioning:** Hypopituitarism from compression

### Neurological
- Headache
- Pituitary apoplexy (emergency)
- Cranial nerve palsies (cavernous sinus extension)

## Pituitary Apoplexy

### Definition
- Sudden hemorrhage or infarction of pituitary
- Medical emergency
- Sudden severe headache, vision loss

### Ocular Findings
- Sudden vision loss
- Ophthalmoplegia
- Ptosis
- CN III, IV, VI involvement

### Management
- Urgent neurosurgical consultation
- Hormone replacement
- Surgical decompression

## Imaging

### MRI with Contrast
- Gold standard imaging
- Shows tumor size and extension
- Visualizes optic chiasm

### CT
- Bone detail
- Acute hemorrhage

## Treatment

### Medical
- **Prolactinoma:** Dopamine agonists (cabergoline, bromocriptine)
- Often shrink tumor significantly
- May reverse visual field defects

### Surgical
- Transsphenoidal surgery
- Most common approach
- Indications: non-functioning tumors, medical failure

### Radiation
- Adjunct to surgery
- Residual or recurrent tumor
- Stereotactic radiosurgery

## Visual Recovery

### Prognosis Factors
- Duration of compression
- Severity of field defect
- Preoperative visual acuity
- Disc pallor

### Timeline
- Improvement may begin within days
- Maximum recovery 3-6 months
- Some may continue improving 1 year

## Examination Points

**Key Facts for Licensing Exams:**
1. Most common cause of chiasmal compression
2. Bitemporal hemianopia classic finding
3. Superior fields affected first
4. Bow-tie atrophy indicates chronic compression
5. Pituitary apoplexy = emergency

**Clinical Pearls:**
- Microadenomas rarely cause visual symptoms
- Post-fixation blindness from bitemporal hemianopia
- Prolactinomas often treatable medically
- Visual recovery possible after decompression
