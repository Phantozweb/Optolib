# Migraine Aura and Visual Symptoms: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Migraine aura consists of transient neurological symptoms that typically precede or accompany migraine headache. Visual aura is the most common type and produces distinctive visual phenomena that are important to differentiate from other causes of transient visual loss.

## Visual Aura Characteristics

### Typical Features
- Duration: 5-60 minutes (usually 20-30 minutes)
- Progressive development over minutes
- Positive visual phenomena
- Often followed by headache
- Fully reversible

### Scintillating Scotoma
- Most common visual aura
- "Fortification spectrum" or "teichopsia"
- Begins as small paracentral scotoma
- Expands over 15-30 minutes
- Zigzag, shimmering edges
- Often has colors (typically silver/white)
- May leave temporary scotoma

### Other Visual Aura Types
- **Photopsias:** Flashes, sparkles
- **Geometric patterns:** Lines, circles, dots
- **Visual distortion:** Metamorphopsia, micropsia
- **Field defects:** Homonymous hemianopia (rare)

## Pathophysiology

### Cortical Spreading Depression
- Wave of neuronal depolarization
- Spreads across visual cortex at 3-5 mm/min
- Explains gradual progression of aura
- Followed by neuronal depression

### Affected Areas
- Visual cortex (occipital lobe)
- Often starts in V1, spreads to V2-V3
- Produces homonymous visual symptoms

## Migraine Types with Visual Features

### Migraine with Aura
- Visual symptoms preceding headache
- Meets diagnostic criteria for migraine
- Aura develops gradually

### Retinal Migraine
- Monocular visual symptoms
- Scintillations or scotoma
- Lasts 5-60 minutes
- Rare diagnosis of exclusion

### Ophthalmoplegic Migraine
- Rare condition
- Cranial nerve palsy with migraine
- CN III most common
- Now considered variant of demyelination

## Differential Diagnosis

### Transient Visual Loss Causes

| Condition | Duration | Features |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| Migraine aura | 5-60 min | Positive phenomena, progressive |
| Amaurosis fugax | 1-5 min | Curtain-like, abrupt |
| TIA | Variable | Neurological deficits |
| Papilledema TVO | Seconds | Posture-related |
| GCA | Variable | Elderly, systemic symptoms |

### Important Exclusions
- TIA/stroke
- Retinal detachment
- Seizure (occipital)
- Giant cell arteritis
- Retinal migraine (monocular)

## Diagnostic Criteria (ICHD-3)

### Typical Aura with Migraine Headache
1. One or more aura symptoms
2. Each symptom lasts 5-60 minutes
3. At least one symptom unilateral
4. Headache within 60 minutes of aura
5. Not attributed to other disorder

## Treatment

### Acute Treatment
- NSAIDs
- Triptans (sumatriptan, rizatriptan)
- Ergotamine derivatives
- Rest in dark, quiet room

### Preventive Treatment
- Beta-blockers (propranolol)
- Calcium channel blockers
- Anticonvulsants (topiramate)
- Antidepressants (amitriptyline)

### Visual Aura-Specific
- Usually no specific treatment needed
- Aura resolves spontaneously
- Treat headache that follows

## Red Flags

### Concerning Features
- Sudden onset (not progressive)
- Duration >60 minutes
- First occurrence after age 50
- Associated with other neurological deficits
- No headache after aura
- Change in pattern

### Action Required
- Urgent evaluation for stroke
- MRI brain
- Exclude secondary causes

## Examination Points

**Key Characteristics:**
1. Progressive development over minutes
2. Duration 5-60 minutes
3. Positive visual phenomena
4. Often homonymous (both eyes)
5. Followed by headache

**Differentiating Features:**
- Amaurosis fugax: sudden onset, brief
- Retinal migraine: truly monocular
- Stroke: sudden, persistent
- Seizure: rapid spread, other symptoms
