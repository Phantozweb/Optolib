# Clinical Features of Adie Pupil: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Adie tonic pupil is a disorder characterized by a dilated pupil that reacts poorly to light but constricts slowly to near stimuli (light-near dissociation). It results from damage to the ciliary ganglion or postganglionic parasympathetic fibers.

## Classic Presentation

### Patient Profile
- Typically young adults (20-40 years)
- Female predominance (2.6:1)
- Often presents with sudden onset of blurred near vision
- May notice enlarged pupil incidentally

## Clinical Features

### Pupil Findings
1. **Dilated pupil** (initially 4-6 mm)
2. **Poor or absent light reaction** 
3. **Slow, tonic constriction to near** (takes seconds)
4. **Slow redilation** after near effort (takes minutes)
5. **Light-near dissociation**

### Sectoral Palsy
- Portions of iris sphincter may be spared
- Creates irregular pupil shape
- Vermiform (worm-like) movements may be seen

### Accommodation
- May be affected initially
- Blurred near vision
- Usually recovers over weeks to months

## Holmes-Adie Syndrome

### Associated Features
- Deep tendon hyporeflexia or areflexia
- Usually affects lower extremities
- Present in 70% of patients
- Not progressive

### Ross Syndrome
- Adie pupil + hyporeflexia + segmental anhidrosis
- Loss of sweating
- May cause heat intolerance

## Pharmacologic Testing

### Dilute Pilocarpine (0.1%)
- Demonstrates cholinergic supersensitivity
- Normal pupil: no constriction
- Adie pupil: constricts to dilute pilocarpine
- 80% sensitive after 5-7 days

### Mechanism
- Denervation supersensitivity
- Upregulation of acetylcholine receptors
- Sphincter becomes sensitive to very low ACh concentrations

## Differential Diagnosis

### Light-Near Dissociation Causes
1. **Adie pupil**
2. **Argyll Robertson pupil** (neurosyphilis)
3. **Dorsal midbrain syndrome** (Parinaud)
4. **Severe optic neuropathy**
5. **Cranial nerve III aberrant regeneration**

### Differentiating Features

| Feature | Adie | Argyll Robertson | Dorsal Midbrain |
|---------|------|------------------|-----------------|
| Pupil size | Dilated | Small/miotic | Normal or dilated |
| Shape | Irregular | Irregular | Round |
| Near response | Tonic | Brisk | Poor |
| Light response | Absent | Absent | Poor |
| Associated | Areflexia | Neurosyphilis | Upgaze palsy |

## Etiology

### Idiopathic
- Most common
- No identifiable cause
- Presumed autoimmune or viral

### Secondary Causes
- Viral infections (varicella, influenza)
- Orbital trauma
- Orbital surgery
- Diabetes
- Autoimmune conditions

## Management

### Observation
- Most cases require no treatment
- Condition is benign
- Pupil may become smaller over years

### Treatment Options
- Pilocarpine 0.1% for cosmetic concerns
- Reading glasses for accommodation
- Sunglasses for photophobia

## Prognosis

### Long-term
- Pupil often becomes smaller over time
- Eventually may become miotic ("little old Adie")
- May become bilateral in 20% per year
- Vision remains good

## Examination Points

**Key Clinical Features:**
1. Young female with dilated pupil
2. Light-near dissociation
3. Tonic constriction to near
4. Supersensitivity to dilute pilocarpine
5. Deep tendon hyporeflexia (Holmes-Adie)

**Diagnostic Algorithm:**
1. Confirm light-near dissociation
2. Check for vermiform movements
3. Perform dilute pilocarpine test
4. Check deep tendon reflexes
5. Exclude neurosyphilis if indicated
