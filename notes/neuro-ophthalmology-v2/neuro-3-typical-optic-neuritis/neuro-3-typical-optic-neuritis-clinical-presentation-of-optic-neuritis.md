# Clinical Presentation of Optic Neuritis: Comprehensive Guide

## Introduction

Optic neuritis is an inflammatory demyelinating condition of the optic nerve that represents one of the most common neuro-ophthalmic presentations. Understanding its clinical features, differential diagnosis, and management is essential for optometric practice.

## Classic Clinical Triad

### 1. Vision Loss
- Usually unilateral (95% of adult cases)
- Acute to subacute onset over hours to days
- Variable severity: mild blurring to no light perception
- Central vision predominantly affected
- Color vision disproportionately affected (red desaturation)

### 2. Pain with Eye Movement
- Present in 90% of cases
- Dull, aching retro-orbital pain
- Worse with eye movement
- Due to inflammation of optic nerve sheath and surrounding tissues
- May precede visual symptoms by 1-2 days

### 3. Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)
- Present in all unilateral cases
- Detected with swinging flashlight test
- Indicates optic nerve dysfunction
- Graded 1+ to 4+

## Fundus Examination

### Acute Findings
- Optic disc typically appears normal (retrobulbar neuritis)
- Papillitis (disc swelling) in 35% of cases
- No vitreous cells in typical optic neuritis

### Chronic Findings
- Optic disc pallor develops 4-6 weeks after onset
- Temporal pallor common (papillomacular bundle)
- Nerve fiber layer thinning

## Visual Field Defects

### Common Patterns
- Central scotoma (most common)
- Diffuse depression
- Altitudinal defects
- Arcuate scotomas

## Optic Neuritis Treatment Trial (ONTT)

### Key Findings
1. IV methylprednisolone followed by oral prednisone hastened recovery but did not improve final visual outcome
2. Oral prednisone alone increased recurrence rate
3. MRI white matter lesions predicted MS risk

### Treatment Recommendations
- IV methylprednisolone 1g/day for 3 days
- Followed by oral prednisone taper
- Oral prednisone alone NOT recommended
- No treatment is also acceptable for visual recovery

## MS Risk Stratification

### MRI Findings
- No white matter lesions: 15% 5-year MS risk
- 1-2 lesions: 25% 5-year MS risk
- 3+ lesions: 50% 5-year MS risk

## Differential Diagnosis

### Must Consider
- Neuromyelitis optica (NMOSD)
- MOG antibody disease
- Ischemic optic neuropathy
- Infiltrative optic neuropathy
- Infectious optic neuritis

## Examination Points

**DOH/DHA/MOH High-Yield Facts:**
1. Pain with eye movement in 90%
2. Central scotoma most common field defect
3. RAPD present in all unilateral cases
4. IV steroids hasten but don't improve final vision
5. MRI for MS risk stratification
