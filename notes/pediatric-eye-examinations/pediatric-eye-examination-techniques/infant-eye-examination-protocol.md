# Infant Eye Examination Protocol (0-12 months)

## Introduction

The infant eye examination represents one of the most critical components of pediatric vision care. During the first year of life, the visual system undergoes rapid development, and early detection of abnormalities can prevent lifelong visual impairment. This comprehensive protocol outlines age-appropriate examination techniques for infants from birth through 12 months of age.

## Developmental Considerations

### Visual Milestones in the First Year

**Birth to 1 Month:**
- Fixates on faces and high-contrast targets
- Pupillary responses present from birth
- Brief periods of alert visual behavior
- Eye movements may be jerky and uncoordinated
- Visual acuity approximately 20/400 to 20/800

**1-3 Months:**
- Follows moving objects horizontally
- Begins developing smooth pursuit movements
- Social smile emerges (6-8 weeks)
- Fixation becomes more sustained
- Visual acuity improving to approximately 20/200

**4-6 Months:**
- Follows objects in all directions
- Reaches for objects with improving accuracy
- Stereopsis begins developing
- Visual acuity approximately 20/100 to 20/150
- Convergence developing

**7-12 Months:**
- Depth perception maturing
- Hand-eye coordination improving
- Visual acuity approaching 20/50 to 20/100
- Full range of eye movements
- Stable fixation and tracking

## Examination Components

### 1. History Taking

**Prenatal and Birth History:**
- Maternal health during pregnancy
- Gestational age at birth
- Birth weight
- Complications during delivery
- NICU admission and duration
- Oxygen therapy or mechanical ventilation
- Infections during pregnancy (TORCH)

**Family History:**
- Congenital cataracts
- Strabismus
- Amblyopia
- Genetic syndromes
- Retinoblastoma
- Congenital glaucoma
- High refractive errors

**Developmental History:**
- Achievement of motor milestones
- Social development
- Any developmental concerns
- Behavior and temperament

### 2. External Examination

**Observation:**
- Head position and face symmetry
- Orbital structure and spacing
- Eyelid position and symmetry
- Presence of epicanthal folds
- Ptosis or lid retraction
- Lacrimal system (tearing, discharge)

**Key Findings to Document:**
- Hypertelorism or hypotelorism
- Ptosis (unilateral vs. bilateral, severity)
- Epicanthal folds (may create pseudostrabismus)
- Lid margin abnormalities
- Eyelid coloboma
- Periorbital masses

### 3. Pupillary Assessment

**Technique in Infants:**
- Use dim room lighting
- Distract infant with toys or sounds
- Observe both pupils simultaneously
- Use transilluminator or penlight

**Assessment Points:**
- Pupil size and symmetry
- Direct and consensual responses
- Presence of afferent pupillary defect
- Shape irregularities (coloboma, aniridia)
- Leukocoria (white pupil reflex)

**Normal Findings:**
- Pupils 3-5mm in dim light
- Equal size (anisocoria <1mm acceptable)
- Brisk direct and consensual responses
- Round, regular shape

### 4. Red Reflex Testing (Brückner Test)

**Technique:**
- Darken room slightly
- Use direct ophthalmoscope at arm's length
- Focus on both pupils simultaneously
- Use +4.00 to +6.00 diopter setting
- Compare symmetry of red reflexes

**Interpretation:**
- Symmetric red reflexes: normal
- Asymmetric reflexes: possible strabismus or media opacity
- White reflex: retinoblastoma, cataract, or other pathology
- Dark reflex: cataract, vitreous hemorrhage
- Crescent shape: significant refractive error

**Clinical Significance:**
The Brückner test serves as a screening tool for multiple conditions:
- Media opacities (cataract, corneal)
- Strabismus
- Significant refractive errors
- Retinoblastoma
- Retinal abnormalities

### 5. Fixation Assessment

**Central, Steady, and Maintained (CSM) Method:**
- Central: Fixation on the fovea
- Steady: No nystagmus, smooth pursuit
- Maintained: Fixation held when other eye uncovered

**Technique:**
1. Attract infant's attention with target (face, toy, light)
2. Observe fixation behavior
3. Note if fixation is central (on fovea)
4. Assess steadiness (absence of nystagmus)
5. Check if maintained (no shift when other eye covered)

**Grading:**
- CSM: Normal fixation
- CS but not M: May indicate amblyopia
- Not central: Eccentric fixation, amblyopia
- Not steady: Nystagmus, sensory deficit

### 6. Ocular Motility Assessment

**Techniques for Infants:**
- Use interesting targets (faces, toys with sounds)
- Move target in H-pattern
- Observe smooth pursuit and saccades
- Note any limitations or overactions

**Assessment Points:**
- Range of motion in all directions
- Smooth pursuit quality
- Presence of nystagmus
- Strabismus in primary position
- Head turn or tilt

**Age-Specific Considerations:**
- Newborns: Movements may be jerky, normal to have inconsistent tracking
- 2-4 months: Smooth pursuit developing
- 6+ months: Should follow smoothly in all directions

### 7. Cover Testing

**Technique for Infants:**
- Use interesting fixation target
- May use thumb or hand for cover
- Brief cover-uncover technique
- Alternate cover for larger deviations

**Interpretation:**
- No movement: Orthophoria or microtropia
- Inward movement: Exotropia
- Outward movement: Esotropia
- Vertical movement: Vertical strabismus

**Challenges in Infants:**
- Limited cooperation
- Variable attention
- May need multiple attempts
- Consider Hirschberg/Krimsky as alternatives

### 8. Hirschberg and Krimsky Tests

**Hirschberg Test:**
- Shine light at bridge of nose
- Observe corneal light reflex position
- Normally centered or slightly nasal
- 1mm decentration = approximately 7° or 15 PD

**Krimsky Test:**
- Add prisms until reflex centered
- Useful for measuring angle in uncooperative infants
- Prism before fixating eye
- Measure deviation in prism diopters

### 9. Cycloplegic Refraction

**Indications in Infants:**
- All infants with strabismus
- Family history of amblyopia or strabismus
- Premature infants
- Down syndrome and other genetic conditions
- Signs of high refractive error

**Cycloplegic Agents:**
| Agent | Concentration | Onset | Duration | Notes |
|-------|--------------|-------|----------|-------|
| Cyclopentolate | 0.5% | 30-45 min | 24 hours | First-line for infants |
| Cyclopentolate | 1% | 30-45 min | 24 hours | Over 6 months |
| Atropine | 0.5% or 1% | 60-90 min | 10-14 days | Strong cycloplegia |
| Tropicamide | 1% | 20-30 min | 4-6 hours | Minimal cycloplegia |

**Retinoscopy Technique:**
1. Instill cycloplegic drops (wait appropriate time)
2. Use retinoscope with working lens
3. Neutralize reflex in both meridians
4. Document spherical equivalent and cylinder
5. Consider total and net cycloplegic refraction

### 10. Fundus Examination

**Techniques:**
- Indirect ophthalmoscopy with 20D or 28D lens
- May need scleral depression for peripheral retina
- Direct ophthalmoscopy for disc assessment
- Consider sedation if detailed exam needed

**Key Structures to Assess:**
- Optic disc (size, shape, cupping)
- Macula and fovea
- Retinal vessels
- Peripheral retina
- Choroid and RPE

**Common Findings in Infants:**
- Mild optic disc pallor may be normal
- Small disc cups common
- Macular reflex developing
- Peripheral retina may be pigmented

## Special Populations

### Premature Infants

**Additional Considerations:**
- ROP screening per gestational age and weight criteria
- Higher risk of myopia, strabismus
- Consider cortical visual impairment
- May have delayed visual development

### Infants with Developmental Concerns

**Modified Approach:**
- Shorter examination sessions
- More reliance on objective measures
- Consider sedation for complete exam
- Behavioral observations crucial
- Family communication essential

### Infants with Known Genetic Syndromes

**Syndrome-Specific Considerations:**

**Down Syndrome:**
- High refractive errors common
- Strabismus (esotropia)
- Nasolacrimal duct obstruction
- Cataracts
- Keratoconus (later)

**Marfan Syndrome:**
- Lens subluxation (superior-temporal)
- High myopia
- Retinal detachment risk

**Neurofibromatosis:**
- Optic nerve glioma
- Lisch nodules
- Choroidal hamartomas

## Documentation and Follow-up

### Essential Documentation:
1. Visual behavior observations
2. Fixation quality (CSM)
3. Red reflex findings
4. Pupillary responses
5. Ocular motility
6. Strabismus measurements
7. Cycloplegic refraction
8. Fundus findings
9. Clinical impressions
10. Recommendations

### Follow-up Schedule:

**Normal Examination:**
- Next routine pediatric visit
- Return if concerns arise

**Abnormal Findings:**
- High refractive error: 4-6 weeks for glasses
- Strabismus: 2-4 weeks for further evaluation
- Media opacity: Urgent ophthalmology referral
- Retinoblastoma: Immediate referral

## Parent Communication

### Key Points to Discuss:
1. Normal visual development
2. Signs of visual problems to watch for
3. Importance of follow-up appointments
4. Treatment options if abnormalities found
5. Prognosis and expectations

### Common Questions:
- "When will my baby see clearly?"
- "Why do my baby's eyes cross sometimes?"
- "How can I help my baby's vision develop?"
- "When should my baby have another eye exam?"

## Licensing Exam Points

### Key Concepts for Board Examinations:
1. **Red reflex testing** is critical for detecting retinoblastoma and cataracts
2. **Cycloplegic refraction** is essential in infants with strabismus
3. **CSM notation** for fixation assessment
4. **Premature infants** require ROP screening per specific criteria
5. **Pseudostrabismus** vs. true strabismus differentiation
6. **Critical periods** for amblyopia development begin in infancy

### Common Test Questions:
- Differentiate pseudostrabismus from true esotropia
- Appropriate timing for congenital cataract surgery
- Cycloplegic agent selection by age
- Red reflex interpretation
- Visual development milestones

## Summary

The infant eye examination requires patience, flexibility, and thorough knowledge of normal visual development. By employing age-appropriate techniques and understanding the unique characteristics of infant vision, practitioners can effectively screen for and diagnose vision-threatening conditions during this critical period of visual development.
