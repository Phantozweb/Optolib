# Toddler Eye Examination Techniques (1-3 years)

## Introduction

Examining toddlers presents unique challenges that require modified techniques, creative approaches, and significant patience. The period from 1-3 years represents a critical time in visual development where detection of strabismus, amblyopia, and refractive errors can prevent lifelong visual impairment. This comprehensive guide outlines strategies for successful examination of this challenging age group.

## Developmental Context

### Visual Development in Toddlers

**1 Year:**
- Visual acuity approximately 20/50 to 20/100
- Good fixation and tracking
- Reaches accurately for objects
- Beginning depth perception
- Social eye contact developing

**2 Years:**
- Visual acuity approaching 20/30 to 20/40
- Can match pictures and shapes
- Developing stereopsis (60-100 arc seconds)
- Improving visual-motor coordination
- Beginning to understand simple commands

**3 Years:**
- Visual acuity approximately 20/25 to 20/30
- Can perform picture acuity tests
- Full stereopsis developing
- Better cooperation for examination
- Can follow simple instructions

### Behavioral Considerations

**Common Challenges:**
- Stranger anxiety (peaks 12-24 months)
- Separation anxiety
- Short attention span (2-5 minutes)
- Fear of equipment
- Language limitations
- Resistance to examination

**Effective Strategies:**
- Parent holding child during exam
- Use of toys and distraction
- Gradual introduction of equipment
- Play-based examination techniques
- Minimizing waiting time
- Positive reinforcement and praise

## Examination Setup and Environment

### Room Preparation

**Optimal Environment:**
- Child-friendly décor
- Dimmable lights
- Age-appropriate toys for distraction
- Comfortable seating for parent
- Equipment within easy reach
- Non-threatening appearance

**Equipment Needed:**
- Picture acuity charts (Lea, Allen)
- Tumbling E chart
- Preferential looking cards (Teller)
- Retinoscope and lenses
- Cycloplegic drops
- Prisms
- Cover paddle
- Penlight or transilluminator
- Direct ophthalmoscope
- Indirect ophthalmoscope

### Approach Techniques

**Building Rapport:**
1. Enter room calmly and slowly
2. Speak softly and gently
3. Address child by name
4. Get down to child's eye level
5. Offer toy or sticker
6. Comment on child's clothing or toy
7. Demonstrate equipment on parent or toy first
8. Use play language

**Examination Order:**
1. History from parent
2. Observation and external exam
3. Visual acuity (least threatening)
4. Cover testing and motility
5. Pupils and red reflex
6. Refraction (objective)
7. Fundus examination (most invasive)

## Visual Acuity Assessment

### Test Selection by Age and Ability

**12-24 Months:**
- Preferential looking (Teller cards)
- Cardiff cards
- Behavioral observations
- Fixation preference testing

**24-36 Months:**
- Lea symbols
- Allen pictures
- HOTV matching
- Tumbling E (may be too difficult)

### Preferential Looking Technique

**Principle:**
Children instinctively look at a patterned stimulus rather than a homogeneous gray field. By presenting grating patterns of increasing fineness, grating acuity can be measured.

**Procedure:**
1. Position child on parent's lap
2. Hold cards at appropriate distance (typically 55cm)
3. Present cards with grating on left or right
4. Observe child's eye movements
5. Increase spatial frequency until child no longer shows preference
6. Record finest grating detected

**Interpretation:**
- Compare to age-appropriate norms
- Note any asymmetry between eyes
- Grating acuity ≠ Snellen acuity
- Useful for detecting moderate-severe vision loss

### Picture Acuity Testing

**Lea Symbols:**
- Four symbols: apple, house, circle, square
- Available in multiple formats
- Can use matching or naming
- Crowded and single symbol versions

**Allen Pictures:**
- Familiar objects: cake, bird, horse, etc.
- May be culturally dependent
- Less standardized than Lea

**Technique:**
1. Start with largest symbols
2. Present at appropriate distance (10-20 feet)
3. Use matching card if child won't verbalize
4. Progress to smaller symbols
5. Record smallest line where child correctly identifies >50%

**Monocular Testing:**
- Attempt if cooperation allows
- May need to use pirate patch, tape on glasses, or hand occlusion
- Accept binocular acuity if monocular not possible

### Fixation Preference Testing

**Indications:**
- Cannot obtain monocular acuity
- Suspected amblyopia
- Strabismus present

**Technique:**
1. Observe fixation behavior under cover
2. Grade as: CSM (central, steady, maintained)
3. Compare fixation quality between eyes
4. Use 10 PD base-down prism to induce fixation switch

**Fixation Grading:**
- **C** - Central: Fixation on fovea
- **S** - Steady: No nystagmus, smooth pursuit
- **M** - Maintained: Holds fixation when other eye uncovered

**Amblyopia Indicators:**
- Not maintained fixation with affected eye
- Prefers one eye consistently
- Fixation not central or not steady

## Refraction Techniques

### Cycloplegic Retinoscopy

**Agent Selection:**
| Age | Agent | Concentration | Notes |
|-----|-------|--------------|-------|
| 12-24 months | Cyclopentolate | 0.5% | May combine with phenylephrine |
| 24-36 months | Cyclopentolate | 0.5-1% | Standard choice |
| Dark irides | Cyclopentolate | 1% + phenylephrine 2.5% | Enhanced dilation |
| Prior reaction | Tropicamide | 1% | Less cycloplegia |

**Instillation Technique:**
1. Explain to parent
2. Have parent hold child securely
3. Gently pull down lower lid
4. Place drop in inferior cul-de-sac
5. Apply gentle pressure to punctum
6. Wait 30-45 minutes for cyclopentolate
7. Assess residual accommodation if possible

**Retinoscopy Procedure:**
1. Darken room
2. Have child look at distance target (cartoon, light)
3. Use working distance lens (+1.50 or +2.00)
4. Neutralize each meridian
5. Work quickly due to limited attention
6. Use lens rack or loose lenses

**Documentation:**
- Note cycloplegic agent used
- Record spherical and cylindrical components
- Note axis
- Calculate net correction (total minus working distance)

### Dry Retinoscopy

**When to Consider:**
- Child already dilated from previous visit
- Follow-up examination
- Assessment of accommodation
- Prior to cycloplegic drops

**Limitations:**
- Active accommodation affects results
- May underestimate hyperopia
- Not definitive for prescribing

## Ocular Alignment Assessment

### Cover Testing

**Technique Modifications:**
- Use engaging fixation target (toy with sound, video)
- Cover paddle with picture or toy
- Brief cover periods
- Multiple attempts if needed
- May need to examine while child distracted

**Cover-Uncover Test:**
1. Cover one eye while observing other
2. Note any movement of uncovered eye
3. Repeat for other eye
4. Detects manifest strabismus (tropia)

**Alternate Cover Test:**
1. Cover one eye, then quickly switch to other
2. Observe movement under cover
3. Detects total deviation (tropia + phoria)
4. May use prism to measure

**Prism Alternate Cover Test:**
1. Place prism before one eye
2. Alternate cover
3. Adjust prism until no movement
4. Record deviation in prism diopters

### Hirschberg and Krimsky Tests

**Indications:**
- Child cannot cooperate with cover testing
- Large angle strabismus
- Poor fixation
- Quick screening

**Hirschberg Method:**
1. Shine light at bridge of nose
2. Observe corneal light reflex position
3. 1mm decentration = approximately 15 PD
4. Temporal displacement = esotropia
5. Nasal displacement = exotropia

**Krimsky Method:**
1. Place prism before fixating eye
2. Increase prism until reflex centered
3. Record deviation in prism diopters
4. More accurate than Hirschberg alone

### Pseudostrabismus Assessment

**Common Causes:**
- Epicanthal folds
- Broad, flat nasal bridge
- Narrow interpupillary distance
- Positive angle kappa

**Differentiation:**
| Feature | Pseudostrabismus | True Strabismus |
|---------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Cover test | No movement | Movement detected |
| Hirschberg | Symmetric reflexes | Asymmetric reflexes |
| Red reflex (Brückner) | Symmetric | Asymmetric |
| Angle | Appears crossed but measures straight | Measures deviated |

**Parent Education:**
- Demonstrate pseudostrabismus with lighting
- Explain anatomical causes
- Reassure regarding normal development
- Discuss when to return

## Motility Examination

### Versions and Ductions

**Assessment Techniques:**
- Move toy in H-pattern
- Use interesting target with sound
- Observe range and smoothness
- Note overactions and underactions
- Check for strabismus in positions of gaze

**Grading Scale (-4 to +4):**
- 0: Normal movement
- -1 to -4: Underaction (progressive limitation)
- +1 to +4: Overaction

**Common Findings:**
- Infantile esotropia: Cross-fixation, limited abduction
- Duane syndrome: Limitation of abduction/adduction
- Superior oblique palsy: Head tilt, hypertropia
- Congenital fibrosis: Severe restriction

### Near Point of Convergence (NPC)

**Technique:**
1. Use interesting small target
2. Move slowly toward nose
3. Observe for break point (eye drifts out)
4. Note distance from nose

**Normal Values:**
- Break point: <5 cm from nose
- Recovery: <7 cm from nose

**Significance:**
- Near point >6 cm: Consider convergence insufficiency
- May not be reliable in this age group
- Repeat if initial results abnormal

## Binocular Vision Assessment

### Stereopsis Testing

**Available Tests:**

**Lang Stereotest:**
- No glasses required
- Pass/fail assessment
- Useful for screening
- Quick and easy

**Titmus Fly:**
- Polarized glasses required
- Fly, animals, circles
- May be too complex for toddlers

**Randot Preschool:**
- Glasses required
- Age-appropriate shapes
- Multiple levels

**Technique:**
1. Demonstrate on parent first
2. Use positive reinforcement
3. Don't force if child refuses glasses
4. Record any response suggesting stereopsis

**Interpretation:**
- Presence of stereopsis: Generally good binocularity
- Absent stereopsis: May indicate strabismus or amblyopia
- May be unreliable in very young toddlers

## Pupillary and Neurological Assessment

### Pupil Examination

**Technique:**
- Use dim room
- Distract with toy or video
- Shine light from below
- Observe both pupils

**Assessment:**
- Size (mm)
- Symmetry
- Direct response
- Consensual response
- Afferent pupillary defect (swinging flashlight)

**Normal Findings:**
- Equal pupils (anisocoria <1mm normal)
- Brisk responses
- No afferent defect

**Abnormal Findings:**
- Anisocoria >1mm: Investigate cause
- Sluggish response: Optic nerve pathology, pharmacological
- Afferent defect: Optic nerve or retinal disease

### Red Reflex Testing

**Brückner Test:**
1. Darken room slightly
2. Use direct ophthalmoscope at arm's length
3. Focus on both pupils simultaneously
4. Compare symmetry

**Interpretation:**
- Symmetric red reflexes: Normal
- Asymmetric: Consider strabismus or media opacity
- White reflex: Urgent evaluation for retinoblastoma

## Fundus Examination

### Techniques

**Indirect Ophthalmoscopy:**
- Most common method
- Use 20D or 28D lens
- May need to hold child's head
- Quick sweep for major pathology

**Direct Ophthalmoscopy:**
- Better disc visualization
- More difficult to perform
- May need to wait until end of exam

**Tips for Success:**
- Keep examination brief
- Use parent to hold child
- Target key structures: disc, macula, vessels
- May need to defer to sedated exam if needed

### Key Structures

**Optic Disc:**
- Size and shape
- Cup-to-disc ratio
- Pallor
- Swelling or edema

**Macula:**
- Foveal reflex
- Abnormalities

**Peripheral Retina:**
- If cooperative
- Check for abnormalities

## Special Considerations

### The Uncooperative Child

**Strategies:**
1. Take breaks during examination
2. Return to less threatening tests
3. Use parent to comfort and distract
4. Consider second attempt later
5. May need sedation for complete exam

**Sedation Indications:**
- Incomplete examination due to behavior
- Suspicion of serious pathology
- Need for detailed fundus exam
- Intraocular pressure measurement

### Developmental Delay

**Modified Approach:**
- Adapt to developmental age, not chronological age
- More reliance on objective measures
- Parent assistance crucial
- Consider multiple shorter visits
- Behavioral observations important

### Autism Spectrum Disorder

**Special Considerations:**
- Prepare environment (low stimulation)
- Visual schedule may help
- Allow time for adjustment
- Use preferred objects as targets
- May need modified examination sequence
- Consider desensitization visits

## Documentation

### Essential Elements

1. **History:**
   - Birth history
   - Developmental milestones
   - Family history
   - Current concerns

2. **Visual Acuity:**
   - Method used
   - Binocular and monocular if obtained
   - Comparison to age norms

3. **Ocular Alignment:**
   - Cover test findings
   - Hirschberg/Krimsky measurements
   - Deviation magnitude

4. **Motility:**
   - Range of motion
   - Overactions/underactions
   - Nystagmus

5. **Refraction:**
   - Cycloplegic agent
   - Findings in each eye

6. **Anterior Segment:**
   - External findings
   - Pupils
   - Red reflex

7. **Fundus:**
   - Disc, macula, vessels
   - Any abnormalities

8. **Impression and Plan:**
   - Diagnosis
   - Treatment recommendations
   - Follow-up schedule

## Parent Communication

### Explaining Findings

**Normal Examination:**
- Reassure parents
- Discuss normal development
- Review warning signs
- Schedule next assessment

**Abnormal Findings:**
- Explain condition clearly
- Use diagrams when helpful
- Discuss treatment options
- Provide written information
- Allow time for questions

**Common Concerns:**
- "Will my child need glasses?"
- "Is this lazy eye?"
- "Will the crossing go away?"
- "Can my child see okay?"

## Licensing Exam Points

### Key Concepts:

1. **Visual Acuity Methods:**
   - Preferential looking for preverbal children
   - Picture acuity (Lea) for verbal toddlers
   - Crowded vs. single optotype significance

2. **Strabismus Assessment:**
   - Cover testing technique
   - Hirschberg/Krimsky alternatives
   - Pseudostrabismus differentiation

3. **Cycloplegic Refraction:**
   - Agent selection by age
   - Timing of instillation
   - Interpretation of results

4. **Amblyopia Detection:**
   - Fixation preference testing
   - CSM method
   - Screening for risk factors

5. **Red Flags:**
   - Leukocoria (urgent evaluation)
   - Nystagmus
   - Large angle strabismus
   - Significant refractive error

### Common Test Questions:
- Choosing appropriate acuity test by age
- Differentiating pseudostrabismus from true esotropia
- Cycloplegic agent selection
- Fixation preference interpretation
- Management of high refractive error

## Summary

Successful examination of toddlers requires flexibility, creativity, and patience. By understanding normal visual development, employing age-appropriate techniques, and maintaining a child-friendly approach, practitioners can obtain valuable diagnostic information while minimizing stress for both child and parent. Early detection of vision problems during this critical period can prevent lifelong visual impairment.
