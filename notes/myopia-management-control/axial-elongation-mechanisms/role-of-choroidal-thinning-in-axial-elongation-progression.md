# Role of Choroidal Thinning in Axial Elongation Progression

## Introduction

The choroid, a highly vascularized tissue between the retina and sclera, has emerged as a critical player in myopia development and progression. Choroidal thinning precedes and predicts axial elongation, making it both a biomarker for myopia risk and a potential therapeutic target. Understanding choroidal dynamics is essential for modern myopia management.

### Clinical Relevance for Licensing Examinations

Choroidal assessment and its relationship to myopia is tested in:
- Ocular Disease sections (myopia complications)
- Clinical Optometry sections (myopia monitoring)
- Diagnostic Procedures sections (OCT interpretation)

## Normal Choroidal Anatomy and Physiology

### Gross Structure

The choroid extends from the ora serrata to the optic nerve and consists of four primary layers:

| Layer | Location | Primary Function |
|-------|----------|------------------|
| Bruch's membrane | Innermost | Structural support, exchange barrier |
| Choriocapillaris | Inner vascular layer | Nutrient supply to outer retina |
| Sattler's layer | Medium vessel layer | Blood flow regulation |
| Haller's layer | Outer large vessel layer | Blood drainage |

### Vascular Supply

- **Blood flow**: Highest of any tissue (relative to weight)
- **Primary supply**: Short posterior ciliary arteries
- **Venous drainage**: Vortex veins
- **Autonomic regulation**: Sympathetic vasoconstriction, parasympathetic vasodilation

### Functions in the Normal Eye

1. **Nutritional support**: Supplies outer retinal layers (photoreceptors, RPE)
2. **Thermoregulation**: Heat dissipation
3. **Waste removal**: Removes metabolic byproducts
4. **Light absorption**: Melanin reduces scatter
5. **Growth signaling**: Produces factors influencing scleral remodeling

## Choroidal Changes in Myopia Development

### The Temporal Sequence

Research demonstrates that choroidal thinning precedes axial elongation:

```
Normal eye → Choroidal thinning → Scleral remodeling → Axial elongation → Myopia progression
              (weeks to months)    (concurrent)         (result)
```

### Quantitative Changes

#### Subfoveal Choroidal Thickness (SFCT)

| Population | Average SFCT (μm) | Reference |
|------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Emmetropes | 300-350 | Various studies |
| Low myopia | 250-300 | Margolis et al. |
| Moderate myopia | 200-250 | Fujiwara et al. |
| High myopia | 100-200 | Nishida et al. |
| Pathological myopia | <100 | Ohno-Matsui et al. |

#### Regional Variations
- **Subfoveal**: Most commonly measured
- **Nasal**: Generally thinner than temporal
- **Superior**: Often thicker than inferior
- **Peripapillary**: Important for glaucoma assessment

### Mechanisms of Choroidal Thinning

#### 1. Mechanical Stretching
- Globe elongation stretches all tunics
- Choroidal surface area increases without proportional volume increase
- Results in thinning proportional to axial length

#### 2. Vascular Changes
- Reduced choriocapillaris density
- Decreased vessel lumen diameter
- Increased vascular resistance
- Reduced choroidal blood flow

#### 3. Stroma Reduction
- Decreased interstitial tissue
- Reduced fibroblast content
- Altered extracellular matrix composition

#### 4. Active Remodeling
- Choroid is not merely passively stretched
- Active tissue remodeling occurs
- Growth factors influence choroidal thickness

## The Choroid as a Signaling Interface

### Retinal-Scleral Communication

The choroid serves as an intermediate signaling layer between the retina (which detects blur) and the sclera (which determines eye size):

```
Peripheral Retina (blur detection)
        ↓
RPE (signal modulation)
        ↓
Choroid (growth factor production)
        ↓
Sclera (remodeling response)
        ↓
Eye Growth
```

### Key Signaling Molecules

#### Dopamine
- **Source**: Retina
- **Effect on choroid**: Maintains normal thickness
- **Myopia association**: Reduced dopamine associated with thinning
- **Therapeutic implication**: Outdoor light increases dopamine

#### Vascular Endothelial Growth Factor (VEGF)
- **Role**: Angiogenesis and vascular permeability
- **Altered in myopia**: Expression changes noted
- **Clinical relevance**: Anti-VEGF for myopic CNV

#### Transforming Growth Factor-β (TGF-β)
- **Role**: Tissue remodeling
- **Effect**: Influences both choroid and sclera
- **Altered signaling in myopia**

#### Fibroblast Growth Factor (FGF)
- **Role**: Cell proliferation and survival
- **Relevance**: Choroidal fibroblast activity

### Choroidal Response to Defocus

#### Hyperopic Defocus (Myopic Blur)
- **Effect**: Choroidal thinning
- **Result**: Signals for axial elongation
- **Mechanism**: Growth factor release

#### Myopic Defocus (Hyperopic Blur)
- **Effect**: Choroidal thickening
- **Result**: Signals for growth inhibition
- **Mechanism**: Different growth factor profile

This bidirectional response forms the basis for myopia control treatments that induce myopic defocus.

## Clinical Assessment of Choroidal Thickness

### Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

#### Enhanced Depth Imaging (EDI-OCT)
- **Technique**: Inverted image, closer zero delay
- **Advantage**: Better choroidal visualization
- **Clinical standard**: Most practices use EDI protocols

#### Swept-Source OCT
- **Wavelength**: 1050 nm (better choroidal penetration)
- **Advantages**: 
  - Full choroidal thickness visualization
  - Less signal attenuation
  - Better scleral imaging

#### Measurement Technique
1. Identify RPE/Bruch's membrane complex (inner boundary)
2. Identify choroid-sclera interface (outer boundary)
3. Measure perpendicular distance between boundaries

### Normal Choroidal Thickness Values

#### Age-Related Changes
- **Children (6-12 years)**: 300-350 μm average
- **Young adults (18-30 years)**: 320-370 μm average
- **Middle age (40-60 years)**: 280-330 μm average
- **Elderly (>70 years)**: 200-250 μm average

#### Sex Differences
- Females: Generally 10-20 μm thinner than males
- Adjust for age when assessing

### Factors Affecting Choroidal Thickness

| Factor | Effect on Thickness | Clinical Consideration |
|--------|---------------------|------------------------|
| Age | Decreases with age | Age-matched comparisons needed |
| Axial length | Inverse relationship | Myopia adjustment |
| Refractive error | Thinner in myopia | Confounding in myopia studies |
| Time of day | Thinner in evening | Standardize timing |
| Blood pressure | Variable effects | Consider systemic conditions |
| Intraocular pressure | Complex relationship | IOP measurement timing |

## Choroidal Thickness as a Predictor of Myopia Progression

### Prospective Studies

Research demonstrates that thinner choroids predict faster progression:

| Study | Finding | Implication |
|-------|---------|-------------|
| Read et al. (2018) | Thin choroid → faster AL growth | Monitoring tool |
| Jin et al. (2016) | Choroid thins before progression | Early warning sign |
| Chung et al. (2021) | Choroid thickens with OK treatment | Treatment monitoring |

### Clinical Application: Progression Risk Assessment

A practical approach to using choroidal thickness clinically:

1. **Baseline measurement**: EDI-OCT at initial myopia assessment
2. **Age-adjusted comparison**: Compare to age-matched norms
3. **Trend analysis**: Serial measurements over time
4. **Risk stratification**:
   - Normal thickness: Standard risk
   - Moderately thin: Elevated risk
   - Significantly thin: High risk, aggressive intervention indicated

## Choroidal Response to Myopia Control Interventions

### Atropine
- **Effect**: Dose-dependent choroidal thickening
- **Mechanism**: Muscarinic receptor effects on vasculature
- **Clinical correlation**: Thickening associated with treatment efficacy

### Orthokeratology
- **Effect**: Choroidal thickening with OK wear
- **Mechanism**: Induced peripheral myopic defocus
- **Evidence**: Multiple studies show 10-20 μm increase

### DIMS and HAL Spectacles
- **Effect**: Modest choroidal thickening
- **Mechanism**: Peripheral myopic defocus induction
- **Clinical significance**: Suggests active mechanism

### Multifocal Contact Lenses
- **Effect**: Variable choroidal response
- **Mechanism**: Add power creates myopic defocus
- **Research**: Ongoing studies

## Choroidal Abnormalities in High Myopia

### Choroidal Folds
- **Appearance**: Linear or curvilinear folds
- **Cause**: Compression from scleral shape changes
- **Significance**: May indicate structural stress

### Chorioretinal Atrophy
- **Types**: 
  - Diffuse atrophy
  - Patchy atrophy
- **Progression**: Associated with visual field loss
- **Management**: Regular monitoring, reduced vision risk

### Choroidal Neovascularization (CNV)
- **Risk**: Increases with axial length
- **Presentation**: Metamorphopsia, decreased vision
- **Treatment**: Anti-VEGF therapy
- **Prognosis**: Variable, better with early treatment

## Examination Preparation: Key Points

### High-Yield Facts

1. **Choroid thins BEFORE axial elongation occurs**
2. **SFCT decreases approximately 10-15 μm per diopter of myopia**
3. **Choroid is thickest in young adults, thins with age**
4. **EDI-OCT or swept-source OCT required for accurate measurement**
5. **Choroidal thickening with treatment suggests efficacy**

### Clinical Case Scenarios

**Scenario 1**: A 10-year-old myopic child has SFCT of 220 μm. What does this indicate?
- Analysis: Below average for age (300+ μm expected)
- Implication: Elevated risk for progression
- Action: Consider more aggressive intervention

**Scenario 2**: A child on atropine shows 25 μm choroidal thickening after 6 months. Interpret.
- Analysis: Positive treatment response
- Implication: Atropine is effective
- Action: Continue current treatment

### Differential Diagnosis of Choroidal Thinning

1. **Myopia** (most common)
2. **Age-related thinning**
3. **Age-related macular degeneration**
4. **High myopia with staphyloma**
5. **Choroidal ischemia**
6. **Previous inflammation**

## Summary Table: Choroidal Assessment in Myopia Management

| Assessment | Method | Finding | Clinical Action |
|------------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| Baseline SFCT | EDI-OCT | Thin vs normal | Risk stratification |
| Progression prediction | Serial SFCT | Thinning trend | Intensify intervention |
| Treatment monitoring | Serial SFCT | Thickening | Confirm efficacy |
| Complication risk | Wide-field OCT | Atrophy, CNV | Refer/monitor |

## Conclusion

Choroidal thickness provides a valuable window into myopia development and progression. As both a predictor and a treatment response marker, choroidal assessment should be considered in comprehensive myopia management. Understanding choroidal physiology and pathology is essential for optometrists managing myopia and for success on licensing examinations.
