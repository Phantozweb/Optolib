# Myopia Control Strategies

## Introduction

Myopia prevalence is increasing globally, with significant implications for ocular health. Myopia control strategies aim to slow progression and reduce associated risks of sight-threatening complications.

## Rationale for Myopia Control

### Why Control Myopia?

**High Myopia Risks:**
- Retinal detachment (8-10x increased risk)
- Myopic macular degeneration
- Glaucoma
- Cataracts
- Visual impairment

**Economic Impact:**
- Lifetime costs of myopia management
- Productivity losses from visual impairment
- Healthcare system burden

## Treatment Options

### 1. Low-Dose Atropine

**Efficacy:**
- ATOM studies: 0.01% reduces progression by ~50%
- Sustained effect with rebound minimized
- Well-tolerated with minimal side effects

**Protocol:**
- Start at 0.01% nightly
- Monitor for efficacy
- Increase concentration if needed (0.025%, 0.05%)
- Continue until myopia stabilizes

### 2. Orthokeratology

**Mechanism:**
- Corneal reshaping
- Peripheral retinal defocus
- Temporarily corrects myopia

**Efficacy:**
- 40-60% reduction in axial elongation
- Daytime freedom from glasses
- Requires careful fitting and monitoring

### 3. Specialty Spectacle Lenses

**Types:**
- DIMS (Defocus Incorporated Multiple Segments)
- HALT (Highly Aspheric Lenslet Target)
- Peripheral defocus lenses

**Efficacy:**
- 50-60% reduction in progression
- Non-invasive option
- Good for younger children

### 4. Soft Multifocal Contact Lenses

**Design:**
- Center distance with near add
- Creates peripheral myopic defocus
- Daily disposable options available

**Efficacy:**
- 40-50% reduction in progression
- Good compliance
- Suitable for active children

### 5. Increased Outdoor Time

**Evidence:**
- 2+ hours daily outdoor activity
- 40-50% reduced incidence
- Dopamine-mediated mechanism
- Bright light exposure

## Comparative Efficacy

| Intervention | Progression Reduction | Rebound Risk | Age Suitability |
|-------------|----------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Atropine 0.01% | 40-50% | Low | All ages |
| Ortho-K | 45-55% | Moderate | 8+ years |
| DIMS lenses | 50-60% | Unknown | All ages |
| Soft MF contacts | 40-50% | Low | 8+ years |
| Outdoor time | Prevention | None | All ages |

## Monitoring Protocol

### Assessment Schedule

| Visit | Measurements |
|-------|-------------|
| Baseline | VA, cycloplegic Rx, axial length, corneal topography |
| 3 months | VA, subjective Rx, compliance check |
| 6 months | Full assessment including axial length |
| Annual | Comprehensive review |

### Treatment Decisions

**Efficacy Assessment:**
- <0.25 D progression: Continue current treatment
- 0.25-0.50 D: Consider combination therapy
- >0.50 D: Change or augment treatment

## Combination Therapy

### Rationale

Combining treatments may enhance efficacy through different mechanisms.

**Common Combinations:**
- Atropine + Ortho-K
- Atropine + specialty lenses
- Multiple interventions for rapid progressors

## Patient Selection

### Ideal Candidates

**High-Risk Features:**
- Age <12 at onset
- Parental myopia
- Asian ethnicity
- Limited outdoor time
- High near work demands
- Previous rapid progression

## Counseling Points

### Parent Discussion

1. Natural history of myopia
2. Treatment options and efficacy
3. Commitment required
4. Monitoring schedule
5. Realistic expectations

## Licensing Exam Points

1. Treatment mechanisms
2. Comparative efficacy
3. Monitoring parameters
4. Combination therapy rationale
5. High-risk patient identification

## Summary

Multiple evidence-based myopia control options exist. Treatment selection should consider efficacy, safety, patient age, lifestyle, and parental preferences.
