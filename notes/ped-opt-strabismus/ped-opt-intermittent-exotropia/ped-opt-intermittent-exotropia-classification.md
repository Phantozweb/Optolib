# Intermittent Exotropia Classification

## Introduction

Intermittent exotropia (IXT) is the most common form of childhood exotropia, characterized by an exophoria that intermittently breaks down to exotropia. Understanding classification systems guides management decisions.

## Classification Systems

### By Distance-Near Relationship

**1. Basic Type**
- Distance deviation ≈ Near deviation (within 10 PD)
- Most common type
- Standard surgical approach

**2. Divergence Excess**
- Distance deviation > Near deviation (>10 PD)
- May have high AC/A
- Challenge in surgical planning

**3. Convergence Insufficiency Type**
- Near deviation > Distance deviation
- Often associated with symptoms
- Nonsurgical treatment first

### By Control

**Minnesota Control Scale:**

| Score | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| 0 | Excellent - Rarely breaks, rapid recovery |
| 1 | Good - Occasional break, easy recovery |
| 2 | Fair - Frequent break, variable recovery |
| 3 | Poor - Constant break, difficult recovery |
| 4 | Very Poor - Constant exotropia |

**Newcastle Control Score:**
Combines home and clinic observations to guide intervention timing.

## Clinical Features

### Symptoms

**Common Complaints:**
- Closing one eye in bright light
- Eye fatigue with prolonged viewing
- Blurred vision
- Headaches
- Difficulty with reading
- Diplopia (rare in children)

### Signs

**Examination Findings:**
- Exophoria that breaks to exotropia
- Larger deviation at distance typically
- May have suppression
- Often good stereopsis when aligned
- Increasing deviation with fatigue

## Evaluation

### Assessment Protocol

1. **Distance Cover Test:**
   - At 20 feet or beyond
   - Note frequency of breakdown
   - Assess control quality

2. **Near Cover Test:**
   - At 33 cm
   - Compare to distance

3. **Prolonged Occlusion:**
   - 30-60 minutes if divergence excess suspected
   - Reveals true near deviation

4. **Measurement:**
   - Prism alternate cover test
   - Distance and near
   - Multiple measurements

## Management Considerations

### Nonsurgical Options

**1. Observation:**
- Small, well-controlled deviations
- Good stereopsis maintained
- No progression

**2. Overminus Lenses:**
- -1.50 to -2.50 additional power
- Stimulates convergence
- Trial for 3-6 months
- More effective in younger children

**3. Vision Therapy:**
- Convergence training
- Anti-suppression exercises
- Fusion enhancement
- Better evidence for CI type

### Surgical Indications

**Criteria for Surgery:**
- Poor control (≥3 on control scales)
- Increasing frequency of tropia phase
- Deteriorating stereopsis
- Large angle (>15-20 PD)
- Significant symptoms
- Social concerns

## Surgical Planning

### Procedure Selection

**1. Bilateral Lateral Rectus Recession (BLR):**
- Most common procedure
- For basic and divergence excess types
- Predictable outcomes

**2. Unilateral Recession-Resection (R&R):**
- Alternative approach
- May be preferred for smaller deviations

**3. Augmented Procedures:**
- For larger deviations
- Combined with prism adaptation

## Licensing Exam Points

1. Classification types (basic, divergence excess, CI)
2. Control assessment scales
3. Nonsurgical management options
4. Surgical indications
5. Procedure selection

## Summary

Intermittent exotropia classification guides management decisions. Understanding control mechanisms and progression risk helps determine optimal intervention timing.
