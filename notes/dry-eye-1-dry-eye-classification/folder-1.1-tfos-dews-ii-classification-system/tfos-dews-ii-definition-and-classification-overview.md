# TFOS DEWS II Definition and Classification Overview

## Introduction

The Tear Film and Ocular Surface Society (TFOS) Dry Eye Workshop II (DEWS II), published in 2017, represents the most comprehensive and evidence-based consensus on dry eye disease classification, diagnosis, and management. This landmark publication updated the original DEWS I report from 2007, incorporating a decade of scientific advances and clinical insights.

## Definition of Dry Eye Disease

The DEWS II definition states:

**"Dry eye is a multifactorial disease of the ocular surface characterized by a loss of homeostasis of the tear film, and accompanied by ocular symptoms, in which tear film instability and hyperosmolarity, ocular surface inflammation and damage, and neurosensory abnormalities play etiological roles."**

### Key Components of the Definition

1. **Multifactorial Nature**: Dry eye disease cannot be attributed to a single cause. Multiple interacting factors contribute to the pathophysiology, including lacrimal gland dysfunction, meibomian gland disorders, ocular surface changes, and neurological factors.

2. **Loss of Homeostasis**: The tear film exists in a delicate balance. When this balance is disrupted, a cascade of pathological changes occurs. Homeostasis refers to the stable state of the tear film, including proper tear volume, osmolarity, stability, and composition.

3. **Ocular Symptoms**: Patient-reported symptoms are essential for diagnosis. The discrepancy between signs and symptoms is well-documented, making patient history and symptom assessment crucial.

4. **Tear Film Instability**: The first sign of dry eye is often tear film instability, measured by reduced tear break-up time (TBUT). This instability leads to increased evaporation and osmolarity changes.

5. **Hyperosmolarity**: Elevated tear osmolarity (>308 mOsm/L) is a core mechanism driving ocular surface damage and inflammation.

6. **Ocular Surface Inflammation**: Inflammation is both a cause and consequence of dry eye, creating a self-perpetuating cycle.

7. **Neurosensory Abnormalities**: The role of corneal nerve function in dry eye is increasingly recognized, including hypersensitivity and hyposensitivity patterns.

## Classification System

### Aqueous Deficient Dry Eye (ADDE)

Aqueous deficient dry eye results from inadequate tear secretion by the lacrimal glands. This category includes:

#### Sjögren's Syndrome-Related
- **Primary Sjögren's Syndrome**: Autoimmune exocrinopathy without associated connective tissue disease
- **Secondary Sjögren's Syndrome**: Associated with other autoimmune conditions (rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, scleroderma)

#### Non-Sjögren's Aqueous Deficiency
- Age-related lacrimal gland atrophy
- Lacrimal gland duct obstruction
- Reflex hyposecretion (sensory loss, cranial nerve damage)
- Systemic medications (anticholinergics, antihistamines, diuretics)
- Graft-versus-host disease

### Evaporative Dry Eye (EDE)

Evaporative dry eye results from excessive water loss from the exposed ocular surface. Causes include:

#### Intrinsic Factors
- **Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)**: The most common cause, affecting the lipid layer
- **Disorders of Lid Aperture**: Excessive ocular surface exposure
- **Blink Disorders**: Reduced blink rate, incomplete blinking
- **Ocular Surface Disorders**: Mucin deficiency, allergy

#### Extrinsic Factors
- **Vitamin A Deficiency**: Affects mucin production
- **Topical Preservatives**: Benzalkonium chloride toxicity
- **Contact Lens Wear**: Alters tear film dynamics
- **Ocular Surface Disease**: Allergic conjunctivitis

### Mixed Mechanism Dry Eye

Most patients present with features of both aqueous deficiency and evaporative components. DEWS II recognizes that pure forms are uncommon in clinical practice.

## Disease Staging

### Stage 1: Mild
- Symptoms present but intermittent
- TBUT slightly reduced (<10 seconds)
- Minimal or no staining
- Normal Schirmer test
- No visual impact

### Stage 2: Moderate
- Frequent symptoms
- TBUT significantly reduced (<5 seconds)
- Mild corneal and conjunctival staining
- Schirmer test may be reduced
- Some visual fluctuation

### Stage 3: Severe
- Constant symptoms
- Marked TBUT reduction
- Significant corneal staining
- Reduced Schirmer values
- Marked visual disturbance

### Stage 4: Very Severe
- Severe, disabling symptoms
- Severe ocular surface damage
- Possible corneal ulceration
- Marked visual impairment

## Risk Factors

### Non-Modifiable Risk Factors
- Age (increasing prevalence with age)
- Female sex (hormonal factors)
- Asian ethnicity (higher prevalence)
- Genetics/family history

### Modifiable Risk Factors
- Digital device use
- Contact lens wear
- Environmental conditions
- Medications
- Smoking

## Clinical Implications

The DEWS II classification provides a framework for:

1. **Diagnosis**: Systematic evaluation using standardized tests
2. **Subtyping**: Differentiating aqueous deficiency from evaporative dry eye
3. **Staging**: Assessing severity to guide treatment intensity
4. **Management**: Stepwise approach based on disease stage
5. **Research**: Standardized definitions for clinical studies

## Examination of Licensing Exams

For optometry licensing examinations (NBEO, DHA, MOH, DOH), candidates should:

1. Know the DEWS II definition verbatim
2. Understand the classification system (ADDE vs EDE)
3. Recognize key diagnostic criteria
4. Apply staging to clinical scenarios
5. Identify risk factors and their modification

## Key Takeaways

- DEWS II provides the current gold standard for dry eye classification
- Loss of tear film homeostasis is central to the definition
- Both aqueous deficiency and evaporation contribute to most cases
- Staging guides treatment intensity
- Symptoms are essential but may not correlate with signs
