# Punctal Occlusion in Aqueous Deficiency

## Introduction

Punctal occlusion is a mechanical intervention to preserve tears on the ocular surface by blocking the drainage system. It is particularly beneficial for patients with aqueous deficient dry eye.

## Indications

**Primary Indication:**
- Moderate to severe aqueous deficient dry eye
- Inadequate response to artificial tears
- Low Schirmer test values
- Significant ocular surface damage

**Relative Indications:**
- Contact lens intolerance due to dryness
- Post-refractive surgery dry eye
- Neurotrophic keratopathy
- Patients using preserved drops frequently

## Types of Punctal Plugs

### Collagen (Dissolvable) Plugs

**Characteristics:**
- Temporary (1-2 weeks)
- Used for trial before permanent plugs
- Dissolve naturally
- No removal needed

**Use:**
- Test tolerance to occlusion
- Predict therapeutic effect
- Identify epiphora risk

### Silicone Plugs

**Characteristics:**
- Semi-permanent
- Removable if needed
- Available in various sizes
- Can be visible at punctum

**Types:**
- Umbrella design (visible cap)
- Intraductal (inserted deeper)
- Tailless designs

### Thermosensitive Polymer Plugs

**Characteristics:**
- Liquid when cold, solid at body temperature
- Intraductal placement
- Less visible
- May last months

## Punctal Cautery

**Types:**
- Thermal cautery
- Electrocautery
- Laser cautery

**Considerations:**
- Permanent occlusion
- For patients who respond well to plugs
- Recanalization possible
- Requires local anesthesia

## Procedure for Silicone Plug Insertion

**Pre-procedure:**
1. Document baseline Schirmer
2. Assess for epiphora risk
3. Consider collagen plug trial

**Procedure:**
1. Apply topical anesthetic
2. Measure punctum size
3. Dilate punctum if needed
4. Insert plug with forceps
5. Verify proper placement
6. Confirm patient comfort

**Post-procedure:**
- Patient may use artificial tears
- Follow-up in 1-2 weeks
- Document retention

## Complications

**Common:**
- Plug loss (20-40% over 2 years)
- Foreign body sensation
- Epiphora

**Less Common:**
- Canaliculitis
- Pyogenic granuloma
- Plug migration
- Scarring

## Clinical Pearls for Exams

1. Try collagen plugs first to test effect
2. Silicone plugs are semi-permanent and removable
3. Cautery is permanent
4. Avoid if Schirmer >10 mm without anesthesia
5. Upper and lower plugs may be needed in severe cases
6. Watch for canaliculitis as complication
