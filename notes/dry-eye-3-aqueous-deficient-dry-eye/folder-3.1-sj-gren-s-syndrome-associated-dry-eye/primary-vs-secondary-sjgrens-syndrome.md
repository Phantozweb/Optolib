# Primary vs Secondary Sjögren's Syndrome

## Introduction

Sjögren's syndrome is a chronic autoimmune disorder characterized by lymphocytic infiltration and destruction of exocrine glands, primarily the lacrimal and salivary glands. Understanding the distinction between primary and secondary forms is essential for appropriate management and patient counseling.

## Primary Sjögren's Syndrome

### Definition
Primary Sjögren's syndrome occurs as an isolated autoimmune condition without the presence of another defined connective tissue disease.

### Clinical Features

**Sicca Symptoms:**
- Dry eyes (keratoconjunctivitis sicca)
- Dry mouth (xerostomia)
- May affect other exocrine glands

**Systemic Manifestations:**
- Arthralgias/arthritis
- Fatigue
- Raynaud's phenomenon
- Pulmonary involvement
- Renal involvement
- Vasculitis
- Lymphoproliferative disorders

### Diagnostic Criteria (ACR/EULAR 2016)

The 2016 ACR/EULAR classification criteria require:
- Score ≥4 points

**Scoring System:**
- Focal lymphocytic sialadenitis with focus score ≥1: 3 points
- Anti-SSA/Ro positive: 3 points
- Ocular Staining Score ≥5 (or van Bijsterveld score ≥4): 1 point
- Schirmer test ≤5 mm/5 min: 1 point
- Unstimulated whole salivary flow ≤0.1 mL/min: 1 point

### Autoantibodies

**Anti-SSA/Ro:**
- Present in 60-70% of patients
- More specific for Sjögren's
- Associated with more severe disease

**Anti-SSB/La:**
- Present in 40-50% of patients
- Rarely found without anti-SSA
- Associated with later disease onset

**ANA:**
- Present in 80% of patients
- Less specific
- Screening tool

**Rheumatoid Factor:**
- Present in 60-70% of patients
- Less specific

## Secondary Sjögren's Syndrome

### Definition
Secondary Sjögren's syndrome occurs in association with another defined autoimmune connective tissue disease.

### Associated Conditions

**Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA):**
- Most common association
- 20-30% of RA patients develop secondary Sjögren's
- May affect disease prognosis

**Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE):**
- 15-20% of SLE patients
- May indicate overlapping features

**Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma):**
- 15-20% of patients
- Can complicate management

**Mixed Connective Tissue Disease:**
- Common feature
- Multiple overlapping symptoms

**Primary Biliary Cholangitis:**
- Strong association
- Consider in patients with liver involvement

### Clinical Differences

**Compared to Primary Sjögren's:**
- Sicca symptoms may be less prominent
- Underlying disease may dominate
- Autoantibody profile reflects primary condition
- Treatment focuses on primary disease

## Ocular Manifestations

### Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca
- Severe aqueous deficiency
- Persistent symptoms despite treatment
- Higher risk of complications

### Characteristic Findings
- Severe conjunctival staining
- Corneal filaments
- Reduced TBUT
- Very low Schirmer values

### Management Challenges
- Requires aggressive treatment
- Multiple modalities often needed
- May need serum tears
- Regular monitoring essential

## Clinical Pearls for Licensing Exams

1. Primary Sjögren's is isolated; secondary is with other autoimmune disease
2. Anti-SSA/Ro is most specific antibody
3. RA is most common associated condition
4. Lymphoma risk is increased (5-10%)
5. Ocular involvement is typically severe
6. Multidisciplinary care is essential
