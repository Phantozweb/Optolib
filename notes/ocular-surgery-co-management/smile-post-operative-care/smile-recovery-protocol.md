# SMILE Recovery Protocol

## Introduction and Overview

SMILE Recovery Protocol represents a critical aspect of SMILE Procedure - SMILE Post-Operative Care that requires comprehensive understanding for optimal patient care. This detailed guide covers all aspects of clinical assessment, pre-operative considerations, surgical planning, post-operative management, and complication recognition essential for optometric co-management.

The optometrist's role in surgical co-management has expanded significantly, requiring in-depth knowledge of surgical procedures, expected outcomes, potential complications, and appropriate referral criteria. This note provides the foundational knowledge necessary for effective co-management of patients undergoing ocular surgery.

## Anatomy and Pathophysiology

### Relevant Anatomy

Understanding the anatomical structures involved in smile post-operative care is essential for proper patient assessment and surgical planning. Key anatomical considerations include:

**Anterior Segment Structures**
- Cornea: Central thickness typically 500-560 μm, endothelial cell density decreases with age
- Anterior chamber: Depth normally 2.5-3.0 mm, shallower in hyperopes and with age
- Iris and pupil: Normal pupil size 2-4 mm in ambient light, critical for surgical access
- Lens: Crystalline lens approximately 4-5 mm in thickness, increases with age
- Zonular apparatus: Provides lens support, may be compromised in pseudoexfoliation

**Posterior Segment Structures**
- Vitreous: Normally clear gel, may have opacities affecting visualization
- Retina: Macular anatomy critical for visual outcomes
- Optic nerve: Cup-to-disc ratio assessment essential for glaucoma screening
- Choroid and sclera: Consider in trauma and inflammatory conditions

### Pathophysiological Changes

Understanding the disease process helps guide treatment decisions and patient counseling:

1. **Structural Changes**
   - Tissue alterations affecting surgical approach
   - Progressive changes that may impact timing of intervention
   - Secondary effects on surrounding structures
   - Age-related modifications

2. **Functional Implications**
   - Visual acuity changes and progression patterns
   - Impact on quality of life and daily activities
   - Bilateral vs unilateral involvement considerations
   - Potential for improvement with intervention

3. **Risk Factors for Progression**
   - Systemic conditions accelerating disease
   - Medications affecting outcomes
   - Environmental and lifestyle factors
   - Genetic predisposition

## Pre-Operative Assessment

### Patient History

A comprehensive patient history forms the foundation of pre-operative assessment. Essential elements include:

**Chief Complaint Documentation**
- Onset and duration of visual symptoms
- Progression pattern (stable, slowly progressive, rapidly worsening)
- Impact on daily activities (driving, reading, work requirements)
- Previous treatments attempted and their outcomes

**Ocular History**
- Previous ocular surgeries or trauma
- Current and past ocular medications
- Contact lens wear history
- History of ocular inflammation or infection
- Previous refractive surgery (LASIK, PRK, RK)

**Medical History Review**
- Systemic diseases affecting healing (diabetes, autoimmune disorders)
- Cardiovascular status and blood pressure control
- Anticoagulation therapy and bleeding risk
- Allergies (medications, latex, iodine)
- Current medications affecting surgery (alpha-blockers, anticoagulants)

**Family History**
- Ocular conditions (glaucoma, macular degeneration, retinal detachment)
- Anesthetic complications
- Bleeding disorders
- Genetic conditions affecting outcomes

### Clinical Examination Protocol

#### Visual Function Assessment

**Visual Acuity Testing**
- Distance visual acuity (Snellen or LogMAR notation)
- Near visual acuity with appropriate correction
- Pinhole visual acuity to assess refractive potential
- Best-corrected visual acuity (BCVA) with manifest refraction
- Potential acuity meter (PAM) testing when indicated

**Refraction Protocol**
- Manifest refraction with cross-cylinder refinement
- Cycloplegic refraction in appropriate cases
- Comparison with previous prescriptions
- Assessment of refractive stability
- Anisometropia documentation

**Contrast Sensitivity and Color Vision**
- Pelli-Robson contrast sensitivity testing
- Color vision assessment (Ishihara, Farnsworth D-15)
- Functional vision implications
- Baseline for post-operative comparison

#### Anterior Segment Examination

**Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy Protocol**

| Structure | Parameters to Assess | Documentation Required |
|-----------|---------------------|----------------------|
| Eyelids | Position, margin integrity, meibomian glands | Abnormal findings |
| Conjunctiva | Injection, follicles, papillae, scarring | Location and severity |
| Cornea | Clarity, thickness, sensation, staining | Grading scale |
| Anterior chamber | Depth (Van Herick), cells, flare | Cell/flare grade |
| Iris | Architecture, transillumination, synechiae | Descriptive |
| Lens | Opacity type and grade (LOCS III) | Nuclear, cortical, PSC |
| Vitreous | Face integrity, opacities | Present/absent |

**Corneal Assessment**
- Central corneal thickness (pachymetry)
- Corneal topography/tomography
- Endothelial cell count (specular microscopy)
- Corneal sensation testing

**Intraocular Pressure Measurement**
- Goldmann applanation tonometry (gold standard)
- Correlation with corneal thickness
- Diurnal variation when indicated
- Comparison with previous readings

#### Posterior Segment Examination

**Dilated Fundus Examination**
- Vitreous status (PVD, opacities)
- Optic nerve evaluation (C/D ratio, rim integrity)
- Macular assessment (OCT when indicated)
- Peripheral retina survey (scleral depression when needed)
- Vascular assessment

**Special Testing When Indicated**
- Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
- B-scan ultrasonography
- Visual field testing
- Fundus photography

### Pre-Operative Testing Requirements

#### Essential Testing

| Test | Purpose | Normal Values | Action if Abnormal |
|------|---------|--------------|-------------------|
| Visual acuity | Baseline function | Varies by condition | Further investigation |
| Manifest refraction | Accurate prescription | Within refractive goals | Assess stability |
| IOP | Glaucoma screening | 10-21 mmHg | Glaucoma evaluation |
| Pachymetry | Corneal assessment | 500-560 μm | Modify calculations |
| Keratometry | IOL calculation | 40-46 D average | Topography if irregular |

#### Biometry for IOL Calculations

**Optical Biometry (Preferred)**
- Axial length measurement (normal: 22-25 mm)
- Keratometry readings (K1 and K2)
- Anterior chamber depth
- Lens thickness
- White-to-white measurement

**Ultrasound Biometry**
- Required for dense cataracts
- Immersion technique preferred
- Contact technique acceptable
- Document measurement method

**IOL Formula Selection**
- Standard eyes: SRK/T, Hoffer Q, or Holladay 1
- Short eyes (< 22 mm): Hoffer Q or Haigis
- Long eyes (> 26 mm): SRK/T or Hill-RBF
- Post-refractive surgery: Special formulas required

### Risk Stratification

#### Low Risk Criteria
- No ocular comorbidities
- Normal ocular anatomy
- No significant systemic disease
- Clear media for visualization
- Cooperative patient with realistic expectations

#### Moderate Risk Criteria
- Pseudoexfoliation syndrome
- Previous ocular surgery
- Controlled glaucoma
- Well-controlled diabetes
- Mild corneal guttata

#### High Risk Criteria
- Subluxated or dislocated lens
- Advanced corneal endothelial disease
- Uncontrolled glaucoma
- Active ocular inflammation
- Dense brunescent cataract
- Previous complex ocular surgery

## Patient Education and Informed Consent

### Pre-Operative Counseling

**Procedure Explanation**
Patients should understand:
- What the procedure involves
- Expected benefits and improvement timeline
- Alternative treatment options
- What to expect on the day of surgery
- Recovery period expectations

**Risk Discussion**

Common risks to discuss:
1. **Infection** - Endophthalmitis (approximately 1 in 1000)
2. **Bleeding** - Suprachoroidal hemorrhage (rare but serious)
3. **Retinal detachment** - Increased risk after intraocular surgery
4. **Cystoid macular edema** - Can affect visual outcomes
5. **IOL complications** - Dislocation, incorrect power
6. **Posterior capsule rupture** - May require additional surgery
7. **Corneal edema** - May be temporary or permanent

**Expected Outcomes Discussion**
- Realistic visual expectations
- Need for glasses after surgery
- Recovery timeline
- Activity restrictions

### Documentation Requirements

**Informed Consent Elements**
- Procedure explained in understandable terms
- Risks and benefits discussed
- Alternatives presented
- Questions answered
- Written consent obtained
- Witness signature when required

## Surgical Procedure Overview

### Anesthesia Options

| Type | Indications | Advantages | Considerations |
|------|-------------|------------|----------------|
| Topical | Most cataract procedures | No injection, rapid recovery | Limited duration, patient cooperation required |
| Peribulbar | Complex cases, anxious patients | Good akinesia | Longer acting, risk of orbital hemorrhage |
| Retrobulbar | Specific indications | Excellent akinesia | Higher risk of complications |
| General | Pediatric, uncooperative patients | Complete control | Systemic risks, longer recovery |

### Surgical Technique Overview

**Standard Procedure Steps**
1. Anesthesia and akinesia (as required)
2. Sterile draping and speculum placement
3. Wound construction (clear corneal or scleral tunnel)
4. Viscoelastic injection
5. Capsulorhexis creation
6. Hydrodissection and hydrodelineation
7. Phacoemulsification of nucleus
8. Cortical cleanup
9. IOL implantation
10. Wound closure (sutures if needed)
11. Antibiotic/steroid injection

### Intraoperative Considerations

**Factors Affecting Surgery**
- Pupil size and dilation adequacy
- Nuclear density and brunescence
- Zonular integrity
- Capsule status
- Previous ocular surgery effects
- Surgeon experience and technique

## Post-Operative Care Protocol

### Day One Post-Operative Examination

**Essential Assessment**
1. Visual acuity (uncorrected and pinhole)
2. Slit lamp examination
   - Wound integrity
   - Corneal status (edema, clarity)
   - Anterior chamber depth and reaction
   - IOL position and centration
3. Intraocular pressure
4. Patient comfort assessment

**Expected Findings**
- Mild corneal edema (usually clears in days)
- Mild anterior chamber reaction (1+ cells acceptable)
- IOP may be slightly elevated initially
- IOL well-positioned in capsular bag

**Concerning Findings Requiring Action**
- Significant corneal edema
- Flat or shallow anterior chamber
- Significant IOP elevation (>30 mmHg)
- Wound leak (Seidel positive)
- Marked inflammation

### Post-Operative Medication Regimen

**Standard Protocol**

| Medication | Frequency | Duration | Purpose |
|------------|-----------|----------|---------|
| Topical antibiotic | 4 times daily | 1-2 weeks | Infection prevention |
| Topical steroid | 4 times daily, taper | 4-6 weeks | Inflammation control |
| Topical NSAID | 3-4 times daily | 2-4 weeks | CME prevention, comfort |
| Artificial tears | As needed | Ongoing | Lubrication |

**Tapering Schedule**
- Week 1-2: Steroid 4 times daily
- Week 3-4: Steroid 3 times daily
- Week 5-6: Steroid 2 times daily
- Week 7: Steroid once daily, then stop

### Activity Restrictions

**Week 1**
- No rubbing or pressing on the eye
- No water in eyes (shower from neck down)
- Sleep with protective shield
- Light activities only
- Avoid heavy lifting (>10 lbs)
- No bending from waist (bend at knees)

**Weeks 2-4**
- Gradual return to normal activities
- Continue eye protection during sleep
- May wash face carefully
- No swimming
- Avoid eye makeup

**After 4 Weeks**
- Most restrictions lifted
- May resume exercise gradually
- Swimming allowed with goggles
- Contact lens wear possible
- Final spectacle prescription possible

### Follow-up Schedule

| Visit | Timing | Purpose |
|-------|--------|---------|
| Post-op day 1 | 24 hours | Assess for early complications |
| Week 1 | 5-7 days | Check healing, adjust medications |
| Week 4 | 3-4 weeks | Assess vision, consider refraction |
| Month 3 | Optional | Final outcome assessment |

## Complications Recognition and Management

### Early Post-Operative Complications

#### Endophthalmitis

**Recognition**
- Severe pain (often disproportionate)
- Rapid vision loss
- Marked conjunctival injection
- Hypopyon formation
- Eyelid edema

**Management**
- URGENT referral to surgeon
- Vitreous tap and intravitreal antibiotics
- Possible pars plana vitrectomy
- Intensive topical and systemic therapy

#### Wound Leak

**Recognition**
- Shallow or flat anterior chamber
- Low intraocular pressure
- Seidel test positive (fluorescein passes through wound)
- Patient may note excessive tearing

**Management**
- Small leaks: Bandage contact lens, aqueous suppressants
- Moderate leaks: May require surgical revision
- Large leaks: Surgical repair

#### Intraocular Pressure Elevation

**Causes**
- Retained viscoelastic material
- Inflammation
- Pigment dispersion
- Pre-existing glaucoma
- Steroid response

**Management**
- IOP > 30 mmHg: Immediate treatment
- Aqueous suppressants (timolol, brimonidine, acetazolamide)
- Consider oral acetazolamide for marked elevation
- Close monitoring

#### Corneal Edema

**Recognition**
- Reduced visual acuity
- Corneal thickening on pachymetry
- Stromal haze
- Epithelial microcysts

**Management**
- Hypertonic saline (5% NaCl)
- Await resolution (usually days to weeks)
- If persistent > 3 months: Consider endothelial keratoplasty

### Late Post-Operative Complications

#### Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO)

**Recognition**
- Gradual visual decline after initial improvement
- Glare and light sensitivity
- Hazy view on retroillumination
- May simulate capsular wrinkling

**Management**
- YAG laser capsulotomy
- Quick outpatient procedure
- Immediate visual improvement expected

#### Cystoid Macular Edema (CME)

**Risk Factors**
- Diabetes mellitus
- Previous uveitis
- Intraoperative complications
- Epiretinal membrane

**Recognition**
- Visual decline 2-12 weeks post-op
- Central visual distortion
- OCT shows cystoid spaces

**Management**
- Topical NSAIDs and steroids
- Consider oral acetazolamide
- Intravitreal steroids if severe
- Chronic cases: Consider anti-VEGF

#### IOL Dislocation or Decentration

**Recognition**
- Visual symptoms (glare, double vision)
- Edge of IOL visible
- Sunset or sunrise syndrome
- Complete dislocation into vitreous

**Management**
- Mild decentration: May observe if asymptomatic
- Significant dislocation: Surgical repositioning
- Complete dislocation: Vitrectomy with IOL exchange

#### Retinal Detachment

**Risk Factors**
- High myopia
- Previous retinal pathology
- Complicated surgery (PCR)
- Laser capsulotomy (small increased risk)

**Recognition**
- New floaters and flashes
- Visual field defect
- Curtain-like vision loss
- Reduced IOP

**Management**
- URGENT referral to retina specialist
- Surgical repair (pneumatic retinopexy, scleral buckle, or vitrectomy)

## Referral Criteria

### Immediate Referral (Same Day)

- Suspected endophthalmitis
- Flat anterior chamber with wound leak
- Significant corneal edema with pain
- Retinal detachment suspected
- Suprachoroidal hemorrhage suspected
- Severe pain uncontrolled with medications

### Urgent Referral (Within 24-48 Hours)

- IOP > 30 mmHg despite treatment
- Significant anterior chamber reaction (3+ cells or hypopyon)
- Wound abnormalities requiring assessment
- Unexpected visual loss
- Patient concerns beyond routine post-operative course

### Routine Referral

- Posterior capsule opacification for YAG capsulotomy
- Refractive surprise requiring intervention
- IOL decentration
- Persistent dry eye symptoms
- Minor complications manageable with observation

### Information Transfer

**Pre-Operative Communication**
- Complete examination findings
- Biometry results and IOL selection rationale
- Patient expectations and concerns
- Special considerations for surgery
- Medical clearance status

**Post-Operative Communication**
- Operative report review
- Any intraoperative complications
- Specific post-operative instructions
- Recommended follow-up schedule
- Warning signs to monitor

### Documentation Standards

**Required Documentation**
1. Complete pre-operative examination
2. Risk assessment and stratification
3. Patient education provided
4. Informed consent documentation
5. All post-operative findings
6. Communication with surgeon
7. Patient instructions and understanding

**Quality Assurance**
- Outcome tracking
- Complication monitoring
- Patient satisfaction assessment
- Regular communication with surgical team

## Special Considerations

### Diabetic Patients

- Tight glycemic control pre-operatively
- Assess for diabetic retinopathy
- Increased risk of CME and infection
- May need more intensive follow-up
- Consider fluorescein angiography if vision not improving

### Patients on Anticoagulation

- Coordinate with prescribing physician
- Generally continue aspirin for most procedures
- Warfarin: Check INR, may not need to stop
- DOACs: Timing considerations
- Document discussion and plan

### Patients with Glaucoma

- IOP monitoring essential
- Avoid steroid-induced pressure spike
- May need earlier steroid taper
- Continue glaucoma medications post-op
- Consider MIGS combined with cataract surgery

### Patients with Previous Refractive Surgery

- Special IOL calculation formulas required
- May have altered corneal biomechanics
- Discuss expectations carefully
- Document previous refractive procedure
- May require topography-guided calculations

## Summary

Successful co-management of surgical patients requires comprehensive knowledge of pre-operative assessment, surgical procedures, post-operative care, and complication recognition. The optometrist serves as a critical link in the care continuum, ensuring appropriate patient selection, thorough preparation, careful post-operative monitoring, and timely intervention when complications arise.

Key principles include:
- Systematic pre-operative evaluation
- Clear patient communication and education
- Understanding of surgical technique and expected outcomes
- Vigilant post-operative monitoring
- Early recognition and appropriate management of complications
- Effective communication with the surgical team
- Thorough documentation throughout the process
