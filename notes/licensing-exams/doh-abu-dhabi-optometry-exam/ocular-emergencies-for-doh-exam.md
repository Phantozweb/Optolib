# Ocular Emergencies for DOH Exam

## Introduction

Ocular emergencies represent a critical subset of ophthalmic conditions that require immediate recognition and prompt intervention to preserve vision and ocular integrity. For optometrists preparing for the DOH Abu Dhabi Optometry Exam, a thorough understanding of these conditions is essential, as they frequently appear in clinical scenarios and multiple-choice questions. This comprehensive guide covers the four major categories of ocular emergencies: chemical injuries, ocular trauma, acute angle-closure glaucoma, and retinal detachment. Each section provides detailed clinical information, classification systems, management protocols, and key examination points that are commonly tested in the DOH licensing examination.

## Chemical Injuries

### Classification and Severity Assessment

Chemical injuries to the eye constitute one of the most devastating ocular emergencies, with the potential for severe visual impairment and permanent structural damage. The severity of chemical burns is classified using the Roper-Hall classification system, which grades injuries from Grade I (mild) to Grade IV (severe) based on corneal appearance and limbal ischemia. Grade I injuries show corneal epithelial damage with no limbal ischemia and have an excellent prognosis. Grade II injuries demonstrate corneal haze but with visible iris details, with less than one-third limbal ischemia and a good prognosis. Grade III injuries present with total corneal epithelial loss, stromal haze obscuring iris details, and between one-third to one-half limbal ischemia, carrying a guarded prognosis. Grade IV injuries are the most severe, with opaque cornea obscuring pupil, greater than one-half limbal ischemia, and a poor prognosis.

Alternatively, the Dua classification provides a more detailed assessment based on the extent of limbal involvement and conjunctival ischemia, ranging from Grade I (0 clock hours of limbal involvement) to Grade VI (total limbal involvement with severe conjunctival ischemia). This newer classification system has shown better correlation with visual outcomes and helps guide treatment decisions.

Alkali burns are generally more severe than acid burns due to their mechanism of injury. Alkaline substances such as ammonia, lye (sodium hydroxide), and lime (calcium hydroxide) cause saponification of fatty acids in cell membranes, leading to deep penetration and extensive tissue damage. The liquefactive necrosis caused by alkalis allows the chemical to penetrate deeper into ocular tissues, potentially affecting the anterior chamber, lens, and even the ciliary body. Acidic substances like sulfuric acid, hydrochloric acid, and hydrofluoric acid cause protein denaturation and coagulation, which creates a barrier that limits deeper penetration. However, hydrofluoric acid is an exception, as it can penetrate deeply like alkalis due to its small molecular size and ability to form fluoride ions that bind calcium and magnesium.

### Immediate Management Protocol

The management of chemical injuries follows a strict time-sensitive protocol. Immediate and copious irrigation is the cornerstone of treatment and should begin at the scene of injury before any detailed examination. Time is critical - every minute of delay in irrigation results in progressively worse outcomes. Irrigation should continue for a minimum of 30 minutes using at least 2-3 liters of sterile saline or Ringer's lactate solution. The goal is to achieve a neutral pH (7.0-7.4) as measured by pH paper applied to the inferior fornix. The Morgan Lens or similar irrigation devices facilitate continuous irrigation while minimizing patient discomfort. If these devices are unavailable, an IV tubing connected to the irrigation solution can be used, with the patient's head positioned to allow runoff.

Before irrigation, any particulate matter should be removed from the ocular surface using a cotton-tipped applicator or forceps. Double eversion of the eyelids is essential to check for retained particles in the superior fornix, particularly in cases involving cement, plaster, or lime. After initial irrigation, wait 5-10 minutes before measuring pH to allow equilibration. Irrigation should continue until neutral pH is achieved on two consecutive measurements.

After initial stabilization, a thorough examination is performed to assess the extent of damage. This includes measuring visual acuity, intraocular pressure, and documenting the degree of corneal epithelial defect, stromal clarity, limbal ischemia, and conjunctival involvement. Photography should be obtained for documentation purposes and for monitoring progression.

### Pharmacological Treatment

Medical management of chemical injuries involves a multi-faceted approach. Topical antibiotics are prescribed to prevent secondary infection, with broad-spectrum options such as moxifloxacin or tobramycin being commonly used. Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone acetate 1% or dexamethasone) are initiated to reduce inflammation, though their use must be carefully monitored beyond the first 10-14 days due to the risk of corneal melting. The anti-inflammatory benefits of steroids in the acute phase must be balanced against their potential to inhibit collagen synthesis and promote stromal lysis.

Doxycycline 100mg twice daily provides matrix metalloproteinase inhibition and anti-inflammatory effects, helping to prevent corneal melting. Topical vitamin C (ascorbic acid 10%) promotes collagen synthesis and corneal healing by acting as a cofactor in collagen production. Cycloplegic agents such as atropine or cyclopentolate can provide comfort and prevent posterior synechiae formation.

In cases with severe ischemia, additional interventions may include limbal stem cell transplantation, amniotic membrane transplantation, or even keratoprosthesis in eyes with severe damage. Patients with chemical injuries require close monitoring for complications including symblepharon formation, corneal neovascularization, glaucoma, and cataract development.

## Ocular Trauma

### Types of Ocular Trauma

Ocular trauma encompasses a wide spectrum of injuries ranging from minor surface abrasions to devastating globe rupture. The Birmingham Eye Trauma Terminology System (BETTS) provides standardized terminology for classifying ocular trauma. The primary distinction is between open globe injuries (full-thickness wound of the eye wall) and closed globe injuries (no full-thickness wound). Open globe injuries are further subdivided into rupture (caused by blunt force) and laceration (caused by sharp objects). Lacerations may be penetrating (entrance wound only), perforating (entrance and exit wounds), or intraocular foreign body (IOFB). Closed globe injuries include contusion and lamellar laceration.

### Blunt Trauma Assessment

Blunt trauma to the eye can result in multiple structural injuries. Hyphema, the accumulation of blood in the anterior chamber, is a common finding that requires careful management. Grading of hyphema ranges from Grade I (microscopic or less than one-third of anterior chamber) to Grade IV (total filling of anterior chamber). Total hyphema, also called "eight-ball hyphema," appears black or dark red due to deoxygenated blood and indicates a poor prognosis. Patients with hyphema should be placed on bed rest with head elevation at 30-45 degrees, and treatment may include topical corticosteroids, cycloplegics, and in some cases, systemic antifibrinolytics (aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid). Careful monitoring of intraocular pressure is essential, as elevated IOP can lead to corneal blood staining and optic nerve damage.

Sickle cell patients with hyphema require special attention as sickling can occur in the hypoxic, acidic environment of the anterior chamber, leading to severe IOP elevation. Treatment with hyperosmotic agents and carbonic anhydrase inhibitors should be avoided in these patients, and early surgical intervention may be necessary.

Blunt trauma can also cause angle recession (tear in the ciliary body), which predisposes to glaucoma years after the injury. Iris sphincter tears cause irregular pupil shape and may lead to traumatic mydriasis. Iridodialysis (separation of iris root from ciliary body) creates a D-shaped pupil and may require surgical repair if large. Cyclodialysis (separation of ciliary body from scleral spur) can cause hypotony due to increased uveoscleral outflow. Zonular dehiscence may lead to lens subluxation or dislocation.

Fundus examination may reveal commotio retinae (Berlin's edema), which appears as whitening of the outer retina due to photoreceptor outer segment disruption. Choroidal rupture typically occurs concentric to the optic nerve and may lead to subretinal hemorrhage and later choroidal neovascularization. Retinal tears and dialyses may occur, requiring prompt treatment to prevent retinal detachment. Traumatic macular holes can develop following severe contusion.

Scleral rupture should be suspected in cases with profound visual loss, extensive subconjunctival hemorrhage with chemosis, shallow anterior chamber, and low intraocular pressure. The most common sites of rupture are at the limbus (where the sclera is thinnest) and behind the rectus muscle insertions.

### Penetrating and Perforating Injuries

Penetrating injuries require urgent evaluation to assess the extent of intraocular damage. A detailed history should be obtained regarding the mechanism of injury, including the type of object, velocity, and possibility of foreign body. Examination should be gentle and non-invasive initially to avoid exacerbating the injury. Orbital CT scan is indicated when foreign body is suspected, particularly for metallic or radiopaque materials. MRI is contraindicated if metallic foreign body is suspected.

Management of corneal lacerations depends on the size and location. Small self-sealing wounds may be managed with a bandage contact lens, while larger wounds require surgical repair with 10-0 nylon sutures. Corneal sutures should be placed to minimize astigmatism, with deeper bites for larger wounds. Post-operative management includes topical antibiotics, anti-inflammatory medications, and regular follow-up for suture removal and refractive management.

Intraocular foreign bodies represent a surgical emergency. Metallic IOFBs pose particular risk due to the potential for siderosis bulbi (iron deposition causing retinal toxicity) or chalcosis (copper deposition causing severe inflammation). Symptoms of siderosis include iris heterochromia, mydriasis, cataract, and retinal pigmentary changes with visual field loss. Chalcosis causes a more acute inflammatory response with greenish copper deposition in Descemet's membrane and lens capsule. Surgical removal is typically performed via pars plana vitrectomy, with the timing depending on the material and associated injuries.

### Orbital Trauma

Orbital fractures commonly involve the orbital floor (blowout fracture) or medial wall. Clinical signs include enophthalmos (posterior displacement of the globe), diplopia due to extraocular muscle entrapment, restricted ocular motility, and infraorbital nerve hypoesthesia causing numbness of the cheek and upper lip. CT imaging is essential for diagnosis and surgical planning. Indications for surgical repair include significant enophthalmos (>2mm), persistent diplopia in primary gaze, and large fractures (>50% of orbital floor).

Orbital compartment syndrome, characterized by proptosis, elevated IOP, and vision loss, requires immediate lateral canthotomy and cantholysis to decompress the orbit. This emergency procedure involves cutting the lateral canthal tendon, first the inferior crus and then if necessary the superior crus, allowing the globe to prolapse forward and relieve pressure on the optic nerve.

## Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

### Pathophysiology

Acute angle-closure glaucoma (AACG) is an ophthalmic emergency resulting from sudden obstruction of aqueous outflow through the trabecular meshwork. The primary mechanism involves pupillary block, where the iris becomes apposed to the lens, preventing aqueous flow from the posterior to anterior chamber. This leads to forward bowing of the peripheral iris (iris bombe) and closure of the angle. Risk factors include hyperopia, shallow anterior chamber, narrow angle, increased lens thickness (as in cataract), and anterior positioned ciliary body. Predisposing anatomical features are more common in certain populations, with higher prevalence in Asians, Inuit populations, and women.

Precipitating factors include pharmacological dilation, emotional stress, dim illumination, and supine position. ### Clinical Presentation

The classic presentation of AACG includes sudden onset of severe unilateral eye pain, headache, nausea and vomiting, blurred vision with halos around lights (due to corneal edema), and red eye. The pain may be severe enough to mimic abdominal emergencies. Examination reveals elevated intraocular pressure (often 40-80 mmHg), corneal epithelial edema giving a "steamy" appearance, shallow anterior chamber, closed angle on gonioscopy, and a mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil. The iris may show sector atrophy from previous episodes, and the lens may demonstrate glaukomflecken (anterior subcapsular opacities from previous attacks).

The affected eye typically has a fixed, mid-dilated pupil due to ischemia of the iris sphincter muscle. Corneal edema may be so severe that it obscures the view of the anterior chamber structures. Gonioscopy reveals a completely closed angle with iris apposition to the trabecular meshwork. The fellow eye typically shows a narrow, occludable angle.

### Immediate Management

The management of AACG follows a step-wise approach aimed at rapidly lowering IOP and opening the angle. Initial medical therapy includes:

1. **Aqueous suppressants**: Topical beta-blockers (timolol 0.5%), alpha-2 agonists (apraclonidine 0.5% or brimonidine 0.2%), and carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (acetazolamide 500mg IV or oral, or topical dorzolamide 2%). Acetazolamide is typically given as 500mg IV or two 250mg tablets orally.

2. **Pilocarpine 1-2%**: Applied after IOP has been reduced (ineffective when IOP is very high due to iris ischemia). Constricts pupil and pulls iris away from angle. Must wait until IOP drops below 40-50 mmHg before pilocarpine will be effective.

3. **Hyperosmotic agents**: Mannitol 1-2 g/kg IV or oral glycerol 50% solution to reduce vitreous volume and lower IOP. Mannitol is given as 20% solution over 30-45 minutes.

4. **Topical steroids**: Prednisolone acetate 1% every 15-30 minutes initially to reduce inflammation and prevent posterior synechiae.

After medical stabilization, definitive treatment involves laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI), which creates a direct communication between posterior and anterior chambers, bypassing the pupillary block. LPI is typically performed using Nd:YAG laser, creating a full-thickness opening in the peripheral iris. The optimal location is typically at 10-11 o'clock or 1-2 o'clock position, under the upper eyelid to minimize glare symptoms. Prophylactic LPI is recommended for the fellow eye due to high risk of similar attack (50% risk within 5 years without treatment).

### Chronic Angle-Closure

Chronic angle-closure glaucoma may develop after repeated subacute attacks or progressive synechial closure of the angle. Patients may have fewer symptoms, but examination reveals elevated IOP, peripheral anterior synechiae, and glaucomatous optic neuropathy. Management may require goniosynechialysis (surgical separation of synechiae), cataract extraction (which deepens the anterior chamber), or filtering surgery depending on the extent of angle closure and IOP control.

## Retinal Detachment

### Types and Pathophysiology

Retinal detachment (RD) is the separation of the neurosensory retina from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). Three main types exist, each with distinct pathophysiology:

**Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (RRD)** is the most common type, resulting from a retinal break (tear or hole) that allows liquefied vitreous to enter the subretinal space. Risk factors include posterior vitreous detachment (PVD), high myopia, lattice degeneration, previous intraocular surgery (particularly cataract extraction), trauma, and family history. The characteristic progression of symptoms includes flashes (photopsia) from vitreoretinal traction, floaters from PVD or vitreous hemorrhage, and a curtain-like visual field defect as detachment progresses toward the macula.

**Tractional retinal detachment (TRD)** occurs when fibrovascular or fibrotic membranes on the retinal surface contract and pull the retina away from the RPE. This is most commonly seen in proliferative diabetic retinopathy, but also occurs in other conditions with retinal neovascularization such as retinal vein occlusion, ocular ischemic syndrome, and sickle cell retinopathy. Unlike RRD, TRD typically does not have associated photopsia and may have a more gradual onset. The detachment has a concave appearance and may be stable for long periods.

**Exudative (serous) retinal detachment** results from accumulation of fluid in the subretinal space without a retinal break or traction. Causes include inflammatory conditions (posterior scleritis, Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome), neoplastic processes (choroidal melanoma, metastatic tumors), vascular abnormalities (choroidal hemangioma), and systemic conditions (hypertension, preeclampsia). The fluid shifts with changes in head position, and the retina appears smooth without the corrugated appearance of RRD.

### Clinical Examination

Diagnosis of retinal detachment requires thorough fundus examination. Indirect ophthalmoscopy with scleral depression is essential for evaluating the peripheral retina. Key findings include an elevated retina with a corrugated appearance, loss of underlying choroidal detail, and possible identification of retinal breaks. In macula-off detachments, visual acuity is typically reduced to counting fingers or worse. The presence of a superior retinal break causes rapid progression due to gravity-dependent subretinal fluid accumulation.

B-scan ultrasonography is invaluable when the fundus view is obscured by media opacities such as cataract, vitreous hemorrhage, or corneal opacity. Characteristic findings include a highly reflective membrane attached at the optic nerve head and ora serrata, with after-movement on dynamic scanning. In RRD, the retina shows high-amplitude motion; in TRD, the retina is more tethered with limited movement. Optical coherence tomography (OCT) can confirm subretinal fluid and assess macular involvement.

### Management Principles

Treatment of rhegmatogenous retinal detachment depends on multiple factors including number, size, and location of breaks; extent of detachment; lens status; and presence of proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR). Options include:

**Pneumatic retinopexy**: Intravitreal gas injection (SF6 or C3F8) combined with laser retinopexy or cryotherapy. Suitable for superior detachments with single break or multiple breaks within one to two clock hours in the superior two-thirds of retina. Postoperative positioning is essential for success - the patient must position their head so the gas bubble covers the break. Contraindications include inability to maintain positioning, extensive PVR, and inferior breaks.

**Scleral buckle**: A silicone band or sponge is sutured to the sclera to indent the eye wall, relieving vitreoretinal traction and approximating the RPE to the retina. Can be combined with cryotherapy or laser, and may be performed with or without drainage of subretinal fluid. The buckle remains in place permanently but can be removed if causing complications. Scleral buckling is particularly useful for phakic patients and those with multiple breaks.

**Pars plana vitrectomy (PPV)**: Removal of vitreous and internal drainage of subretinal fluid, with endolaser treatment of breaks and tamponade with gas (SF6, C3F8) or silicone oil. Increasingly used as primary procedure, particularly for pseudophakic or complex detachments, those with vitreous hemorrhage, and cases with PVR. Silicone oil is used for long-term tamponade in cases with severe PVR or when positioning is not possible.

Timing of surgery depends on macular status. Macula-on detachments require urgent repair (within 24-48 hours), while macula-off detachments are typically repaired within one week, though earlier intervention may improve outcomes for recent detachments. The duration of macular detachment correlates with visual recovery - detachments less than one week have better prognosis than those lasting longer.

### Prognosis and Complications

Anatomical success rates for primary retinal detachment surgery are approximately 80-90%, with final visual outcome dependent primarily on macular involvement. Macula-on detachments generally achieve good visual recovery (often 20/40 or better), while macula-off detachments have more variable outcomes depending on duration (typically 20/50 to 20/200). Approximately 10-15% of cases require additional surgery.

Complications of retinal detachment surgery include cataract progression (particularly after PPV, occurring in 60-80% of cases within 2 years), PVR (the most common cause of failure, occurring in 5-10% of cases), diplopia (after scleral buckle due to extraocular muscle imbalance), and recurrent detachment. Patients require long-term follow-up for monitoring and early detection of complications in the operated eye and prophylaxis in the fellow eye.

## Conclusion

Ocular emergencies demand immediate recognition and appropriate intervention to preserve vision and ocular integrity. Chemical injuries require immediate and copious irrigation as the primary intervention - time is of the essence. Ocular trauma requires systematic evaluation using standardized classification systems such as BETTS. Acute angle-closure glaucoma necessitates rapid IOP reduction followed by definitive treatment with laser iridotomy. Retinal detachment requires timely surgical intervention based on type and extent of detachment. For the DOH Abu Dhabi Optometry Exam, understanding these principles and being able to apply them to clinical scenarios is essential for successful patient management and examination success.

## Key Points for DOH Exam

1. **Chemical Injuries**: Irrigation is the first priority - start before examination, use 2-3 liters minimum, aim for pH 7.0-7.4. Time to irrigation is the most critical prognostic factor.

2. **Trauma Classification**: Use BETTS terminology - differentiate open vs closed globe, rupture vs laceration, penetrating vs perforating injuries.

3. **Hyphema Management**: Bed rest, head elevation at 30-45 degrees, avoid aspirin and NSAIDs, monitor IOP, consider antifibrinolytics. Special caution in sickle cell patients.

4. **Orbital Compartment Syndrome**: Emergency lateral canthotomy and cantholysis required. Look for proptosis, elevated IOP, and vision loss.

5. **Angle Closure**: Medical therapy to lower IOP first, then laser peripheral iridotomy. Prophylactic LPI for fellow eye is essential.

6. **Pilocarpine Timing**: Only effective after IOP reduction - ischemic iris cannot constrict at high IOP (above 40-50 mmHg).

7. **Retinal Detachment Types**: Rhegmatogenous (break with vitreous fluid entering subretinal space), Tractional (fibrovascular membrane contraction), Exudative (fluid accumulation without break).

8. **RD Warning Signs**: Flashes, floaters, field defect (curtain) - urgent referral required. PVD is the most common precursor.

9. **Macula Status**: Macula-on = urgent surgery within 24-48 hours; Macula-off = within one week ideally. Earlier intervention improves outcomes.

10. **Prophylaxis**: Prophylactic LPI for fellow eye in angle closure (50% risk without treatment); monitor fellow eye in retinal detachment for breaks and lattice degeneration.
