# Clinical Optometry Knowledge for DOH Exam

## Introduction

The Department of Health (DOH) Abu Dhabi licensing examination for optometrists assesses clinical competency across multiple domains essential for safe and effective practice. This comprehensive guide covers the core clinical optometry knowledge areas including eye examination techniques, refraction principles, binocular vision assessment, intraocular pressure measurement, and visual field testing. Mastery of these subjects is essential for successful examination performance and professional practice in Abu Dhabi.

## 1. Comprehensive Eye Examination

### 1.1 Overview of the Eye Examination Process

A comprehensive eye examination is a systematic evaluation of the visual system and ocular health. The examination should follow a logical sequence that maximizes efficiency while ensuring thorough assessment of all relevant structures and functions.

### 1.2 Patient History

**Chief Complaint (CC):**
Document the primary reason for the visit in the patient's own words. Common chief complaints include blurred vision, eye strain, headaches, double vision, eye pain, and redness.

**History of Present Illness (HPI):**
- Onset: When did the symptom begin?
- Location: Which eye(s) are affected?
- Duration: How long does the symptom last?
- Character: Describe the nature of the symptom
- Aggravating factors: What makes it worse?
- Relieving factors: What makes it better?
- Timing: When does it occur?

**Past Ocular History (POH):**
Previous eye conditions, surgeries, injuries, treatments, and use of corrective lenses or contact lenses.

**Past Medical History (PMH):**
Systemic conditions affecting ocular health including diabetes mellitus, hypertension, thyroid disease, autoimmune disorders, and neurological conditions.

**Medication History:**
Current medications with attention to those with ocular side effects:
- Corticosteroids (cataract, glaucoma risk)
- Hydroxychloroquine (retinal toxicity)
- Ethambutol (optic neuropathy)
- Amiodarone (corneal deposits)
- Anticholinergics (angle closure risk)

**Family History:**
Hereditary conditions including glaucoma, macular degeneration, retinal detachment, strabismus, and amblyopia.

### 1.3 Visual Acuity Testing

**Distance Visual Acuity:**
- Snellen chart: Standard at 6 meters (20 feet)
- Normal acuity: 6/6 (20/20)
- Recording format: OD (right eye), OS (left eye), OU (both eyes)
- If unable to read largest letter: Count fingers (CF), Hand motion (HM), Light perception (LP)

**Near Visual Acuity:**
- Tested at 40 cm (16 inches)
- Jaeger notation or N notation
- Important for presbyopic patients

**Pinhole Visual Acuity:**
- Used to differentiate refractive error from ocular pathology
- Improvement with pinhole suggests refractive error
- No improvement suggests ocular disease or amblyopia

### 1.4 External Examination

**General Observation:**
- Head position and posture
- Facial symmetry
- Orbital appearance
- Eyelid position and function

**Eyelid Assessment:**
- Palpebral fissure width (normal: 8-11 mm)
- Margin-reflex distance (MRD1: 4-5 mm, MRD2: 5-6 mm)
- Levator function (normal: 12-15 mm)
- Presence of ptosis, entropion, ectropion

**Lacrimal System:**
- Inspection of lacrimal gland
- Puncta evaluation
- Tear meniscus height
- Fluorescein dye disappearance test

**Conjunctiva and Sclera:**
- Color and clarity
- Presence of injection, discharge, follicles, papillae
- Scleral thinning or discoloration

### 1.5 Pupil Examination

**Pupil Characteristics:**
- Size: Normal 3-5 mm in ambient light
- Shape: Round, regular
- Equality: Anisocoria less than 1 mm is physiologic

**Pupillary Light Reflex:**
- Direct response: Constriction of illuminated pupil
- Consensual response: Constriction of fellow pupil
- Swinging flashlight test: Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD)

**Accommodative Reflex:**
- Near triad: Convergence, accommodation, miosis
- Testing with near target

**Clinical Significance:**
- Marcus Gunn pupil (RAPD): Optic nerve pathology
- Adie's tonic pupil: Light-near dissociation
- Horner's syndrome: Miosis, ptosis, anhidrosis
- Argyll Robertson pupil: Light-near dissociation (neurosyphilis)

### 1.6 Ocular Motility

**Extraocular Muscles:**
| Muscle | Primary Action | Secondary Action | Innervation |
|--------|---------------|------------------|-------------|
| Lateral Rectus | Abduction | - | CN VI |
| Medial Rectus | Adduction | - | CN III |
| Superior Rectus | Elevation | Intorsion, Adduction | CN III |
| Inferior Rectus | Depression | Extorsion, Adduction | CN III |
| Superior Oblique | Intorsion | Depression, Abduction | CN IV |
| Inferior Oblique | Extorsion | Elevation, Abduction | CN III |

**Assessment Methods:**
- H-pattern testing: Nine cardinal positions
- Cover-uncover test: Detects tropia
- Alternate cover test: Detects phoria
- Duction vs. version testing

**Common Findings:**
- Esotropia: Inward deviation
- Exotropia: Outward deviation
- Hypertropia: Upward deviation
- Cranial nerve palsies
- Restrictive vs. paralytic strabismus

### 1.7 Biomicroscopy (Slit Lamp Examination)

**Structures Examined:**

**Eyelids and Lashes:**
- Margin architecture
- Gland openings (meibomian, Zeis, Moll)
- Trichiasis, madarosis, collarettes

**Conjunctiva:**
- Bulbar conjunctiva: Transparency, vessels
- Palpebral conjunctiva: Follicles, papillae, scarring
- Semilunar fold and caruncle

**Cornea:**
- Layers: Epithelium, stroma, Descemet's membrane, endothelium
- Assessment: Clarity, thickness, sensation
- Pathology: Abrasions, ulcers, dystrophies, deposits

**Anterior Chamber:**
- Depth: Van Herick grading
- Angle estimation: Open vs. narrow
- Cells and flare: Uveitis grading

**Iris:**
- Color and architecture
- Transillumination defects
- Synechiae (anterior and posterior)
- Iridodonesis

**Lens:**
- Clarity and position
- Cataract grading (LOCS III)
- Subluxation, phacodonesis

**Vitreous:**
- Anterior vitreous face
- Cells, opacities, pigment

## 2. Refraction

### 2.1 Principles of Refraction

Refraction is the process of determining the refractive error of the eye and prescribing corrective lenses to achieve optimal visual acuity. Understanding the optical principles is fundamental to accurate refraction.

**Refractive Errors:**
- **Emmetropia**: Parallel light focuses on retina without accommodation
- **Myopia**: Parallel light focuses in front of retina; corrected with minus lenses
- **Hyperopia**: Parallel light focuses behind retina; corrected with plus lenses
- **Astigmatism**: Different meridians have different focal points; corrected with cylindrical lenses
- **Presbyopia**: Age-related loss of accommodation; corrected with near addition

### 2.2 Objective Refraction

**Retinoscopy:**
The gold standard for objective refraction. The examiner neutralizes the reflex from the fundus using lenses.

**Retinoscopy Techniques:**
- With movement: Add plus power
- Against movement: Add minus power
- Neutralization: Reflex fills the pupil instantly

**Streak Retinoscopy:**
- Determines both sphere and cylinder
- Cylinder axis determined by aligning the streak
- Power determined by neutralizing each meridian

**Autorefraction:**
- Automated determination of refractive error
- Useful starting point for subjective refraction
- Less accurate in patients with small pupils, cataracts, or irregular astigmatism

### 2.3 Subjective Refraction

**Monocular Refraction Process:**

1. **Starting Point**: Use retinoscopy or autorefraction findings
2. **Maximum Plus to Best Visual Acuity (MPMVA)**: Determine the most plus (or least minus) power that provides best acuity
3. **Spherocylinder Refraction**: Jackson Cross Cylinder (JCC) technique

**Jackson Cross Cylinder Technique:**

**Cylinder Axis Determination:**
- Present cross cylinder at 45° to current cylinder axis
- Ask patient: "Which is better, 1 or 2?"
- Move axis toward preferred position
- Repeat until neutral

**Cylinder Power Determination:**
- Align cross cylinder with cylinder axis
- Present both orientations
- Adjust cylinder power based on preference
- Adjust sphere to maintain MPMVA principle

**Binocular Balance:**
Ensures both eyes are equally corrected:
- Prism dissociation method
- Alternating occlusion
- Humphriss immediate contrast method

### 2.4 Near Refraction

**Determining Near Add:**
1. Measure amplitude of accommodation (push-up method)
2. Calculate working distance requirement
3. Apply the formula: Add = Working Distance Diopters - (Amplitude/2)

**Age-Based Guidelines for Near Add:**
| Age | Expected Add |
|-----|--------------|
| 40-45 | +0.75 to +1.00 D |
| 45-50 | +1.00 to +1.50 D |
| 50-55 | +1.50 to +2.00 D |
| 55-60 | +2.00 to +2.50 D |
| 60+ | +2.50 to +3.00 D |

### 2.5 Special Refraction Considerations

**Cycloplegic Refraction:**
Indicated for:
- Children (accommodative esotropia)
- Young adults with high accommodation
- Pseudomyopia evaluation
- Hyperopia assessment

**Cycloplegic Agents:**
- Cyclopentolate 1% (duration: 24-48 hours)
- Tropicamide 1% (duration: 4-6 hours)
- Atropine 1% (duration: 1-2 weeks)

## 3. Binocular Vision

### 3.1 Fundamentals of Binocular Vision

Binocular vision involves the coordinated use of both eyes to achieve single, clear, comfortable vision. Normal binocular vision requires:
- Similar visual acuity in both eyes
- Aligned visual axes
- Corresponding retinal points
- Intact neural pathways

**Levels of Binocular Vision:**
1. **Simultaneous Perception**: Ability to perceive images from both eyes simultaneously
2. **Fusion**: Ability to merge similar images from both eyes
3. **Stereopsis**: Three-dimensional perception from slight differences in images

### 3.2 Assessment of Binocular Vision

**Cover Testing:**
- **Cover-Uncover Test**: Detects manifest deviation (tropia)
- **Alternate Cover Test**: Detects total deviation (tropia + phoria)
- Measurement with prism bar or loose prisms

**Hirschberg Test:**
- Assess corneal reflex position
- Each mm deviation = 15 prism diopters
- Normal: Reflex centered or slightly nasal

**Krimsky Test:**
- Prism placed before fixating eye
- Neutralizes corneal reflex deviation
- Useful for uncooperative patients

**Near Point of Convergence (NPC):**
- Normal: 6-10 cm
- Break point: Diplopia occurs
- Recovery point: Fusion restored

** fusional Vergence Amplitudes:**
Measured with prism bars:
- **Blur point**: First blur reported
- **Break point**: Diplopia occurs
- **Recovery point**: Fusion regained

**Normal Fusional Vergence Values:**
| Type | Distance Blur | Distance Break | Near Blur | Near Break |
|------|---------------|----------------|-----------|------------|
| Base-out (Convergence) | 7-9 | 15-20 | 14-16 | 20-25 |
| Base-in (Divergence) | 6-8 | 12-15 | 18-20 | 22-25 |

### 3.3 Sensory Testing

**Worth 4-Dot Test:**
- Four lights: 2 green, 1 red, 1 white
- Results interpretation:
  - 4 lights: Fusion or suppression
  - 2 red lights: Left suppression
  - 3 green lights: Right suppression
  - 5 lights: Diplopia

**Stereopsis Testing:**
- Titmus Fly Test
- Randot Stereotest
- Lang Stereotest
- TNO Random Dot Stereotest

**Normal Stereopsis Values:**
- Normal stereopsis: 40-60 seconds of arc
- Reduced stereopsis: 100-400 seconds of arc
- Absent stereopsis: >400 seconds of arc

### 3.4 Common Binocular Vision Disorders

**Heterophoria:**
- **Esophoria**: Tendency for eyes to deviate inward
- **Exophoria**: Tendency for eyes to deviate outward
- **Hyperphoria**: Vertical deviation tendency

**Heterotropia:**
- **Esotropia**: Inward deviation
  - Congenital/infantile
  - Accommodative
  - Non-accommodative
- **Exotropia**: Outward deviation
  - Intermittent
  - Constant

**Accommodative Dysfunction:**
- Accommodative insufficiency
- Accommodative excess (pseudomyopia)
- Accommodative infacility
- Ill-sustained accommodation

**Convergence Insufficiency:**
- Receded NPC (>10 cm)
- Reduced positive fusional vergence
- Symptoms: Eyestrain, headaches, blurred near vision
- Treatment: Vision therapy, prism correction

## 4. Intraocular Pressure (IOP) Measurement

### 4.1 Physiology of Intraocular Pressure

Intraocular pressure is maintained by the balance between aqueous humor production and outflow. Normal IOP ranges from 10-21 mmHg, with average around 15-16 mmHg.

**Aqueous Humor Dynamics:**
- Production: Ciliary body epithelium (2-3 μL/min)
- Outflow: Trabecular meshwork (conventional) and uveoscleral (unconventional)
- Circulation: Posterior to anterior chamber

**Factors Affecting IOP:**
- Diurnal variation (highest in morning)
- Body position (higher when supine)
- Age (slight increase with age)
- Systemic conditions
- Medications

### 4.2 Tonometry Techniques

**Goldmann Applanation Tonometry (GAT):**
Gold standard for clinical IOP measurement.

**Principle:**
- Based on Imbert-Fick law
- Force required to flatten a known area
- 3.06 mm diameter applanated area

**Technique:**
1. Instill topical anesthetic and fluorescein
2. Position patient at slit lamp
3. Align biprism with cornea
4. Adjust dial until inner edges of semicircles touch
5. Record reading in mmHg

**Sources of Error:**
- Central corneal thickness (CCT)
- Corneal edema
- Corneal astigmatism (>3D)
- Examiner pressure on globe
- Patient holding breath

**CCT Correction:**
- Thin cornea (<540 μm): Underestimates IOP
- Thick cornea (>550 μm): Overestimates IOP
- Correction: Approximately 2-3 mmHg per 50 μm deviation

**Non-Contact Tonometry (NCT):**
- Air-puff tonometry
- No corneal contact required
- Useful for screening
- Less accurate than GAT

**Tono-Pen:**
- Portable applanation tonometer
- Useful for bedbound patients
- Small contact area
- Average of multiple readings

**iCare Rebound Tonometry:**
- No anesthetic required
- Small probe makes brief contact
- Useful for children and sensitive patients

### 4.3 Glaucoma Risk Assessment

**IOP as a Risk Factor:**
- IOP >21 mmHg: Considered ocular hypertension
- IOP >30 mmHg: Significant glaucoma risk
- Each 1 mmHg increase: 10-13% increased glaucoma risk

**Additional Glaucoma Risk Factors:**
- Family history
- Age >60 years
- African or Hispanic descent
- Thin central cornea (<555 μm)
- Myopia
- Diabetes mellitus
- Systemic hypertension
- Vasospastic conditions

## 5. Visual Field Testing

### 5.1 Anatomy of the Visual Pathway

Understanding visual field defects requires knowledge of the visual pathway anatomy:

1. **Retina**: Nasal retina sees temporal field; temporal retina sees nasal field
2. **Optic Nerve**: Carries signals from one eye
3. **Optic Chiasm**: Fibers from nasal retinas cross
4. **Optic Tract**: Carries signals from both eyes to LGN
5. **Lateral Geniculate Nucleus**: Thalamic relay station
6. **Optic Radiations**: Meyer's loop (inferior) and Baum's loop (superior)
7. **Occipital Cortex**: Primary visual cortex (V1)

### 5.2 Types of Visual Field Tests

**Confrontation Visual Field Testing:**
- Quick screening method
- Compares patient's field to examiner's
- Detects gross defects
- Limited sensitivity

**Automated Perimetry:**
**Standard Automated Perimetry (SAP):**
- Gold standard for functional assessment
- Tests central 30° of visual field
- White stimuli on white background
- Threshold sensitivity measurement

**Test Strategies:**
- Full threshold: Tests each point multiple times
- SITA Standard: Swedish Interactive Thresholding Algorithm (faster)
- SITA Fast: Even faster, slightly less reliable

**Frequency Doubling Technology (FDT):**
- Tests magnocellular pathway
- High contrast, flickering stimuli
- Sensitive to early glaucoma
- Useful for screening

**Short-Wavelength Automated Perimetry (SWAP):**
- Blue stimuli on yellow background
- Tests koniocellular pathway
- May detect defects earlier than SAP

### 5.3 Visual Field Defects

**Pre-chiasmal Defects:**
- **Blind spot enlargement**: Papilledema, optic nerve drusen
- **Central/cecocentral scotoma**: Optic neuritis, toxic neuropathy
- **Arcuate scotoma**: Glaucoma, ischemic optic neuropathy
- **Altitudinal defect**: AION, retinal vascular occlusion

**Chiasmal Defects:**
- **Bitemporal hemianopia**: Pituitary adenoma, craniopharyngioma
- **Junctional scotoma**: Lesion at optic nerve-chiasm junction

**Post-chiasmal Defects:**
- **Homonymous hemianopia**: Contralateral lesion
  - Complete: Entire hemifield affected
  - Incomplete: Variable patterns
- **Pie-in-the-sky**: Temporal lobe lesion (Meyer's loop)
- **Pie-on-the-floor**: Parietal lobe lesion
- **Homonymous quadrantanopia**: Partial post-chiasmal lesion

### 5.4 Glaucoma Visual Field Patterns

**Typical Progression:**
1. **Normal field**: No defects
2. **Early damage**: Paracentral scotoma, nasal step
3. **Moderate damage**: Arcuate scotoma extending from blind spot
4. **Advanced damage**: Ring scotoma, peripheral constriction
5. **End-stage**: Central island, temporal island

**Reliability Indices:**
- **Fixation losses**: Patient looking away
- **False positives**: Responding when no stimulus
- **False negatives**: Not responding to visible stimulus
- Acceptable: Fixation losses <20%, FP/FN <15%

**Global Indices:**
- **Mean Deviation (MD)**: Overall deviation from normal
- **Pattern Standard Deviation (PSD)**: Localized defect measure
- **Visual Field Index (VFI)**: Percentage of normal function

### 5.5 Interpreting Visual Field Results

**Steps for Interpretation:**

1. **Check Reliability**: Assess reliability indices
2. **Review Gray scale**: Overall impression
3. **Examine Total Deviation**: Compare to age-matched normals
4. **Analyze Pattern Deviation**: Identify localized defects
5. **Consider Glaucoma Hemifield Test (GHT)**: Statistical comparison
6. **Compare to Previous Exams**: Assess progression

**Staging Systems:**
- Hodapp-Parrish-Anderson criteria
- Advanced Glaucoma Intervention Study (AGIS) score
- Visual Field Index progression analysis

## Conclusion

Clinical optometry knowledge for the DOH Abu Dhabi examination encompasses five fundamental domains: comprehensive eye examination, refraction, binocular vision, intraocular pressure measurement, and visual field testing. Each domain requires thorough understanding of theoretical principles, practical techniques, and clinical interpretation skills. Successful candidates must demonstrate competency in performing these assessments accurately and interpreting findings appropriately to ensure optimal patient care.

## Key Examination Points

1. **Eye Examination**: Follow systematic approach; document thoroughly
2. **Refraction**: Master retinoscopy and subjective techniques; understand MPMVA principle
3. **Binocular Vision**: Assess motor and sensory components; identify phorias and tropias
4. **IOP**: Understand factors affecting measurement; account for corneal thickness
5. **Visual Fields**: Interpret defects based on anatomical pathways; assess reliability
