# Clinical Case Studies for DOH Optometry Exam

## Introduction

This comprehensive collection of clinical case studies is designed to help optometry professionals prepare for the Department of Health (DOH) Abu Dhabi Optometry licensing examination. Each case study presents realistic clinical scenarios that test diagnostic reasoning, clinical decision-making, and patient management skills essential for optometric practice in the UAE. The cases span the major domains tested in the examination including red eye conditions, vision loss emergencies, refraction techniques, contact lens complications, and pediatric optometry.

## Section 1: Red Eye Cases

### Case Study 1: Acute Bacterial Conjunctivitis

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Ahmed M.
- Age: 28 years
- Occupation: Office worker
- Chief Complaint: "My right eye has been red and sticky for two days."

**History of Present Illness:**
Ahmed presents with a 2-day history of right eye redness, discharge, and mild discomfort. He reports waking up with his eyelids "stuck together" and has noticed a yellowish discharge throughout the day. He denies any significant pain, photophobia, or visual changes. He works in an air-conditioned office and has no recent history of contact lens use or eye trauma. The symptoms began gradually and have progressively worsened over the past 48 hours.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: 6/6 OU (both eyes normal)
- External Examination: Right eye shows diffuse conjunctival injection, more prominent in the fornices with a papillary reaction on the palpebral conjunctiva
- Lids: Mild crusting on right eyelashes, especially at the lash line
- Discharge: Mucopurulent, yellowish discharge that reaccumulates quickly after wiping
- Cornea: Clear, no staining with fluorescein, no infiltrates
- Pupils: Equal, round, reactive to light bilaterally
- Anterior Chamber: Quiet, no cells or flare
- Lymph Nodes: Palpable pre-auricular lymphadenopathy on the right side, mildly tender

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Acute bacterial conjunctivitis (most likely given mucopurulent discharge and unilateral onset)
2. Viral conjunctivitis (adenoviral - less likely given discharge characteristics)
3. Allergic conjunctivitis (typically bilateral with itching)
4. Toxic conjunctivitis (no history of chemical exposure)

**Diagnosis:**
Acute Bacterial Conjunctivitis - The presence of mucopurulent discharge, glued eyelids in the morning, and unilateral involvement strongly suggest bacterial etiology. The pre-auricular lymphadenopathy can occur with both bacterial and viral conjunctivitis but is more commonly associated with viral cases. However, the character of the discharge and the unilateral presentation favor a bacterial cause.

**Management Plan:**
1. Prescribe topical antibiotic: Moxifloxacin 0.5% ophthalmic solution, one drop every 2 hours for the first 2 days, then four times daily for a total of 5-7 days
2. Alternative options include Tobramycin 0.3% or Gentamicin 0.3% if fluoroquinolones are contraindicated
3. Educate patient on hygiene measures to prevent spread to the other eye and to family members
4. Warm compresses for eyelid crusting in the morning
5. Advise patient to avoid touching or rubbing eyes and to wash hands frequently
6. Follow-up in 5-7 days or sooner if symptoms worsen or vision decreases

**Patient Education Points:**
- Condition is highly contagious for 24-48 hours after starting antibiotics
- Avoid sharing towels, pillows, face cloths, or eye makeup
- Dispose of any eye makeup products currently in use to prevent reinfection
- May return to work after 24 hours of antibiotic treatment if symptoms improve
- Wash hands thoroughly before and after instilling eye drops

### Case Study 2: Herpes Simplex Keratitis

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Fatima K.
- Age: 35 years
- Occupation: School teacher
- Chief Complaint: "My left eye is painful, red, and sensitive to light for three days."

**History of Present Illness:**
Fatima presents with a 3-day history of left eye pain, redness, photophobia, and foreign body sensation. She reports a history of recurrent "cold sores" on her lips, with the last episode approximately 6 months ago. She denies any recent eye trauma or contact lens use. She describes the pain as a deep, aching sensation rather than surface irritation. The photophobia has worsened progressively over the three days.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/6, Left eye 6/12 (reduced from baseline)
- External Examination: Left eye shows circumcorneal injection (more intense around the limbus)
- Lids: No lid edema or discharge noted
- Cornea: Fluorescein staining reveals a classic dendritic ulcer with terminal bulbs in the central cornea, measuring approximately 2mm in length
- Corneal Sensation: Reduced in the left eye compared to the right (tested with cotton wisp)
- Pupils: Left pupil slightly miotic due to ciliary spasm and ocular irritation
- Anterior Chamber: 1+ cells visible, no flare
- Intraocular Pressure: 14 mmHg in both eyes (normal)
- Iris: Normal architecture, no transillumination defects

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Herpes simplex keratitis (dendritic) - classic presentation
2. Herpes zoster keratitis (would typically have skin involvement)
3. Acanthamoeba keratitis (usually in contact lens wearers)
4. Bacterial keratitis (typically more acute with purulent discharge)

**Diagnosis:**
Herpes Simplex Keratitis (Dendritic) - The classic dendritic ulcer with terminal bulbs, history of oral herpes recurrences, reduced corneal sensation, photophobia, and ciliary injection are pathognomonic for HSV keratitis. The dendritic pattern is created by the virus replicating in the corneal epithelium.

**Management Plan:**
1. Topical antiviral therapy: Acyclovir 3% ophthalmic ointment, apply 5 times daily (every 4 hours while awake) for 10-14 days
2. Alternative: Ganciclovir 0.15% gel, apply 5 times daily for 7-14 days
3. Consider oral antiviral: Acyclovir 400mg three times daily for 7-10 days as adjunctive therapy
4. Cycloplegic for comfort: Cyclopentolate 1% twice daily for ciliary spasm and pain relief
5. CONTRAINDICATED: Topical corticosteroids (can worsen HSV epithelial keratitis significantly)
6. Follow-up in 2-3 days initially, then weekly until resolution

**Critical Teaching Points:**
- Never prescribe topical steroids in epithelial HSV keratitis - this can lead to geographic ulceration and deeper involvement
- Monitor closely for stromal keratitis or uveitis development
- Recurrence is common (25-50% recurrence within 2 years)
- Document visual acuity carefully at each visit as stromal involvement can affect vision
- Patient education about recognizing early symptoms for prompt treatment

### Case Study 3: Contact Lens-Related Bacterial Keratitis

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Sarah J.
- Age: 22 years
- Occupation: University student
- Chief Complaint: "My right eye started hurting yesterday and now I can barely open it."

**History of Present Illness:**
Sarah is a daily soft contact lens wearer for the past 4 years. She admits to occasionally sleeping in her lenses (2-3 times per week) and sometimes extending wear beyond the recommended replacement schedule. Three days ago, she noticed mild irritation in her right eye after removing her lenses, which she attributed to "tired eyes." Yesterday, the pain worsened significantly, and today she reports severe pain, light sensitivity, and decreased vision. She removed her lenses this morning but the pain persists and has intensified.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/60 (severely reduced), Left eye 6/6 with glasses
- External Examination: Right eye shows severe circumcorneal injection with significant eyelid edema and conjunctival chemosis
- Cornea: Central 2mm x 2mm stromal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect, surrounding corneal edema extends 1mm beyond infiltrate
- Anterior Chamber: 2+ cells, 1+ flare, hypopyon present measuring 1mm
- Discharge: Mucopurulent discharge present
- Pain: Severe, requiring examination with topical anesthetic to complete evaluation
- Lids: Marked blepharospasm and photophobia

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Bacterial keratitis (most likely - contact lens related, Pseudomonas suspected)
2. Acanthamoeba keratitis (consider given contact lens history, but usually more chronic)
3. Fungal keratitis (less likely without trauma history)
4. Herpes simplex keratitis (less likely presentation)

**Diagnosis:**
Bacterial Keratitis (Contact Lens Related) - The acute presentation with stromal infiltrate, epithelial defect, hypopyon, and severe inflammatory response in a contact lens wearer with poor compliance strongly suggests bacterial keratitis. Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the most common causative organism in contact lens-related keratitis and can rapidly progress to corneal perforation if not treated aggressively.

**Immediate Management:**
1. Discontinue contact lens wear immediately and permanently until cleared
2. Corneal scraping for Gram stain, culture, and sensitivity before starting antibiotics if possible
3. Intensive topical antibiotics: Fluoroquinolone (Moxifloxacin 0.5% or Levofloxacin 1.5%) every hour around the clock for first 24-48 hours
4. Consider fortified antibiotics if no improvement in 24-48 hours (Cefazolin 50mg/mL + Gentamicin 14mg/mL)
5. Cycloplegic: Atropine 1% twice daily for pain and photophobia relief
6. DO NOT patch the eye (creates environment for bacterial growth)
7. Oral analgesics for pain management (NSAIDs or opioids if severe)
8. Urgent referral to cornea specialist
9. Daily follow-up until improvement noted, then less frequent

**Risk Factors Identified:**
- Extended/overnight wear of contact lenses (major risk factor)
- Poor lens hygiene practices
- Extended replacement schedule beyond recommendations
- Possible contaminated lens storage case

**Patient Education:**
- This is a sight-threatening condition requiring immediate and aggressive treatment
- Compliance with medication schedule (hourly around the clock) is critical
- Contact lens wear must be discontinued during treatment
- Risk of corneal scarring, visual impairment, and potential need for corneal transplant
- Importance of proper contact lens care if resuming wear in future
- Never sleep in contact lenses unless specifically designed for extended wear

### Case Study 4: Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Mohammed A.
- Age: 62 years
- Occupation: Retired
- Chief Complaint: "My right eye suddenly became very painful with blurry vision and I'm seeing halos around lights."

**History of Present Illness:**
Mohammed presents to the emergency department at 10 PM with sudden onset of severe right eye pain, blurred vision, halos around lights (rainbow-colored rings around light sources), nausea, and two episodes of vomiting. Symptoms started approximately 4 hours ago while watching television in a dimly lit room. He has no previous history of eye problems but mentions his mother had glaucoma and was on eye drops. He denies any trauma or recent eye examinations.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/60 (severely reduced), Left eye 6/9
- Intraocular Pressure: Right eye 52 mmHg (critically elevated), Left eye 16 mmHg (normal)
- External Examination: Right eye shows diffuse conjunctival injection with ciliary flush, corneal edema giving cloudy/hazy appearance
- Cornea: Microcystic epithelial edema, stromal edema
- Anterior Chamber: Shallow peripherally, van Herick grade 1 (very narrow angle)
- Iris: Mid-dilated, fixed pupil with iris bombe appearance (forward bowing of iris)
- Lens: No obvious cataract visible through edema, but shallow anterior chamber noted
- Gonioscopy (left eye when pressure controlled): Narrow angle, appositional closure possible

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Primary acute angle-closure glaucoma (confirmed)
2. Secondary angle-closure (phacomorphic, uveitic, neovascular)
3. Inflammatory glaucoma (would have different anterior chamber findings)
4. Pigment dispersion syndrome (different clinical picture)

**Diagnosis:**
Primary Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma - The classic presentation of sudden onset severe eye pain, halos around lights, nausea/vomiting, severely elevated IOP, mid-dilated fixed pupil, corneal edema, and shallow anterior chamber confirms the diagnosis. This is an ophthalmic emergency.

**Emergency Management Protocol:**
1. Immediate IOP reduction (multiple agents simultaneously):
   - IV Acetazolamide 500mg (if no sulfa allergy or other contraindications)
   - Topical beta-blocker: Timolol 0.5% twice daily
   - Topical alpha-2 agonist: Brimonidine 0.15% or Apraclonidine 1% three times daily
   - Topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitor: Dorzolamide 2% or Brinzolamide 1% three times daily

2. Pilocarpine 2% (start 1-2 hours after initial IOP-lowering treatment when IOP begins to decrease and cornea starts to clear):
   - Mechanism: Miosis pulls iris away from angle
   - Note: Less effective when IOP is very high due to ischemic iris sphincter

3. If IOP remains dangerously high (>40 mmHg) despite maximal medical therapy:
   - Oral hyperosmotic agent: Glycerol 1-1.5g/kg or Isosorbide
   - IV Mannitol 1-2g/kg over 45 minutes (requires close monitoring)

4. Definitive Treatment:
   - Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) once cornea clears and IOP normalizes
   - YAG laser iridotomy for both eyes (prophylactic for fellow eye)
   - Consider surgical peripheral iridectomy if laser not possible

5. Follow-up:
   - Urgent ophthalmology consultation
   - Monitor IOP every 1-2 hours until stabilized
   - Gonioscopy after resolution to assess angle status
   - Prophylactic LPI for fellow eye within days to weeks

### Case Study 5: Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Khalid H.
- Age: 55 years
- Occupation: Construction engineer
- Chief Complaint: "I'm seeing flashing lights and a dark curtain coming across my right eye."

**History of Present Illness:**
Khalid reports seeing intermittent flashes of light (photopsia) in his right eye for the past week, described as "camera flashes" in the peripheral vision. Two days ago, he noticed numerous new floaters appearing suddenly, described as "cobwebs" and "little bugs" in his vision. This morning, he noticed a "dark curtain" starting from the top of his vision and progressively extending downward. He is highly myopic (-8.00D in both eyes) and had uncomplicated cataract surgery with posterior chamber IOL implantation 6 months ago. He denies any trauma.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/18 (reduced), Left eye 6/9 with correction
- Visual Fields: Superior visual field defect in right eye (curtain-like)
- External Examination: Normal, no trauma signs
- Anterior Segment: Unremarkable; pseudophakic right eye with well-positioned posterior chamber IOL
- Dilated Fundus Examination:
  - Right eye: Superior temporal retinal detachment with a visible horseshoe tear at 10 o'clock position, extending posteriorly. The macula is still attached (macula-on status). Subretinal fluid present.
  - Left eye: Peripheral lattice degeneration noted at 2 o'clock and 8 o'clock, no retinal breaks or detachment

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (confirmed with visible tear)
2. Tractional retinal detachment (associated with proliferative diabetic retinopathy)
3. Exudative retinal detachment (associated with inflammation or tumors)
4. Retinoschisis (different OCT characteristics)
5. Posterior vitreous detachment without retinal tear (excluded by finding tear)

**Diagnosis:**
Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment (Macula-On) - The classic symptom triad of photopsia, sudden new floaters, and progressive visual field defect (curtain), combined with fundus findings of elevated retina and a retinal tear, confirms the diagnosis. The macula remains attached, making this an urgent but not emergent situation.

**Risk Factors Identified:**
- High myopia (-8.00D) - major risk factor
- Recent cataract surgery within 6 months - increased risk of PVD and tears
- Lattice degeneration present in fellow eye
- Age over 50

**Urgent Management:**
1. Immediate referral to vitreoretinal surgeon (same day or next morning)
2. Surgical options (within 24-48 hours for macula-on detachment):
   - Pars plana vitrectomy with gas tamponade (SF6 or C3F8 gas)
   - Scleral buckle procedure
   - Pneumatic retinopexy (for selected superior detachments with single tear)
   - Combination of vitrectomy and scleral buckle

3. Pre-operative instructions:
   - Strict bed rest with specific head positioning
   - Head position: keep tear in dependent position (for this superior tear, head should remain upright or slightly forward)
   - Avoid sudden head movements or strenuous activity
   - Nothing to eat or drink (NPO) after midnight if surgery planned

**Prognosis and Follow-up:**
- Macula-on detachment: 85-90% single surgery success rate
- Macula-off detachment: Lower success rate, potential for permanent visual acuity reduction
- Time is critical - every hour the macula remains attached improves prognosis
- Post-operative positioning requirements with gas bubble
- Vision recovery may take weeks to months
- Cataract progression common after vitrectomy (if patient was phakic)

**Patient Education:**
- Warning signs of recurrence or new detachment: new flashes, floaters, curtain
- If gas bubble used: cannot fly or go to high altitudes until gas absorbs
- Fellow eye needs monitoring due to lattice degeneration
- Annual dilated fundus examinations essential

### Case Study 6: Optic Neuritis

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Noor S.
- Age: 28 years
- Occupation: Marketing manager
- Chief Complaint: "My right eye vision has become blurry and colors look washed out over the past three days."

**History of Present Illness:**
Noor presents with a 3-day history of progressive vision loss in her right eye, associated with mild aching pain on eye movement, especially when looking upward or laterally. She describes colors as appearing "dull," "washed out," or "faded" compared to her left eye - particularly noticeable with red objects. She denies any headache, eye redness, trauma, or recent infections. She has no significant past medical history and takes no regular medications.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/60 (severely reduced), Left eye 6/6
- Color Vision Testing: Right eye 0/11 Ishihara plates correctly identified, Left eye 11/11 (normal)
- Pupils: Right relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) present - right pupil dilates when light swings from left to right
- Extraocular Movements: Pain on right eye movement in all directions, especially upward gaze
- Fundus Examination:
  - Right eye: Mild optic disc swelling, hyperemia, blurred disc margins
  - Left eye: Normal optic disc, sharp margins

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Optic neuritis (most likely given age, symptoms, and findings)
2. Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (typically older patients, altitudinal defect)
3. Compressive optic neuropathy (typically progressive proptosis)
4. Infiltrative optic neuropathy (associated with systemic disease)
5. Leber's hereditary optic neuropathy (male predominance, positive family history)

**Diagnosis:**
Optic Neuritis - The classic triad of subacute unilateral vision loss, pain on eye movement, and relative afferent pupillary defect with optic disc changes is diagnostic for optic neuritis. This presentation is highly suggestive of demyelinating disease, and the patient requires evaluation for multiple sclerosis.

**Management Plan:**
1. MRI brain and orbits with gadolinium contrast (essential for MS evaluation)
   - Look for periventricular white matter lesions
   - Optic nerve enhancement
   - Assess risk of developing MS

2. Treatment options (based on ONTT study):
   - High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1g/day for 3 days), followed by oral prednisone taper (1mg/kg/day for 11 days, then taper over 4 days)
   - Oral prednisone alone is CONTRAINDICATED (increased recurrence rate per ONTT)
   - Treatment speeds visual recovery but does not improve final visual outcome

3. Lumbar puncture if MRI suggests demyelinating disease
   - Oligoclonal bands in CSF support MS diagnosis

4. Neurology referral for MS evaluation and long-term management

5. Ophthalmology follow-up:
   - 1 week, 1 month, and 3 months
   - Monitor visual recovery
   - Assess for steroid side effects

**Prognosis:**
- Visual recovery typically begins within 2-3 weeks
- Most patients recover good vision (6/9 or better) within 6 months
- Risk of developing MS: 50% within 15 years (higher if brain MRI abnormal)
- Recurrence in same or fellow eye common in MS patients
- Some permanent color vision deficit may persist despite visual acuity recovery

### Case Study 7: High Myopia with Compound Myopic Astigmatism

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Aisha R.
- Age: 24 years
- Occupation: Medical student
- Chief Complaint: "I need new glasses; my current ones are 3 years old and I'm getting headaches while studying."

**History of Present Illness:**
Aisha has been wearing glasses since age 8 for myopia. Her current prescription is approximately 3 years old. She reports increasing difficulty seeing the board in lecture halls and frequent frontal headaches after studying for more than 2 hours. She has never worn contact lenses but is interested in exploring options for sports and social activities. Her last eye examination was 3 years ago.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Uncorrected Visual Acuity: Right eye 6/120, Left eye 6/90
- Current Prescription (3 years old): OD: -7.50 DS, OS: -7.00 DS
- Cover Test: Orthophoric at distance and near (no deviation)
- Keratometry:
  - OD: 44.00 D @ 180 / 44.75 D @ 90 (0.75D WTR astigmatism)
  - OS: 43.75 D @ 180 / 44.50 D @ 90 (0.75D WTR astigmatism)
- Cycloplegic Refraction (after Cyclopentolate 1%):
  - OD: -8.25 -0.75 x 180 (corrects to 6/5)
  - OS: -7.75 -0.75 x 180 (corrects to 6/5)
- Axial Length: OD 26.2mm, OS 26.0mm (elongated, consistent with high myopia)

**Diagnosis:**
High Myopia with Mild With-The-Rule Astigmatism - There has been a myopic shift of approximately 0.75D in both eyes over 3 years, with previously uncorrected astigmatism now manifesting symptomatically. The astigmatism was likely present before but may not have been fully corrected.

**Management Plan:**
1. Prescribe new glasses with full cycloplegic correction:
   - OD: -8.25 -0.75 x 180
   - OS: -7.75 -0.75 x 180

2. Lens recommendations:
   - High-index lenses (1.67 or 1.74 refractive index) for cosmetic appearance and reduced weight
   - Anti-reflective coating to reduce glare and improve night vision
   - Photochromic option for UV protection and convenience
   - Polycarbonate or Trivex material for impact resistance

3. Discuss contact lens options:
   - Soft toric contact lenses for daily wear
   - Rigid gas permeable lenses (provide sharper vision for high myopia)
   - Orthokeratology for temporary myopia reduction (discuss risks)

4. Discuss refractive surgery options for future consideration:
   - LASIK (may be option depending on corneal thickness)
   - PRK (if cornea too thin for LASIK)
   - ICL (implantable collamer lens) for very high myopia

5. Annual dilated fundus examination mandatory for high myopia surveillance

**Patient Education - Critical Points:**
- High myopia increases risk of retinal detachment - seek immediate care for flashes, floaters, or curtain
- Annual dilated examinations essential to monitor for myopic changes
- Proper ergonomics for studying: 20-20-20 rule
- Good lighting essential for prolonged reading
- Consider blue-light filtering for extensive digital device use

### Case Study 8: Presbyopia Management

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Rashid M.
- Age: 48 years
- Occupation: Architect
- Chief Complaint: "I'm having trouble reading blueprints and my phone, especially at the end of the day."

**History of Present Illness:**
Rashid has never worn glasses and has always had excellent distance vision. Over the past year, he has noticed increasing difficulty focusing on near work. He finds himself holding blueprints, his phone, and restaurant menus farther away to read them clearly ("my arms are too short"). By the end of the workday, he experiences significant eye strain, frontal headaches, and occasional blurry distance vision after prolonged close work. He works long hours at a computer designing buildings.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Uncorrected Visual Acuity: 6/5 OU at distance (excellent)
- Near Vision Testing: Jaeger 3 readable at 40cm with significant effort and squinting; Jaeger 5 comfortably readable at 50cm (pushing material away)
- Distance Refraction: OD +0.25 DS, OS +0.25 DS (minimal hyperopia)
- Near Add Determination: +1.75 D OU for comfortable reading at 40cm
- Near Point of Convergence: 8cm (normal)
- Accommodative Amplitude: 3.50 D OU (measured with push-up method)
- Cover Test: Orthophoric at distance and near
- Amplitude of accommodation per Hofstetter's formula expected for age 48: 15 - 0.25(48) = 3.00 D

**Diagnosis:**
Presbyopia - Age-related loss of accommodation resulting in difficulty with near vision tasks. The accommodative amplitude of 3.50D is consistent with expected values for age 48, confirming physiological presbyopia rather than accommodative insufficiency.

**Management Options Discussed:**

**1. Reading Glasses (Single Vision Near):**
- Prescription: +1.75 D OU
- Advantages: Simple, inexpensive, wide field of clear near vision
- Disadvantages: Must remove glasses for distance viewing; not suitable for tasks requiring both distance and near vision

**2. Progressive Addition Lenses (Recommended):**
- Distance: Plano OU
- Intermediate corridor: +0.75 D (for computer work at 60-70cm)
- Near add: +1.75 D
- Advantages: Seamless transitions between distances; cosmetically appealing; suitable for architect work
- Disadvantages: Adaptation period required; peripheral distortion

**3. Occupational/Computer Glasses:**
- Enhanced intermediate zone for computer work
- Can have separate dedicated computer glasses for office use
- Consider trifocals for varied working distances

**4. Contact Lens Options:**
- Monovision: Dominant eye corrected for distance, non-dominant for near
- Multifocal soft contact lenses: Simultaneous vision design
- Modified monovision: One eye distance, one eye multifocal

**Prescription Given:**
Progressive addition lenses with:
- Distance: Plano OU
- Near Add: +1.75 D OU
- Lens material: Polycarbonate for impact resistance
- Anti-reflective coating mandatory
- Consider blue-light filtering for extended computer use

**Patient Education:**
- Presbyopia will continue to progress until approximately age 60-65, then stabilize
- Will need stronger add power over time (approximately +0.25D every 2-3 years)
- Importance of good lighting for near work
- 20-20-20 rule for digital eye strain: every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds
- Return for re-evaluation if vision changes or new symptoms develop

## Section 4: Contact Lens Cases

### Case Study 9: Rigid Gas Permeable Lens Fitting for Keratoconus

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Omar F.
- Age: 32 years
- Occupation: IT professional
- Chief Complaint: "I want to try contact lenses, but I have keratoconus and was told I can't wear regular lenses."

**History of Present Illness:**
Omar was diagnosed with keratoconus 5 years ago following blurred vision that couldn't be fully corrected with glasses. He currently wears glasses with moderate success but is interested in contact lenses for sports activities and social events. His right eye has more advanced disease, while his left eye has mild involvement. He has never worn any type of contact lenses before.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Uncorrected Visual Acuity: OD 6/30, OS 6/12
- Best Corrected Visual Acuity (spectacles): OD 6/12 (limited by irregular astigmatism), OS 6/6
- Keratometry:
  - OD: 48.50 @ 180 / 52.00 @ 90 (irregular mires, unable to obtain reliable K readings initially, 3.50D corneal cylinder)
  - OS: 45.00 @ 180 / 46.50 @ 90 (regular, 1.50D WTR astigmatism)
- Corneal Topography:
  - OD: Classic inferior steepening with "bow-tie" pattern consistent with keratoconus; apex at inferior-temporal location
  - OS: Mild inferior steepening, early keratoconus pattern
- Slit Lamp Findings:
  - OD: Vogt's striae visible in deep stroma, Fleischer's ring partially surrounding the cone, thinned central cornea
  - OS: Minimal findings, mild stromal thinning
- Pachymetry: OD 460 microns (thinned), OS 510 microns

**Diagnosis:**
Keratoconus - Moderate stage in right eye, mild/early stage in left eye. The irregular astigmatism limits visual acuity with spectacle correction in the right eye. Contact lens fitting will improve visual acuity by neutralizing the irregular corneal surface.

**Contact Lens Fitting Approach:**

**Right Eye - RGP Fitting:**
- Lens Design: Three-point touch design for keratoconus
- Base Curve: 7.20mm (chosen to be flatter than steep K, allowing tear lens to fill irregularities)
- Diameter: 9.0mm (smaller diameter for better centration on cone)
- Power: -6.50 D (determined by over-refraction)
- Lens Material: Boston XO (high Dk material for oxygen permeability)
- Peripheral curves: Designed to allow adequate tear exchange

**Fitting Evaluation:**
- Fluorescein pattern shows central touch (apex bearing) with acceptable pattern
- 0.5mm edge lift peripherally
- No excessive impingement on the cone
- Good centration with slight inferior-temporal displacement (acceptable for keratoconus)
- Over-refraction: -1.00 D spherical
- Final Visual Acuity with RGP: 6/6 (significant improvement from 6/12 with glasses)

**Left Eye Options:**
1. Soft toric contact lens: -2.00 -1.25 x 180 (may be sufficient for mild KC)
2. RGP lens: Base curve 7.60mm, Power -3.00 D (for better visual quality)

**Management Plan:**
1. Dispense RGP lenses with detailed insertion and removal training
2. Follow-up at 1 week, 1 month, then every 6 months
3. Discuss corneal cross-linking if progression noted
4. Never sleep in RGP lenses
5. Proper lens care: Daily cleaning, weekly enzymatic cleaning

**Patient Education:**
- Adaptation period of 1-2 weeks expected for RGP lenses
- Proper hygiene essential to prevent infection
- Never use tap water on lenses (Acanthamoeba risk)
- Return immediately if pain, redness, or vision decrease
- Regular monitoring for keratoconus progression is essential
- Future surgical options if lens intolerance develops

### Case Study 10: Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (Contact Lens Intolerance)

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Layla T.
- Age: 38 years
- Occupation: Accountant
- Chief Complaint: "I can't wear my contact lenses anymore; my eyes get red, itchy, and irritated after just an hour."

**History of Present Illness:**
Layla has been wearing soft contact lenses for 15 years with previously excellent tolerance. Over the past 6 months, she has developed increasing discomfort, redness, and itching with lens wear. She can now only tolerate lenses for 2-3 hours before removing them due to discomfort and a sensation of "something in the eye." She uses monthly disposable lenses and admits to occasionally sleeping in them (2-3 times per week) and sometimes extending replacement beyond the recommended schedule. She uses a multipurpose solution for cleaning.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity with glasses: 6/6 OU
- External Examination: Bilateral mild conjunctival injection, more pronounced in the superior bulbar conjunctiva
- Upper Lid Eversion:
  - OD: Giant papillae (>1mm diameter) covering the superior tarsal conjunctiva, injected, "cobblestone" appearance
  - OS: Similar findings with moderate papillary reaction
- Fluorescein Staining: Bilateral superior punctate epithelial keratopathy (SPK) - mechanical irritation from papillae
- Cornea: No infiltrates, no ulcers
- Tear Film Assessment:
  - TBUT (Tear Break-Up Time): 5 seconds (reduced, normal >10 seconds)
  - Schirmer's Test: 8mm OU in 5 minutes (borderline dry eye, normal >15mm)
- Lower Lid: Mild meibomian gland dysfunction

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis (GPC) - Contact lens induced (confirmed)
2. Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (typically younger patients, seasonal)
3. Atopic keratoconjunctivitis (associated with atopic dermatitis)
4. Dry eye disease (contributing factor)
5. Contact lens solution toxicity (consider in differential)

**Diagnosis:**
Contact Lens Intolerance due to Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis and Concurrent Dry Eye Disease - Chronic contact lens wear with poor compliance (overnight wear, extended replacement) has led to GPC, a type 1 hypersensitivity reaction to lens deposits and mechanical irritation. The coexisting dry eye further compromises lens tolerance.

**Management Plan:**

**Phase 1 - Acute Treatment (4-6 weeks):**
1. Complete discontinuation of contact lens wear (mandatory)
2. Topical therapy:
   - Olopatadine 0.1% (mast cell stabilizer/antihistamine) twice daily
   - Loteprednol 0.5% (soft steroid) four times daily for 1-2 weeks if severe inflammation
   - Preservative-free artificial tears four times daily
3. Warm compresses and lid hygiene for meibomian gland dysfunction
4. Omega-3 fatty acid supplementation (2000mg EPA + DHA daily)

**Phase 2 - Rehabilitation (after papillae resolve):**
1. Refit with new lens modality:
   - Daily disposable soft lenses (minimum deposit accumulation)
   - Consider silicone hydrogel material for better oxygen permeability
2. Strict wearing time limits:
   - Maximum 8-10 hours per day
   - No overnight wear under any circumstances
3. Solution change: Preservative-free or hydrogen peroxide-based systems
4. Consider RGP lenses if soft lens intolerance persists

**Phase 3 - Maintenance:**
1. Continue preservative-free artificial tears during lens wear
2. Regular lens replacement as scheduled
3. Quarterly follow-up visits

**Patient Education - Critical Points:**
- GPC is reversible with proper management
- Lens hygiene and compliance are essential to prevent recurrence
- Never sleep in contact lenses (major risk factor for GPC and infection)
- Replace lenses as directed; do not extend beyond recommended schedule
- Use fresh solution daily; never top off old solution
- Regular follow-up essential to monitor for recurrence
- If symptoms return, discontinue lenses and seek care promptly

### Case Study 11: Anisometropic Amblyopia with Esotropia

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Zayed (accompanied by parent)
- Age: 5 years
- Parent's Concern: "My son squints his left eye when watching TV, and sometimes it turns inward."

**History of Present Illness:**
Zayed's parents noticed him squinting his left eye during television viewing over the past 6 months. Additionally, they have observed his left eye occasionally turning inward, especially when he is tired or focusing on near objects. There is no history of eye trauma, infection, or previous eye examination. The father had amblyopia as a child and wore a patch. The family is concerned about the possibility of a "lazy eye."

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity (Allen pictures or Lea symbols): OD 6/6, OS 6/24 (significant difference)
- Cover Test: Right esotropia of 30 prism diopters at distance, 30 prism diopters at near (comitant)
- Hirschberg Test: Left eye shows inward light reflex deviation
- Cycloplegic Refraction (after Cyclopentolate 1% drops, 30 minutes wait):
  - OD: +2.00 DS
  - OS: +6.50 DS (significant anisometropia - 4.50D difference)
- Fundus Examination: Normal OU, optic nerves healthy, no pathology
- Stereoacuity: Nil (no measurable stereopsis due to strabismus)
- Fixation: Alternating fixation pattern, but right eye preferred

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Anisometropic amblyopia with accommodative esotropia (most likely)
2. Congenital esotropia (typically presents earlier, before age 6 months)
3. Acquired non-accommodative esotropia
4. Sixth nerve palsy (would have different motility findings)

**Diagnosis:**
Anisometropic Amblyopia with Accommodative Esotropia - The significant anisometropia (4.50D difference between eyes) has caused amblyopia of the more hyperopic left eye. The left eye requires +6.50D to focus clearly, but when accommodating to focus, the excessive convergence causes esotropia. This is a treatable condition if caught early.

**Management Plan:**

**Phase 1 - Optical Correction:**
1. Prescribe full cycloplegic correction (essential):
   - OD: +2.00 DS
   - OS: +6.50 DS
2. Lens recommendations:
   - High-index material for left eye (cosmetically important)
   - Polycarbonate for safety
   - Anti-reflective coating
3. Glasses wear must be constant during all waking hours

**Phase 2 - Amblyopia Treatment:**
1. Part-time occlusion therapy:
   - Patch right eye 2-4 hours daily
   - Active near activities during patching (coloring, puzzles, reading)
   - Alternative: Bangerter filter on right lens if patching not tolerated
2. If patching unsuccessful: Atropine penalization 1% weekly in right eye
3. Monitor visual acuity every 4-6 weeks initially

**Phase 3 - Strabismus Management:**
1. Reassess esotropia after 4-6 weeks of full-time glasses wear
2. Glasses may fully correct the deviation (fully accommodative esotropia)
3. If residual esotropia >10 PD after glasses, consider:
   - Bifocal lenses (for near esotropia)
   - Strabismus surgery (for non-accommodative component)
4. Monitor stereopsis development

**Prognosis:**
- Excellent prognosis for visual improvement if treated before age 7
- Earlier treatment = better outcome
- May need continued maintenance patching until age 8-9
- Stereopsis may develop if eyes align with glasses
- Glasses will likely be needed long-term

**Parent Education:**
- Compliance with glasses wear is absolutely critical
- Patching compliance directly impacts outcome
- Regular follow-up appointments are essential
- Visual improvement takes time (weeks to months)
- Contact lenses may be an option in the future
- Report any new symptoms immediately

### Case Study 12: Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Baby Amna (accompanied by parent)
- Age: 11 months
- Parent's Concern: "My baby's left eye has been watering since birth and now has yellow discharge for the past week."

**History of Present Illness:**
Amna's parents report that her left eye has been watering (epiphora) since shortly after birth. The watering is worse when she cries or goes outside in cold weather. In the past week, they noticed yellowish discharge and increased matting of the eyelashes, especially upon waking. There is no redness of the eye itself, swelling around the eye, or fever. The right eye is completely unaffected. She was born at term via normal vaginal delivery.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Behavior: Fixes and follows OU appropriately for age
- External Examination: Left eye shows epiphora (tear overflow) and mucoid discharge at medial canthus
- Eyelids: Mild crusting on left eyelashes, no lid edema
- Conjunctiva: Mild injection in left eye, predominantly medially
- Cornea: Clear OU, no abrasions or ulcers
- Lacrimal System Examination:
  - Left eye: Regurgitation of mucopurulent material from lower punctum upon pressure over the lacrimal sac area
  - Right eye: Normal
- No swelling, redness, or tenderness over the lacrimal sac area (no dacryocystitis)

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (CNLDO) - most likely
2. Acute dacryocystitis (excluded - no swelling or systemic signs)
3. Neonatal conjunctivitis (different time course, usually bilateral)
4. Dacryocele (would have visible cystic mass)
5. Punctal atresia (would not show regurgitation from sac)

**Diagnosis:**
Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction with Secondary Bacterial Colonization - The classic presentation of epiphora since birth with regurgitation of mucopurulent material on lacrimal sac compression confirms CNLDO. The obstruction is typically at the valve of Hasner (membranous obstruction at the distal end of the nasolacrimal duct). The recent yellow discharge indicates bacterial colonization.

**Management Plan:**

**Initial Treatment:**
1. Crigler massage technique:
   - Instruct parents to perform lacrimal sac massage 2-3 times daily
   - Technique: Apply firm pressure over the lacrimal sac (medial canthal area) and stroke downward toward the nostril
   - Purpose: Creates hydrostatic pressure to rupture the membranous obstruction
   - Perform before feedings when baby is calm

2. Topical antibiotic for secondary infection:
   - Erythromycin 0.5% ophthalmic ointment three times daily for 7 days
   - Apply after massage

3. Lid hygiene:
   - Clean crusts with warm water and clean cloth
   - Separate cloth for each eye to prevent cross-contamination

**If Persistent After 12 Months of Age:**
1. Probing of the nasolacrimal duct:
   - Performed under brief general anesthesia (15-20 minutes)
   - Success rate: >90% before 18 months of age
   - Metal probe passed through punctum, canaliculus, lacrimal sac, and nasolacrimal duct to rupture the membrane

**If Probing Fails:**
1. Silicone intubation (silicone tube left in place for 3-6 months)
2. Balloon dacryoplasty (dilation of nasolacrimal duct)
3. Dacryocystorhinostomy (rarely needed in children)

**Parent Education:**
- CNLDO is very common (affects 6% of newborns)
- 90% resolve spontaneously by 12 months of age with massage alone
- Proper massage technique demonstration essential
- Watch for signs of acute dacryocystitis: fever, swelling, redness over inner corner of eye (requires immediate medical attention)
- If no improvement by 12 months, probing will be recommended
- The condition is not dangerous to vision

### Case Study 13: Pseudostrabismus

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Hassan (accompanied by parent)
- Age: 3 years
- Parent's Concern: "Sometimes I think my son's eyes don't look straight, especially in photos."

**History of Present Illness:**
Hassan's mother has noticed intermittently that his eyes appear misaligned, especially when he is tired, looking to the side, or in photographs taken with flash. She is concerned because his cousin has strabismus and wears glasses. There is no history of previous eye problems. Hassan was born full-term with no perinatal complications. His developmental milestones are appropriate for age, and he has no significant medical history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity (Teller Acuity Cards or picture optotypes): Age-appropriate vision OU
- Hirschberg Test (corneal light reflex): Symmetric and centered in both pupils
- Cover-Uncover Test: No manifest deviation (no movement when cover removed)
- Alternate Cover Test: Small exophoria at distance and near (within normal limits, no phoria-breaking)
- Near Point of Convergence: To the nose, excellent convergence
- Stereopsis (Randot or Titmus): 40 seconds of arc (excellent for age)
- Cycloplegic Refraction: OD +1.50 DS, OS +1.75 DS (physiological hyperopia for age)
- Fundus Examination: Normal OU, healthy optic nerves and macula
- External Features: Prominent epicanthal folds, flat nasal bridge

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Pseudostrabismus (apparent misalignment without true deviation) - most likely
2. Intermittent exotropia (would show deviation on cover test)
3. Accommodative esotropia (would show esophoria/esotropia)
4. True strabismus (excluded by clinical examination)

**Diagnosis:**
Pseudostrabismus - The appearance of misalignment is due to anatomical facial features rather than true strabismus. Prominent epicanthal folds and a flat nasal bridge create an optical illusion where less sclera is visible medially, making the eye appear turned inward (pseudo-esotropia). The clinical examination confirms orthophoria with excellent binocular function.

**Key Clinical Features of Pseudostrabismus:**
- Prominent epicanthal folds (skin folds extending from upper eyelid to medial canthus)
- Flat nasal bridge (common in young children)
- Positive angle kappa (optic nerve position creates illusion of exotropia)
- Symmetric corneal light reflex (confirms alignment)
- No movement on cover test (rules out true deviation)
- Normal stereopsis (confirms binocular function)

**Management:**
1. Thorough clinical examination to rule out true strabismus (completed)
2. Reassurance of parents (most important intervention)
3. Explanation of why the eyes appear misaligned (epicanthal folds, nasal bridge)
4. No treatment needed
5. Routine pediatric ophthalmology follow-up
6. Educate about warning signs of true strabismus

**Parent Education:**
- The eyes are healthy and properly aligned
- The appearance is caused by normal facial anatomy (epicanthal folds)
- The appearance will improve naturally as facial features mature
- Epicanthal folds typically flatten by age 5-7 years
- The nasal bridge will become more prominent with growth
- Return if they notice: constant misalignment, head tilt, squinting, covering one eye, or any vision concerns

### Case Study 14: Retinopathy of Prematurity Screening

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Baby Mariam (NICU patient)
- Age: 32 weeks postmenstrual age (PMA) - born at 26 weeks gestation, currently 6 weeks old
- Referral: Routine ROP screening per NICU protocol

**History:**
Baby Mariam was born at 26 weeks gestational age with birth weight of 850 grams (extremely low birth weight). She required supplemental oxygen for 3 weeks and is currently on minimal respiratory support. She has no intraventricular hemorrhage, necrotizing enterocolitis, or other major complications of prematurity. This is her second ROP screening examination.

**Risk Factors for ROP:**
- Extreme prematurity (born at 26 weeks, <30 weeks GA)
- Very low birth weight (850g, <1500g)
- Prolonged oxygen therapy (3 weeks)
- Multiple risk factors present

**Clinical Findings (ROP Screening Examination):**
- Examination Technique: Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy with 28D lens, scleral depression performed by trained pediatric ophthalmologist
- Premedication: Topical anesthetic, pacifier with sucrose
- Right Eye Findings:
  - Zone I: No vessels visible in Zone I (good - Zone I involvement indicates severe disease)
  - Zone II: Normal vascularization in progress, vessels reaching mid-periphery
  - Zone III: Not yet vascularized (expected for this gestational age)
  - Stage: No ROP identified
  - Plus Disease: None (no dilation or tortuosity of posterior vessels)
- Left Eye Findings:
  - Similar to right eye, no ROP identified
  - Normal vascular development progressing

**ROP Classification:**
- Zone: The retinal area divided into 3 concentric circles centered on the optic disc
  - Zone I: Most posterior, highest risk
  - Zone II: Mid-peripheral
  - Zone III: Most peripheral, lowest risk
- Stage: Severity of abnormal vessel development (1-5)
- Plus Disease: Posterior pole vessel dilation and tortuosity (indicates aggressive disease)

**Diagnosis:**
Immature Retina - No Retinopathy of Prematurity at this examination. The retina is still developing with incomplete vascularization, which is normal for a 32-week PMA infant. Continued monitoring is essential as ROP can still develop.

**Management Plan:**
1. Continue weekly screening examinations (ROP can develop rapidly)
2. Next examination scheduled at 33 weeks PMA
3. Maintain stable oxygen saturation levels (target 90-95% as per NICU protocol)
4. Document findings per international ROP classification
5. Communicate results to NICU team and parents

**ROP Screening Schedule:**
- First examination: At 31 weeks PMA OR 4 weeks after birth (whichever is later)
- Follow-up frequency:
  - Weekly if normal vascularization or early ROP
  - Every 3-5 days if advanced ROP or type 2 ROP
  - Every 2-3 days if pre-threshold ROP
- Continue screening until:
  - Retina fully vascularized to Zone III, OR
  - ROP resolved without treatment, OR
  - After treatment with documented stability

**Treatment Thresholds (Type 1 ROP):**
- Zone I, any stage with plus disease
- Zone I, stage 3 without plus disease
- Zone II, stage 2 or 3 with plus disease
- Treatment within 48-72 hours of identification

**Treatment Options:**
- Laser photocoagulation (gold standard)
- Anti-VEGF injection (Bevacizumab or Ranibizumab)
- Combination therapy

**Documentation Required:**
- Postmenstrual age at examination
- Zone of any ROP
- Stage of ROP (1-5)
- Clock hours of involvement
- Presence or absence of plus disease
- Comparison with previous examination

### Case Study 15: Progressive Myopia in Children

**Patient Profile:**
- Name: Sultan
- Age: 9 years
- Parent's Concern: "My son's vision is getting worse every year, and his glasses prescription keeps getting stronger."

**History of Present Illness:**
Sultan was first prescribed glasses at age 6 for mild myopia (-1.00D OU). Over the past 3 years, his prescription has increased by approximately 0.75-1.00D per year. Both parents are myopic (father -5.00D, mother -3.50D). Sultan is an avid reader and spends significant time on tablets, computers, and phones for both schoolwork and entertainment. He spends minimal time outdoors for physical activities. The parents are concerned about the rapid progression and potential long-term eye health implications.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual Acuity with current glasses: OD 6/9, OS 6/9 (reduced from 6/6 with current prescription)
- Current Prescription (6 months old): OD -2.75 DS, OS -2.50 DS
- Cycloplegic Refraction (after Cyclopentolate 1%):
  - OD: -3.50 DS
  - OS: -3.25 DS
- Axial Length Measurement:
  - OD: 25.2mm (increased from 24.6mm one year ago - significant elongation)
  - OS: 25.1mm (increased from 24.5mm one year ago)
- Anterior Segment: Normal OU, no keratoconus signs
- Fundus Examination:
  - Mild peripapillary atrophy OU (early myopic changes)
  - Tessellated fundus appearance
  - No posterior staphyloma
  - No lattice degeneration or retinal breaks
- Intraocular Pressure: Normal OU

**Diagnosis:**
Progressive Myopia - Annual progression of 0.75-1.00D with increasing axial length is considered significant myopia progression. Risk factors include parental myopia, increased near work, and reduced outdoor time. The axial length measurement confirms eye elongation, the primary anatomical change in myopia.

**Myopia Control Strategies:**

**1. Low-Dose Atropine Therapy:**
- Mechanism: Non-selective muscarinic antagonist that may slow axial elongation
- Dose: Atropine 0.01% nightly (both eyes)
- Efficacy: 50-60% reduction in progression rate in clinical trials
- Side effects: Minimal at 0.01% concentration; mild photophobia possible
- Duration: Typically 2-3 years until myopia stabilizes
- Follow-up: Every 6 months with cycloplegic refraction and axial length

**2. Orthokeratology (OK Lenses):**
- Mechanism: Overnight wear of rigid lenses that temporarily reshape cornea
- Efficacy: 40-50% reduction in axial elongation
- Advantages: No daytime glasses needed; freedom for sports
- Disadvantages: Cost; risk of infection; requires motivated patient
- Consideration: May combine with atropine for enhanced effect

**3. Multifocal Soft Contact Lenses:**
- Design: Center-distance with peripheral add zones
- Mechanism: Create peripheral myopic defocus
- Efficacy: 30-50% reduction in progression
- Options: Daily disposable multifocals for convenience

**4. Specialized Spectacle Lenses:**
- DIMS (Defocus Incorporated Multiple Segments) technology
- HAL (Highly Aspherical Lenslet) technology
- Peripheral defocus-modifying designs
- Efficacy: 50-60% reduction in progression

**5. Lifestyle Modifications:**
- Increase outdoor time: 2+ hours daily (proven protective effect)
- Implement 20-20-20 rule during near work
- Maintain proper reading distance (30-40cm / Harmon distance)
- Ensure adequate lighting for all near tasks
- Limit screen time, especially handheld devices
- Take breaks during prolonged near work

**Recommended Management for Sultan:**
1. Low-dose atropine 0.01% nightly to both eyes
2. Continue distance glasses for school and activities
3. Encourage outdoor activities (join a sports team)
4. Implement strict screen time limits and breaks
5. 6-month follow-up with axial length measurement
6. Consider orthokeratology if progression continues despite atropine

**Parent Education:**
- Myopia progression is common but can be slowed
- Goal is to reduce final myopia level to lower long-term risks
- Higher myopia increases risk of: retinal detachment, myopic maculopathy, glaucoma, early cataracts
- Treatment requires long-term commitment (years)
- Compliance with atropine and lifestyle changes is essential
- Regular monitoring with axial length is key to tracking effectiveness
- Not all treatments work for all children - may need to adjust approach

## Summary and Examination Preparation Tips

These 15 comprehensive clinical case studies represent scenarios that optometrists commonly encounter in practice and are representative of cases that may appear in the DOH Abu Dhabi Optometry licensing examination.

### Key Learning Points by Domain:

**Red Eye Cases:**
- Always consider the differential diagnosis based on clinical features
- Distinguish between infectious, inflammatory, and mechanical causes
- Remember that topical steroids are CONTRAINDICATED in HSV epithelial keratitis
- Contact lens wearers with red eye require urgent evaluation

**Vision Loss Cases:**
- Sudden vision loss is an emergency requiring immediate evaluation
- Time is critical in retinal detachment and acute angle-closure glaucoma
- Always document visual acuity at initial presentation
- Consider neurological causes when indicated

**Refraction Cases:**
- A comprehensive refraction includes objective and subjective methods
- Consider patient lifestyle and occupation when prescribing
- Presbyopia management requires discussion of multiple options
- Children require cycloplegic refraction for accurate measurement

**Contact Lens Cases:**
- Proper fitting technique and patient education are essential
- Recognize and manage contact lens complications promptly
- GPC is a common cause of contact lens intolerance
- Keratoconus patients require specialized lens designs

**Pediatric Cases:**
- Early detection and treatment of amblyopia is critical (before age 7)
- Pseudostrabismus must be differentiated from true strabismus
- Myopia control is an increasingly important aspect of pediatric optometry
- ROP screening protocols must be followed precisely

### Examination Tips:
- Read each question carefully and identify key clinical features
- Consider the most likely diagnosis, then formulate differential diagnoses
- Be prepared to justify management decisions with clinical reasoning
- Know the red flags and emergency conditions requiring urgent referral
- Understand pharmacological management principles and contraindications
- Be familiar with referral guidelines and when to escalate care
- Practice time management during the examination
