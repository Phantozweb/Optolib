# Systemic Conditions & Ocular Manifestations for DOH

## Introduction

The relationship between systemic diseases and ocular manifestations is a critical area of knowledge for optometrists preparing for the Department of Health (DOH) Abu Dhabi licensing examination. The eye serves as a unique window into systemic health, with many systemic conditions producing characteristic ocular signs that can be detected during comprehensive eye examinations. Understanding these associations enables optometrists to play a vital role in early disease detection, monitoring disease progression, and facilitating timely referrals to appropriate healthcare specialists.

This comprehensive guide covers the major systemic conditions that commonly present with ocular manifestations, focusing on diabetes mellitus, hypertension, thyroid disorders, and autoimmune diseases. Each section provides detailed information on pathophysiology, clinical presentations, diagnostic considerations, and management implications relevant to optometric practice in Abu Dhabi and the broader UAE healthcare context.

## 1. Diabetes Mellitus and Ocular Manifestations

### Overview of Diabetes Mellitus

Diabetes mellitus (DM) represents a group of metabolic disorders characterized by chronic hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. The global prevalence of diabetes has reached epidemic proportions, with the Middle East region, including the UAE, experiencing some of the highest rates worldwide. The International Diabetes Federation estimates that approximately 19% of the UAE adult population has diabetes, making diabetic eye disease a significant public health concern.

### Classification of Diabetes

**Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM):**
- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Absolute insulin deficiency
- Typically presents in childhood or adolescence
- Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes cases

**Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM):**
- Insulin resistance with progressive beta-cell dysfunction
- Associated with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and genetic factors
- Most common form, accounting for 90-95% of cases
- Increasingly diagnosed in younger populations

**Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM):**
- Develops during pregnancy
- Increases risk of developing T2DM later in life
- Requires careful monitoring during pregnancy

### Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) remains the leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults worldwide. The pathophysiology involves chronic hyperglycemia-induced damage to retinal microvasculature, leading to microaneurysms, capillary non-perfusion, and retinal ischemia.

**Classification of Diabetic Retinopathy:**

**Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):**

*Mild NPDR:*
- At least one microaneurysm present
- No other signs of retinopathy
- Risk of progression to PDR: approximately 5% within 1 year

*Moderate NPDR:*
- More than just microaneurysms
- Hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots
- Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA) may be present
- Risk of progression to PDR: 12-27% within 1 year

*Severe NPDR:*
- Characterized by the "4-2-1 rule":
  - Severe hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants
  - Venous beading in 2 or more quadrants
  - Prominent IRMA in 1 or more quadrants
- Risk of progression to PDR: approximately 50% within 1 year

**Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR):**
- Neovascularization of the disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- Neovascularization may lead to vitreous hemorrhage
- Tractional retinal detachment can occur
- High risk of severe visual loss if untreated

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
- Accumulation of fluid in the macula
- Can occur at any stage of DR
- Leading cause of visual impairment in diabetes
- Classified as center-involved or non-center-involved

**Clinical Features:**

*Microaneurysms:*
- Focal outpouchings of capillary walls
- Appear as small red dots on fundus examination
- Best visualized on fluorescein angiography

*Retinal Hemorrhages:*
- Dot-blot hemorrhages in the inner nuclear layer
- Flame-shaped hemorrhages in the nerve fiber layer
- Indicate vascular incompetence

*Hard Exudates:*
- Yellow, waxy deposits in the outer plexiform layer
- Composed of lipoprotein material
- Often form circinate patterns around leaking microaneurysms

*Cotton-Wool Spots:*
- Fluffy white lesions in the nerve fiber layer
- Represent localized infarcts
- Indicate retinal ischemia

*Venous Abnormalities:*
- Venous beading, looping, and dilation
- Indicate severe retinal hypoxia

**Management and Referral Guidelines:**

Optometrists play a crucial role in diabetic eye screening. The DOH emphasizes regular screening intervals based on disease severity:

- **No DR:** Annual screening
- **Mild NPDR:** Every 9-12 months
- **Moderate NPDR:** Every 6-9 months
- **Severe NPDR:** Every 3-4 months, ophthalmology referral
- **PDR or DME:** Immediate ophthalmology referral

### Other Ocular Manifestations of Diabetes

**Refractive Changes:**
- Fluctuating vision due to blood glucose changes
- Hyperglycemia can cause temporary myopia
- Stabilize blood glucose before prescribing new glasses

**Cataract:**
- Earlier onset in diabetic patients
- May progress more rapidly
- Sorbitol accumulation in lens contributes to cataract formation

**Glaucoma:**
- Increased risk of primary open-angle glaucoma
- Neovascular glaucoma in PDR
- Higher IOP associated with diabetic population

**Cranial Nerve Palsies:**
- Typically microvascular in origin
- Third, fourth, and sixth cranial nerves affected
- Usually spontaneous recovery within 3 months
- Pupil-sparing in diabetic third nerve palsy

**Anterior Segment Changes:**
- Recurrent corneal erosions
- Delayed corneal healing
- Increased risk of post-surgical complications
- Reduced corneal sensation

**Ocular Surface Disease:**
- Dry eye syndrome common in diabetic patients
- Reduced tear production and altered tear film
- Requires lubrication and management

## 2. Hypertension and Ocular Manifestations

### Overview of Hypertension

Hypertension, or elevated blood pressure, is a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and renal failure. The prevalence of hypertension in the UAE is estimated at 30-35% of the adult population. Ocular manifestations of hypertension provide valuable insights into systemic vascular health and end-organ damage.

### Classification of Blood Pressure

| Category | Systolic (mmHg) | Diastolic (mmHg) |
|----------|-----------------|------------------|
| Normal | < 120 | < 80 |
| Elevated | 120-129 | < 80 |
| Stage 1 Hypertension | 130-139 | 80-89 |
| Stage 2 Hypertension | ≥ 140 | ≥ 90 |
| Hypertensive Crisis | > 180 | > 120 |

### Hypertensive Retinopathy

Hypertensive retinopathy (HR) results from sustained elevated blood pressure causing damage to retinal arterioles, capillaries, and veins. The changes reflect both acute and chronic vascular stress.

**Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification:**

**Grade 1:**
- Mild generalized arteriolar narrowing
- Arteriovenous (AV) ratio less than 2:3
- Good prognosis with treatment

**Grade 2:**
- Definite focal arteriolar narrowing
- AV nicking (arteriovenous nipping)
- Silver wiring of arterioles
- Associated with increased cardiovascular risk

**Grade 3:**
- Retinal hemorrhages (flame-shaped)
- Cotton-wool spots
- Hard exudates
- Indicates significant end-organ damage

**Grade 4:**
- All Grade 3 changes plus papilledema
- Malignant hypertension
- Medical emergency requiring immediate treatment

**Clinical Features:**

*Arteriolar Changes:*
- Generalized narrowing (vasoconstriction)
- Focal narrowing (segments of severe constriction)
- Arteriosclerosis with thickened vessel walls
- Copper wiring and silver wiring appearances

*Arteriovenous Changes:*
- AV nicking: Vein appears compressed under crossing artery
- Tapering of veins on either side of crossing
- Can lead to branch retinal vein occlusion

*Retinal Hemorrhages:*
- Flame-shaped in nerve fiber layer
- May be accompanied by dot-blot hemorrhages

*Exudates:*
- Hard exudates in outer plexiform layer
- Cotton-wool spots in nerve fiber layer

*Optic Disc Changes:*
- Disc edema in malignant hypertension
- Chronic ischemic optic neuropathy

### Hypertensive Choroidopathy

In acute severe hypertension, choroidal vascular changes may occur:

- **Elschnig spots:** Focal choroidal infarcts appearing as black spots with yellow halos
- **Siegrist streaks:** Linear hyperpigmented lesions along choroidal vessels
- **Serous retinal detachment:** Can occur in malignant hypertension

### Management Considerations

- Optometrists should measure blood pressure during comprehensive examinations
- Refer patients with Grade 2 or higher changes to ophthalmology and primary care
- Grade 4 changes require immediate medical referral
- Educate patients about medication compliance and lifestyle modifications
- Coordinate care with primary care physicians

### Hypertension and Other Ocular Conditions

**Retinal Vein Occlusion:**
- Hypertension is a major risk factor
- Both central (CRVO) and branch (BRVO) types
- May require anti-VEGF treatment

**Retinal Artery Occlusion:**
- Hypertension contributes to atherosclerotic plaque formation
- Central (CRAO) and branch (BRAO) types
- Ocular emergency requiring immediate management

**Optic Neuropathy:**
- Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION)
- Can be arteritic (GCA) or non-arteritic
- Hypertension is a risk factor for non-arteritic AION

## 3. Thyroid Disorders and Ocular Manifestations

### Overview of Thyroid Disease

Thyroid disorders are among the most common endocrine conditions, with autoimmune thyroid disease being particularly prevalent in the Middle East region. The relationship between thyroid dysfunction and ocular health is complex, with both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism producing distinct ocular manifestations.

### Thyroid Eye Disease (Graves' Ophthalmopathy)

Thyroid eye disease (TED), also known as Graves' orbitopathy or ophthalmopathy, is an autoimmune inflammatory condition affecting the orbital tissues. It most commonly occurs in association with Graves' disease, though it can also occur in euthyroid or hypothyroid individuals.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Autoantibodies target thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor (TSH-R)
- Cross-reactivity with orbital fibroblasts
- Inflammatory infiltration of extraocular muscles and orbital fat
- Glycosaminoglycan deposition leads to tissue expansion

**Clinical Features:**

*Soft Tissue Involvement:*
- Eyelid edema and erythema
- Conjunctival injection and chemosis
- Periorbital edema
- Caruncle swelling

*Eyelid Signs:*
- Lid retraction (most common sign)
- Lid lag on downward gaze (von Graefe's sign)
- Upper lid retraction exposing sclera above the limbus
- Dalrymple's sign: Wide palpebral fissure

*Proptosis:*
- Anterior displacement of the globe
- Asymmetric in up to 70% of cases
- Measured using Hertel exophthalmometry
- Normal: 12-21mm; TED often >21mm

*Extraocular Muscle Involvement:*
- Muscle enlargement (inferior and medial rectus most common)
- Restrictive strabismus
- Diplopia (initially upgaze, then multi-directional)
- Positive forced duction test

*Optic Nerve Compression:*
- Occurs in 5-8% of TED patients
- Caused by enlarged muscles at orbital apex
- Vision-threatening complication
- Requires urgent intervention

**Clinical Activity Score (CAS):**
Used to assess disease activity and guide treatment:

1. Spontaneous retrobulbar pain
2. Pain on eye movement
3. Eyelid redness
4. Eyelid swelling
5. Conjunctival redness
6. Conjunctival swelling (chemosis)
7. Swelling of caruncle

- Score ≥ 3 indicates active disease
- Increase in score over 1-3 months indicates active disease

**NOSPECS Classification:**

| Class | Features |
|-------|----------|
| 0 | No signs or symptoms |
| 1 | Only signs, no symptoms (lid retraction) |
| 2 | Soft tissue involvement |
| 3 | Proptosis |
| 4 | Extraocular muscle involvement |
| 5 | Corneal involvement |
| 6 | Sight loss (optic nerve compression) |

**Management:**

*Mild Disease:*
- Lubricating eye drops and ointments
- Selenium supplementation
- Smoking cessation (crucial for TED management)
- Sleep with head elevated
- Sunglasses for photophobia

*Moderate-Severe Active Disease:*
- Intravenous methylprednisolone (first-line)
- Oral corticosteroids
- Radiotherapy in selected cases

*Sight-Threatening Disease:*
- High-dose intravenous steroids
- Urgent surgical decompression
- Optic neuropathy requires immediate intervention

*Rehabilitative Surgery:*
- Orbital decompression for proptosis
- Strabismus surgery for diplopia
- Eyelid surgery for retraction

### Hypothyroidism and Ocular Manifestations

Hypothyroidism, whether primary or secondary, produces distinct ocular changes:

**Common Features:**
- Periorbital edema (myxedema)
- Loss of lateral eyebrow hairs (madarosis)
- Dry eye syndrome
- Decreased tear production
- Blepharitis
- Slow pupillary light reflex
- Decreased accommodation

**Less Common Features:**
- Optic nerve edema
- Extraocular muscle enlargement (rare)
- Decreased visual acuity

### Thyroid Function Tests

Optometrists should be familiar with thyroid function tests:

| Test | Normal Range | Interpretation |
|------|--------------|----------------|
| TSH | 0.4-4.0 mIU/L | Elevated in hypothyroidism |
| Free T4 | 0.8-1.8 ng/dL | Decreased in hypothyroidism |
| Free T3 | 2.3-4.2 pg/mL | May be normal early in thyroid disease |
| TSH-R Antibodies | <1.75 IU/L | Elevated in Graves' disease |

## 4. Autoimmune Diseases and Ocular Manifestations

### Overview of Autoimmune Diseases

Autoimmune diseases represent a diverse group of conditions characterized by immune system dysregulation leading to self-tissue attack. The eye is commonly involved in many autoimmune conditions, making optometrists essential in the multidisciplinary management of these patients.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

Rheumatoid arthritis is a chronic systemic inflammatory disease primarily affecting joints but with significant ocular manifestations.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Dry Eye Syndrome (Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca):*
- Most common ocular manifestation (15-25% of RA patients)
- Secondary Sjögren's syndrome overlap
- Schirmer test: <5mm in 5 minutes
- Requires aggressive lubrication and management

*Episcleritis:*
- Simple or nodular types
- Mild discomfort, sectoral redness
- Usually self-limiting
- Topical NSAIDs or steroids if symptomatic

*Scleritis:*
- More severe than episcleritis
- Deep, boring pain radiating to forehead
- Reddish-purple scleral discoloration
- Types: Diffuse, nodular, necrotizing
- Requires systemic treatment (NSAIDs, steroids)
- Scleral thinning and staphyloma may result

*Peripheral Ulcerative Keratitis:*
- Corneal thinning at limbus
- Risk of perforation
- Requires urgent ophthalmology referral

*Other Features:*
- Brown's syndrome (superior oblique tendon sheath involvement)
- Drug-induced ocular effects (hydroxychloroquine retinopathy)

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

SLE is a multisystem autoimmune disease with diverse ocular manifestations ranging from mild dry eye to sight-threatening retinopathy.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Ocular Surface:*
- Dry eye syndrome (common)
- Discoid lupus of eyelids
- Keratoconjunctivitis sicca

*Retinal Manifestations:*
- Cotton-wool spots (most common)
- Retinal hemorrhages
- Vascular occlusions
- Lupus retinopathy indicates active systemic disease

*Choroidal Involvement:*
- Serous retinal detachment
- Choroidal infarcts

*Neuro-ophthalmic Manifestations:*
- Optic neuritis
- Ischemic optic neuropathy
- Pseudotumor cerebri (idiopathic intracranial hypertension)
- Cranial nerve palsies

*Antiphospholipid Syndrome Association:*
- Increased risk of retinal vascular occlusions
- Recurrent thrombosis
- Requires anticoagulation

### Sjögren's Syndrome

Sjögren's syndrome is a chronic autoimmune disorder characterized by lymphocytic infiltration of exocrine glands, leading to dry eyes and dry mouth.

**Classification:**
- Primary: Occurs alone
- Secondary: Associated with other autoimmune diseases (RA, SLE)

**Ocular Features:**
- Severe dry eye
- Conjunctival injection
- Filamentary keratitis
- Corneal epithelial defects
- Decreased visual acuity

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Schirmer test: ≤5mm in 5 minutes
- Rose Bengal or Lissamine Green staining
- Positive anti-SS-A (Ro) and anti-SS-B (La) antibodies
- Salivary gland biopsy

**Management:**
- Preservative-free artificial tears
- Punctal plugs
- Cyclosporine ophthalmic emulsion
- Secretagogues (pilocarpine, cevimeline)
- Autologous serum eye drops

### Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Multiple sclerosis is a chronic inflammatory demyelinating disease of the central nervous system with significant neuro-ophthalmic manifestations.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Optic Neuritis:*
- Most common presenting symptom of MS
- Unilateral vision loss
- Pain on eye movement
- Relative afferent pupillary defect
- Central or paracentral scotoma
- Optic disc may be normal (retrobulbar neuritis) or swollen

*Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia (INO):*
- Impaired adduction on lateral gaze
- Jerk nystagmus in abducting eye
- Indicates lesion in medial longitudinal fasciculus
- Highly suggestive of MS in young patients

*Other Features:*
- Nystagmus
- Sixth nerve palsy
- Visual field defects
- Uveitis (intermediate uveitis/pars planitis)

### Ankylosing Spondylitis

Ankylosing spondylitis is a chronic inflammatory disease primarily affecting the axial skeleton, with strong association to HLA-B27.

**Ocular Manifestations:**
- Acute anterior uveitis (most common extra-articular manifestation)
- 25-40% of patients develop uveitis
- Unilateral, recurrent attacks
- Symptoms: Pain, redness, photophobia, blurred vision
- Cellular reaction and flare in anterior chamber
- Posterior synechiae may form
- Requires topical steroids and cycloplegics
- Referral to ophthalmology for management

### Sarcoidosis

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disorder that frequently involves the eye.

**Ocular Manifestations:**
- Anterior uveitis (most common)
- Posterior uveitis
- Retinal periphlebitis ("candle-wax drippings")
- Choroidal granulomas
- Optic nerve involvement
- Lacrimal gland enlargement

### Behçet's Disease

Behçet's disease is a systemic vasculitis characterized by recurrent oral and genital ulcers, with ocular involvement in 70% of patients.

**Ocular Manifestations:**
- Recurrent anterior uveitis (often with hypopyon)
- Posterior uveitis
- Retinal vasculitis
- Branch retinal vein occlusion
- Optic neuritis
- Vision-threatening without treatment

### Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) Syndrome

VKH is a rare systemic autoimmune disease affecting pigmented tissues.

**Clinical Phases:**
1. **Prodromal:** Flu-like symptoms, headache, tinnitus
2. **Acute Uveitic:** Bilateral panuveitis, serous retinal detachment
3. **Convalescent:** Depigmentation (poliosis, vitiligo)
4. **Chronic Recurrent:** Recurrent anterior uveitis

## Clinical Assessment and Documentation

### Essential Components of Systemic Disease Evaluation

**Patient History:**
- Medical history (diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disease)
- Medication history (hydroxychloroquine, corticosteroids)
- Family history of systemic diseases
- Systemic symptoms (fatigue, weight changes, joint pain)

**Ocular Examination:**
- Visual acuity
- Pupil examination
- Intraocular pressure
- Anterior segment examination
- Fundus examination (dilated)
- Visual field assessment if indicated

### Documentation Guidelines

For DOH examination purposes, document:
1. Systemic diagnosis and duration
2. Current medications
3. Ocular findings with clinical significance
4. Referral recommendations
5. Patient education provided

## Referral Guidelines

### Urgent Referral (Within 24-48 hours)
- Proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Diabetic macular edema with central involvement
- Grade 3-4 hypertensive retinopathy
- Thyroid eye disease with optic nerve compression
- Scleritis with necrotizing features
- Severe uveitis

### Routine Referral
- Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (moderate-severe)
- Grade 2 hypertensive retinopathy
- Thyroid eye disease (mild-moderate)
- Dry eye requiring specialized management
- Suspected autoimmune disease with ocular involvement

## Conclusion

Understanding the relationship between systemic diseases and ocular manifestations is essential for optometrists preparing for the DOH Abu Dhabi examination. The eye provides a unique opportunity to assess systemic health, and optometrists play a critical role in early detection, monitoring, and appropriate referral of patients with systemic conditions.

Key points for the examination:
1. Diabetes mellitus: Regular screening, recognition of DR stages, appropriate referral timing
2. Hypertension: Classification of hypertensive retinopathy, recognition of malignant hypertension
3. Thyroid disease: Recognition of TED signs, CAS scoring, management of sight-threatening complications
4. Autoimmune diseases: Recognition of common ocular manifestations, appropriate referral
