# Ocular Pharmacology for DOH Exam

## Introduction to Ocular Pharmacology

Ocular pharmacology is a fundamental component of optometric practice and a critical subject area for the Department of Health (DOH) Abu Dhabi Optometry Licensing Examination. This comprehensive guide covers the essential topical medications, antibiotics, corticosteroids, and glaucoma medications that every optometrist must master for safe and effective clinical practice. Understanding pharmacokinetics, mechanisms of action, clinical indications, contraindications, and potential adverse effects is essential for passing the DOH examination and providing optimal patient care.

## Topical Ocular Medications: An Overview

### Pharmacokinetics of Topical Ocular Drugs

Topical ocular medications represent the primary route of drug delivery for anterior segment conditions. The unique anatomy and physiology of the eye create specific challenges and opportunities for drug delivery. When a topical medication is instilled into the conjunctival sac, it must traverse multiple barriers to reach its target tissue.

The corneal epithelium serves as the primary barrier to drug penetration. The lipophilic nature of the epithelium favors the absorption of lipophilic drugs, while the hydrophilic stroma preferentially allows passage of hydrophilic compounds. This dual barrier system means that drugs with both lipophilic and hydrophilic properties (amphiphilic drugs) demonstrate optimal corneal penetration.

Factors affecting drug bioavailability include:
- **Instillation volume**: The conjunctival sac can only hold approximately 30 microliters; excess volume drains through the nasolacrimal system
- **Blink rate**: Increased blinking accelerates drug clearance from the ocular surface
- **Tear film turnover**: Tears are produced at approximately 1-2 microliters per minute, diluting topical medications
- **Protein binding**: Drugs bound to tear proteins are pharmacologically inactive
- **Nasolacrimal drainage**: Up to 80% of instilled drug may be systemically absorbed through the nasal mucosa

### Vehicle Systems and Formulations

Modern ophthalmic formulations employ various vehicle systems to enhance drug delivery and patient compliance:

**Solutions**: The most common formulation, providing rapid drug delivery but short contact time. Preservatives such as benzalkonium chloride (BAK) enhance corneal penetration by disrupting epithelial tight junctions but may cause corneal toxicity with chronic use.

**Suspensions**: Contain undissolved drug particles that provide prolonged drug release. Patients must be instructed to shake suspensions before use to ensure uniform dosing. Examples include prednisolone acetate and brinzolamide.

**Ointments**: Provide extended contact time and enhanced corneal penetration due to their viscous nature. Primarily used for nighttime application or when prolonged drug delivery is needed. May cause temporary blurred vision.

**Gels and Gel-forming Solutions**: Utilize polymers such as carbomers, gellan gum, or polycarbophil to increase viscosity and prolong drug contact time. Timolol gel-forming solution allows once-daily dosing compared to twice-daily solutions.

**Preservative-free Formulations**: Essential for patients with ocular surface disease, those requiring chronic therapy, or individuals with preservative sensitivity. Available in single-use containers or multi-dose bottles with special valve systems.

**Emulsions and Nanoparticle Systems**: Advanced drug delivery systems that enhance bioavailability and reduce dosing frequency. Cyclosporine ophthalmic emulsion (Restasis) is an example of this technology.

## Topical Antibiotics

### Classification and Mechanisms of Action

Antibiotics are classified based on their mechanism of action and chemical structure. Understanding these classifications is essential for appropriate antibiotic selection and understanding resistance patterns.

**Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors**:
- **Penicillins**: Not commonly used topically due to allergenicity
- **Cephalosporins**: Cephazolin (compounding), broad-spectrum coverage
- **Bacitracin**: Effective against gram-positive organisms, commonly used in combination ointments

**Protein Synthesis Inhibitors**:
- **Aminoglycosides**: Gentamicin, tobramycin - inhibit bacterial protein synthesis by binding to 30S ribosomal subunit
- **Macrolides**: Erythromycin, azithromycin - bind to 50S ribosomal subunit
- **Tetracyclines**: Tetracycline, doxycycline - broad-spectrum, commonly used for meibomian gland dysfunction
- **Chloramphenicol**: Broad-spectrum, risk of aplastic anemia limits use in some regions

**DNA Synthesis Inhibitors**:
- **Fluoroquinolones**: Ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin, levofloxacin, moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin, besifloxacin - inhibit DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV
- **Trimethoprim**: Often combined with polymyxin B for broad-spectrum coverage

**Metabolic Pathway Inhibitors**:
- **Sulfonamides**: Sulfacetamide - competitive antagonists of para-aminobenzoic acid
- **Trimethoprim**: Inhibits dihydrofolate reductase

### Fluoroquinolones: First-line Agents

Fluoroquinolones represent the most commonly prescribed topical antibiotics for bacterial conjunctivitis and keratitis. They are classified into generations based on their antimicrobial spectrum:

**Second Generation**: Ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin
- Excellent gram-negative coverage
- Moderate gram-positive coverage
- Ciprofloxacin associated with white corneal precipitates
- Good penetration into ocular tissues

**Third Generation**: Levofloxacin
- Enhanced gram-positive coverage compared to second generation
- Good penetration into ocular tissues
- Improved coverage against Streptococcus pneumoniae

**Fourth Generation**: Moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin, besifloxacin
- Excellent gram-positive coverage including resistant streptococci
- Enhanced coverage against atypical pathogens
- Lower minimum inhibitory concentrations (MICs) against many pathogens
- Besifloxacin is the only fluoroquinolone developed exclusively for ophthalmic use, potentially reducing systemic resistance development

**Clinical Pearls for DOH Exam**:
- Fluoroquinolones are contraindicated in pregnancy due to cartilage damage risk
- Not recommended for children under 18 for systemic use, but topical use is generally considered safe
- Resistance is increasing, particularly among methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA)
- Besifloxacin suspension requires shaking before instillation
- Moxifloxacin and gatifloxacin have better solubility and don't form corneal precipitates

### Aminoglycosides: Gentamicin and Tobramycin

Aminoglycosides remain valuable agents for gram-negative infections, particularly Pseudomonas aeruginosa. Key considerations include:

**Gentamicin**:
- Available as 0.3% solution and ointment
- Effective against gram-negative organisms including Pseudomonas
- Not effective against streptococci or anaerobes
- May cause punctate keratopathy with prolonged use
- Concentration-dependent killing

**Tobramycin**:
- Similar spectrum to gentamicin
- May be more effective against Pseudomonas aeruginosa
- Generally better tolerated with less corneal toxicity
- Available as 0.3% solution and ointment
- Often combined with dexamethasone for combined anti-inflammatory and antibiotic coverage

**Dosing**: Typically 1-2 drops every 2-4 hours for acute infections, reducing as symptoms improve. For severe keratitis, more frequent dosing may be required.

### Macrolides and Tetracyclines

**Erythromycin**:
- Available as 0.5% ointment
- Effective against gram-positive organisms
- Commonly used for prophylaxis of neonatal ophthalmia
- Not effective against Pseudomonas or Enterobacteriaceae
- Safe in pregnancy and children

**Azithromycin**:
- Available as 1% solution (AzaSite)
- Extended dosing regimen due to tissue binding
- Effective for bacterial conjunctivitis and blepharitis
- Anti-inflammatory properties may benefit meibomian gland dysfunction
- Dosed twice daily for first two days, then once daily

**Tetracycline/Doxycycline**:
- Topical tetracycline available as 1% solution or ointment
- Oral doxycycline (20-100 mg daily) commonly used for meibomian gland dysfunction and rosacea
- Anti-inflammatory and anti-metalloproteinase properties
- Photosensitivity risk with oral use; advise sun protection
- Not recommended in pregnancy or children under 8

### Antibiotic Resistance Considerations

Antibiotic resistance is a growing concern in ophthalmic practice. The DOH examination may include questions on:

- **MRSA**: Increasing prevalence; treat with vancomycin (requires compounding), besifloxacin, or combination therapy
- **MRSE (Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus epidermidis)**: Common cause of post-operative endophthalmitis; similar treatment approach as MRSA
- **Fluoroquinolone resistance**: Increasing resistance among staphylococcal species; consider alternative agents or combination therapy
- **Principles of antibiotic stewardship**: Use narrow-spectrum agents when possible, complete prescribed courses, avoid unnecessary prophylactic use
- **Culture and sensitivity testing**: Essential for severe or persistent infections

## Topical Corticosteroids

### Mechanism of Action

Corticosteroids exert their anti-inflammatory effects through multiple mechanisms:

1. **Inhibition of phospholipase A2**: Reduces arachidonic acid release, decreasing production of prostaglandins and leukotrienes
2. **Suppression of inflammatory cytokines**: Reduces IL-1, IL-6, TNF-alpha, and other pro-inflammatory mediators
3. **Reduction of vascular permeability**: Decreases edema and cellular infiltration
4. **Inhibition of fibroblast proliferation**: Reduces scar formation
5. **Modulation of immune cell function**: Decreases T-cell activation and migration

### Classification by Potency

Topical corticosteroids are classified by their anti-inflammatory potency:

**High Potency**:
- Prednisolone acetate 1%
- Dexamethasone 0.1%
- Diffuprednate 0.05%
- Fluorometholone acetate 0.1%

**Medium Potency**:
- Loteprednol etabonate 0.5%
- Rimexolone 1%
- Prednisolone sodium phosphate 1%

**Low Potency**:
- Fluorometholone 0.1% (alcohol)
- Hydrocortisone 0.5%

**Clinical Note**: The ester form of fluorometholone (fluorometholone acetate) has higher potency than the alcohol form due to better corneal penetration. Prednisolone acetate has better penetration than prednisolone phosphate.

### Clinical Applications

**Allergic Conjunctivitis**: Low to medium potency steroids for severe cases unresponsive to antihistamines and mast cell stabilizers. Loteprednol is often preferred due to lower IOP elevation risk.

**Keratitis**: High-potency steroids for severe inflammation, but must rule out infectious causes before initiation. Herpes simplex keratitis is a contraindication.

**Uveitis**: High-potency steroids are first-line for anterior uveitis. Prednisolone acetate 1% is commonly used, with dosing frequency based on severity (from q1-2h to qid). Periocular steroids may be needed for intermediate and posterior uveitis.

**Post-operative Inflammation**: Routine use after cataract and other intraocular surgeries. Diffuprednate and loteprednol are commonly used due to their efficacy and safety profiles.

### Adverse Effects and Monitoring

**Intraocular Pressure Elevation**:
- Most common serious adverse effect
- Risk factors: History of glaucoma, family history, high myopia, diabetes, connective tissue disease
- "Steroid responders": Approximately 5% of general population and 25-30% of glaucoma patients develop IOP elevation
- IOP elevation typically occurs 2-4 weeks after initiation but can occur at any time
- Dexamethasone and prednisolone have highest risk; loteprednol and fluorometholone have lower risk

**Cataract Formation**:
- Posterior subcapsular cataracts with chronic use
- Risk increases with duration and potency of therapy
- May require surgical intervention

**Delayed Wound Healing**:
- Inhibits fibroblast activity and collagen synthesis
- May delay corneal epithelial healing
- Use with caution in corneal epithelial defects

**Infection Risk**:
- Immunosuppression increases risk of secondary infection
- Fungal and viral (herpes simplex) keratitis are particular concerns
- Never prescribe steroids without thorough examination and appropriate follow-up

**Monitoring Guidelines**:
- Baseline IOP measurement before initiating therapy
- Follow-up IOP checks at 2 weeks, 4-6 weeks, then periodically
- More frequent monitoring for high-risk patients
- Educate patients about warning signs of infection

### Loteprednol: A Soft Steroid

Loteprednol etabonate represents a unique "soft steroid" design. The molecule contains an ester linkage that undergoes rapid hydrolysis to inactive metabolites, reducing the risk of IOP elevation and cataract formation. Available formulations include:

- Loteprednol 0.5% (Lotemax) solution
- Loteprednol 0.5% ointment
- Loteprednol 0.5% gel (Lotemax gel)
- Loteprednol 0.2% (Alrex) for allergic conjunctivitis

Clinical studies demonstrate significant anti-inflammatory efficacy with IOP elevation rates similar to placebo, making loteprednol an excellent choice for conditions requiring chronic steroid therapy.

## Glaucoma Medications

### Overview of Glaucoma Pharmacotherapy

Glaucoma pharmacotherapy aims to lower intraocular pressure (IOP) to prevent or slow optic nerve damage. Multiple drug classes are available, each with distinct mechanisms of action, efficacy profiles, and side effect profiles. The DOH examination requires comprehensive knowledge of all major glaucoma medication classes.

### Prostaglandin Analogues (PGA)

Prostaglandin analogues are the most commonly prescribed first-line agents for open-angle glaucoma due to their once-daily dosing, excellent efficacy, and favorable systemic side effect profile.

**Available Agents**:
- Latanoprost 0.005% (Xalatan)
- Travoprost 0.004% (Travatan)
- Bimatoprost 0.01% (Lumigan)
- Tafluprost 0.0015% (Saflutan) - preservative-free

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Increase uveoscleral outflow (approximately 50% of aqueous outflow)
- May also increase trabecular outflow
- IOP reduction: 25-35%

**Side Effects**:
- **Iris pigmentation**: Permanent darkening, particularly in mixed-color irides
- **Eyelash growth**: Increased length, thickness, and pigmentation
- **Periorbital skin darkening**: Reversible with discontinuation
- **Conjunctival hyperemia**: Common, may decrease with continued use
- **Prosthetic-associated changes**: Deepening of upper eyelid sulcus
- **Cystoid macular edema**: Risk in post-surgical patients and those with uveitis

**Dosing**: Once daily at bedtime (evening dosing may enhance efficacy)

### Beta-Adrenergic Antagonists (Beta-blockers)

Beta-blockers were historically first-line therapy and remain important adjunctive agents.

**Available Agents**:
- Timolol 0.25% and 0.5% (Timoptic, various generics)
- Timolol gel-forming solution 0.25% and 0.5% (Timoptic XE)
- Levobunolol 0.25% and 0.5% (Betagan)
- Betaxolol 0.25% and 0.5% (Betoptic) - cardioselective

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Decrease aqueous humor production by 20-50%
- Block beta-1 and beta-2 receptors on ciliary epithelium
- IOP reduction: 20-30%

**Side Effects**:
- **Cardiovascular**: Bradycardia, hypotension, heart block
- **Respiratory**: Bronchospasm (contraindicated in asthma and COPD)
- **Central nervous system**: Fatigue, depression, impotence
- **Ocular**: Ocular surface irritation, corneal anesthesia

**Contraindications**:
- Asthma and COPD
- Bradycardia and heart block
- Severe heart failure
- Betaxolol (cardioselective) may be used with caution in respiratory conditions

**Dosing**: Once or twice daily; gel-forming solution allows once-daily dosing

### Alpha-2 Adrenergic Agonists

Alpha-2 agonists provide dual mechanism of action and are useful as adjunctive therapy or in patients intolerant of first-line agents.

**Available Agents**:
- Brimonidine 0.1%, 0.15%, 0.2% (Alphagan)
- Apraclonidine 0.5%, 1% (Iopidine) - primarily used short-term after laser procedures
- Brimonidine 0.1% with Purite preservative (Alphagan P) - improved tolerability

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Decrease aqueous humor production
- Increase uveoscleral outflow
- IOP reduction: 20-25%

**Side Effects**:
- **Ocular**: Conjunctival hyperemia, allergic conjunctivitis, follicular conjunctivitis (up to 20% with chronic use)
- **Systemic**: Dry mouth, fatigue, drowsiness, headache
- **Caution**: CNS depression in children; contraindicated in infants under 2 years

**Dosing**: Twice or three times daily (less effective as monotherapy when used tid)

### Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (CAI)

Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors reduce aqueous production by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase in the ciliary epithelium.

**Topical Agents**:
- Dorzolamide 2% (Trusopt)
- Brinzolamide 1% (Azopt)

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Inhibit carbonic anhydrase isoenzyme II
- Decrease bicarbonate production, reducing aqueous humor formation
- IOP reduction: 15-25%

**Side Effects**:
- **Ocular**: Burning and stinging (dorzolamide), bitter taste, blurred vision (brinzolamide suspension)
- **Systemic**: Minimal with topical use; no systemic acidosis or electrolyte disturbances
- **Contraindication**: Sulfonamide allergy

**Oral CAI**: Acetazolamide and methazolamide are reserved for refractory glaucoma due to systemic side effects (paresthesias, fatigue, kidney stones, metabolic acidosis)

**Dosing**: Topical CAI - two to three times daily

### Rho Kinase Inhibitors

Rho kinase inhibitors represent a newer class of glaucoma medications that enhance trabecular outflow.

**Available Agents**:
- Netarsudil 0.02% (Rhopressa)
- Netarsudil/latanoprost combination (Rocklatan)
- Ripasudil 0.4% (Glanatec) - available in Japan

**Mechanism of Action**:
- Inhibit Rho kinase, causing cytoskeletal changes in trabecular meshwork
- Increase trabecular outflow
- May also decrease episcleral venous pressure
- Netarsudil also inhibits norepinephrine transporter, decreasing aqueous production
- IOP reduction: 20-30%

**Side Effects**:
- **Conjunctival hyperemia**: Very common (up to 50%)
- **Corneal verticillata**: Whorl-like corneal deposits (typically visually insignificant)
- **Conjunctival hemorrhages**: Due to vascular effects
- **Blepharitis**: Uncommon

**Dosing**: Once daily (evening)

### Combination Therapy

Combination medications improve compliance by reducing the number of bottles needed:

**Available Combinations**:
- **Dorzolamide/timolol** (Cosopt) - bid dosing
- **Brimonidine/timolol** (Combigan) - bid dosing
- **Bimatoprost/timolol** (Ganfort) - qd dosing
- **Travoprost/timolol** (DuoTrav) - qd dosing
- **Brinzolamide/brimonidine** (Simbrinza) - bid-tid dosing
- **Netarsudil/latanoprost** (Rocklatan) - qd dosing

### Clinical Approach to Glaucoma Treatment

**First-line Therapy**:
- Prostaglandin analogues are typically first-line due to efficacy, once-daily dosing, and safety
- Consider beta-blockers as alternative first-line if cost is a concern
- Consider patient factors: Age, comorbidities, ocular surface status, cost

**Add-on Therapy**:
- If IOP target not met with first agent, add complementary mechanism
- Common combinations: PGA + beta-blocker, PGA + alpha-agonist, PGA + CAI
- Consider combination products to improve compliance

**Monitoring**:
- Target IOP based on disease severity and progression
- Visual field testing and optic nerve imaging to assess for progression
- Monitor for medication side effects and patient compliance

## Preservatives in Ophthalmic Medications

### Common Preservatives

Preservatives are essential in multi-dose ophthalmic containers to prevent microbial contamination. Common preservatives include:

**Benzalkonium Chloride (BAK)**: The most common preservative. It disrupts microbial cell membranes but can also damage corneal epithelial cells with chronic use. Associated with dry eye symptoms and ocular surface toxicity.

**Purite (Stabilized Oxychloro Complex)**: A milder oxidative preservative that breaks down into sodium chloride, oxygen, and water upon contact with tear film. Better tolerability than BAK.

**Polyquad (Polyquaternium-1)**: A detergent preservative with reduced corneal toxicity compared to BAK. Used in many glaucoma medications.

**SofZia**: An ionic-buffered oxidative system used in travoprost. Generally well-tolerated.

### Preservative-Related Considerations

Chronic preservative exposure can cause:
- Punctate keratopathy
- Conjunctival hyperemia
- Allergic reactions
- Tear film instability
- Reduced patient compliance

For patients requiring chronic therapy or those with ocular surface disease, preservative-free formulations should be considered.

This comprehensive guide has covered the fundamental aspects of topical medications, antibiotics, corticosteroids, and glaucoma medications. Key examination topics include understanding mechanisms of action, appropriate clinical applications, contraindications, adverse effects, and monitoring requirements.
