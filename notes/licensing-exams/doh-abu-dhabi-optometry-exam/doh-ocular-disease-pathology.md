# Ocular Disease & Pathology for DOH Exam

## Introduction

The Department of Health (DOH) Abu Dhabi Optometry licensing examination places significant emphasis on ocular disease and pathology knowledge. Optometrists practicing in Abu Dhabi must demonstrate comprehensive understanding of anterior segment conditions, cataract management, glaucoma diagnosis and monitoring, retinal pathology, and uveitis recognition. This guide provides essential clinical knowledge for examination preparation and professional practice.

---

## 1. Anterior Segment Disorders

### 1.1 Ocular Surface Disease

#### Dry Eye Disease (Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca)

Dry eye disease represents one of the most common anterior segment conditions encountered in optometric practice. Understanding its classification, pathophysiology, and management is essential for DOH examination success.

**Classification:**
- **Aqueous-deficient dry eye**: Reduced tear production from lacrimal gland dysfunction
- **Evaporative dry eye**: Excessive tear film evaporation, commonly due to meibomian gland dysfunction

**Clinical Features:**
- Gritty, sandy sensation
- Foreign body feeling
- Fluctuating vision
- Photophobia
- Eye fatigue
- Symptoms often worse in air-conditioned environments

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Tear Break-Up Time (TBUT) < 10 seconds
- Schirmer test < 10mm in 5 minutes
- Ocular Surface Disease Index (OSDI) score > 12
- Corneal fluorescein staining patterns
- Lissamine green conjunctival staining

**Management Approach:**
1. **Lifestyle modifications**: Humidifier use, screen breaks (20-20-20 rule)
2. **Artificial tears**: Preservative-free formulations for frequent use
3. **Anti-inflammatory agents**: Cyclosporine 0.05%, lifitegrast 5%
4. **Meibomian gland therapy**: Warm compresses, lid hygiene, expression
5. **Punctal plugs**: For aqueous-deficient cases
6. **Advanced treatments**: IPL therapy, thermal pulsation devices

#### Meibomian Gland Dysfunction (MGD)

MGD is the leading cause of evaporative dry eye and requires systematic evaluation.

**Staging:**
- **Stage 1**: No symptoms, no ocular signs, abnormal gland expression
- **Stage 2**: Minimal symptoms, mild clinical signs
- **Stage 3**: Moderate symptoms, telangiectasia, gland dropout
- **Stage 4**: Severe symptoms, significant gland loss

**Clinical Assessment:**
- Meibum quality grading (clear to inspissated)
- Gland expression testing
- Meibography for gland architecture
- Lid margin evaluation for telangiectasia

### 1.2 Conjunctival Disorders

#### Allergic Conjunctivitis

**Types and Features:**

**Seasonal Allergic Conjunctivitis (SAC):**
- Related to airborne allergens (pollen, grass)
- Bilateral itching, redness, watering
- Cobblestone papillae on upper tarsus
- Seasonal pattern

**Perennial Allergic Conjunctivitis (PAC):**
- Year-round symptoms
- Dust mites, pet dander triggers
- Similar signs to SAC but chronic

**Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis (VKC):**
- More severe, affects young males
- Giant papillae ("cobblestones")
- Shield ulcers possible
- Requires specialist management

**Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis (AKC):**
- Associated with atopic dermatitis
- Chronic, potentially sight-threatening
- Requires aggressive management

**Management:**
- Avoidance of allergens
- Cold compresses
- Antihistamine drops (olopatadine, ketotifen)
- Mast cell stabilizers
- Combination antihistamine/mast cell stabilizers
- Topical steroids for severe cases (short-term)

#### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

**Common Pathogens:**
- Staphylococcus aureus
- Streptococcus pneumoniae
- Haemophilus influenzae
- Moraxella catarrhalis

**Clinical Features:**
- Purulent discharge
- Matting of eyelids
- Conjunctival injection
- Usually unilateral initially

**Management:**
- Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics
- Fluoroquinolones, aminoglycosides
- Hygiene measures to prevent spread
- Usually self-limiting in 7-14 days

#### Viral Conjunctivitis

**Adenoviral Conjunctivitis:**
- Most common viral cause
- Highly contagious
- Associated with upper respiratory infection
- Preauricular lymphadenopathy

**Clinical Features:**
- Watery discharge
- Follicular reaction
- Pseudomembranes in severe cases
- Subepithelial infiltrates (late stage)

**Management:**
- Supportive care
- Cold compresses
- Lubrication
- Strict hygiene to prevent transmission
- No antibiotics needed unless bacterial superinfection

### 1.3 Corneal Disorders

#### Infectious Keratitis

**Bacterial Keratitis:**
- Risk factors: Contact lens wear, corneal trauma, ocular surface disease
- Common organisms: Pseudomonas aeruginosa (contact lens), Staphylococcus, Streptococcus
- Clinical features: Corneal infiltrate, epithelial defect, hypopyon, pain
- Management: Empirical broad-spectrum antibiotics, culture in severe cases

**Fungal Keratitis:**
- Risk factors: Trauma with vegetative matter, chronic topical steroids
- Clinical features: Feathery borders, satellite lesions, endothelial plaque
- Management: Topical antifungals (natamycin, voriconazole)

**Acanthamoeba Keratitis:**
- Risk factors: Contact lens wear, exposure to contaminated water
- Clinical features: Ring infiltrate, severe pain disproportionate to signs, radial keratoneuritis
- Management: Biguanides (polyhexamethylene biguanide, chlorhexidine)

#### Herpes Simplex Keratitis

**Types:**
- **Epithelial keratitis**: Dendritic ulcer with terminal bulbs, responds to antivirals
- **Stromal keratitis**: Disciform edema, immune-mediated, requires steroids with antiviral cover
- **Endotheliitis**: Keratic precipitates, corneal edema

**Management:**
- Topical antivirals (acyclovir, ganciclovir)
- Oral antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir) for stromal disease
- Topical steroids for stromal keratitis (under antiviral cover)
- Long-term prophylaxis for recurrent disease

#### Keratoconus

**Clinical Features:**
- Progressive corneal thinning
- Irregular astigmatism
- Scissoring reflex on retinoscopy
- Munson's sign, Rizzuti's sign
- Vogt's striae

**Diagnostic Evaluation:**
- Corneal topography/tomography
- Pachymetry (thinnest point)
- Corneal biomechanics (Corvis, ORA)

**Management:**
- Glasses and contact lenses (early stages)
- Corneal collagen cross-linking (CXL)
- Intrastromal corneal ring segments
- Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK)
- Penetrating keratoplasty (PK)

## 2. Cataract

### 2.1 Classification and Types

Cataract, defined as any opacity of the crystalline lens, remains the leading cause of reversible blindness worldwide. Optometrists play a crucial role in detection, monitoring, and timely referral.

**Age-Related Cataract Types:**

**Nuclear Sclerotic Cataract:**
- Central lens nucleus opacification
- Gradual progression
- Associated with myopic shift ("second sight")
- Brunescence or discoloration
- Visual acuity affected late

**Cortical Cataract:**
- Wedge-shaped opacities in lens cortex
- Spoke-like appearance
- Glare symptoms prominent
- Visual acuity variable depending on pupil size

**Posterior Subcapsular Cataract (PSC):**
- Opacity at posterior capsule
- Significantly affects near vision
- Glare and halos prominent
- Rapid progression
- Associated with steroid use, diabetes

### 2.2 Congenital Cataract

**Etiology:**
- Idiopathic (most common)
- Inherited (autosomal dominant)
- Metabolic (galactosemia, diabetes)
- Intrauterine infection (TORCH)
- Associated syndromes (Down, Lowe)

**Clinical Significance:**
- Risk of amblyopia
- Urgent ophthalmology referral required
- Surgery timing critical for visual development
- Bilateral vs unilateral affects management

### 2.3 Clinical Assessment

**Symptoms:**
- Painless progressive vision loss
- Glare and light sensitivity
- Halos around lights
- Monocular diplopia
- Color perception changes
- Frequent prescription changes

**Examination:**
- Visual acuity testing
- Contrast sensitivity assessment
- Glare testing
- Slit lamp examination with pupil dilation
- Lens opacity grading (LOCS III system)
- Fundus examination to rule out other pathology

### 2.4 Cataract Surgery Indications

**Visual Indications:**
- Visual acuity affecting daily activities
- Significant glare disability
- Anisometropia from cataract
- Interference with fundus examination for diabetic patients

**Medical Indications:**
- Phacolytic glaucoma
- Phacomorphic glaucoma
- Lens-induced uveitis
- Dislocated lens

### 2.5 Pre-operative Assessment

**Essential Evaluations:**
- Biometry (axial length, keratometry)
- IOL power calculation (SRK/T, Hoffer Q, Haigis formulas)
- Anterior chamber depth
- Pupil dilation assessment
- Corneal topography
- Macular function assessment

**IOL Options:**
- Monofocal IOLs (standard care)
- Toric IOLs (astigmatism correction)
- Multifocal IOLs (presbyopia correction)
- Extended depth of focus IOLs
- Accommodating IOLs

### 2.6 Post-operative Management

**Recovery Timeline:**
- Day 1: Initial postoperative check
- Week 1: Suture removal if applicable
- Week 4-6: Final refraction and IOL calculation for second eye

**Postoperative Complications:**
- Endophthalmitis (urgent referral)
- Cystoid macular edema
- Posterior capsule opacification (YAG capsulotomy)
- Corneal edema
- IOL dislocation
- Retinal detachment

## 3. Glaucoma

### 3.1 Definition and Classification

Glaucoma encompasses a group of progressive optic neuropathies characterized by damage to the optic nerve head with corresponding visual field loss. It is the leading cause of irreversible blindness globally.

**Classification by Mechanism:**
- **Open-angle glaucoma**: Normal anterior chamber angle
- **Angle-closure glaucoma**: Mechanically closed angle

**Classification by Cause:**
- **Primary**: No identifiable cause
- **Secondary**: Identifiable cause (trauma, inflammation, steroid use)

### 3.2 Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

**Epidemiology:**
- Most common form of glaucoma
- Prevalence increases with age
- Family history significant risk factor
- Higher prevalence in certain ethnicities

**Risk Factors:**
- Elevated intraocular pressure (IOP > 21 mmHg)
- Age > 60 years
- Family history
- Myopia
- African or Hispanic descent
- Thin central cornea (< 555 microns)
- Vascular factors (diabetes, hypertension)

**Clinical Features:**
- Asymmetric optic nerve cupping
- Vertical cup-to-disc ratio > 0.6
- Cup-to-disc ratio asymmetry > 0.2
- Notching of neuroretinal rim
- Splinter hemorrhages
- Visual field defects (arcuate, nasal step, peripheral)

**Diagnostic Tests:**
- Applanation tonometry
- Gonioscopy (open angle)
- Optic nerve head imaging (OCT, HRT, GDx)
- Visual field testing (Humphrey, Goldmann)
- Central corneal thickness measurement
- Optic disc photography for documentation

### 3.3 Angle-Closure Glaucoma

**Mechanisms:**
- **Pupil block**: Most common mechanism
- **Plateau iris**: Anterior positioned ciliary body
- **Lens-induced**: Lens position/displacement
- **Malignant glaucoma**: Aqueous misdirection

**Acute Angle-Closure Crisis:**
**Symptoms:**
- Severe ocular pain
- Headache
- Nausea and vomiting
- Blurred vision
- Halos around lights
- Red eye

**Signs:**
- Elevated IOP (often > 40 mmHg)
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Closed angle on gonioscopy
- Mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil
- Corneal edema
- Conjunctival injection
- Glaucomatous optic nerve changes (if prolonged)

**Management:**
- Immediate reduction of IOP
- Systemic osmotic agents (mannitol, glycerol)
- Topical aqueous suppressants (beta-blockers, alpha-2 agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors)
- Pilocarpine after IOP reduction
- Laser peripheral iridotomy (definitive treatment)
- Prophylactic iridotomy for fellow eye

**Chronic Angle-Closure Glaucoma:**
- Gradual synechial closure of angle
- May be asymptomatic until advanced
- Requires gonioscopy for diagnosis
- Management with iridotomy and glaucoma medications

### 3.4 Normal-Tension Glaucoma

**Characteristics:**
- Glaucomatous optic neuropathy with IOP consistently ≤ 21 mmHg
- May be associated with vascular factors
- More common in Japanese population
- Often associated with disc hemorrhages

**Risk Factors:**
- Female gender
- Vasospasm (Raynaud's, migraine)
- Nocturnal hypotension
- Sleep apnea
- Autoimmune diseases

**Management Considerations:**
- Lower target IOP (30% reduction minimum)
- Address vascular risk factors
- Consider neuroprotective strategies

### 3.5 Secondary Glaucomas

**Pigment Dispersion Syndrome/Pigmentary Glaucoma:**
- Mid-peripheral iris transillumination defects
- Krukenberg spindle (pigment on corneal endothelium)
- Heavily pigmented trabecular meshwork
- Myopic males commonly affected
- Exercise-induced IOP spikes

**Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma:**
- White, flaky material on lens and pupillary margin
- Higher risk of cataract surgery complications
- Pupil dilation difficulties
- More aggressive IOP elevation

**Steroid-Induced Glaucoma:**
- Elevated IOP following steroid use
- Higher risk in POAG patients
- Usually reversible with discontinuation
- May require glaucoma surgery

**Uveitic Glaucoma:**
- Inflammatory debris blocking trabecular meshwork
- Peripheral anterior synechiae
- Requires anti-inflammatory and IOP management
- Avoid prostaglandin analogues in active inflammation

### 3.6 Glaucoma Treatment

**Target IOP:**
- Individualized based on stage of disease
- Generally 20-30% reduction from baseline
- Lower targets for advanced disease

**Medical Therapy:**
1. **Prostaglandin Analogues**: Latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost
   - First-line therapy
   - Once daily dosing
   - Side effects: iris darkening, lash growth, conjunctival hyperemia

2. **Beta-Blockers**: Timolol, betaxolol
   - Aqueous suppressant
   - Twice daily dosing
   - Contraindications: asthma, bradycardia, heart failure

3. **Alpha-2 Agonists**: Brimonidine, apraclonidine
   - Decrease aqueous production, increase uveoscleral outflow
   - Side effects: allergic reactions, fatigue

4. **Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors**: Dorzolamide, brinzolamide (topical); acetazolamide (oral)
   - Aqueous suppressant
   - Three times daily dosing

5. **Miotics**: Pilocarpine
   - Increase trabecular outflow
   - Used in angle-closure and aphakic glaucoma

**Laser Therapy:**
- **Selective Laser Trabeculoplasty (SLT)**: First-line or adjunct therapy for open-angle glaucoma
- **Laser Peripheral Iridotomy**: Angle-closure glaucoma
- **Cyclophotocoagulation**: Refractory glaucoma

**Surgical Management:**
- Trabeculectomy
- Glaucoma drainage devices (Ahmed, Baerveldt)
- Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS)
- Canaloplasty

## 4. Retinal Disorders

### 4.1 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy remains a leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults. Optometrists play a critical role in screening and early detection.

**Classification:**

**Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):**
- **Mild**: Microaneurysms only
- **Moderate**: Microaneurysms, retinal hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots
- **Severe**: Extensive intraretinal hemorrhages, venous beading, IRMA (4-2-1 rule)

**Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR):**
- Neovascularization on disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- Preretinal or vitreous hemorrhage
- Tractional retinal detachment
- High-risk PDR requires urgent treatment

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
- Leading cause of visual impairment in diabetic patients
- OCT assessment essential
- May occur at any stage of DR
- Center-involving vs non-center involving

**Management:**
- Optimal glycemic control (HbA1c < 7%)
- Blood pressure and lipid control
- Laser photocoagulation for high-risk PDR
- Anti-VEGF therapy for DME and PDR
- Vitrectomy for non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage
- Regular screening: Type 1 diabetes from 5 years after diagnosis; Type 2 from diagnosis

### 4.2 Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

**Dry AMD (Non-Neovascular):**
- Drusen accumulation
- RPE abnormalities
- Geographic atrophy
- Gradual central vision loss
- AREDS2 supplements for intermediate AMD

**Wet AMD (Neovascular):**
- Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)
- Rapid vision loss
- Metamorphopsia (distortion)
- Subretinal fluid and hemorrhage
- Urgent anti-VEGF treatment required

**Clinical Features:**
- Drusen (small, medium, large)
- Pigmentary changes
- Geographic atrophy (late dry AMD)
- Subretinal fluid, hemorrhage (wet AMD)

**Diagnostic Imaging:**
- Fundus photography
- Optical coherence tomography (OCT)
- Fluorescein angiography
- Indocyanine green angiography

**Management:**
- AREDS2 formulation for intermediate AMD
- Smoking cessation
- Anti-VEGF injections for wet AMD (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab)
- Regular Amsler grid monitoring
- Low vision rehabilitation for advanced disease

### 4.3 Retinal Vascular Occlusions

**Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO):**
- Ocular emergency
- Sudden painless vision loss
- "Cherry-red spot" appearance
- Retinal whitening
- Box-carring of blood columns
- Poor visual prognosis
- Immediate referral within 90 minutes for potential intervention

**Branch Retinal Artery Occlusion (BRAO):**
- Localized ischemia
- Altitudinal visual field defect
- Carotid and cardiac evaluation essential

**Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO):**
- Sudden vision loss
- "Blood and thunder" fundus appearance
- Dilated, tortuous veins
- Disc edema
- Ischemic vs non-ischemic types
- Risk of neovascularization (rubeosis)

**Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO):**
- Segmental retinal hemorrhages
- Macular edema common
- Grid laser for macular edema
- Anti-VEGF therapy

### 4.4 Retinal Detachment

**Types:**

**Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment:**
- Most common type
- Caused by retinal break (tear, hole)
- Subretinal fluid accumulates
- Urgent surgical repair required

**Tractional Retinal Detachment:**
- Fibrovascular proliferation pulls retina
- Common in proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Gradual onset

**Exudative Retinal Detachment:**
- Fluid accumulation without break
- Associated with tumors, inflammation
- Treat underlying cause

**Warning Signs:**
- Flashes (photopsia)
- Floaters (new onset or sudden increase)
- Curtain or shadow in vision
- Sudden vision loss

**Predisposing Factors:**
- High myopia
- Previous ocular surgery
- Ocular trauma
- Family history
- Lattice degeneration

**Management:**
- Urgent ophthalmology referral
- Laser retinopexy for small detachments
- Scleral buckle
- Pars plana vitrectomy
- Pneumatic retinopexy for selected cases

## 5. Uveitis

### 5.1 Classification

Uveitis refers to inflammation of the uveal tract (iris, ciliary body, choroid) and may involve adjacent structures. Classification guides diagnosis and management.

**Anatomical Classification:**

**Anterior Uveitis (Iritis/Iridocyclitis):**
- Most common form
- Involves iris and anterior ciliary body
- Acute or chronic presentation

**Intermediate Uveitis:**
- Vitreous inflammation
- Pars planitis (idiopathic form)
- Snowbank and snowball opacities

**Posterior Uveitis:**
- Retina and choroid involvement
- May include retinitis, choroiditis, retinochoroiditis

**Panuveitis:**
- Inflammation of all uveal structures

**Etiological Classification:**
- **Infectious**: Viral, bacterial, fungal, parasitic
- **Non-infectious**: Autoimmune, idiopathic
- **Masquerade syndromes**: Malignancies mimicking uveitis

### 5.2 Anterior Uveitis

**Clinical Features:**
- Ocular pain
- Photophobia
- Red eye (circumcorneal injection)
- Tearing
- Decreased vision (with hypopyon or severe inflammation)

**Signs:**
- Keratic precipitates (fine or mutton-fat)
- Anterior chamber cells and flare
- Hypopyon (in severe cases)
- Iris synechiae (anterior or posterior)
- Iris nodules (Koeppe, Busacca)

**Common Causes:**
- Idiopathic (most common)
- HLA-B27 associated conditions
- Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
- Herpes simplex and zoster
- Sarcoidosis
- Behçet's disease

**Management:**
- Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone, dexamethasone)
- Cycloplegic agents (atropine, cyclopentolate, homatropine)
- Periocular or systemic steroids for severe cases
- Workup for underlying cause if recurrent

### 5.3 Intermediate Uveitis

**Clinical Features:**
- Floaters
- Decreased vision
- Minimal pain or redness

**Signs:**
- Vitreous cells
- Snowball opacities
- Snowbank (pars plana exudate)
- Peripheral retinal vasculitis
- Cystoid macular edema

**Associated Conditions:**
- Multiple sclerosis
- Sarcoidosis
- Lyme disease
- Tuberculosis
- Idiopathic (pars planitis)

**Management:**
- Periocular steroids
- Systemic immunosuppression
- Vitrectomy for persistent cases
- Treat underlying cause

### 5.4 Posterior Uveitis

**Infectious Causes:**

**Toxoplasmosis:**
- Most common cause of infectious retinitis
- Focal necrotizing retinitis
- "Headlight in fog" appearance
- Adjacent chorioretinal scar
- Reactivation common
- Treatment: pyrimethamine, sulfadiazine, clindamycin

**Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Retinitis:**
- Immunocompromised patients
- "Pizza pie" fundus appearance
- Hemorrhagic retinitis
- Antiviral treatment: ganciclovir, foscarnet

**Acute Retinal Necrosis (ARN):**
- Herpes viruses (HSV, VZV)
- Peripheral necrotizing retinitis
- Rapid progression
- High risk of retinal detachment
- Intravenous antiviral therapy

**Non-Infectious Causes:**

**Sarcoidosis:**
- Multisystem granulomatous disease
- Candle-wax drippings
- Retinal periphlebitis
- Requires systemic workup

**Behçet's Disease:**
- Recurrent oral and genital ulcers
- Hypopyon uveitis
- Retinal vasculitis
- Requires immunosuppression

**Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) Syndrome:**
- Bilateral panuveitis
- Exudative retinal detachment
- Poliosis, vitiligo
- Meningeal symptoms

### 5.5 Uveitis Complications

**Ocular Complications:**
- Cataract (common)
- Glaucoma (multiple mechanisms)
- Cystoid macular edema
- Band keratopathy
- Synechiae formation
- Retinal detachment
- Epiretinal membrane

**Management Principles:**
- Treat underlying cause when identified
- Control inflammation promptly
- Prevent complications
- Monitor for treatment side effects
- Multidisciplinary approach for systemic diseases

## 6. DOH Examination Focus Areas

### 6.1 Key Clinical Skills Assessed

1. **Differential Diagnosis**: Ability to differentiate between similar presenting conditions
2. **Clinical Reasoning**: Understanding when to treat, observe, or refer
3. **Investigation Selection**: Appropriate use of diagnostic tests
4. **Management Planning**: Evidence-based treatment decisions
5. **Emergency Recognition**: Identification of sight-threatening conditions

### 6.2 Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral

- Acute angle-closure glaucoma
- Retinal detachment
- Central retinal artery occlusion
- Infectious keratitis
- Endophthalmitis
- Neovascular glaucoma
- High-risk proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Wet age-related macular degeneration

### 6.3 Commonly Tested Topics

1. **Glaucoma**: IOP measurement, gonioscopy interpretation, visual field analysis, treatment selection
2. **Diabetic Retinopathy**: Staging, referral criteria, management options
3. **Anterior Segment**: Differential diagnosis of red eye, management of common conditions
4. **Cataract**: Assessment, timing of referral, postoperative complications
5. **Uveitis**: Classification, recognition, initial management, referral timing

---

## Conclusion

Ocular disease and pathology knowledge is fundamental to optometric practice in Abu Dhabi. The DOH examination tests comprehensive understanding across anterior segment disorders, cataract management, glaucoma diagnosis and treatment, retinal pathology recognition, and uveitis management. Clinical competence in these areas ensures safe and effective patient care while meeting the regulatory standards set by the Department of Health Abu Dhabi.

## Key Takeaways for DOH Examination

1. **Anterior Segment**: Master differential diagnosis of red eye, recognize infectious vs inflammatory conditions, understand when topical steroids are appropriate

2. **Cataract**: Know classification systems, recognize visual and medical indications for surgery, understand postoperative complications

3. **Glaucoma**: Understand all forms of glaucoma, be proficient in risk assessment, know IOP targets and treatment algorithms

4. **Retina**: Recognize diabetic retinopathy stages, identify AMD types, understand urgency of retinal vascular events

5. **Uveitis**: Know anatomical classification, recognize serious causes, understand when immunosuppression is needed
