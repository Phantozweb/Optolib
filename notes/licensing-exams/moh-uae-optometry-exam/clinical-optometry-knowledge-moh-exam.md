# Clinical Optometry Knowledge for MOH UAE Exam

## Introduction

This comprehensive guide covers essential clinical optometry knowledge required for the Ministry of Health (MOH) UAE Optometry Examination. The content encompasses fundamental and advanced concepts in eye examination, refraction, binocular vision, intraocular pressure measurement, and visual field assessment. Mastery of these topics is crucial for providing quality eye care and passing the MOH licensing examination.

---

## 1. Eye Examination

### 1.1 Patient History and Chief Complaint

A thorough patient history forms the foundation of any eye examination. The optometrist must systematically gather information about the patient's chief complaint, history of present illness, past ocular history, medical history, family history, and current medications.

**Key Components of Patient History:**

- **Chief Complaint (CC):** Document the primary reason for the visit using the patient's own words. Common complaints include blurred vision, eye strain, headaches, floaters, and eye pain.

- **History of Present Illness (HPI):** Use the OLD CARTS mnemonic:
  - **O**nset: When did the symptoms begin?
  - **L**ocation: Which eye(s) are affected?
  - **D**uration: How long do symptoms last?
  - **C**haracteristics: Describe the nature of symptoms
  - **A**ggravating/Alleviating factors: What makes it better or worse?
  - **R**adiation: Does the pain spread?
  - **T**iming: When do symptoms occur?
  - **S**everity: Rate on a scale of 1-10

- **Past Ocular History (POH):** Previous eye surgeries, injuries, infections, and treatments
- **Past Medical History (PMH):** Systemic conditions like diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disorders
- **Family History:** Hereditary conditions such as glaucoma, macular degeneration, retinal detachment
- **Medications:** Current medications including eye drops and systemic drugs with ocular side effects

### 1.2 Visual Acuity Assessment

Visual acuity testing is the primary measure of central vision function. It quantifies the eye's ability to resolve fine detail.

**Distance Visual Acuity Testing:**

The Snellen chart is the most commonly used method, expressing visual acuity as a fraction (e.g., 6/6 or 20/20). The numerator represents the testing distance (6 meters or 20 feet), and the denominator indicates the smallest line read.

**Key Points:**
- Test each eye separately (right eye first: OD, then left eye: OS)
- Use proper illumination (85-120 cd/m²)
- Patient should not squint or press on the eyelid
- Record the smallest line where more than half the letters are correctly identified
- Document any improvements with pinhole testing

**Near Visual Acuity Testing:**
- Test at 40 cm (16 inches) using a near card
- Document using Jaeger notation (J1, J2, etc.) or N notation
- Essential for presbyopic patients and those with near vision complaints

**LogMAR Charts:**
The Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS) chart uses LogMAR units, providing more precise measurements:
- Each line has 5 letters
- Equal crowding effect between lines
- More accurate for research and low vision assessment

### 1.3 External Eye Examination

External examination begins with general observation and proceeds systematically.

**Inspection:**
- Facial symmetry and expression
- Orbital appearance
- Eyelid position (ptosis, retraction, entropion, ectropion)
- Palpebral fissure width (normal: 8-11 mm)
- Blink rate and pattern
- Lacrimal apparatus
- Conjunctiva appearance (color, injection, discharge)

**Palpation:**
- Orbital rims for fractures or masses
- Lymph nodes (preauricular, submandibular)
- Lacrimal sac for dacryocystitis
- Sinus tenderness

### 1.4 Pupillary Examination

Pupil assessment provides valuable information about the afferent visual pathway and autonomic nervous system.

**Pupil Characteristics:**
- **Size:** Normal 3-4 mm in room light, 6-8 mm in darkness
- **Shape:** Should be round and symmetric
- **Position:** Centered in the iris
- **Equality:** Anisocoria (difference >0.4 mm) may be physiological or pathological

**Testing Procedures:**

1. **Light Reaction:**
   - Direct response: Constriction when light is shone directly
   - Consensual response: Constriction of fellow eye
   - Record speed and extent of constriction

2. **Swinging Flashlight Test:**
   - Alternating light between eyes
   - Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD): Dilatation instead of maintained constriction
   - Indicates optic nerve or severe retinal disease

3. **Near Reaction:**
   - Accommodative miosis with near fixation
   - Light-near dissociation suggests neurosyphilis, diabetes, or dorsal midbrain lesion

### 1.5 Ocular Motility Assessment

Ocular motility evaluation ensures proper eye movement and alignment.

**H-version Testing:**
- Patient follows a target in an H pattern
- Observe for smooth pursuit, nystagmus, or lagging
- Grade on scale -4 to +4 (0 = normal)

**Cover Tests:**
1. **Cover-Uncover Test:** Detects manifest deviation (tropia)
2. **Alternate Cover Test:** Detects total deviation (phoria + tropia)
3. **Prism Alternate Cover Test:** Measures angle of deviation

**Near Point of Convergence (NPC):**
- Normal: 6-10 cm from the nose
- Records break point and recovery point
- Important in assessing binocular vision disorders

### 1.6 Slit Lamp Biomicroscopy

The slit lamp provides magnified examination of anterior segment structures.

**Examination Sequence:**
1. **Lids and Lashes:** Margin, position, lesions, lashes (trichiasis, madarosis)
2. **Conjunctiva:** Bulbar, palpebral, fornices
3. **Sclera:** Color, thinning, nodules
4. **Cornea:** Layers, clarity, thickness, sensitivity
5. **Anterior Chamber:** Depth (Van Herick's method), cells, flare
6. **Iris:** Color, architecture, transillumination defects
7. **Lens:** Clarity, position, opacities
8. **Anterior Vitreous:** Cells, pigment

**Special Techniques:**
- **Sclerotic Scatter:** Detects subtle corneal edema
- **Retroillumination:** Evaluates lens and iris
- **Specular Reflection:** Examines corneal endothelium

### 1.7 Fundus Examination

Posterior segment evaluation is critical for detecting retinal and optic nerve pathology.

**Direct Ophthalmoscopy:**
- Provides approximately 15x magnification
- Limited field of view (approximately 10°)
- Good for examining optic disc and macula

**Indirect Ophthalmoscopy:**
- Wider field of view (approximately 30-40°)
- Stereoscopic view
- Requires pupil dilation for optimal visualization

**Biomicroscopy with Condensing Lenses:**
- 78D or 90D lenses for stereoscopic view
- Combined with slit lamp for detailed evaluation

**Fundus Features to Document:**
- Optic disc: Cup-to-disc ratio, color, margins, RNFL
- Macula: Foveal reflex, exudates, hemorrhages, edema
- Vessels: Arteriovenous ratio, tortuosity, crossings
- Periphery: Tears, holes, degenerations, lesions

## 2. Refraction

### 2.1 Objective Refraction

Objective refraction methods determine the refractive state without patient response.

**Retinoscopy:**
The gold standard objective technique using a retinoscope.

**Procedure:**
1. Patient fixates on a distant target (or cycloplegic agent for children)
2. Examiner works at 67 cm or 50 cm working distance
3. Neutralize the reflex with trial lenses
4. Subtract working distance (-1.50D for 67 cm, -2.00D for 50 cm)

**Reflex Characteristics:**
- **With motion:** Reflex moves in same direction as streak (hyperopia)
- **Against motion:** Reflex moves opposite to streak (myopia)
- **Neutrality:** Reflex fills the pupil instantly

**Automated Refraction:**
- Quick and objective
- Useful for screening
- May be less accurate with media opacities, small pupils, or irregular astigmatism

### 2.2 Subjective Refraction

Subjective refraction refines the prescription based on patient response.

**Monocular Subjective Refraction:**

1. **Spherical Refinement:**
   - Start with retinoscopy or autorefraction result
   - Use ±0.25D or ±0.50D lenses to find best vision
   - "Which is better, one or two?"
   - Maximum plus for best visual acuity (MPMVA)

2. **Jackson Cross Cylinder (JCC):**
   - Refines cylinder axis and power
   - Axis: Position JCC at 45° to cylinder axis, flip and compare
   - Power: Align JCC with cylinder axis, flip and compare

3. **Binocular Balancing:**
   - Prism dissociation method
   - Alternate occlusion
   - Ensure equal accommodation

**Duochrome Test (Red-Green Test):**
- Chromatic aberration principle
- Equal clarity on red and green = spherical endpoint
- Red clearer = over-minused or under-plussed
- Green clearer = over-plussed or under-minused

### 2.3 Cycloplegic Refraction

Cycloplegic refraction paralyzes accommodation for accurate measurement, especially in children and hyperopes.

**Common Cycloplegic Agents:**

| Agent | Concentration | Onset | Duration | Indications |
|-------|---------------|-------|----------|-------------|
| Cyclopentolate | 0.5-1% | 20-40 min | 24-48 hours | Routine cycloplegia |
| Atropine | 0.5-1% | 30-60 min | 7-14 days | Children with high AC/A, accommodative esotropia |
| Tropicamide | 0.5-1% | 20-30 min | 4-6 hours | Elderly, brief exam |

**Indications:**
- Children under 5 years
- Accommodative esotropia
- Hyperopia assessment
- Pseudomyopia evaluation
- Contact lens fitting in high ametropia

### 2.4 Prescription Considerations

Translating refraction findings into a wearable prescription requires clinical judgment.

**General Principles:**
- Maximum plus for hyperopia that provides clear vision
- Maximum minus for myopia that provides clear vision
- Correct significant astigmatism (≥0.75D)
- Consider vertex distance for high powers (>±4.00D)

**Special Situations:**
- **First-time wearers:** Reduce cylinder by 50% if adaptation is a concern
- **Children:** Full correction of refractive error
- **Presbyopes:** Determine appropriate near addition
- **Anisometropia:** Consider contact lenses or iseikonic lenses

## 3. Binocular Vision

### 3.1 Normal Binocular Vision

Binocular vision involves the coordination of both eyes to achieve single, comfortable, and efficient vision.

**Levels of Binocular Vision (Worth's Classification):**

1. **Simultaneous Perception:** Ability to perceive images from both eyes simultaneously
2. **Fusion:** Cortical integration of two similar images
   - Sensory fusion: Physical merging of images
   - Motor fusion: Convergence/divergence to maintain fusion
3. **Stereopsis:** Three-dimensional perception from retinal disparity

**Panum's Fusional Area:**
- Small retinal areas that permit single vision
- Extends approximately ±0.25° at the fovea
- Increases peripherally

### 3.2 Assessment of Binocular Vision

**Sensory Tests:**

1. **Worth Four Dot Test:**
   - Red-green glasses, four lights (one red, two green, one white)
   - Four lights: Normal binocular vision
   - Five lights: Diplopia
   - Two or three lights: Suppression

2. **Stereopsis Tests:**
   - Titmus Fly: Gross stereopsis (3000 arc seconds)
   - Randot: Fine stereopsis (20-400 arc seconds)
   - TNO: Random dot stereogram, no monocular cues

3. **Bagolini Striated Glasses:**
   - Detects suppression and abnormal retinal correspondence
   - Patient sees streaks forming an X

**Motor Tests:**

1. **Cover Tests:**
   - Distance and near
   - Primary position and cardinal positions
   - Measures manifest and latent deviations

2. **Prism Fusion Range:**
   - Base-out (convergence) and base-in (divergence)
   - Normal values: BO blur/break/recovery at near: 18/21/13Δ
   - Normal values: BI blur/break/recovery at near: 12/20/10Δ

3. **AC/A Ratio:**
   - Accommodative Convergence/Accommodation ratio
   - Normal: 3-5:1
   - Calculated or gradient method
   - Important in prescribing bifocals or managing esotropia

### 3.3 Common Binocular Vision Disorders

**Heterophoria:**
- Latent deviation maintained by fusion
- Types: Esophoria, exophoria, hyperphoria
- Symptoms: Asthenopia, headaches, blurred vision

**Heterotropia:**
- Manifest deviation
- Types: Esotropia (convergent), exotropia (divergent)
- May be constant or intermittent

**Convergence Insufficiency:**
- Receded near point of convergence (>10 cm)
- Low fusional convergence amplitudes
- Common in young adults and students
- Treatment: Orthoptic exercises (pencil push-ups, computer exercises)

**Accommodative Dysfunction:**
- Accommodative insufficiency: Reduced amplitude
- Accommodative excess: Pseudomyopia
- Accommodative infacility: Difficulty changing focus

### 3.4 Management of Binocular Vision Disorders

**Non-Surgical Management:**
- Refractive correction
- Prism glasses (Fresnel or ground-in)
- Vision therapy/orthoptics
- Near addition for convergence excess

**Vision Therapy Protocols:**
- Convergence exercises for convergence insufficiency
- Divergence exercises for divergence insufficiency
- Accommodative exercises for accommodative dysfunction
- Stereopsis training for amblyopia

## 4. Intraocular Pressure (IOP)

### 4.1 Normal IOP and Physiology

**Normal Range:** 10-21 mmHg (mean: 15.5 mmHg)

**Physiological Variation:**
- Diurnal fluctuation: 3-5 mmHg normal
- Peak typically in morning hours
- Postural changes: Higher in supine position
- Influenced by age, race, and systemic conditions

**Aqueous Humor Dynamics:**
- Production: Ciliary body epithelium (active secretion and ultrafiltration)
- Outflow: Trabecular meshwork (80%) and uveoscleral (20%)
- Balance determines IOP

### 4.2 Tonometry Techniques

**Goldmann Applanation Tonometry (GAT):**
- Gold standard for clinical IOP measurement
- Based on Imbert-Fick principle
- Applanates 3.06 mm diameter area
- One division on dial = 1 mmHg

**Procedure:**
1. Anesthetize cornea and apply fluorescein
2. Position tonometer tip perpendicular to cornea
3. Adjust until inner edges of semicircles just touch
4. Record reading

**Error Sources:**
- Central corneal thickness (CCT): Thin corneas underestimate, thick overestimate
- Corneal edema: Underestimates IOP
- High astigmatism: Measure at 45° to cylinder axis
- Technician factors: Pressure on globe, breath holding

**Correction Formulas:**
- Ehlers formula: Adjusted IOP = Measured IOP + ((520 - CCT)/50) × 5
- Or use corrected IOP tables

**Other Tonometry Methods:**

1. **Non-Contact Tonometry (NCT):**
   - Air-puff method
   - Good for screening
   - Less accurate than GAT

2. **Tono-Pen:**
   - Portable, handheld
   - Useful for irregular corneas
   - Multiple readings averaged

3. **iCare/Rebound Tonometry:**
   - No anesthetic required
   - Good for children and home monitoring
   - Slightly higher readings than GAT

4. **Pascal Dynamic Contour Tonometry:**
   - Least affected by CCT
   - Provides ocular pulse amplitude

### 4.3 Clinical Significance of IOP

**Glaucoma:**
- IOP >21 mmHg is a risk factor
- Target IOP reduction: 20-30% from baseline
- Progressive optic neuropathy with visual field loss

**Ocular Hypertension:**
- IOP >21 mmHg without glaucomatous damage
- Risk of conversion: 4.4% over 5 years
- Treatment decision based on risk factors

**Secondary Causes of Elevated IOP:**
- Uveitic glaucoma
- Angle-closure glaucoma
- Pseudoexfoliation syndrome
- Pigment dispersion syndrome
- Steroid response
- Traumatic angle recession

### 4.4 Gonioscopy

Gonioscopy evaluates the anterior chamber angle, essential for glaucoma classification.

**Techniques:**

1. **Direct Gonioscopy:**
   - Koeppe lens
   - Patient supine
   - Good for children and surgery

2. **Indirect Gonioscopy:**
   - Goldmann, Zeiss, Sussman lenses
   - Mirror reflects angle structures
   - Performed at slit lamp

**Angle Structures (Anterior to Posterior):**
1. Schwalbe's line
2. Trabecular meshwork (pigmented and non-pigmented)
3. Scleral spur
4. Ciliary body band
5. Iris root

**Shaffer Grading:**
- Grade 4: Wide open (35-45°)
- Grade 3: Open (20-35°)
- Grade 2: Narrow (20°) - possible closure
- Grade 1: Very narrow (10°) - likely closure
- Grade 0: Closed (0°)

**Spaeth Grading:**
- More comprehensive
- Includes iris insertion, angle approach, and pigmentation

## 5. Visual Fields

### 5.1 Visual Field Anatomy

**Normal Visual Field:**
- Extends approximately 60° nasally, 90° temporally
- 50° superiorly, 60° inferiorly
- Blind spot at 15° temporal to fixation

**Visual Pathway:**
1. Retina (ganglion cells)
2. Optic nerve
3. Optic chiasm (nasal fibers cross)
4. Optic tract
5. Lateral geniculate nucleus
6. Optic radiations
7. Visual cortex (occipital lobe)

### 5.2 Visual Field Testing Methods

**Confrontation Testing:**
- Quick screening at bedside
- Finger counting, hand comparison
- Detects large defects (sensitivity 35-60%)

**Automated Perimetry:**

**Standard Automated Perimetry (SAP):**
- Most common clinical test
- White stimulus on white background
- Measures differential light sensitivity

**Common Protocols:**
1. **24-2 SITA Standard/FAST:**
   - Tests central 24°
   - 54 test points
   - Standard for glaucoma

2. **30-2:**
   - Tests central 30°
   - 76 test points
   - More comprehensive

3. **10-2:**
   - Tests central 10°
   - 68 test points
   - Detailed macular assessment

**Reliability Indices:**
- **Fixation losses:** Should be <20%
- **False positives:** Should be <15%
- **False negatives:** Should be <20%

**Interpretation Parameters:**
- **Total Deviation:** Age-corrected sensitivity
- **Pattern Deviation:** Removes generalized depression
- **Mean Deviation (MD):** Overall field sensitivity
- **Pattern Standard Deviation (PSD):** Localized defect measure

### 5.3 Visual Field Defects

**Retinal/Pre-chiasmal Defects:**
- Monocular
- Respects retinal nerve fiber distribution
- Central scotoma: Macular disease
- Ring scotoma: Retinitis pigmentosa
- Arcuate scotoma: Glaucoma

**Chiasmal Defects:**
- Bitemporal hemianopia: Pituitary adenoma
- Junctional scotoma: Optic nerve/chiasm junction

**Post-chiasmal Defects:**
- Homonymous hemianopia: Contralateral lesion
- Congruity increases posteriorly
- Pupillary reactions normal

**Glaucomatous Field Defects:**
1. **Early:**
   - Paracentral scotoma
   - Nasal step
   - Temporal wedge defect

2. **Moderate:**
   - Arcuate scotoma
   - Partial ring scotoma

3. **Advanced:**
   - Ring scotoma
   - Central/paracentral island remaining

### 5.4 Visual Field Interpretation

**Steps for Analysis:**

1. **Check Reliability:**
   - Review fixation losses, false positives, false negatives
   - Repeat if unreliable

2. **Assess General Sensitivity:**
   - Overall depression suggests media opacity
   - Review total deviation plot

3. **Identify Localized Defects:**
   - Pattern deviation plot
   - Look for clustered depressed points

4. **Assess Severity:**
   - MD and PSD values
   - Glaucoma Hemifield Test (GHT)
   - Visual Field Index (VFI)

5. **Consider Artifact:**
   - Lid droop (superior depression)
   - Lens rim (peripheral ring scotoma)
   - Inattention

### 5.5 Follow-up and Progression

**Glaucoma Progression Analysis:**
- Trend-based: GPA (Glaucoma Progression Analysis), VFI slope
- Event-based: Point-wise comparison to baseline
- Requires at least 5-6 tests for reliable trend

**Frequency:**
- Stable glaucoma: Annually
- Unstable: Every 3-6 months
- New diagnosis: Baseline and 1-2 follow-up tests

## 6. Integration and Clinical Application

### 6.1 Examination Sequence for MOH Exam

A systematic approach ensures comprehensive evaluation:

1. Patient history and chief complaint
2. Visual acuity (distance and near)
3. External examination
4. Pupillary assessment
5. Ocular motility
6. Visual field screening (confrontation)
7. Tonometry
8. Anterior segment examination (slit lamp)
9. Fundus examination
10. Refraction
11. Binocular vision assessment
12. Additional tests as indicated

### 6.2 Documentation Standards

**SOAP Format:**
- **Subjective:** Patient's complaints and symptoms
- **Objective:** Examination findings
- **Assessment:** Diagnosis and clinical impression
- **Plan:** Management, treatment, and follow-up

**Minimum Documentation:**
- Visual acuity (corrected and uncorrected)
- IOP with method and time
- Anterior segment findings
- Fundus findings
- Refraction (if performed)
- Diagnosis
- Management plan

### 6.3 Clinical Pearls for MOH Exam

1. **Always document thoroughly** - medicolegal and continuity of care
2. **Use systematic approach** - avoid missing findings
3. **Consider differential diagnosis** - don't jump to conclusions
4. **Know normal values** - essential for detecting abnormalities
5. **Understand test limitations** - appropriate referrals
6. **Practice clinical reasoning** - connect findings to diagnosis

This comprehensive guide covers the essential clinical optometry knowledge required for the MOH UAE Optometry Examination. Key areas include:

- **Eye Examination:** Systematic approach from history to fundus evaluation

Mastery of these fundamental concepts, combined with clinical experience, will prepare candidates for successful examination performance and competent clinical practice in the UAE healthcare system.
