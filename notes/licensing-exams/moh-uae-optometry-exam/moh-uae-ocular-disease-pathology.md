# Ocular Disease & Pathology for MOH Exam

## Introduction

The Ministry of Health (MOH) UAE Optometry licensing examination represents a critical milestone for optometrists seeking to practice in the United Arab Emirates. Ocular disease and pathology constitute a fundamental domain of this examination, requiring comprehensive understanding of anterior segment conditions, cataract management, glaucoma diagnosis and treatment, retinal disorders, and uveitis classification. This comprehensive guide provides the essential knowledge required for successful examination performance while emphasizing clinical reasoning and patient management principles.

The MOH examination places significant emphasis on practical clinical knowledge, expecting candidates to demonstrate competence in disease recognition, differential diagnosis, and appropriate management decisions including referral criteria. Understanding the unique patient demographics and disease patterns encountered in the UAE healthcare environment is essential for examination success.

## 1. Anterior Segment Disorders

### 1.1 Conjunctival Pathology

#### Bacterial Conjunctivitis

Bacterial conjunctivitis remains one of the most frequently encountered ocular infections in optometric practice. The condition presents with characteristic mucopurulent discharge, conjunctival injection, and matting of eyelids, particularly noticeable upon waking. Understanding the microbiology and clinical patterns is essential for appropriate management.

**Causative Organisms:**
- *Staphylococcus aureus* (most common in adults)
- *Streptococcus pneumoniae*
- *Haemophilus influenzae*
- *Moraxella catarrhalis*
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa* (contact lens wearers)

**Clinical Presentation:**
Bacterial conjunctivitis typically presents with unilateral or bilateral conjunctival injection, yellow-green mucopurulent discharge, morning crusting with eyelash matting, foreign body sensation, and occasionally pre-auricular lymphadenopathy. Hyperacute presentations with profuse purulent discharge should raise suspicion for *Neisseria gonorrhoeae*, requiring urgent ophthalmology referral.

**Management Protocol:**
Management includes broad-spectrum topical antibiotics such as fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, gatifloxacin), aminoglycosides (tobramycin, gentamicin), or macrolides (azithromycin). Treatment duration typically spans 5-7 days. Patient education regarding hygiene measures to prevent transmission is essential. Most cases are self-limiting within 7-14 days without treatment; however, antibiotic therapy reduces contagious period and accelerates symptom resolution.

#### Viral Conjunctivitis

Viral conjunctivitis, predominantly caused by adenovirus, presents significant challenges due to its highly contagious nature and lack of specific antiviral therapy. Recognition of different viral syndromes is crucial for appropriate patient counseling and infection control measures.

**Adenoviral Syndromes:**

*Simple Follicular Conjunctivitis:*
The most common presentation featuring watery discharge, follicular conjunctival reaction, pre-auricular lymphadenopathy, and associated upper respiratory symptoms. Typically self-limiting within 2-3 weeks.

*Pharyngoconjunctival Fever (PCF):*
Caused by adenovirus serotypes 3 and 7, PCF presents with the triad of fever, pharyngitis, and conjunctivitis. More common in pediatric populations, often occurring in outbreaks associated with swimming pools. Systemic symptoms may precede ocular findings by several days.

*Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (EKC):*
The most severe adenoviral ocular infection, caused by serotypes 8, 19, and 37. Features include severe conjunctivitis with chemosis, pseudomembrane formation, and characteristic subepithelial corneal infiltrates developing after 2 weeks. These infiltrates may persist for months to years, potentially affecting visual acuity and causing glare symptoms.

**Management Approach:**
Treatment is primarily supportive with cold compresses, artificial tears, and strict hygiene education. Pseudomembrane removal may be necessary in severe cases. Topical steroids may be considered for severe corneal infiltrates but should be used judiciously due to potential for prolonged viral shedding. Patients should be counseled regarding the highly contagious nature and advised on infection control measures including hand hygiene, avoiding face towel sharing, and remaining home from work or school during the acute phase.

#### Allergic Conjunctivitis

Allergic conjunctivitis represents a Type I hypersensitivity reaction mediated by IgE, triggered by environmental allergens. The condition significantly impacts quality of life and requires appropriate classification for targeted management.

**Classification and Clinical Features:**

*Seasonal Allergic Conjunctivitis (SAC):*
The most prevalent form, triggered by seasonal allergens including tree, grass, and weed pollens. Characterized by bilateral itching (the hallmark symptom), watery discharge, conjunctival injection, and chemosis. Symptoms correlate with allergen exposure periods.

*Perennial Allergic Conjunctivitis (PAC):*
Year-round symptoms triggered by dust mites, pet dander, and mold. Similar presentation to SAC but persistent throughout the year with possible seasonal exacerbations.

*Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis (VKC):*
A severe, chronic form predominantly affecting young males in warm climates. Characterized by intense itching, giant papillae on the upper tarsal conjunctiva (cobblestone appearance), Horner-Trantas dots (eosinophil aggregates at the limbus), and potential for sight-threatening complications including shield ulcers and corneal scarring.

*Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis (AKC):*
Associated with atopic dermatitis, presenting with chronic, potentially blinding inflammation. Eyelid involvement is common with eczematous changes. Increased risk of corneal complications including neovascularization, scarring, and keratoconus.

**Management Strategy:**
A stepwise approach includes allergen avoidance, cold compresses, topical antihistamines (olopatadine, ketotifen, epinastine), mast cell stabilizers (cromolyn sodium, lodoxamide), dual-action agents combining antihistamine and mast cell stabilizer properties, and topical corticosteroids for severe cases. Systemic antihistamines may provide additional benefit for associated allergic symptoms. Referral is indicated for vision-threatening complications or steroid-responsive disease requiring long-term management.

#### Pinguecula and Pterygium

**Pinguecula:**
A pinguecula represents elastotic degeneration of conjunctival tissue secondary to ultraviolet exposure and environmental irritants. The yellowish, elevated lesion typically occurs at the nasal or temporal limbus within the interpalpebral zone. While generally asymptomatic, it may cause irritation, dry eye symptoms, and cosmetic concerns. Management includes UV protection and lubricating drops; surgical intervention is rarely indicated.

**Pterygium:**
A pterygium represents progressive fibrovascular proliferation extending from the conjunctiva onto the cornea. Characterized by a triangular growth with the head on the cornea and body on the conjunctiva, it may cause irregular astigmatism, visual axis obstruction, and significant cosmetic deformity.

**Clinical Features:**
- Triangular, wing-shaped fibrovascular growth
- Stocker's line (iron deposition at the advancing edge)
- Fuchs' patches (grayish opacities at the head)
- Irregular astigmatism from corneal flattening
- Progressive encroachment toward visual axis

**Surgical Indications:**
Surgical excision is indicated for visual axis involvement, significant induced astigmatism affecting visual acuity, persistent inflammation unresponsive to medical therapy, restrictive ocular motility, and cosmetic concerns. Conjunctival autograft with fibrin glue or sutures reduces recurrence rates to 5-15%. Adjuvant therapies including mitomycin C and amniotic membrane transplantation may be utilized in high-risk cases.

### 1.2 Corneal Pathology

#### Bacterial Keratitis

Bacterial keratitis represents a sight-threatening emergency requiring prompt diagnosis and aggressive management. Risk factors include contact lens wear (particularly extended wear), corneal trauma, ocular surface disease, and immunocompromised states.

**Microbiology:**
- *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*: Most common in contact lens wearers; rapidly progressive with characteristic ground-glass appearance and ring infiltrate
- *Staphylococcus species*: Common in blepharitis and chronic ocular surface disease
- *Streptococcus pneumoniae*: Associated with corneal trauma
- *Moraxella species*: More common in alcoholics and immunocompromised patients

**Clinical Presentation:**
Patients present with severe pain, photophobia, decreased vision, conjunctival injection, mucopurulent discharge, and a corneal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect. Hypopyon formation indicates severe infection and may represent sterile inflammatory reaction or actual infectious seeding.

**Management Protocol:**
Management requires corneal scraping for Gram stain, culture, and antibiotic sensitivity testing. Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotic therapy should be initiated immediately. Options include monotherapy with fourth-generation fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, gancifloxacin) or combination therapy with fortified antibiotics (cefazolin + tobramycin/gentamicin). Initial intensive dosing (every 15-30 minutes around the clock) with tapering based on clinical response. Hospital admission should be considered for severe or rapidly progressive cases.

#### Herpes Simplex Keratitis

Herpes simplex keratitis (HSK) represents a leading cause of infectious corneal blindness in developed countries. The condition may present with various clinical manifestations requiring different management approaches.

**Clinical Types:**

*Epithelial Keratitis:*
The classic dendritic ulcer with branching pattern and terminal bulbs represents active viral replication. Fluorescein staining outlines the defect, while rose bengal or lissamine green stains virus-infected cells at the margins. Patients present with pain, photophobia, tearing, and decreased vision.

*Stromal Keratitis:*
Immune-mediated inflammation of the corneal stroma presenting as disciform keratitis (round area of stromal edema) or necrotizing stromal keratitis with dense infiltration and potential ulceration. Requires careful distinction from infectious keratitis.

*Endotheliitis:*
Inflammation of the corneal endothelium presenting with focal or diffuse corneal edema, keratic precipitates, and possible anterior chamber reaction. May cause secondary glaucoma.

**Management:**
Epithelial disease responds to topical antiviral therapy (acyclovir ointment, ganciclovir gel) or oral antiviral agents (acyclovir, valacyclovir). Debridement may accelerate healing. Stromal and endothelial disease require topical corticosteroids with concurrent antiviral coverage. Long-term suppressive oral antiviral therapy reduces recurrence rates in patients with frequent episodes.

#### Fungal Keratitis

Fungal keratitis presents diagnostic and therapeutic challenges, particularly in tropical climates and agricultural settings. Risk factors include trauma with vegetable matter, contact lens wear, chronic topical steroid use, and immunocompromise.

**Clinical Features:**
Fungal keratitis typically has a more indolent course than bacterial infection. Characteristic findings include gray-white infiltrate with feathery margins, satellite lesions, ring infiltrate, hypopyon with irregular margins, and endothelial plaque formation.

**Causative Organisms:**
- Filamentous fungi: *Fusarium*, *Aspergillus* species (most common)
- Yeasts: *Candida* species (associated with chronic ocular surface disease)

**Diagnosis and Management:**
Diagnosis requires corneal scraping for microscopy (potassium hydroxide preparation, Gram stain, Giemsa stain) and culture on Sabouraud agar. Confocal microscopy may demonstrate fungal hyphae in vivo. Treatment involves topical natamycin 5% suspension (first-line for filamentous fungi) or topical amphotericin B 0.15-0.3% (for yeasts). Voriconazole may be used topically or systemically for resistant cases. Treatment duration extends weeks to months with careful monitoring for progression.

#### Acanthamoeba Keratitis

Acanthamoeba keratitis represents a rare but devastating infection most commonly associated with contact lens wear and exposure to contaminated water. Early recognition is critical for preventing severe visual outcomes.

**Risk Factors:**
- Contact lens wear (especially soft lenses)
- Swimming, showering, or hot tub use with contact lenses
- Use of tap water or homemade saline for lens care
- Corneal trauma

**Clinical Features:**
The hallmark feature is severe pain disproportionate to clinical findings. Early signs include epithelial irregularity and perineuritis (radial keratoneuritis). Late findings include ring infiltrate (characteristic but not pathognomonic) and progressive stromal involvement. The condition is frequently misdiagnosed as herpes simplex keratitis, leading to treatment delays.

**Diagnosis:**
Confocal microscopy allows in vivo visualization of cysts and trophozoites. Culture on non-nutrient agar with Escherichia coli overlay demonstrates characteristic tracks. PCR testing provides rapid diagnosis. Corneal biopsy may be necessary when non-invasive methods are inconclusive.

**Management:**
Treatment requires combination therapy with biguanides (polyhexamethylene biguanide 0.02% or chlorhexidine 0.02%) and aromatic diamidines (propamidine isethionate 0.1% or hexamidine 0.1%). Intensive hourly dosing initially with prolonged treatment duration (months). Cysts are resistant to killing, necessitating extended therapy. Corneal transplantation may be required for severe scarring or perforation but should be delayed until infection is controlled due to risk of graft reinfection.

## 2. Cataract

### 2.1 Age-Related Cataract Classification

Cataract, defined as any opacity of the crystalline lens, represents the leading cause of reversible blindness worldwide. Understanding cataract types, grading systems, and surgical indications is fundamental for MOH examination success.

#### Nuclear Sclerosis Cataract

Nuclear sclerosis represents the most common form of age-related cataract, characterized by gradual hardening and yellowing of the lens nucleus.

**Clinical Features:**
Progressive yellowing progressing to brunescence, increased optical density of the nucleus, progressive myopia due to increased refractive index (the "second sight" phenomenon where presbyopes experience improved near vision), color vision changes particularly affecting blue-yellow discrimination, and glare symptoms in bright illumination.

**Grading (LOCS III):**
The Lens Opacities Classification System III provides standardized grading using slit-lamp examination compared to photographic standards. Nuclear opalescence (NO) is graded from NO1 to NO6, with NO1-NO2 representing early changes and NO5-NO6 indicating advanced cataract with significant visual impact.

#### Cortical Cataract

Cortical cataracts involve opacification of the lens cortex, appearing as wedge-shaped or radial spoke-like opacities extending from the periphery toward the center.

**Clinical Features:**
Radial spoke-like opacities, often beginning inferonasally, progress centrally with advancing age. Patients experience glare symptoms, particularly in bright sunlight or night driving with oncoming headlights. Monocular diplopia or polyopia may occur when cortical spokes intersect the visual axis. Vision may fluctuate with pupil size changes.

**Grading (LOCS III):**
Cortical cataracts are graded C1 through C5 based on the proportion of cortex involved. C1 represents minimal cortical changes while C5 indicates significant cortical opacification affecting visual function.

#### Posterior Subcapsular Cataract (PSC)

PSC involves opacification at the posterior cortical layer immediately anterior to the posterior capsule. Despite often being small, PSC causes disproportionate visual symptoms.

**Clinical Features:**
Granular, plaque-like opacities under the posterior capsule, located axially in the visual axis. Patients experience severe glare disability, difficulty with near vision, and symptoms worse in bright illumination due to pupil constriction concentrating light through the opacity. Progression may be rapid, particularly in steroid users.

**Risk Factors:**
- Corticosteroid use (topical, inhaled, or systemic)
- Diabetes mellitus
- High myopia
- Retinitis pigmentosa
- Ocular trauma
- Younger patient age

### 2.2 Cataract Grading Systems

**LOCS III (Lens Opacities Classification System III):**
The internationally recognized standard for cataract grading using slit-lamp biomicroscopy compared to photographic standards:

| Component | Grades | Description |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| Nuclear Opalescence | NO1-NO6 | Degree of nuclear scattering |
| Nuclear Color | NC1-NC6 | Color change from yellow to brown |
| Cortical | C1-C5 | Percentage of cortex involved |
| Posterior Subcapsular | P1-P5 | Area of posterior capsule involved |

### 2.3 Indications for Surgery

**Visual Function Indications:**
- Best corrected visual acuity insufficient for patient's daily activities
- Glare disability affecting function (driving, reading, occupational requirements)
- Anisometropia from cataract-induced refractive change
- Monocular diplopia not correctable with refraction

**Ocular Health Indications:**
- Phacomorphic glaucoma (angle closure from intumescent lens)
- Phacolytic glaucoma (lens protein leakage causing inflammatory glaucoma)
- Phacoantigenic uveitis
- Interference with posterior segment examination or treatment

### 2.4 Pre-operative Assessment

**Essential Evaluations:**
Complete pre-operative assessment includes visual acuity measurement (distance and near), manifest and cycloplegic refraction, pupil examination, intraocular pressure measurement, slit-lamp biomicroscopy of anterior segment, dilated fundus examination (when possible), and biometry (axial length, keratometry).

**IOL Power Calculation:**
Modern formulas include SRK/T, Hoffer Q, Holladay 1 and 2, Haigis, and Barrett Universal II. The Barrett Universal II formula provides excellent accuracy across a wide range of axial lengths and is increasingly preferred. Accurate keratometry and axial length measurement are critical for achieving target refraction.

### 2.5 Post-operative Complications

**Early Complications:**
- Corneal edema (usually transient)
- Wound leak
- Anterior uveitis
- Toxic anterior segment syndrome (TASS)
- Endophthalmitis (rare but devastating)
- Retained lens material
- Intraocular lens dislocation

**Late Complications:**
- Posterior capsule opacification (PCO) - most common, occurring in 10-50% within 5 years
- Cystoid macular edema (Irvine-Gass syndrome)
- Retinal detachment (increased risk with high myopia)
- Corneal decompensation (pseudophakic bullous keratopathy)
- Intraocular lens dislocation or subluxation
- Refractive surprise

## 3. Glaucoma

### 3.1 Overview and Classification

Glaucoma encompasses a group of progressive optic neuropathies characterized by acquired loss of retinal ganglion cells and their axons, resulting in characteristic optic nerve head changes and visual field loss. It represents the second leading cause of irreversible blindness globally.

**Classification:**
- Primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG)
- Primary angle-closure glaucoma (PACG)
- Secondary glaucomas (pigmentary, pseudoexfoliation, neovascular, uveitic)
- Developmental glaucomas (congenital, infantile, juvenile)

### 3.2 Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

POAG is characterized by open anterior chamber angles, characteristic optic neuropathy, and typically elevated intraocular pressure, though normal-tension variants exist.

**Risk Factors:**
- Elevated intraocular pressure (>21 mmHg)
- Age > 40 years
- Family history of glaucoma
- African, Hispanic, or Asian descent
- Myopia
- Diabetes mellitus
- Systemic hypertension
- Corticosteroid responsiveness

**Clinical Features:**

*Intraocular Pressure:*
While typically elevated above 21 mmHg, significant overlap exists with normal populations. Diurnal variation occurs with peaks typically in the morning hours. Normal-tension glaucoma demonstrates optic neuropathy with IOP consistently below 21 mmHg.

*Optic Nerve Changes:*
Characteristic findings include increased cup-to-disc ratio (>0.6 or asymmetry >0.2 between eyes), vertical cup elongation, notching of the neuroretinal rim, splinter hemorrhages (important prognostic sign), peripapillary atrophy, and retinal nerve fiber layer defects visible with red-free illumination or OCT imaging.

*Visual Field Progression:*
Early changes include paracentral scotomas and nasal step defects. Moderate disease demonstrates arcuate scotomas involving Bjerrum's area. Advanced disease shows ring scotoma with preservation of temporal and central islands. End-stage disease leaves only a small central island of remaining vision.

**Diagnosis:**
Comprehensive evaluation includes Goldmann applanation tonometry (gold standard for IOP), gonioscopy to confirm open angles, optic nerve head assessment with documentation, visual field testing (standard automated perimetry), optical coherence tomography of retinal nerve fiber layer and ganglion cell complex, and central corneal thickness measurement (pachymetry) to adjust IOP interpretation.

**Management:**
Treatment targets IOP reduction, typically aiming for 20-30% reduction from baseline. Medical therapy includes prostaglandin analogs (latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost) as first-line agents due to efficacy and once-daily dosing. Adjunctive agents include beta-blockers, alpha-2 agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, and rho kinase inhibitors. Laser trabeculoplasty (selective laser trabeculoplasty, argon laser trabeculoplasty) provides effective IOP reduction. Surgical options include trabeculectomy, aqueous shunts, and minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS).

### 3.3 Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)

PACG results from mechanical obstruction of the trabecular meshwork by the peripheral iris, preventing aqueous outflow.

**Risk Factors:**
- Hyperopia
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Short axial length (nanophthalmos)
- Older age (lens thickening)
- Female gender
- Asian or Inuit descent
- Plateau iris configuration

**Classification (ISGEO System):**

1. **Primary Angle-Closure Suspect (PACS):** Narrow angles with ≥180° of trabecular meshwork not visible on gonioscopy, no peripheral anterior synechiae, normal IOP, no optic nerve damage.

2. **Primary Angle-Closure (PAC):** Narrow angles with peripheral anterior synechiae and/or elevated IOP, without optic neuropathy.

3. **Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG):** Narrow angles with evidence of glaucomatous optic neuropathy and visual field loss.

**Acute Angle-Closure Crisis:**
Medical emergency characterized by sudden onset of severe ocular pain, blurred vision with colored halos around lights, nausea and vomiting, conjunctival injection, corneal microcystic edema, shallow anterior chamber, mid-dilated non-reactive pupil, and IOP often exceeding 40-60 mmHg.

**Immediate Management:**
- Topical beta-blocker and alpha-2 agonist
- Topical or systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitor
- Topical steroid for inflammation
- Systemic osmotic agents (mannitol IV)
- Pilocarpine after IOP reduction (ineffective at high IOP due to iris ischemia)
- Laser peripheral iridotomy (definitive treatment)

### 3.4 Secondary Glaucomas

**Pigmentary Glaucoma:**
Associated with pigment dispersion syndrome, characterized by Krukenberg spindle (pigment deposits on corneal endothelium), iris transillumination defects in mid-peripheral radial pattern, and pigment accumulation in the trabecular meshwork. More common in young myopic males. Exercise or pupil dilation may precipitate acute IOP elevation.

**Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma:**
Associated with pseudoexfoliation syndrome, characterized by white, fluffy deposits on the lens capsule, pupillary margin, and zonules. Classic "target" appearance on lens with clear zone surrounded by deposits. Associated with weak zonules and increased risk of complications during cataract surgery.

**Neovascular Glaucoma:**
Results from fibrovascular proliferation on the iris and angle (rubeosis iridis), typically associated with retinal ischemia. Common causes include proliferative diabetic retinopathy, central retinal vein occlusion, and carotid artery disease. Management requires panretinal photocoagulation, anti-VEGF therapy, and glaucoma surgery.

## 4. Retinal Disorders

### 4.1 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy represents a leading cause of blindness in working-age populations. Understanding classification and management is essential for optometric practice.

**Classification:**

*Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):*
- Mild NPDR: Microaneurysms only
- Moderate NPDR: Microaneurysms, hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton wool spots
- Severe NPDR: 4-2-1 rule (hemorrhages in 4 quadrants, venous beading in 2 quadrants, IRMA in 1 quadrant)

*Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR):*
Characterized by neovascularization of the disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE), vitreous hemorrhage, and tractional retinal detachment.

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
Retinal thickening involving or threatening the macula. Center-involved DME affects the fovea and requires treatment. Treatment includes anti-VEGF therapy (first-line), laser photocoagulation, and dexamethasone implant.

**Management:**
Optimal glycemic control (HbA1c <7%), blood pressure management, lipid control, regular screening, and timely referral for treatment. Screening recommendations: Type 1 diabetes beginning 5 years after diagnosis, Type 2 diabetes at diagnosis, with annual examinations thereafter.

### 4.2 Age-Related Macular Degeneration

AMD represents the leading cause of central vision loss in elderly populations in developed countries.

**Classification:**
- Early AMD: Small to medium drusen, RPE pigmentary changes
- Intermediate AMD: Large drusen (>125 μm), geographic atrophy not involving fovea
- Late AMD: Neovascular (wet) AMD or geographic atrophy involving fovea

**Dry AMD:**
Characterized by drusen accumulation, RPE atrophy, and geographic atrophy. Slowly progressive with no specific treatment. AREDS2 supplements (vitamins C and E, lutein, zeaxanthin, zinc, copper) recommended for intermediate AMD to reduce progression risk.

**Wet AMD:**
Characterized by choroidal neovascularization causing sudden vision loss, metamorphopsia, subretinal fluid, and hemorrhage. Requires prompt anti-VEGF therapy (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab) with regular monitoring using OCT imaging.

### 4.3 Retinal Vascular Occlusions

**Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO):**
Presents with widespread retinal hemorrhages in all quadrants ("blood and thunder" appearance), venous dilation and tortuosity, cotton wool spots, and macular edema. Ischemic type has poor prognosis and risk of neovascular glaucoma ("90-day glaucoma").

**Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO):**
Localized hemorrhages and edema in affected sector. Better prognosis than CRVO. Treatment includes anti-VEGF therapy for macular edema.

**Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO):**
Ophthalmic emergency presenting with sudden profound vision loss, "cherry-red spot" at fovea, retinal whitening, and attenuated arteries. Treatment must be initiated within hours for any chance of visual recovery.

### 4.4 Retinal Detachment

**Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment:**
Results from retinal break allowing fluid entry into subretinal space. Associated with posterior vitreous detachment, lattice degeneration, and myopia. Symptoms include flashes, floaters, and progressive visual field defect (curtain). Requires urgent surgical repair.

**Tractional Retinal Detachment:**
Results from fibrovascular membranes pulling retina away from RPE. Associated with proliferative diabetic retinopathy. Concave surface with limited mobility.

**Exudative Retinal Detachment:**
Results from subretinal fluid accumulation without retinal break. Causes include inflammation, tumors, and vascular disease. Characterized by shifting fluid with position changes.

## 5. Uveitis

### 5.1 Classification

Uveitis refers to inflammation of the uveal tract and adjacent structures. Classification is primarily anatomical:

| Type | Primary Site | Includes |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Anterior | Iris, ciliary body | Iritis, iridocyclitis |
| Intermediate | Vitreous, pars plana | Pars planitis |
| Posterior | Choroid, retina | Choroiditis, retinitis |
| Panuveitis | All structures | Diffuse inflammation |

Additional classification by onset (acute vs. chronic), course (persistent, recurrent), and pathology (granulomatous vs. non-granulomatous).

### 5.2 Anterior Uveitis

The most common form of uveitis, accounting for approximately 75% of cases.

**Clinical Features:**
Patients present with eye pain, photophobia, red eye (perilimbal injection), tearing, and variable decreased vision. Examination reveals ciliary flush, keratic precipitates (fine in non-granulomatous, mutton-fat in granulomatous), anterior chamber cells and flare, hypopyon in severe cases, and posterior synechiae.

**Etiology:**
- Idiopathic (most common)
- HLA-B27 associated conditions (ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, psoriatic arthritis)
- Infectious (herpes simplex, varicella zoster, syphilis, tuberculosis)
- Non-infectious (sarcoidosis, juvenile idiopathic arthritis, Fuchs' heterochromic iridocyclitis)

**Management:**
Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone acetate, dexamethasone) with appropriate tapering. Cycloplegic agents (atropine, cyclopentolate, homatropine) for comfort and to prevent posterior synechiae. Investigation for underlying cause in recurrent or atypical cases.

### 5.3 Intermediate Uveitis

Primarily involves the vitreous and pars plana region. Patients present with floaters and mild to moderate vision loss with minimal pain or redness. Examination reveals vitreous cells, snowball opacities, and snowbanking at the pars plana.

**Pars Planitis:**
Idiopathic intermediate uveitis, most common in children and young adults. Bilateral in 80% of cases. May cause cystoid macular edema as the primary cause of vision loss.

### 5.4 Posterior Uveitis

Involves the choroid and/or retina with significant potential for vision-threatening complications.

**Infectious Causes:**
- Toxoplasmosis: Most common infectious cause; focal necrotizing retinitis with "headlight in fog" appearance
- Cytomegalovirus: Immunocompromised patients; "pizza pie" appearance with hemorrhages and exudates
- Acute retinal necrosis: Herpes simplex or varicella zoster; rapidly progressive with high risk of retinal detachment
- Tuberculosis: Multifocal choroiditis or serpiginous-like choroiditis
- Syphilis: "Great mimicker" with variable presentations

**Non-Infectious Causes:**
- Sarcoidosis: Multisystem granulomatous disease with characteristic candle-wax drippings
- Behçet's disease: Recurrent oral and genital ulcers, uveitis with hypopyon, retinal vasculitis
- Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada syndrome: Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis with poliosis, vitiligo, and hearing disturbances

## Conclusion

Ocular disease and pathology represent fundamental knowledge domains for the MOH UAE Optometry licensing examination. Successful candidates must demonstrate comprehensive understanding of anterior segment conditions, cataract classification and management, glaucoma diagnosis and treatment algorithms, retinal disease recognition, and uveitis classification. Clinical reasoning skills, including appropriate referral decisions, are equally important as factual knowledge.

Key examination strategies include recognizing red flag signs requiring urgent intervention, understanding the interrelationship between systemic and ocular disease, and demonstrating competence in diagnostic reasoning through case-based scenarios. Regular review of current clinical guidelines and evidence-based management protocols ensures examination preparedness.

---

## Key Examination Points

1. **Anterior Segment:** Distinguish conjunctivitis types, recognize keratitis patterns, understand corneal dystrophy classifications

2. **Cataract:** Master LOCS III grading, know surgical indications, understand IOL calculation principles

3. **Glaucoma:** Differentiate POAG from PACG, recognize secondary glaucoma types, understand treatment algorithms

4. **Retina:** Classify diabetic retinopathy, recognize AMD types, understand retinal vascular occlusions

5. **Uveitis:** Classify anatomically, distinguish granulomatous from non-granulomatous, know infectious and non-infectious etiologies
