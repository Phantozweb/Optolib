# Clinical Case Studies for MOH Exam

## Introduction

Clinical case studies form an essential component of the Ministry of Health (MOH) UAE Optometry licensing examination. These scenarios evaluate the candidate's clinical reasoning, diagnostic acumen, and management decision-making abilities. This comprehensive collection presents 15 detailed case studies spanning five critical domains: red eye presentations, vision loss cases, refraction challenges, contact lens complications, and pediatric optometry scenarios. Each case provides patient presentation, clinical findings, differential diagnosis, evidence-based management approach, and key learning points essential for MOH examination success.

## SECTION 1: RED EYE CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 1: Acute Bacterial Conjunctivitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 28-year-old male construction worker presents with a 3-day history of right eye redness, mucopurulent discharge, and morning crusting of eyelids. He reports waking with his eyelids "stuck together" requiring warm water to open. The discharge has increased over the past 24 hours, now yellowish-green in color. He denies trauma, foreign body exposure, or recent illness. His past ocular history is unremarkable with no known allergies.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OU (unaffected)
- External examination: Diffuse bulbar conjunctival injection OD greater than OS, mild right eyelid edema
- Discharge: Mucopurulent, yellow-green, accumulating at medial canthus
- Preauricular lymph node: Non-palpable bilaterally
- Slit lamp examination: Mild papillary reaction on upper tarsal conjunctiva OD, clear cornea with no epithelial defects, quiet anterior chamber, no follicles noted
- Intraocular pressure: 14 mmHg OD, 15 mmHg OS

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Acute bacterial conjunctivitis (most likely - mucopurulent discharge, absence of preauricular lymphadenopathy)
2. Viral conjunctivitis (typically clear watery discharge, often with preauricular node)
3. Allergic conjunctivitis (bilateral, itchy, stringy discharge)
4. Toxic conjunctivitis (chemical exposure history absent)

**Diagnosis:**
Acute bacterial conjunctivitis, likely Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pneumoniae etiology.

**Management Plan:**
1. Broad-spectrum topical antibiotic: Moxifloxacin 0.5% ophthalmic solution qid for 7 days, or Gentamicin 0.3% qid for 7 days
2. Warm compresses for eyelid hygiene and comfort
3. Patient education on infection control: frequent handwashing, avoid touching eyes, separate towels and linens
4. Work exclusion until 24-48 hours after antibiotic initiation with improvement
5. Follow-up in 5-7 days; sooner if worsening despite treatment
6. Culture not routinely indicated unless treatment failure, contact lens-related, or gonococcal suspicion

**Key Learning Points:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis presents with mucopurulent discharge and matting of eyelids
- Visual acuity should remain normal; decreased vision indicates more serious pathology
- Preauricular lymphadenopathy is typically absent (helps distinguish from viral etiology)
- Hyperacute presentation with copious discharge suggests Neisseria gonorrhoeae - urgent ophthalmology referral required
- Contact lens wearers with bacterial conjunctivitis need evaluation for microbial keratitis

### Case Study 2: Herpes Simplex Keratitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 42-year-old female office worker presents with a 4-day history of right eye redness, pain, photophobia, and blurred vision. She describes the pain as a "deep ache" with discomfort on eye movement. She reports two previous similar episodes, the most recent 8 months ago. She has a history of recurrent oral cold sores but is otherwise healthy with no systemic medications.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/18 OD, 6/6 OS
- External examination: Right eyelid mild edema, circumlimbal conjunctival injection
- Corneal sensation: Reduced OD (compared to OS using cotton wisp)
- Slit lamp examination OD: Dendritic epithelial defect with terminal bulbs visible on fluorescein staining, underlying mild stromal edema, no ulcer
- Anterior chamber: +1 cells, trace flare
- Intraocular pressure: 17 mmHg OD, 16 mmHg OS
- Fundus: Normal OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Herpes simplex virus (HSV) keratitis (dendritic pattern pathognomonic)
2. Herpes zoster ophthalmicus (typically dermatomal skin involvement)
3. Acanthamoeba keratitis (ring infiltrate, severe pain, contact lens history)
4. Bacterial keratitis (usually larger ulcer, more purulent)

**Diagnosis:**
Recurrent HSV epithelial keratitis with mild anterior chamber reaction.

**Management Plan:**
1. Topical antiviral therapy: Acyclovir 3% ophthalmic ointment 5 times daily OR Ganciclovir 0.15% gel 5 times daily
2. Consider oral antiviral for severe cases: Acyclovir 400mg 5 times daily for 7-10 days
3. Cycloplegic for comfort: Cyclopentolate 1% bid
4. Topical corticosteroids CONTRAINDICATED in active epithelial disease (may cause geographic ulcer)
5. Urgent ophthalmology referral for monitoring and management
6. Patient education: recognize prodromal symptoms (tingling, foreign body sensation), seek early treatment
7. Consider prophylactic oral antiviral (acyclovir 400mg bid) if frequent recurrences (>2 episodes/year)

**Key Learning Points:**
- Dendritic ulcer with terminal bulbs is pathognomonic for HSV keratitis
- Reduced corneal sensation is a key diagnostic finding
- Steroids are contraindicated in active epithelial HSV (can worsen infection)
- Recurrence rate is 25% within 1 year, 60% within 5 years
- Always document visual acuity - any decrease warrants urgent referral
- HSV keratitis can lead to stromal keratitis, scarring, and vision loss

### Case Study 3: Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

**Patient Presentation:**
A 65-year-old Asian female presents at 10 PM with sudden onset of severe right eye pain, headache, nausea, and vomiting that began approximately 6 hours ago. She reports blurred vision and seeing "rainbow halos around lights." She had a dilated fundus examination 3 days prior for a routine check-up. She has hyperopia (+3.00 DS spectacles) and no significant medical history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/60 OD (pinhole no improvement), 6/9 OS
- External examination: Right eye severely injected with conjunctival chemosis, right eyelid edema, lacrimation
- Cornea OD: Steamy, edematous with microcystic epithelial edema
- Anterior chamber OD: Extremely shallow, +2 flare
- Pupil OD: Mid-dilated (5-6mm), fixed, non-reactive, mild iris bombe
- Intraocular pressure: 62 mmHg OD, 18 mmHg OS
- Gonioscopy OS: Shallow anterior chamber, narrow angle (Shaffer grade 0-1)
- Left eye examination: Quiet, shallow anterior chamber

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (APACG) - classic presentation
2. Phacomorphic angle closure (lens-induced)
3. Pupillary block secondary angle closure
4. Neovascular glaucoma (check for iris neovascularization)
5. Uveitic glaucoma

**Diagnosis:**
Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (APACG) - ophthalmic emergency.

**Management Plan:**
1. IMMEDIATE ophthalmology referral - sight-threatening emergency
2. Initial medical therapy (initiate while arranging transfer):
   - IV Acetazolamide 500mg (if no sulfonamide allergy)
   - Topical beta-blocker (Timolol 0.5%) - reduce aqueous production
   - Topical alpha-agonist (Apraclonidine 1% or Brimonidine 0.15%)
   - Topical steroid (Prednisolone acetate 1%) - reduce inflammation
   - Oral hyperosmotic agent (Glycerol or Mannitol IV) if needed
3. Pilocarpine 2% AFTER IOP reduced to <40 mmHg (ineffective at high IOP due to iris ischemia)
4. Definitive treatment: Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) once cornea clears
5. Prophylactic LPI in fellow eye within days
6. Long-term follow-up: Monitor for chronic angle closure, cataract progression

**Key Learning Points:**
- APACG is an ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate intervention to prevent permanent vision loss
- Classic triad: severe ocular pain, blurred vision with halos, elevated IOP
- Risk factors: hyperopia, advanced age, Asian ethnicity, shallow anterior chamber
- Pilocarpine is ineffective at very high IOP due to iris sphincter ischemia
- Provoking factors: mydriatic drops, dim lighting, reading in supine position
- Fellow eye is at high risk and requires prophylactic treatment

## SECTION 2: VISION LOSS CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 4: Central Retinal Vein Occlusion

**Patient Presentation:**
A 62-year-old male with hypertension (poorly controlled) and type 2 diabetes mellitus presents with sudden, painless vision loss in his left eye noticed upon awakening 4 days ago. He describes his vision as "dim" with a "dark shadow" covering most of his visual field. His last HbA1c was 8.4% and blood pressure at recent check was 158/96 mmHg. He takes oral hypoglycemics and antihypertensives.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/60 OS
- Pupils: Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) present OS
- Intraocular pressure: 16 mmHg OU
- Anterior segment: Normal OU, no iris neovascularization
- Fundus examination OS: Dilated and tortuous retinal veins in all quadrants, extensive flame-shaped and dot-blot hemorrhages throughout retina ("blood and thunder" appearance), cotton wool spots, optic disc edema, macular edema
- Fundus examination OD: Mild arteriolar narrowing, otherwise normal

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) - most likely given diffuse hemorrhages
2. Ocular ischemic syndrome (typically more rapid onset, painful, carotid disease)
3. Diabetic retinopathy (usually gradual, bilateral, characteristic features)
4. Papillophlebitis (young healthy patients, better prognosis)

**Diagnosis:**
Central retinal vein occlusion with macular edema, likely non-ischemic type (pending fluorescein angiography).

**Management Plan:**
1. Urgent ophthalmology referral within 24-48 hours
2. Systemic evaluation: Blood pressure optimization (target <140/90), glycemic control (HbA1c target <7%), lipid profile, cardiac evaluation
3. Collaboration with primary care physician for vascular risk factor management
4. Ophthalmology management may include:
   - Optical coherence tomography (OCT) for macular edema assessment
   - Fluorescein angiography to distinguish ischemic vs non-ischemic CRVO
   - Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections (ranibizumab, aflibercept) or steroid for macular edema
   - Monitor monthly for iris neovascularization (neovascular glaucoma risk)
   - Panretinal photocoagulation if neovascularization develops
5. Follow-up: Monthly initially, monitor for conversion to ischemic CRVO

**Key Learning Points:**
- CRVO presents with sudden, painless vision loss - "blood and thunder" fundus appearance is classic
- Systemic conditions (hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia) are major risk factors
- Distinguish ischemic from non-ischemic CRVO (prognosis and management differ significantly)
- Ischemic CRVO: >10 disc areas of capillary non-perfusion on FA, higher neovascularization risk
- Monitor for neovascular complications: iris, angle, retinal neovascularization
- Young patients with CRVO should be evaluated for hypercoagulable states

### Case Study 5: Optic Neuritis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 26-year-old female marketing executive presents with a 5-day history of progressive vision loss in her right eye accompanied by pain that worsens with eye movement. She describes colors appearing "washed out" or "desaturated" and notices a central blur affecting her reading. She reports a previous episode of "numbness and tingling" in her legs 8 months ago that resolved without treatment. She is otherwise healthy.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/24 OD, 6/6 OS
- Color vision: Severely reduced OD (Ishihara: 4/21 plates correct), normal OS
- Pupils: Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) present OD
- Visual fields: Central scotoma OD
- Extraocular movements: Pain on right eye movement in all directions
- Fundus examination: Mild optic disc hyperemia OD, no disc edema (consistent with retrobulbar optic neuritis), normal OS
- Intraocular pressure: 15 mmHg OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Optic neuritis (typical presentation - young female, pain with eye movement, central scotoma)
2. Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION) - typically older patients, sudden onset, altitudinal defect
3. Compressive optic neuropathy (requires imaging to rule out)
4. Infiltrative optic neuropathy (sarcoidosis, lymphoma)

**Diagnosis:**
Typical optic neuritis, highly suspicious for multiple sclerosis (MS) given history of previous neurological symptoms.

**Management Plan:**
1. Urgent ophthalmology and neurology referral
2. MRI brain and orbits with gadolinium enhancement:
   - Assess for demyelinating lesions
   - Rule out compressive lesions
   - Determine MS risk (periventricular white matter lesions)
3. High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1g daily for 3 days) followed by oral prednisone taper:
   - Speeds visual recovery but does not affect final visual outcome
   - May delay development of MS in short term
4. Avoid oral prednisone alone (increased recurrence rate per ONTT)
5. Patient education: Visual recovery expected over weeks to months (75% recover to 6/9 or better)
6. Lumbar puncture if MRI inconclusive (oligoclonal bands in CSF)
7. Long-term: Disease-modifying therapy if MS confirmed

**Key Learning Points:**
- Optic neuritis typically affects young women (peak incidence 20-40 years)
- Pain with eye movement is present in >90% of cases
- Retrobulbar optic neuritis has normal fundus appearance initially
- Strong association with multiple sclerosis (50% develop MS within 15 years)
- Visual prognosis is generally good, but color vision and contrast sensitivity may remain impaired
- High-dose IV steroids speed recovery but don't improve final outcome

### Case Study 6: Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment

**Patient Presentation:**
A 58-year-old myopic male accountant presents with a 10-day history of new floaters and flashing lights in his left eye, followed by a "curtain-like shadow" progressing from the superior temporal visual field over the past 3 days. He underwent uncomplicated cataract surgery 8 months ago. He denies trauma. His current spectacle prescription is -5.50 DS OU.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/18 OS (with current correction)
- Intraocular pressure: 16 mmHg OD, 8 mmHg OS (reduced IOP suggestive of RD)
- Anterior segment: Posterior chamber IOL well-positioned OS, mild anterior chamber cells OS
- Fundus examination OS: Bullous superior temporal retinal detachment extending to macula, visible horseshoe tear at 10 o'clock, subretinal fluid extending to fovea, retina appears corrugated
- Fundus examination OD: Peripheral lattice degeneration at 2 and 7 o'clock, no tears

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (confirmed - tear identified)
2. Tractional retinal detachment (diabetes, proliferative retinopathy)
3. Exudative retinal detachment (inflammatory, neoplastic)
4. Retinoschisis (no true detachment, split retinal layers)

**Diagnosis:**
Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment, macula-involving, secondary to horseshoe retinal tear.

**Management Plan:**
1. URGENT ophthalmology referral within 24 hours (macula-involving RD)
2. Surgical repair required - timing depends on macula status:
   - Macula-on RD: Urgent (within 24-48 hours)
   - Macula-off RD: Less urgent but within days to weeks
3. Surgical options:
   - Pars plana vitrectomy (preferred for macula-involving, posterior tears)
   - Scleral buckle (anterior tears, younger patients)
   - Combination of both
   - Pneumatic retinopexy (specific criteria: single superior tear, compliant patient)
4. Preoperative: Limited activity, avoid strenuous movements, head positioning as directed
5. Postoperative: Positioning requirements, activity restrictions for 2-4 weeks
6. Prophylactic laser retinopexy to lattice degeneration OD
7. Patient education regarding symptoms of recurrent RD, PVD progression

**Key Learning Points:**
- Myopia and previous intraocular surgery are major risk factors for rhegmatogenous RD
- Classic symptom progression: floaters → photopsia → visual field defect (curtain)
- Lower IOP compared to fellow eye may suggest subretinal fluid
- Macula-involving RD has worse visual prognosis than macula-sparing
- Fellow eye requires thorough peripheral examination and possible prophylaxis
- Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD) precedes most rhegmatogenous RDs

## SECTION 3: REFRACTION CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 7: High Anisometropic Amblyopia

**Patient Presentation:**
A 22-year-old female university student presents for her first comprehensive eye examination in 6 years. She reports struggling with distance vision in her right eye since childhood and was told she had "lazy eye" with limited treatment options. She experiences eye strain and headaches after prolonged computer use. She has no significant medical or family history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (unaided): 6/120 OD, 6/12 OS
- Visual acuity (pinhole): 6/30 OD, 6/6 OS
- Cover test: Exotropia 12 prism diopters at distance (comitant)
- Refraction:
  - OD: -7.50/-2.25 x 180 → 6/24 (no improvement with further correction)
  - OS: -1.25/-0.50 x 90 → 6/6
- Anisometropia: 6.25D difference
- Binocular vision: Suppression OD on Worth 4-dot at distance
- Fundus examination: Myopic fundus changes OD (tilted disc, peripapillary atrophy, lacquer cracks), normal OS

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Anisometropic amblyopia (primary cause - significant anisometropia)
2. Strabismic amblyopia (concurrent exotropia present)
3. Mixed mechanism amblyopia (combination of anisometropia and strabismus)
4. Organic pathology (rule out macular or optic nerve pathology)

**Diagnosis:**
Mixed mechanism amblyopia (anisometropic + strabismic) in right eye, high myopia with anisometropia.

**Management Plan:**
1. Full optical correction with high-index lenses (reduce weight and edge thickness)
2. Anti-reflective coating for computer work and cosmesis
3. Discuss contact lens option (better image quality and cosmesis in high anisometropia)
4. Vision therapy referral: Discuss potential for improvement at adult age (limited but possible)
5. Patient education: Amblyopia treatment most effective before age 7-8, but some adults show improvement with compliance
6. Annual dilated fundus examination for myopic complications (RD, myopic macular degeneration, glaucoma)
7. Discuss refractive surgery options in future (will correct refractive error but not amblyopia)
8. Trial of perceptual learning or dichoptic training if motivated

**Key Learning Points:**
- Anisometropia >2D in children can cause amblyopia
- Mixed mechanism amblyopia (anisometropic + strabismic) is common
- Adult amblyopia treatment has limited efficacy but some improvement possible with new therapies
- High myopia requires regular monitoring for complications (retinal detachment, myopic macular degeneration)
- Contact lenses provide better visual acuity than spectacles in high anisometropia (less aniseikonia)

### Case Study 8: Presbyopia with Accommodative Insufficiency

**Patient Presentation:**
A 50-year-old female schoolteacher presents with increasing difficulty reading, particularly after work in the evenings. She has worn distance glasses since age 15 for myopia. She purchased over-the-counter reading glasses (+1.50) 18 months ago but finds them increasingly inadequate. She reports eye fatigue, headaches, and blurred near vision after 15-20 minutes of reading. Her working distance for reading is 35-40cm.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (with current distance glasses):
  - Distance: 6/6 OD, 6/6 OS
  - Near: N8 at 40cm initially, blurring after sustained reading
- Current distance prescription: -3.50 DS OU
- Cover test: Orthophoric at distance and near
- Amplitude of accommodation: 2.50D OU (expected for age 50: 2.50-3.00D)
- Near point of convergence: 7cm (normal)
- Cycloplegic refraction: -3.25 DS OU
- Intraocular pressure: 15 mmHg OU
- Fundus: Normal OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Presbyopia with accommodative insufficiency (age-appropriate changes)
2. Uncorrected hyperopia (ruled out by cycloplegic refraction)
3. Convergence insufficiency (NPC normal, ruled out)
4. Systemic causes (diabetes, medications, thyroid dysfunction - consider if symptoms disproportionate)

**Diagnosis:**
Presbyopia with accommodative insufficiency in myopic patient.

**Management Plan:**
1. Discuss presbyopic correction options:
   - Bifocal spectacles: Distance -3.50 DS with +2.00 add
   - Progressive addition lenses (PAL): Distance -3.50 DS with +2.00 add (preferred for active professional)
   - Contact lenses: Multifocal or modified monovision option
2. Near vision assessment at working distance (teacher may need intermediate add for computer)
3. Consider occupational progressive lenses for classroom use
4. Recommend 20-20-20 rule for digital eye strain prevention
5. Ensure adequate lighting for reading tasks
6. Follow-up in 6-12 months (presbyopia progresses in early stages)
7. Presbyopic add typically increases by +0.50D every 2-3 years until age 60, then plateaus

**Key Learning Points:**
- Presbyopia typically begins around age 40-45, complete by age 60
- Accommodative amplitude can be measured using push-up method or minus lens method
- Expected amplitude decreases approximately 0.25D per year from age 40
- Progressive lenses preferred for active presbyopes requiring intermediate vision
- Consider occupational needs when selecting near addition power
- Myopic patients may simply remove glasses for near tasks (low myopes)

### Case Study 9: Keratoconus with Irregular Astigmatism

**Patient Presentation:**
A 32-year-old male IT professional presents with complaints of progressively worsening vision over 2 years. He reports multiple unsuccessful spectacle prescription changes and persistent blurred vision. He has a history of allergic conjunctivitis and admits to frequent eye rubbing, especially after removing contact lenses at night. He has worn soft toric contact lenses for 5 years. No family history of keratoconus.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (unaided): 6/24 OD, 6/30 OS
- Visual acuity (with best spectacle correction): 6/9 OD, 6/12 OS
- Refraction:
  - OD: -2.50/-4.00 x 120 → 6/9 (irregular, poorly correctable)
  - OS: -2.00/-4.50 x 65 → 6/12 (irregular, poorly correctable)
- Keratometry:
  - OD: 45.50 @ 30° / 49.00 @ 120° (3.50D K difference, irregular mires)
  - OS: 46.00 @ 150° / 50.50 @ 60° (4.50D K difference, irregular mires)
- Corneal topography: Inferior steepening pattern, asymmetric bow-tie, consistent with keratoconus
- Slit lamp examination: Inferior corneal thinning OU, Vogt's striae visible OS, Fleischer ring partial OU, no scarring
- Munson's sign: Present OS on downgaze

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Keratoconus (confirmed by topography and clinical signs)
2. Pellucid marginal degeneration (inferior thinning pattern differs)
3. Post-refractive surgery ectasia (no history of refractive surgery)
4. Contact lens-induced corneal warpage (would resolve with lens discontinuation)

**Diagnosis:**
Moderate keratoconus, bilateral, more advanced OS (Stage 2-3 based on Amsler-Krumeich classification).

**Management Plan:**
1. Immediate discontinuation of eye rubbing (critical to prevent progression)
2. Rigid gas permeable (RGP) contact lens fitting - gold standard for visual correction
3. Consider corneal cross-linking (CXL) to halt progression:
   - Standard epithelium-off CXL
   - Accelerated protocols available
   - Earlier intervention improves outcomes
4. Scleral lens option if RGP intolerance develops
5. Piggyback system (soft lens under RGP) for comfort
6. Regular monitoring with topography every 6 months
7. Genetic counseling: Family screening recommended
8. Future surgical options if progression to advanced stage:
   - Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK)
   - Penetrating keratoplasty (PK)

**Key Learning Points:**
- Irregular astigmatism that cannot be fully corrected with spectacles suggests corneal pathology
- Keratoconus typically presents in young adults (teens to 30s) with progressive myopic astigmatism
- Eye rubbing is a modifiable risk factor and must be avoided
- Corneal topography is essential for diagnosis and monitoring
- RGP lenses provide best visual acuity by creating a regular refractive surface
- Corneal cross-linking can halt progression - early diagnosis is crucial
- Association with atopy, connective tissue disorders, and Down syndrome

## SECTION 4: CONTACT LENS CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 10: Contact Lens-Related Microbial Keratitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 24-year-old female university student and soft contact lens wearer presents with a 2-day history of left eye pain, redness, photophobia, and decreased vision. She admits to sleeping in her monthly silicone hydrogel lenses "a few times per week" and occasionally swimming while wearing lenses. She last replaced her lenses 6 weeks ago. She ran out of solution and used tap water to store her lenses 5 days ago.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/30 OS (with current correction)
- External examination: Severe conjunctival injection OS, moderate eyelid edema, lacrimation
- Slit lamp examination OS:
  - Corneal epithelial defect: 2.5mm diameter, paracentral
  - Stromal infiltrate: Dense, white, underlying the epithelial defect
  - Surrounding corneal edema with folds in Descemet's membrane
  - Anterior chamber: +2 cells, 1.5mm hypopyon
  - No satellite lesions, no dendritic pattern
- Corneal sensation: Intact (helps distinguish from HSV)
- Intraocular pressure: 18 mmHg OS (elevated due to inflammation)

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Bacterial keratitis (most likely given hypopyon, contact lens history, acute presentation)
2. Fungal keratitis (consider if culture negative or poor response; more common with organic matter trauma)
3. Acanthamoeba keratitis (severe pain disproportionate to findings, ring infiltrate)
4. Herpes simplex keratitis (dendritic pattern typically seen; reduced sensation)

**Diagnosis:**
Severe contact lens-related microbial keratitis (suspected bacterial) with hypopyon.

**Management Plan:**
1. IMMEDIATE ophthalmology referral - sight-threatening emergency
2. Discontinue ALL contact lens wear immediately
3. Corneal scraping for:
   - Gram stain, KOH preparation
   - Culture on blood agar, chocolate agar, Sabouraud agar
   - Sensitivity testing
4. Intensive topical antibiotics (after scraping):
   - Fluoroquinolone monotherapy (Moxifloxacin 0.5%) hourly for first 24-48 hours
   - OR fortified antibiotics (Cefazolin 5% + Gentamicin 1.4%) if severe
5. Cycloplegic for comfort (Cyclopentolate 1% bid)
6. Consider topical steroid ONLY after confirmed improvement (ophthalmology decision)
7. Daily follow-up initially until improvement confirmed
8. Patient education on proper lens care and risk factors for recurrence
9. Consider delaying re-fitting until complete resolution (minimum 2-4 weeks)

**Key Learning Points:**
- Contact lens-related microbial keratitis is an ophthalmic emergency
- Risk factors: extended overnight wear, poor hygiene, topping off solution, swimming in lenses, tap water exposure
- Hypopyon indicates severe inflammation/infection requiring aggressive treatment
- Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the most common organism in contact lens-related keratitis
- Culture-guided therapy preferred; empirical therapy initiated while awaiting results
- Patient education crucial for prevention of recurrence

### Case Study 11: Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 27-year-old male accountant presents with a 4-week history of increasing discomfort, itching, and mucus discharge in both eyes. He reports his monthly silicone hydrogel lenses feel increasingly uncomfortable and seem to "move around" excessively. He has worn contact lenses for 6 years, typically 12-14 hours per day. He uses multipurpose solution and replaces lenses monthly. He has a history of seasonal allergies.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OU with current contact lenses
- External examination: Mild bilateral conjunctival injection, mucoid discharge
- Slit lamp examination:
  - Eversion of upper lids: Giant papillae (>1mm diameter) on tarsal conjunctiva, cobblestone appearance, most prominent centrally
  - Bulbar conjunctiva: Mild chemosis, papillary reaction
  - Cornea: Clear, no epithelial defects, no superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis
  - Lens fit: Excessive movement and decentration (altered by papillae)
- No preauricular lymphadenopathy

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC) secondary to contact lens wear (confirmed)
2. Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (typically younger patients, seasonal, more severe)
3. Atopic keratoconjunctivitis (atopic dermatitis history, more chronic)
4. Bacterial conjunctivitis (would have mucopurulent discharge, not itching)

**Diagnosis:**
Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC) secondary to soft contact lens wear.

**Management Plan:**
1. Temporary discontinuation of contact lenses for 2-4 weeks
2. Switch to daily disposable lenses upon return (eliminates protein deposit buildup)
3. Topical therapy:
   - Mast cell stabilizer/antihistamine combination (Olopatadine 0.1% bid or Ketotifen bid)
   - Mild topical steroid (Fluorometholone 0.1% qid for 1-2 weeks) for severe cases
4. Cold compresses for symptomatic relief
5. Reduce wearing time to 8-10 hours upon resumption
6. Follow-up in 2-4 weeks to assess improvement before re-fitting
7. Consider rigid gas permeable lenses if GPC recurs despite changes
8. Consider one-day replacement schedule permanently

**Key Learning Points:**
- GPC is a common contact lens complication (mechanical irritation + immune response to deposits)
- Cobblestone papillae on upper tarsal conjunctiva are diagnostic
- Risk factors: extended wear time, protein deposits, history of atopy
- Daily disposable lenses significantly reduce GPC risk by eliminating deposit buildup
- Mast cell stabilizers help prevent recurrence when used prophylactically
- Lens fit parameters may change as papillae resolve - re-evaluate after treatment

### Case Study 12: Tight Lens Syndrome

**Patient Presentation:**
A 36-year-old female presents with a 1-day history of right eye discomfort and blurred vision that began after wearing her new RGP lenses for 4 hours. She was recently refitted from soft toric lenses to RGPs due to high astigmatism (-3.50D cyl). She reports the right lens feels "stuck" to her eye and she had difficulty removing it. The left lens is comfortable.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (with RGP): 6/9 OD, 6/6 OS
- External examination: Moderate conjunctival injection OD, lacrimation
- Slit lamp examination OD:
  - Cornea: Central superficial punctate keratitis, mild stromal edema
  - Lens fit assessment:
    - No post-lens tear film movement on blink
    - Lens centered but completely immobile
    - Impression ring visible after lens removal
  - After lens removal: Corneal indentation ring visible, epithelial staining in ring pattern
- Fluorescein pattern: Central pooling, no lens movement, bearing on mid-peripheral cornea
- Left lens: Normal fit with 0.5-1.0mm movement on blink

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Tight lens syndrome (confirmed - steep base curve fitting)
2. Incorrect lens parameters (base curve, diameter)
3. Lens material incompatibility
4. Corneal edema from other causes

**Diagnosis:**
Tight contact lens syndrome secondary to steep RGP base curve fitting.

**Management Plan:**
1. Remove lens immediately and assess corneal integrity
2. Temporary discontinuation of RGP wear OD (2-3 days for corneal recovery)
3. Topical lubricants and prophylactic antibiotic if epithelial defect present
4. Refit with flatter base curve (increase by 0.05-0.10mm)
5. Verify lens parameters with manufacturer (possible dispensing error)
6. Re-evaluate fit to ensure proper movement (0.5-1.0mm on blink)
7. Patient education on signs of tight lens and importance of removal if uncomfortable
8. Training session for proper lens insertion, removal, and fit assessment
9. Follow-up in 1 week to verify comfortable fit

**Key Learning Points:**
- Tight lens syndrome results from steep base curve or small diameter fitting
- Classic signs: absent lens movement, corneal indentation, discomfort, blurred vision
- RGP lenses should move 0.5-1.0mm on blink to ensure tear exchange
- Prolonged tight lens wear can cause corneal hypoxia and edema
- Corneal indentation ring resolves within hours after lens removal
- Refitting with flatter base curve is the corrective action
- Patient education on lens fit assessment and warning signs is essential

## SECTION 5: PEDIATRIC CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 13: Pediatric Amblyopia with Esotropia

**Patient Presentation:**
A 4-year-old boy is brought by his parents for an eye examination before starting kindergarten. They have noticed he occasionally closes his left eye when watching television and seems to have an "inward turn" of the right eye when tired. He was born full-term with no perinatal complications. Developmental milestones are normal. His father had "lazy eye" as a child.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (Allen pictures):
  - OD: 6/30 (single optotype), no improvement with pinhole
  - OS: 6/6 (single optotype)
- Cover test: Esotropia 30 prism diopters at distance, 28 prism diopters at near (comitant)
- Cycloplegic refraction (Cyclopentolate 1%):
  - OD: +6.00 DS → 6/30 (no improvement)
  - OS: +1.00 DS → 6/6
- Anisometropia: 5.00D difference
- Stereopsis: Absent (suppression on Worth 4-dot, no Titmus fly response)
- Fundus examination: Normal OU, no structural abnormality
- Hirschberg test: Right eye deviation confirmed

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Accommodative esotropia with anisometropic amblyopia (most likely - high hyperopia)
2. Congenital esotropia (presents earlier, usually <6 months)
3. Partial accommodative esotropia
4. Sensory esotropia (rule out structural pathology)

**Diagnosis:**
Accommodative esotropia with anisometropic amblyopia in right eye (mixed mechanism).

**Management Plan:**
1. Full-time spectacle correction with full cycloplegic prescription:
   - OD: +6.00 DS
   - OS: +1.00 DS
   - Consider equalizing glasses if significant aniseikonia
2. Re-evaluate deviation after 4-6 weeks of full-time wear
3. Occlusion therapy for amblyopia:
   - Patch left eye 2-4 hours daily
   - Atropine penalization (1% weekly) as alternative if patching fails
4. Monitor visual acuity every 4-6 weeks initially
5. If residual esotropia persists after correction: Consider bifocals or surgery
6. Vision therapy for binocular function development after acuity equalizes
7. Family education on critical importance of compliance (amblyopia treatment window)
8. Long-term monitoring: May reduce hyperopia over years, monitor for recurrence

**Key Learning Points:**
- Amblyopia treatment most effective before age 7 (critical period of visual development)
- Anisometropia >1.5-2.0D in children requires monitoring for amblyopia development
- Esotropia with significant hyperopia suggests accommodative component
- Full cycloplegic correction is essential for accommodative esotropia management
- Compliance with patching is the key determinant of successful treatment
- Regular follow-up essential to monitor visual acuity improvement and alignment

### Case Study 14: Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

**Patient Presentation:**
A 10-month-old infant is brought by parents with a 7-month history of persistent tearing and discharge from the right eye. The eye has been "watery" since birth with worsening mucoid discharge, especially in the mornings. There is no redness, swelling, or photophobia. The parents have performed massage as directed by their pediatrician for the past 3 months with minimal improvement. The infant was born full-term via normal vaginal delivery.

**Clinical Findings:**
- External examination:
  - Right eye: Increased tear lake, mucoid discharge, no conjunctival injection
  - Left eye: Normal
- Eyelids: Normal position and anatomy, patent puncta bilaterally
- Crigler massage: Reflux of mucopurulent material from right upper punctum on pressure over lacrimal sac
- Visual behavior: Fixation and following age-appropriate bilaterally
- Red reflex: Present, equal, and clear bilaterally
- Corneas: Clear, normal diameter (no enlargement suggesting congenital glaucoma)
- No photophobia

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (CNLDO) (confirmed - classic presentation)
2. Congenital glaucoma (ruled out - no photophobia, corneal clarity, normal eye size)
3. Dacryocystocele (bluish swelling at medial canthus - not present)
4. Lid abnormalities (entropion, ectropion - examination normal)

**Diagnosis:**
Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (membranous obstruction at valve of Hasner), right eye.

**Management Plan:**
1. Continue conservative management with Crigler massage:
   - 10 strokes, 2-3 times daily
   - Proper technique: Pressure from medial canthus downward along nose
2. Topical antibiotic (fluoroquinolone) for acute mucopurulent exacerbation
3. Expected spontaneous resolution: 90% by 12 months of age
4. If no resolution by 12-13 months: Refer to pediatric ophthalmology for probing under general anesthesia
5. Probing success rate: >90% when performed at appropriate age
6. Alternative options for failed probing: Balloon dacryoplasty, nasolacrimal stent
7. Monitor for signs of dacryocystitis (fever, erythema, swelling over lacrimal sac - requires urgent referral)
8. Reassure parents about benign nature and high resolution rate

**Key Learning Points:**
- CNLDO affects approximately 6% of newborns (most common cause of infant epiphora)
- Membranous obstruction at valve of Hasner is the typical cause
- 90% resolve spontaneously by 12 months with conservative management
- Massage technique: Proximal to distal along lacrimal sac, 10 strokes, 2-3 times daily
- Critical to distinguish from congenital glaucoma (triad: epiphora, photophobia, blepharospasm; plus corneal edema, enlarged eye)
- Probing success rate is excellent (>90%) when performed at 12-13 months
- Dacryocystitis requires urgent systemic antibiotics and possible surgical drainage

### Case Study 15: Retinopathy of Prematurity Screening Case

**Patient Presentation:**
A 5-week-old female infant, born at 26 weeks gestational age with birth weight of 780 grams, is due for retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) screening. She has been receiving oxygen therapy in the NICU for respiratory distress syndrome and has a history of intraventricular hemorrhage (Grade II). The neonatology team requests ROP examination at 31 weeks postmenstrual age.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Birth history: 26 weeks GA, 780g birth weight, oxygen therapy for 4 weeks
- Current postmenstrual age: 31 weeks
- Current weight: 1.2 kg
- Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy with 28D lens and scleral depression:
  - Right eye:
    - Zone I: Vessels extend to near nasal ora serrata
    - Stage 3: Extraretinal fibrovascular proliferation with ridge
    - Plus disease: Prominent dilation and tortuosity of posterior pole vessels
    - Extent: 6 clock hours continuous involvement
  - Left eye:
    - Zone I: Similar vessel extension
    - Stage 2: Ridge with small tufts of neovascularization
    - Plus disease: Mild posterior pole vessel changes
    - Extent: 4 clock hours involvement

**Classification (ICROP):**
- Right eye: Zone I, Stage 3 ROP with plus disease (Type 1 ROP - treatment-requiring)
- Left eye: Zone I, Stage 2 ROP with plus disease (Type 1 ROP - treatment-requiring)

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) - confirmed
2. Familial exudative vitreoretinopathy (FEVR) - consider family history, typically full-term infants
3. Persistent fetal vasculature (PFV) - different clinical appearance

**Diagnosis:**
Retinopathy of prematurity, bilateral, Type 1 (treatment-requiring) per ET-ROP criteria.

**Management Plan:**
1. URGENT pediatric ophthalmology/vitreoretinal surgery referral - treatment within 72 hours
2. Treatment indication: Type 1 ROP per ET-ROP study criteria
   - Zone I, any stage with plus disease
   - Zone I, Stage 3 without plus disease
   - Zone II, Stage 2-3 with plus disease
3. Treatment options:
   - Laser photocoagulation (gold standard, destroys peripheral avascular retina)
   - Intravitreal anti-VEGF injection (Bevacizumab/Ranibizumab) - increasingly used for Zone I disease
4. Post-treatment monitoring every 1-2 weeks until retina fully vascularized
5. Long-term follow-up required for:
   - High myopia (very common after ROP)
   - Strabismus
   - Amblyopia
   - Late retinal detachment (can occur years later)
6. Parent counseling regarding prognosis, treatment risks, and importance of follow-up
7. Coordination with neonatology for oxygen management

**Key Learning Points:**
- ROP screening criteria: <30 weeks gestational age OR <1500g birth weight (and selected infants 1500-2000g with unstable clinical course)
- Classification uses: Zone (location - I central, II mid-peripheral, III peripheral), Stage (severity 1-5), Plus disease (vascular activity)
- Type 1 ROP requires treatment within 72 hours per ET-ROP criteria
- Plus disease (dilation and tortuosity of posterior pole vessels) is critical for treatment decisions
- Zone I disease has worse prognosis than Zone II/III
- Laser therapy reduces unfavorable outcomes by approximately 50%
- Anti-VEGF therapy is increasingly used, especially for Zone I disease, but requires long-term monitoring for reactivation

## Summary and Key Examination Points

### Red Eye Clinical Pearls
1. Always measure visual acuity before any other examination
2. Determine if condition is sight-threatening (reduced vision, severe pain, corneal involvement)
3. Check pupil responses and intraocular pressure for all red eye presentations
4. Slit lamp examination essential for accurate diagnosis
5. Know emergency referral criteria: keratitis, acute angle closure, endophthalmitis

### Vision Loss Assessment Framework
1. Establish onset: sudden vs gradual (sudden is more concerning)
2. Determine laterality: unilateral vs bilateral
3. Assess for pain: painful vs painless vision loss
4. Check for RAPD - indicates optic nerve or extensive retinal pathology
5. Urgent referral for: retinal detachment, CRAO/CRVO, optic neuritis, AION

### Refraction Problem-Solving Approach
1. Perform cycloplegic refraction in children, young hyperopes, and suspected latent hyperopia
2. Suspect keratoconus if irregular astigmatism cannot be corrected with spectacles
3. Address anisometropia early to prevent amblyopia in children
4. Consider working distance and occupational requirements for presbyopia
5. Contact lenses may provide better vision in high refractive errors and irregular astigmatism

### Contact Lens Complications Management
1. Educate patients on warning signs requiring immediate lens removal
2. Microbial keratitis is an emergency requiring urgent ophthalmology referral
3. GPC is managed with lens modification, reduced wear time, and pharmacotherapy
4. Proper lens care and replacement schedule prevents most complications
5. Never use tap water with contact lenses

### Pediatric Considerations
1. Early detection and treatment of amblyopia is critical (best before age 7)
2. Cycloplegic refraction is essential in all pediatric examinations
3. CNLDO often resolves spontaneously by 12 months with conservative management
4. ROP screening protocols must be followed for at-risk premature infants
5. Always consider congenital glaucoma in infants with epiphora (distinguish from CNLDO)
