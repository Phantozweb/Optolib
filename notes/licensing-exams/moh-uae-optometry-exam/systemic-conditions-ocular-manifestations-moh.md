# Systemic Conditions & Ocular Manifestations for MOH UAE Exam

## Introduction

Systemic diseases frequently manifest ocular signs and symptoms that can be detected during comprehensive eye examinations. For optometrists preparing for the Ministry of Health (MOH) UAE licensing examination, understanding the relationship between systemic conditions and ocular health is essential for providing comprehensive patient care. This guide covers four major categories of systemic diseases with significant ocular implications: diabetes mellitus, hypertension, thyroid disorders, and autoimmune diseases. Recognition of ocular manifestations not only aids in diagnosing systemic conditions but also helps monitor disease progression and treatment efficacy.

---

## 1. Diabetes Mellitus

### 1.1 Overview and Epidemiology

Diabetes mellitus represents one of the most significant systemic conditions affecting ocular health worldwide. The International Diabetes Federation estimates that approximately 537 million adults globally are living with diabetes, with projections reaching 783 million by 2045. In the United Arab Emirates, diabetes prevalence is notably high, affecting approximately 15-20% of the adult population. For optometrists, diabetic patients constitute a substantial portion of clinical practice, making knowledge of diabetic ocular manifestations crucial for the MOH examination.

### 1.2 Classification and Pathophysiology

**Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus (T1DM):**
- Autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells
- Typically presents in childhood or adolescence
- Absolute insulin deficiency requiring exogenous insulin
- Associated with other autoimmune conditions (thyroid disease, celiac disease)
- Higher risk of diabetic ketoacidosis

**Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus (T2DM):**
- Insulin resistance with progressive beta cell dysfunction
- Strong association with obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and genetic factors
- May remain undiagnosed for years
- Increasing prevalence in younger populations
- Often managed with oral hypoglycemics, may require insulin

**Gestational Diabetes:**
- Develops during pregnancy
- Increases risk of future T2DM
- Requires careful monitoring to prevent fetal complications
- Temporary but carries long-term implications

### 1.3 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is the most significant ocular complication of diabetes and a leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults.

**Classification of Diabetic Retinopathy:**

**Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR):**

*Mild NPDR:*
- Presence of microaneurysms only
- Microaneurysms appear as small red dots on fundoscopy
- Result from weakening of capillary walls due to pericyte loss
- May remain stable for years
- Annual follow-up recommended

*Moderate NPDR:*
- Microaneurysms plus retinal hemorrhages (dot and blot)
- Hard exudates (yellow, waxy deposits from lipid leakage)
- Cotton wool spots (nerve fiber layer infarcts)
- Some venous caliber changes
- Follow-up every 6-9 months

*Severe NPDR:*
- Extensive intraretinal hemorrhages in all four quadrants
- Venous beading in two or more quadrants
- Prominent intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA)
- Follows the "4-2-1 rule" for diagnosis
- High risk of progression to proliferative stage
- Close monitoring required, consider laser treatment

**Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR):**
- Neovascularization (new blood vessel growth) on disc (NVD) or elsewhere (NVE)
- Neovascularization results from ischemia-driven VEGF production
- High risk of vitreous hemorrhage
- Tractional retinal detachment may occur
- Neovascular glaucoma is a serious complication
- Requires urgent ophthalmology referral for panretinal photocoagulation

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME):**
- Can occur at any stage of DR
- Fluid accumulation in the macula from vascular leakage
- Leading cause of vision loss in diabetic patients
- Classified as center-involved or non-center-involved
- Treatment includes anti-VEGF injections, laser, and steroids

### 1.4 Other Ocular Manifestations of Diabetes

**Refractive Changes:**
- Fluctuating blood glucose levels cause refractive instability
- Hyperglycemia causes lens swelling and myopic shift
- Stabilize blood sugar before prescribing new glasses
- May take 6-8 weeks for refraction to stabilize after glycemic control

**Cataract:**
- Earlier onset compared to non-diabetics
- More rapid progression
- "Snowflake" cataracts in young diabetics
- Surgical considerations: increased risk of postoperative inflammation, PCO, and cystoid macular edema

**Glaucoma:**
- Increased risk of primary open-angle glaucoma
- Neovascular glaucoma in PDR
- Careful monitoring of IOP and optic nerve

**Cranial Nerve Palsies:**
- Microvascular infarction of CN III, IV, or VI
- Typically pupil-sparing in diabetes
- Usually resolves spontaneously in 3-6 months
- Third nerve palsy most common, followed by sixth

**Anterior Segment Changes:**
- Decreased corneal sensitivity (diabetic neuropathy)
- Delayed corneal wound healing
- Increased risk of infection
- Caution with contact lens fitting

### 1.5 Management and Screening Guidelines

**Screening Recommendations:**
- Type 1 diabetes: First eye exam 5 years after diagnosis, then annually
- Type 2 diabetes: At diagnosis, then annually
- Pregnancy: Pre-conception counseling and monitoring each trimester

**Glycemic Control:**
- HbA1c target typically <7% (individualized)
- Tight control reduces DR risk by 40-76%
- Rapid improvement in glycemic control may temporarily worsen DR

**Systemic Factor Management:**
- Blood pressure control (<140/90 mmHg)
- Lipid management (statins for cardiovascular risk reduction)
- Smoking cessation
- Weight management

## 2. Hypertension

### 2.1 Overview and Classification

Hypertension affects approximately 1.28 billion adults worldwide and is a major modifiable risk factor for cardiovascular disease. The UAE has high hypertension prevalence, making this a critical topic for the MOH examination. Blood pressure is classified as normal (<120/80 mmHg), elevated (120-129/<80), Stage 1 hypertension (130-139/80-89), and Stage 2 hypertension (≥140/90).

### 2.2 Hypertensive Retinopathy

Hypertensive retinopathy refers to retinal vascular changes resulting from chronically elevated blood pressure.

**Keith-Wagener-Barker Classification:**

*Grade 1:*
- Mild generalized arteriolar narrowing
- "Copper wiring" appearance of arterioles
- Normal light reflex with mild changes
- Usually asymptomatic
- Indicates mild, long-standing hypertension

*Grade 2:*
- Definite arteriolar narrowing with focal constriction
- "Silver wiring" (more severe arteriolosclerosis)
- Arteriovenous (AV) nicking
- Salus sign, Gunn sign
- Increased risk of stroke and cardiovascular disease

*Grade 3:*
- Retinal hemorrhages (flame-shaped)
- Cotton wool spots (nerve fiber layer infarcts)
- Hard exudates
- Indicates severe hypertension with end-organ damage

*Grade 4:*
- All Grade 3 signs plus optic disc swelling (papilledema)
- Macular star formation (hard exudates in radial pattern)
- Medical emergency (malignant hypertension)
- Requires immediate blood pressure management

### 2.3 Clinical Features of Hypertensive Retinopathy

**Arteriolar Changes:**
- Generalized narrowing reflects vasospasm and sclerosis
- Focal narrowing (arteriolar constriction) is more specific for hypertension
- Wall opacification produces copper and silver wiring
- Broadening of the light reflex

**Arteriovenous Crossing Signs:**
- AV nicking: Artery compresses underlying vein
- Venous banking and tapering at crossing points
- Important sign of chronic hypertension
- Can contribute to branch retinal vein occlusion

**Hemorrhage Patterns:**
- Flame-shaped hemorrhages in nerve fiber layer
- Result from rupture of arterioles
- Direction follows nerve fiber layer anatomy
- Dot and blot hemorrhages may also occur

**Cotton Wool Spots:**
- Fluffy white lesions with irregular borders
- Represent nerve fiber layer infarcts
- Typically located along vascular arcades
- May indicate severe vascular compromise

**Hard Exudates:**
- Yellow, waxy deposits in the outer plexiform layer
- Composed of lipid and lipoprotein
- May form macular star pattern
- Result from vascular leakage

### 2.4 Hypertension-Related Ocular Conditions

**Retinal Vein Occlusion:**
- Hypertension is a major risk factor
- Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) and branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO)
- Presents with sudden vision loss and characteristic fundus appearance
- Hemispheric vein occlusion is a variant of BRVO

**Retinal Artery Occlusion:**
- Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO) is an ophthalmic emergency
- Branch retinal artery occlusion (BRAO)
- Associated with carotid artery disease, embolism
- "Cherry-red spot" appearance in CRAO

**Optic Neuropathy:**
- Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION)
- Increased risk with hypertension
- Sudden vision loss with altitudinal field defect

**Choroidopathy:**
- Acute hypertensive crisis can cause choroidal infarction
- Elschnig spots: Focal choroidal infarcts
- Siegrist streaks: Pigmented streaks along choroidal vessels

### 2.5 Management Principles

**Blood Pressure Control:**
- Target <140/90 for most patients
- More aggressive targets in diabetes or kidney disease
- Lifestyle modifications: diet, exercise, weight loss, sodium restriction
- Antihypertensive medications as needed

**Ophthalmic Management:**
- Monitor for progression of retinopathy
- Treat complications (vein occlusion, macular edema)
- Coordinate with primary care physician
- Patient education on importance of BP control

## 3. Thyroid Disorders

### 3.1 Thyroid Physiology and Dysfunction

Thyroid hormones (T3 and T4) regulate metabolism throughout the body. Thyroid dysfunction can manifest with significant ocular signs, particularly in Graves' disease, making this an essential topic for the MOH examination.

**Hypothyroidism:**
- Decreased thyroid hormone production
- Symptoms: fatigue, weight gain, cold intolerance, depression
- Causes: Hashimoto's thyroiditis, iodine deficiency, post-surgical
- Ocular signs typically less severe than hyperthyroidism

**Hyperthyroidism:**
- Excess thyroid hormone production
- Symptoms: weight loss, heat intolerance, palpitations, anxiety
- Most common cause: Graves' disease (autoimmune)
- Significant ocular manifestations

### 3.2 Thyroid Eye Disease (TED)

Thyroid eye disease, also known as Graves' ophthalmopathy or thyroid-associated ophthalmopathy, is an autoimmune condition affecting the orbit. It occurs most commonly in Graves' disease but can also be seen in Hashimoto's thyroiditis and euthyroid patients.

**Pathophysiology:**
- Autoantibodies against TSH receptor also target orbital fibroblasts
- Orbital fibroblast activation leads to glycosaminoglycan (GAG) production
- GAGs attract water, causing tissue edema
- Inflammatory cell infiltration of extraocular muscles and orbital fat
- Expansion of orbital contents within confined bony orbit

**Clinical Features:**

*Soft Tissue Involvement:*
- Periorbital edema and conjunctival chemosis
- Eyelid erythema and swelling
- Caruncular swelling
- Superior limbic keratoconjunctivitis

*Eyelid Signs:*
- Lid retraction (Dalrymple's sign): Upper lid above limbus
- Lid lag (von Graefe's sign): Upper lid lags behind globe on downgaze
- Infrequent blinking (Stellwag's sign)
- Difficulty everting upper lid (Gifford's sign)

*Proptosis:*
- Anterior displacement of the globe
- Measured with exophthalmometer (Hertel)
- Normal: 12-21 mm, asymmetry <2 mm
- Can be asymmetric between eyes
- May cause exposure keratopathy

*Extraocular Muscle Involvement:*
- Myopathy affects specific muscles in order: inferior > medial > superior > lateral rectus
- Causes restrictive strabismus
- Diplopia, particularly on upgaze initially
- Muscles enlarged on imaging, sparing tendons

*Optic Nerve Compression:*
- Most serious complication of TED
- Apical crowding from enlarged muscles
- Presenting symptoms: decreased vision, color vision loss, RAPD
- Visual field defects may be present
- Urgent treatment required

**NOSPECS Classification:**
- N: No signs or symptoms
- O: Only signs (lid retraction), no symptoms
- S: Soft tissue involvement (chemosis, edema)
- P: Proptosis
- E: Extraocular muscle involvement
- C: Corneal involvement
- S: Sight loss (optic nerve compression)

**Clinical Activity Score (CAS):**
- Used to assess inflammatory activity
- Criteria: Spontaneous retrobulbar pain, pain on eye movement, eyelid erythema, eyelid swelling, conjunctival injection, chemosis, caruncular swelling
- Score ≥3 indicates active disease

### 3.3 Management of Thyroid Eye Disease

**Medical Management:**
- Smoking cessation (critical - smoking worsens TED)
- Selenium supplementation for mild disease
- Artificial tears and lubricants for dry eye symptoms
- Oral selenium 100mcg twice daily for 6 months

**Immunosuppressive Therapy:**
- High-dose intravenous steroids for active, moderate-severe disease
- Teprotumumab (IGF-1R inhibitor) for active TED
- Radiotherapy for selected cases

**Surgical Management:**
- Orbital decompression for optic neuropathy or severe proptosis
- Strabismus surgery for persistent diplopia (after stable)
- Eyelid surgery for retraction and corneal exposure

**Monitoring:**
- Regular assessment of visual acuity and color vision
- Visual fields if optic nerve concern
- CT or MRI of orbits for surgical planning

### 3.4 Hypothyroid Ocular Manifestations

**Periorbital Edema:**
- Myxedematous infiltration of tissues
- Puffiness around eyes
- Non-pitting edema

**Eyelid Changes:**
- Loss of outer third of eyebrows (madarosis)
- Mild ptosis
- Thickened, dry skin

**Corneal Changes:**
- Reduced tear production
- Exposure issues if ptosis significant

## 4. Autoimmune Diseases

### 4.1 Overview of Autoimmune Conditions

Autoimmune diseases occur when the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues. Many autoimmune conditions have ocular manifestations that may be the presenting sign of systemic disease. For the MOH examination, understanding these conditions is essential for comprehensive patient care.

### 4.2 Rheumatoid Arthritis

Rheumatoid arthritis (RA) is a chronic inflammatory autoimmune disease primarily affecting joints, but with significant ocular involvement.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Dry Eye Disease (Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca):*
- Most common ocular manifestation (15-25% of RA patients)
- Secondary Sjögren's syndrome
- Symptoms: grittiness, burning, foreign body sensation
- Decreased tear production, reduced tear breakup time
- Management: artificial tears, punctal plugs, immunomodulators

*Scleritis:*
- Deep, boring eye pain worse at night
- Diffuse or nodular scleral inflammation
- Reddish-purple hue to sclera
- Can lead to scleral thinning and staphyloma
- Associated with systemic disease activity
- Treatment: oral NSAIDs, systemic steroids, immunosuppressants

*Episcleritis:*
- More superficial than scleritis
- Typically less painful
- Sectoral or diffuse injection
- Self-limiting, often no treatment needed

*Peripheral Ulcerative Keratitis:*
- Corneal melting and thinning at limbus
- Serious complication requiring aggressive treatment
- May progress to perforation
- Requires systemic immunosuppression

### 4.3 Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

SLE is a multisystem autoimmune disease with diverse ocular manifestations.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Cutaneous Involvement:*
- Malar rash may involve periorbital area
- Discoid lupus can affect eyelids
- Madarosis (eyelash loss)

*Dry Eye Syndrome:*
- Secondary Sjögren's syndrome
- Aqueous tear deficiency
- Management similar to primary Sjögren's

*Retinal Vasculitis:*
- Cotton wool spots most common finding
- Retinal hemorrhages
- Vascular sheathing
- May mimic diabetic or hypertensive retinopathy

*Lupus Choroidopathy:*
- Serous retinal detachment
- More common with nephritic syndrome
- Associated with hypertension and renal disease

*Neuro-Ophthalmic Involvement:*
- Optic neuritis
- Pseudotumor cerebri (idiopathic intracranial hypertension)
- Cranial nerve palsies
- Homonymous hemianopia from CNS vasculitis

**Antimalarial Retinopathy:**
- Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil) can cause retinal toxicity
- Baseline and annual screening recommended after 5 years of use
- Bulls-eye maculopathy in advanced cases
- OCT, visual fields, and fundus autofluorescence for screening

### 4.4 Sjögren's Syndrome

Sjögren's syndrome is an autoimmune disorder affecting exocrine glands, presenting with dry eyes and dry mouth.

**Classification:**
- Primary Sjögren's: Isolated syndrome
- Secondary Sjögren's: Associated with RA, SLE, or other autoimmune diseases

**Ocular Manifestations:**
- Severe aqueous deficient dry eye
- Conjunctival injection and chemosis
- Corneal filaments and mucus plaques
- Punctate epithelial erosions
- Reduced visual acuity from ocular surface disease

**Diagnostic Criteria:**
- Schirmer test: ≤5mm in 5 minutes
- Reduced tear breakup time
- Positive anti-SS-A (Ro) and anti-SS-B (La) antibodies
- Salivary gland biopsy showing lymphocytic infiltration

**Management:**
- Aggressive lubrication with preservative-free artificial tears
- Punctal plugs
- Topical cyclosporine or lifitegrast
- Oral pilocarpine or cevimeline for gland stimulation
- Blepharitis treatment

### 4.5 Ankylosing Spondylitis

Ankylosing spondylitis (AS) is an inflammatory arthritis primarily affecting the spine, with significant ocular involvement.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Acute Anterior Uveitis:*
- Most common extra-articular manifestation (25-40% of AS patients)
- Typically unilateral, but may recur in either eye
- Sudden onset with pain, photophobia, and redness
- May be presenting sign of undiagnosed AS
- Requires prompt treatment with topical steroids
- Referral to rheumatology for HLA-B27 testing and spine evaluation

**Clinical Features of Uveitis:**
- Anterior chamber cells and flare
- Keratic precipitates
- Posterior synechiae may form
- Hypopyon in severe cases
- Complications: cataract, glaucoma, cystoid macular edema

### 4.6 Multiple Sclerosis

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a demyelinating disease of the central nervous system with significant neuro-ophthalmic manifestations.

**Ocular Manifestations:**

*Optic Neuritis:*
- Most common presenting sign of MS
- Unilateral vision loss with pain on eye movement
- Central scotoma or diffuse field loss
- RAPD present
- Most recover spontaneously, but may leave residual deficits

*Internuclear Ophthalmoplegia (INO):*
- Lesion in medial longitudinal fasciculus (MLF)
- Impaired adduction on attempted lateral gaze
- Abducting eye has nystagmus
- Convergence typically preserved
- Highly suggestive of MS in young patients

*Nystagmus:*
- Various patterns possible
- Gaze-evoked, pendular, or acquired jerk nystagmus
- May cause oscillopsia

*Other Manifestations:*
- Sixth nerve palsy (fascicular lesion)
- Pupillary abnormalities (afferent defect, light-near dissociation)
- Visual field defects from retrochiasmal lesions

## 5. Clinical Examination Approach for MOH Exam

### 5.1 Systemic History Taking

A comprehensive systemic history is essential for identifying patients at risk for ocular complications:

**Key Questions:**
- Are you diabetic? If so, type, duration, medications, last HbA1c?
- Do you have high blood pressure? How is it controlled?
- Any thyroid problems or symptoms (weight changes, temperature sensitivity)?
- Do you have any autoimmune conditions (arthritis, lupus)?
- Current medications (some cause ocular side effects)?
- Family history of systemic diseases?

### 5.2 Examination Techniques

**For Diabetic Patients:**
- Dilated fundus examination with attention to retinal vasculature
- Look for microaneurysms, hemorrhages, exudates, neovascularization
- Macular examination for edema
- Document findings for comparison at future visits

**For Hypertensive Patients:**
- Examine retinal vessels for caliber and crossing changes
- Look for hemorrhages, cotton wool spots, exudates
- Assess optic disc for swelling
- Grade retinopathy for documentation

**For Thyroid Patients:**
- Measure proptosis with exophthalmometer
- Assess eyelid position and movement
- Check extraocular movements for restriction
- Evaluate cornea for exposure
- Test visual acuity, color vision, and pupils

**For Autoimmune Disease Patients:**
- Evaluate ocular surface for dryness
- Examine sclera and episclera for inflammation
- Anterior chamber examination for cells/flare
- Dilated fundus exam for vasculitis, cotton wool spots

### 5.3 Referral Guidelines

**Urgent Referral (within 24 hours):**
- Proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Diabetic macular edema with vision loss
- Malignant hypertension (Grade 4 retinopathy)
- Central retinal artery occlusion
- Central retinal vein occlusion
- Thyroid eye disease with optic neuropathy
- Scleritis with necrotizing features

**Non-Urgent Referral:**
- Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy for monitoring
- Hypertensive retinopathy Grades 1-2
- Thyroid eye disease without optic nerve compromise
- Dry eye requiring specialist management

## 6. Key Points for MOH UAE Examination

1. **Diabetic retinopathy** is classified as NPDR (mild, moderate, severe) and PDR based on specific findings
2. **Diabetic macular edema** can occur at any stage and is the leading cause of vision loss in diabetes
3. **Hypertensive retinopathy Grade 4** with papilledema is a medical emergency
4. **Thyroid eye disease** most commonly affects the inferior and medial rectus muscles
5. **Smoking cessation** is critical for patients with thyroid eye disease
6. **Scleritis** in rheumatoid arthritis indicates systemic disease activity
7. **Acute anterior uveitis** may be the presenting sign of ankylosing spondylitis
8. **Optic neuritis** is often the first manifestation of multiple sclerosis
9. **Hydroxychloroquine** requires baseline and annual retinal screening
10. **Systemic disease control** is essential for managing ocular manifestations

## Conclusion

Understanding the ocular manifestations of systemic diseases is fundamental to optometric practice and essential for the MOH UAE licensing examination. Optometrists play a crucial role in detecting these conditions, monitoring progression, and coordinating care with other healthcare providers. By recognizing ocular signs of diabetes, hypertension, thyroid disorders, and autoimmune diseases, optometrists can provide comprehensive care that addresses both ocular and systemic health. This knowledge not only prepares candidates for examination success but also ensures optimal patient outcomes in clinical practice.
