# Ocular Emergencies for MOH Exam

A comprehensive guide to ocular emergencies for the Ministry of Health (MOH) UAE Optometry Licensing Examination. This guide covers recognition, triage, immediate management, and referral criteria for critical eye conditions that optometrists in the UAE must recognize and manage appropriately.

---

## 1. Chemical Injuries to the Eye

### Introduction and Clinical Significance

Chemical injuries to the eye represent one of the most time-critical ophthalmic emergencies that optometrists may encounter. The severity of outcome is directly proportional to the speed and adequacy of initial management. In the UAE's diverse industrial, construction, and household environments, chemical exposure remains a significant cause of ocular morbidity and potential vision loss.

Understanding the pathophysiology, classification systems, and immediate management protocols is essential for MOH-licensed optometrists who may be the first healthcare professionals to assess these patients. The UAE's occupational health regulations require healthcare providers to be competent in managing chemical injuries, making this a high-yield topic for the MOH examination.

### Classification of Chemical Agents

**Alkali Injuries (More Severe and Penetrating)**

Alkali substances cause the most devastating ocular injuries due to their unique mechanism of tissue destruction. Common alkali agents encountered in the UAE include:

- **Ammonia (NH₃)**: Found in fertilizers, cleaning agents, and refrigerants. Extremely rapid penetration due to high water solubility. Can penetrate the cornea within seconds and reach the anterior chamber within minutes.
- **Sodium hydroxide (NaOH)**: Present in drain cleaners, oven cleaners, and industrial processes. Commonly encountered in both household and industrial settings.
- **Calcium hydroxide (Lime)**: Found in cement, plaster, and whitewash. Particularly relevant in the UAE's active construction industry. The particulate nature can cause prolonged exposure if not thoroughly irrigated.
- **Potassium hydroxide**: Used in alkaline batteries and industrial applications.
- **Magnesium hydroxide**: Found in some fire extinguishers and industrial applications.

**Mechanism of Damage**: Alkali substances cause liquefactive necrosis by saponifying fatty acids in cell membranes. This reaction destroys the structural integrity of tissues and allows the alkali to penetrate deeper into the eye, potentially reaching the anterior chamber, lens, and even the ciliary body. The progressive nature of this damage means that the injury continues to worsen even after the initial exposure. The saponification reaction releases fatty acids that further perpetuate tissue damage, creating a vicious cycle of destruction.

**Acid Injuries (Generally Less Severe)**

Acid injuries, while still serious, typically cause less deep tissue damage:

- **Sulfuric acid**: Found in car batteries and industrial applications. Common in automotive workshops throughout the UAE.
- **Hydrochloric acid**: Used in cleaning and industrial processes.
- **Hydrofluoric acid**: Particularly dangerous due to fluoride ion toxicity, which can cause systemic hypocalcemia and cardiac arrhythmias.
- **Acetic acid**: Found in vinegars and some cleaning solutions.
- **Nitric acid**: Used in industrial applications and can cause severe tissue damage.

**Mechanism of Damage**: Acids cause coagulative necrosis, where proteins are denatured and precipitated, forming a protective barrier that limits deeper penetration of the acid. This "self-limiting" characteristic generally results in less severe injuries compared to alkali burns of equivalent concentration and duration. However, strong acids and prolonged exposure can still cause significant damage.

### Clinical Grading Systems

**Roper-Hall Classification**

The Roper-Hall classification system has been the standard for grading chemical injuries for decades and remains essential knowledge for MOH exam preparation:

| Grade | Corneal Appearance | Limbal Ischemia | Prognosis |
|-------|-------------------|-----------------|-----------|
| I | Corneal epithelial defect only | No limbal ischemia | Excellent |
| II | Corneal haze, iris details visible | < 1/3 limbal ischemia | Good |
| III | Total epithelial loss, stromal haze | 1/3-1/2 limbal ischemia | Guarded |
| IV | Opaque cornea, no view of iris | > 1/2 limbal ischemia | Poor |

**Dua Classification (More Detailed and Modern)**

The Dua classification provides a more comprehensive assessment based on limbal involvement and is increasingly used in clinical practice:

| Grade | Clinical Findings | Prognosis |
|-------|------------------|-----------|
| I | 0 clock hours limbal involvement | Excellent |
| II | ≤3 clock hours limbal involvement | Good |
| III | >3-6 clock hours limbal involvement | Good |
| IV | >6-9 clock hours limbal involvement | Good to guarded |
| V | >9-12 clock hours limbal involvement | Poor |
| VI | Total limbal involvement, >50% conjunctival involvement | Very poor |

The Dua classification is particularly useful because it provides more precise prognostic information and guides the need for limbal stem cell transplantation.

### Immediate Management Protocol

**Step 1: Irrigation (CRITICAL - Do Not Delay for Assessment)**

The single most important intervention in chemical injury is immediate, copious irrigation. This principle cannot be overstated for MOH exam purposes:

- **Volume**: Minimum 1-2 liters initially, often requiring much more (up to 10-20 liters in severe cases)
- **Solution**: Sterile normal saline or Ringer's lactate (readily available, balanced pH)
- **Duration**: Continue irrigation until pH neutralizes to 7.0-7.2 (test with litmus paper or pH paper)
- **Method**: Use Morgan lens or IV tubing for continuous irrigation
- **Critical Point**: Irrigation should begin BEFORE any detailed assessment
- **pH Testing**: Wait 5-10 minutes after stopping irrigation before testing pH to allow accurate reading
- **Double Eversion**: Evert the upper and lower eyelids to ensure no particulate matter remains trapped

**Step 2: Assessment (After Initial Irrigation)**

Once irrigation has been initiated and pH is approaching neutral:

1. **Visual acuity**: Document baseline vision (essential for prognosis and medico-legal purposes)
2. **Slit-lamp examination**: Assess extent of epithelial defect, stromal involvement
3. **Fluorescein staining**: Delineate epithelial defects
4. **Intraocular pressure**: May be elevated due to trabecular meshwork damage or inflammation
5. **Limbal assessment**: Clock hours of ischemia for grading (critical for prognosis)
6. **pH testing**: Confirm neutralization with litmus paper
7. **Conjunctival assessment**: Evaluate for necrosis and chemosis

**Step 3: Medical Treatment**

**Topical Therapy**:
- **Antibiotics**: Moxifloxacin 0.5% or Ofloxacin 0.3% QID for infection prophylaxis
- **Cycloplegics**: Atropine 1% BID or Cyclopentolate 1% TID for comfort and prevention of posterior synechiae
- **Steroids**: Prednisolone acetate 1% QID for 7-14 days (controversial in severe cases due to potential for stromal melting; use with caution in Grade III-IV injuries)
- **Preservative-free lubricants**: For comfort and epithelial healing
- **Ascorbic acid drops**: 10% QID to promote collagen synthesis

**Systemic Therapy** (for moderate to severe injuries):
- **Vitamin C (Ascorbic acid)**: 500-1000mg PO TID to promote collagen synthesis
- **Doxycycline**: 100mg PO BID (inhibits collagenase and matrix metalloproteinases)
- **Oral analgesics**: As needed for pain control
- **Oral NSAIDs**: Avoid if there is active bleeding

### Complications and Long-term Management

- **Symblepharon formation**: Adhesions between bulbar and palpebral conjunctiva requiring surgical lysis
- **Corneal scarring and vascularization**: May require penetrating keratoplasty or deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty
- **Limbal stem cell deficiency**: Requires specialized surgical intervention (limbal stem cell transplantation)
- **Glaucoma**: Secondary to trabecular damage, angle closure, or steroid response
- **Cataract**: From direct chemical damage or steroid use
- **Persistent epithelial defects**: May require amniotic membrane transplantation
- **Ocular surface reconstruction**: May be needed in severe cases

### Referral Criteria

- **Immediate referral**: ALL chemical injuries should be referred to ophthalmology
- **Emergency (same day)**: Grade III-IV injuries, pH not normalizing, suspected perforation, bilateral injuries
- **Follow-up**: Daily until epithelium heals, then weekly for the first month

## 2. Ocular Trauma

### Epidemiology and Importance

Ocular trauma is a leading cause of unilateral blindness worldwide and represents a significant proportion of ophthalmic emergencies. In the UAE, with its active construction industry, diverse population engaged in various occupational and recreational activities, and high rates of motor vehicle use, optometrists must be proficient in trauma assessment and triage.

The economic impact of ocular trauma extends beyond immediate medical costs to include lost productivity, rehabilitation expenses, and long-term disability. MOH-licensed optometrists play a crucial role in initial assessment, documentation, and appropriate referral.

### Classification of Ocular Trauma

**Birmingham Eye Trauma Terminology System (BETTS)**

The BETTS provides a standardized terminology for ocular trauma and is essential for accurate communication and documentation:

- **Eyewall**: Sclera and cornea
- **Closed globe injury**: No full-thickness wound of eyewall
  - Contusion: No wound (blunt trauma)
  - Lamellar laceration: Partial-thickness wound
- **Open globe injury**: Full-thickness wound of eyewall
  - Rupture: Full-thickness wound caused by blunt object
  - Laceration: Full-thickness wound caused by sharp object
    - Penetrating: Entrance wound only
    - Perforating: Entrance and exit wounds
    - Intraocular foreign body (IOFB): Retained foreign object

### Types of Trauma and Clinical Features

**Blunt Trauma**

Mechanisms include assault, sports injuries, falls, and motor vehicle accidents. Common findings include:

- **Hyphema**: Blood in the anterior chamber, graded by volume
- **Angle recession**: Tear in the trabecular meshwork (risk of future glaucoma - up to 10% develop glaucoma)
- **Traumatic iritis**: Inflammation of the iris presenting with pain, photophobia, and ciliary flush
- **Iris sphincter tears**: Resulting in notched pupil or traumatic mydriasis
- **Iridodialysis**: Tearing of iris root from ciliary body
- **Lens subluxation/dislocation**: Zonular disruption leading to phacodonesis or visible lens edge
- **Vitreous hemorrhage**: Bleeding into the vitreous cavity
- **Commotio retinae**: Retinal edema (Berlin's edema) appearing as white retinal opacification
- **Retinal detachment**: May occur acutely or be delayed by weeks to months
- **Choroidal rupture**: Crescent-shaped breaks in Bruch's membrane, often concentric to the optic disc

**Penetrating Trauma**

Caused by sharp objects, projectiles, glass, or metal fragments:

- Risk of endophthalmitis (infection) - up to 10% risk with retained foreign body
- Retinal damage and detachment
- Vitreous hemorrhage
- Intraocular foreign body (IOFB)
- Cataract formation
- Sympathetic ophthalmia (rare but devastating bilateral granulomatous uveitis)

**Ruptured Globe**

The most severe form of blunt trauma with complete disruption of the eyewall:

- Severe vision loss (often light perception or worse)
- Hypotony (very low intraocular pressure)
- Peaked or irregular pupil pointing toward the rupture site
- Extensive subconjunctival hemorrhage
- Obvious deformity of the globe
- Uveal tissue prolapse may be visible

### Systematic Examination Approach

**Primary Survey (ABCDE)**

For patients with significant head or facial trauma:

1. **A - Airway/Cervical spine**: Ensure airway patency, protect cervical spine
2. **B - Breathing**: Assess respiratory status
3. **C - Circulation**: Control hemorrhage, assess hemodynamic status
4. **D - Disability**: Neurological assessment including Glasgow Coma Scale
5. **E - Exposure**: Complete examination

**Ocular Assessment Checklist**

1. **Visual acuity**: Document meticulously - critical for prognosis and medico-legal documentation. If unable to read Snellen, document counting fingers (CF), hand motion (HM), or light perception (LP)
2. **Pupillary examination**: Check for RAPD (relative afferent pupillary defect) which suggests optic nerve injury or extensive retinal damage
3. **External examination**: Lacerations, foreign bodies, orbital rim fractures, enophthalmos, proptosis
4. **Slit-lamp examination**: Anterior segment assessment for hyphema, corneal laceration, iris damage, lens status
5. **Intraocular pressure**: Contraindicated if open globe suspected
6. **Dilated fundus examination**: Rule out retinal detachment, commotio retinae, choroidal rupture (defer if open globe suspected)
7. **Seidel test**: To rule out aqueous leak in suspected corneal laceration

### Hyphema Management

**Grading of Hyphema**:
- Grade I: < 1/3 of anterior chamber volume
- Grade II: 1/3 to 1/2 of anterior chamber
- Grade III: 1/2 to nearly total
- Grade IV: Total hyphema ("8-ball hyphema") - black appearance due to deoxygenated blood

**Treatment Protocol**:

- **Activity restriction**: Bed rest with 30-45 degree head elevation
- **Protection**: Protective eye shield (not pressure patch)
- **Cycloplegics**: Atropine 1% BID for comfort and prevention of synechiae
- **Topical steroids**: Prednisolone acetate 1% QID for inflammation control
- **Avoid**: Aspirin, NSAIDs, and anticoagulants (increase rebleeding risk)
- **IOP management**: If IOP > 25 mmHg, initiate topical IOP-lowering agents (beta-blockers, alpha-agonists, carbonic anhydrase inhibitors)
- **Antifibrinolytics**: Aminocaproic acid or tranexamic acid may reduce rebleeding risk

**Complications**:
- Rebleeding (peak risk at 2-5 days post-injury, occurring in 5-25% of cases)
- Corneal blood staining
- Acute and chronic glaucoma
- Peripheral anterior synechiae
- Optic nerve damage from prolonged elevated IOP

### Referral Criteria for Ocular Trauma

- **Emergency (immediate)**: Ruptured globe, penetrating injury with IOFB, severe hyphema (Grade III-IV), suspected endophthalmitis
- **Urgent (24-48 hours)**: Hyphema, traumatic iritis, suspected retinal detachment, orbital fracture
- **Routine**: Minor blunt trauma with normal examination findings

## 3. Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma (AACG)

### Definition and Pathophysiology

Acute angle-closure glaucoma is an ophthalmic emergency characterized by sudden, marked elevation of intraocular pressure (IOP) due to mechanical blockage of the trabecular meshwork by the peripheral iris. This condition requires immediate recognition and intervention to prevent permanent vision loss.

The pathophysiology involves pupillary block, where aqueous humor is prevented from flowing from the posterior to anterior chamber through the pupil. This causes the peripheral iris to bow forward and obstruct the trabecular meshwork, resulting in rapid IOP elevation that can exceed 50-80 mmHg.

### Risk Factors

**Anatomical Risk Factors**:
- Shallow anterior chamber (assessed by Van Herick technique or gonioscopy)
- Narrow iridocorneal angle (Shaffer grade 0-1)
- Hyperopic eye (shorter axial length, more crowded anterior segment)
- Large lens (especially with advancing cataract)
- Plateau iris configuration
- Small corneal diameter
- Thick, anteriorly positioned lens

**Demographic Risk Factors**:
- Age > 60 years (lens enlargement with age)
- Female gender (3:1 predominance due to anatomical factors)
- Asian and Eskimo descent (higher prevalence of narrow angles)
- Family history of angle-closure glaucoma

**Precipitating Factors**:
- Pupillary dilation (dim lighting, pharmacological dilation, emotional stress)
- Certain medications (anticholinergics, adrenergic agents, tricyclic antidepressants)
- Sulfa-derived medications (Topiramate, acetazolamide in sulfa-allergic patients can cause bilateral angle closure)
- Prolonged prone position
- Emotional stress or anxiety

### Clinical Presentation

**Classic Symptoms**:
- Sudden onset of severe unilateral eye pain (often described as deep and boring)
- Headache, often ipsilateral and severe
- Nausea and vomiting (may mimic abdominal emergency or migraine)
- Blurred vision with characteristic halos around lights (due to corneal edema)
- Redness of the affected eye
- Patients may appear systemically unwell

**Clinical Signs**:
- **Intraocular pressure**: Often 50-80 mmHg (normal: 10-21 mmHg)
- **Conjunctival injection**: With characteristic ciliary flush (perilimbal injection)
- **Corneal edema**: Epithelial and stromal edema giving "steamy" or "ground glass" appearance
- **Anterior chamber**: Shallow, may show flare and cells
- **Pupil**: Mid-dilated (5-6mm), fixed, oval-shaped, may be irregular
- **Gonioscopy**: Closed angle (if cornea is clear enough to visualize)
- **Optic disc**: May show hyperemia, edema, or early pallor
- **Anterior chamber**: Shallow centrally and peripherally
- **Iris**: May show sector atrophy from previous attacks

### Differential Diagnosis

- Primary open-angle glaucoma with spike (less acute, typically less symptomatic)
- Secondary glaucoma (pigmentary, uveitic, neovascular)
- Posner-Schlossman syndrome (glaucomatocyclitic crisis)
- Phacolytic glaucoma
- Neovascular glaucoma
- Malignant glaucoma (aqueous misdirection)

### Immediate Management

**Step 1: Lower IOP Rapidly**

Time is critical - permanent optic nerve damage can occur within hours of sustained high IOP.

**Systemic Therapy**:
- **Acetazolamide**: 500mg IV or PO stat, then 250mg QID
  - Contraindicated in sulfa allergy
  - Caution in renal impairment
  - Monitor electrolytes
- **Mannitol**: 1-2 g/kg IV over 45 minutes
  - Monitor for cardiovascular effects
  - Contraindicated in congestive heart failure
  - Monitor urine output and renal function

**Topical Therapy**:
- **Beta-blockers**: Timolol 0.5% BID (aqueous suppressant)
- **Alpha-agonists**: Brimonidine 0.15% TID (aqueous suppressant and increases uveoscleral outflow)
- **Carbonic anhydrase inhibitor**: Dorzolamide 2% TID or Brinzolamide 1% TID
- **Miotic**: Pilocarpine 2% QID
  - Important: Only effective AFTER IOP is lowered
  - Ineffective when IOP is very high due to iris ischemia
  - Contraindicated in angle closure from lens dislocation
- **Steroids**: Prednisolone acetate 1% QID for inflammation

**Step 2: Break the Attack**

- **Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI)**: Definitive treatment
- Creates a hole in the peripheral iris allowing aqueous to bypass pupillary block
- Perform as soon as cornea clears sufficiently
- May be performed on the affected eye and prophylactically on the fellow eye
- Surgical iridectomy if laser not possible

**Step 3: Prophylaxis**

- Fellow eye has 40-50% risk of acute angle closure within 5 years
- Perform prophylactic LPI in fellow eye within 24-48 hours
- Long-term monitoring for chronic angle closure or plateau iris syndrome
- Consider lens extraction in selected cases

### Referral Criteria

- **Immediate emergency referral**: ALL suspected cases of acute angle-closure glaucoma to ophthalmology
- Time-sensitive: Permanent optic nerve damage can occur within hours
- Target: IOP reduction within 1-2 hours of presentation
- Patient education: Explain the urgency and need for immediate ophthalmology evaluation

## 4. Retinal Detachment

### Definition and Types

Retinal detachment is the separation of the neurosensory retina from the underlying retinal pigment epithelium (RPE). This separation deprives the outer retina of its blood supply from the choroid, leading to progressive and potentially permanent vision loss if not treated promptly.

**Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment (Most Common - 95%)**:
- Caused by a retinal break (tear or hole) allowing vitreous fluid to enter the subretinal space
- Risk factors: High myopia, lattice degeneration, previous cataract surgery, trauma, family history, peripheral retinal degenerations
- Most urgent type requiring prompt surgical intervention
- Annual incidence approximately 1 in 10,000

**Tractional Retinal Detachment**:
- Caused by fibrovascular membranes pulling the retina away from the RPE
- Associated with proliferative diabetic retinopathy, proliferative vitreoretinopathy (PVR), trauma, retinopathy of prematurity
- Often less urgent unless macula is threatened
- Concave surface toward the vitreous cavity

**Exudative (Serous) Retinal Detachment**:
- Caused by fluid accumulation beneath an intact retina without a retinal break
- Etiologies: Tumors (choroidal melanoma, hemangioma, metastases), inflammatory conditions (posterior scleritis, VKH syndrome), central serous chorioretinopathy, hypertension, preeclampsia
- Management focuses on treating underlying cause
- Smooth, dome-shaped elevation of retina

### Warning Signs - The Classic Triad

Recognition of warning signs is critical for MOH optometrists:

1. **Photopsia (Flashes of Light)**:
   - Caused by vitreous traction on the retina
   - Typically arc-shaped or lightning-like, in the peripheral visual field
   - More prominent in dark environments
   - Usually unilateral
   - Indicates active vitreoretinal traction

2. **Floaters**:
   - New onset of floaters, especially sudden shower of tiny spots (often described as "cobwebs," "flies," or "soot")
   - May indicate vitreous hemorrhage from torn retinal vessel
   - Large, ring-shaped floaters (Weiss ring) indicate posterior vitreous detachment
   - Sudden increase in number or size warrants urgent evaluation

3. **Visual Field Defect**:
   - Progressive curtain or shadow spreading across the visual field
   - May start peripherally and extend centrally
   - Patient may describe it as "a veil descending" or "a shade being pulled"
   - Respects the horizontal midline in many cases
   - Macular involvement leads to severe central vision loss

### Clinical Examination Techniques

**Direct Ophthalmoscopy**:
- Quick screening tool
- May reveal obvious retinal break or detachment
- Limited view of peripheral retina (only about 5-10 degrees of retina visible)
- Less sensitive for peripheral pathology

**Slit-lamp Biomicroscopy with 90D/78D Lens**:
- Excellent view of posterior pole
- Can identify macular involvement
- Limited peripheral view
- Good for documenting vitreous hemorrhage

**Indirect Ophthalmoscopy with Scleral Depression**:
- Gold standard for peripheral retinal examination
- Essential for identifying retinal breaks
- Scleral depression allows visualization of the ora serrata region
- Requires practice and skill
- Wide field of view (approximately 180 degrees)

**Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)**:
- Confirms subretinal fluid
- Identifies macular involvement
- Differentiates types of subretinal fluid
- Useful for monitoring post-treatment
- Can identify subtle detachments

**Ultrasound (B-scan)**:
- Essential when media opacities prevent visualization
- Can identify retinal detachment, vitreous hemorrhage, tumors
- Dynamic examination shows characteristic retinal movements

### Management Principles

**Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment**:

- **Macula-on RD**: Urgent surgery within 24-48 hours to prevent macular involvement
- **Macula-off RD**: Surgery within 7-10 days (some surgeons advocate sooner for better outcomes)
  - Visual recovery is limited after approximately 1 week of macular detachment
  - Final visual acuity depends on duration of macular detachment

**Surgical Options**:

1. **Pneumatic Retinopexy**:
   - Office-based procedure under local anesthesia
   - Injects gas bubble (SF6 or C3F8) into vitreous cavity
   - Best for small, superior breaks in phakic eyes
   - Requires specific head positioning for 1-2 weeks
   - Success rate approximately 70-80%
   - Avoid air travel with gas bubble

2. **Scleral Buckle**:
   - External approach in operating room
   - Indents the sclera to close retinal breaks
   - Best for multiple breaks, anterior breaks, and in phakic patients
   - May be combined with vitrectomy
   - Success rate approximately 85-95%

3. **Pars Plana Vitrectomy**:
   - Internal approach in operating room
   - Removes vitreous and replaces with gas or silicone oil
   - Best for complex RD, PVR, large tears, vitreous hemorrhage, pseudophakic patients
   - Success rate approximately 85-95%
   - May require positioning and multiple surgeries

### Referral Criteria

- **Emergency (same day)**: Macula-on rhegmatogenous RD, large retinal tear with symptoms, tractional RD with macular involvement
- **Urgent (24-72 hours)**: New PVD with symptoms, peripheral retinal breaks without detachment, lattice degeneration with new symptoms
- **Routine**: Chronic, stable RD (discussion of options, patient factors), asymptomatic retinal holes

## 5. Quick Reference: Referral Criteria Summary

### Emergency Referral (Immediate - Within Hours)

| Condition | Reason for Urgency |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Chemical injury | Time-critical irrigation, assessment for surgical intervention |
| Ruptured globe | Prevent further damage, infection, and loss of eye |
| Intraocular foreign body | Risk of endophthalmitis and toxicity |
| Orbital cellulitis | Sight and life-threatening infection |
| Endophthalmitis | Rapid destruction of intraocular structures |
| Acute angle-closure glaucoma | Prevent irreversible optic nerve damage |
| Macula-on retinal detachment | Prevent macular involvement and permanent central vision loss |
| Central retinal artery occlusion | Time window for intervention is very narrow |

### Urgent Referral (24-72 Hours)

| Condition | Reason |
|-----------|--------|
| Corneal ulcer | Prevent scarring and perforation |
| Hyphema | Monitor IOP and watch for rebleeding |
| Retinal break without detachment | Prevent progression to detachment |
| Traumatic iritis | Prevent complications |
| New PVD with symptoms | Rule out retinal tear |
| Optic neuritis | Prevent further vision loss |

## 6. Key Takeaways for MOH Optometry Exam

1. **Chemical injuries**: Irrigate FIRST, assess LATER. Time equals vision saved. Always test pH after irrigation.
2. **Trauma assessment**: Document visual acuity meticulously - it is critical for prognosis and medico-legal purposes.
3. **Acute angle-closure glaucoma**: Recognize the classic presentation - severe pain, halos, mid-dilated fixed pupil, and very high IOP.
4. **Retinal detachment**: Know the warning signs - photopsia, floaters, and visual field defect.
5. **Referral urgency**: When in doubt, refer urgently. It is better to refer unnecessarily than to miss a vision-threatening condition.
6. **Documentation**: Always document your findings, actions, and communications with patients and specialists.
7. **Patient education**: Ensure patients understand the urgency of their condition and the importance of follow-up.
