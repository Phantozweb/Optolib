# Contact Lens Practice for DHA Exam

## Introduction

Contact lens practice represents one of the most critical competencies assessed in the Dubai Health Authority (DHA) Optometry licensing examination. This comprehensive guide covers all aspects of contact lens fitting, management, and complication recognition essential for safe and effective patient care. Understanding these concepts thoroughly is vital for passing the examination and providing quality care in clinical practice.

## 1. Soft Contact Lenses

### 1.1 Material Properties and Classifications

Soft contact lenses are manufactured from hydrogel or silicone hydrogel materials, each with distinct properties affecting oxygen permeability, water content, and clinical performance.

**Hydrogel Materials**

Traditional hydrogel lenses are composed of hydrophilic polymers that absorb water, ranging from 38% to 79% water content. The water content directly influences oxygen permeability (Dk), calculated as the product of diffusivity (D) and solubility (k). Key hydrogel materials include:

- **Etafilcon A**: 58% water content, FDA Group IV (high water, ionic)
- **Polymacon**: 38% water content, FDA Group I (low water, non-ionic)
- **Omafilcon A**: 62% water content, FDA Group II (high water, non-ionic)
- **Nelfilcon A**: 69% water content, FDA Group IV (high water, ionic)

**Silicone Hydrogel Materials**

Silicone hydrogel lenses represent the gold standard for extended wear and high-oxygen applications. The incorporation of silicone provides dramatically higher oxygen transmissibility (Dk/t) values:

- **Lotrafilcon B**: Dk/t of 110, 33% water content
- **Senofilcon A**: Dk/t of 103, 38% water content
- **Comfilcon A**: Dk/t of 128, 48% water content
- **Delefilcon A**: Dk/t of 156, 55% water content (water gradient technology)

### 1.2 FDA Classification System

The FDA classifies soft contact lenses into four groups based on water content and ionic nature:

| Group | Water Content | Ionic Nature | Examples | Clinical Considerations |
|-------|---------------|--------------|----------|------------------------|
| I | Low (<50%) | Non-ionic | Polymacon | Protein deposits less likely |
| II | High (≥50%) | Non-ionic | Omafilcon A | Better deposit resistance |
| III | Low (<50%) | Ionic | Balafilcon A | Prone to protein deposits |
| IV | High (≥50%) | Ionic | Etafilcon A | Highest deposit tendency |

### 1.3 Fitting Parameters and Assessment

**Base Curve Selection**

The base curve (BC) is the radius of curvature of the central posterior surface. Proper selection ensures adequate tear exchange and corneal alignment:

- Average corneal curvature: 7.80mm (43.00D)
- Flatter than K by 0.50-1.00D typically recommended
- Tighter fits indicated for high astigmatism or steep corneas

**Diameter Selection**

Lens diameter affects coverage, centration, and tear exchange:

- Standard soft lens diameter: 13.8mm to 14.5mm
- Must extend 1.0-1.5mm beyond the limbus
- Larger diameters for flatter corneas or larger palpebral apertures
- Smaller diameters for steep corneas or smaller palpebral apertures

**Assessment Criteria**

A well-fitted soft contact lens should demonstrate:

1. **Complete corneal coverage**: Lens extends beyond limbus in all directions
2. **Good centration**: Centered over the cornea without temporal or nasal shift
3. **Adequate movement**: 0.2-0.5mm with each blink
4. **Lag**: 0.5-1.0mm movement on upward gaze or lateral excursion
5. **Push-up test**: Lens moves easily with gentle lid pressure
6. **No edge lift or fluting**: Edge should smoothly drape over sclera

### 1.4 Fitting Steps

**Step 1: Pre-fitting Assessment**
- Comprehensive eye examination
- Corneal topography/tomography
- Tear film evaluation
- Palpebral aperture measurement
- Lifestyle and visual requirements assessment

**Step 2: Initial Lens Selection**
- Base curve 0.50-1.00D flatter than flattest K reading
- Standard diameter (14.0-14.2mm) as starting point
- Power calculated using vertex distance formula if needed
- Material selected based on wearing schedule and tear film

**Step 3: Trial Lens Evaluation**
- Allow 15-20 minutes for lens settling
- Assess vision, comfort, fit, and lens positioning
- Check movement, centration, and coverage
- Perform over-refraction if needed

**Step 4: Final Prescription**
- Specify brand, material, base curve, diameter, and power
- Provide wearing schedule and replacement schedule
- Educate on insertion, removal, and care procedures

### 1.5 Wearing Schedules and Modalities

**Daily Wear (DW)**
- Worn during waking hours only
- Removed, cleaned, and stored overnight
- Most common and safest modality
- Lower infection risk compared to extended wear

**Extended Wear (EW)**
- Worn continuously, including sleep
- Requires high Dk/t (>87) silicone hydrogel lenses
- Maximum recommended continuous wear: 6-7 nights
- Increased risk of microbial keratitis and inflammatory events

**Replacement Schedules**

| Schedule | Frequency | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|----------|-----------|------------|---------------|
| Daily Disposable | Single use | No cleaning required, lowest infection risk | Higher cost, more waste |
| Two-Week | Replace every 14 days | Balance of cost and hygiene | Requires daily cleaning |
| Monthly | Replace every 30 days | Most economical for planned replacement | Higher deposit accumulation |
| Quarterly | Replace every 3 months | Cost-effective | Less common, higher deposit risk |

## 2. Rigid Gas Permeable (RGP) Contact Lenses

### 2.1 Material Properties and Dk Values

RGP lenses are manufactured from rigid materials that maintain their shape on the eye, providing superior optics for irregular corneas and high astigmatism. Key material properties include:

**Oxygen Permeability (Dk)**
RGP materials are classified by Dk value:
- Low Dk (<30): Limited oxygen transmissibility, rarely used today
- Medium Dk (30-60): Adequate for daily wear
- High Dk (60-90): Suitable for extended wear
- Super Dk (>90): Maximum oxygen transmissibility

Common RGP materials:
- **Fluoro-siloxane acrylate**: Dk 45-141
- **Fluoropolymer**: Dk 20-92
- **Boston XO**: Dk 100
- **Paragon HDS**: Dk 58

### 2.2 Design Parameters

**Base Curve**
- Typically fitted on flattest K or slightly flatter
- Determines the relationship between lens and cornea
- Affects sagittal depth and vault

**Optical Zone Diameter (OZD)**
- Central zone containing the prescription power
- Usually 7.0-8.5mm
- Smaller OZD for steeper corneas or larger pupils may cause glare

**Peripheral Curve System**
Three-curve or four-curve designs optimize tear exchange:
1. **Base curve (central)**: Aligns with corneal apex
2. **Intermediate curve**: Provides transition zone
3. **Peripheral curve**: Creates edge lift for tear exchange
4. **Edge lift**: 0.10-0.20mm for adequate tear pump

**Lens Diameter**
- Standard RGP diameter: 8.8-9.8mm
- Smaller diameters (8.0-8.6mm) for steep corneas
- Larger diameters (9.8-11.0mm) for intralimbal designs

### 2.3 Fitting Philosophy

**Apical Alignment (Alignment Fitting)**
- Base curve matches flattest K reading
- Lens distributes weight evenly across cornea
- Minimal apical bearing or clearance
- Most common approach for regular corneas

**Apical Clearance Fitting**
- Base curve steeper than K
- Creates vault over corneal apex
- Used for post-surgical corneas or keratoconus
- Requires central clearance verified by fluorescein pattern

**Three-Point Touch Fitting**
- Lens touches at apex and mid-periphery
- Provides stability without excessive bearing
- Common for keratoconus fitting
- Slight apical touch with peripheral alignment

### 2.4 Fluorescein Pattern Interpretation

Fluorescein evaluation under cobalt blue light reveals the lens-cornea relationship:

**Alignment Pattern (Optimal Fit)**
- Thin, uniform central pooling
- Gradual transition to mid-periphery
- Moderate edge clearance
- Dark area where lens touches cornea

**Steep Fit Pattern**
- Central pooling (bright green)
- Mid-peripheral bearing (dark areas)
- Reduced lens movement
- May cause corneal edema over time

**Flat Fit Pattern**
- Central bearing (dark area)
- Peripheral pooling (bright green)
- Excessive lens movement
- May cause central corneal abrasion

### 2.5 RGP Advantages and Disadvantages

**Advantages**
- Superior optical quality
- Excellent oxygen permeability
- Durable and long-lasting
- Lower infection risk than soft lenses
- Better for irregular corneas and high astigmatism
- Corrects corneal irregularities
- Slow progression of myopia (possible benefit)

**Disadvantages**
- Initial discomfort during adaptation period
- Visible on eye (smaller than iris)
- Can dislodge during sports
- Requires adaptation period (1-2 weeks)
- More expensive initially
- Debris can get under lens

## 3. Toric Contact Lenses

### 3.1 Principles of Astigmatic Correction

Toric contact lenses correct corneal and lenticular astigmatism through a cylinder power oriented at a specific axis. Unlike spectacle correction, toric lenses must maintain rotational stability to provide consistent vision.

**Indications for Toric Correction**
- Astigmatism ≥0.75D (some clinicians use 0.50D threshold)
- Visual symptoms not corrected with spherical lenses
- Residual astigmatism after spherical soft lens fitting
- Irregular astigmatism (may require RGP)

### 3.2 Stabilization Mechanisms

**Prism Ballast**
- Base-down prism (0.5-2.0 prism diopters) added to lens
- Thicker inferior edge provides gravitational stability
- Most common stabilization method for soft torics
- Lens naturally rotates to position prism base inferiorly

**Truncation**
- Inferior portion of lens cut off (truncated)
- Flat edge rests against lower lid
- Combined with prism ballast for enhanced stability
- Less common in modern lenses

**Double Slab-Off (Thin Zones)**
- Superior and inferior thin zones
- Lens stabilizes where thin zones align with lid margins
- Works with eyelid interaction rather than gravity
- Preferred for smaller diameter lenses

**Peri-ballast**
- Thinning at top and bottom, thickening nasally and temporally
- Provides rotational stability without prism
- Works with lid interaction

**Dynamic Stabilization**
- Incorporates multiple stabilization features
- Adapts to varying lid configurations
- Found in modern silicone hydrogel torics

### 3.3 Soft Toric Lens Fitting

**Fitting Procedure**

1. **Select Base Curve and Diameter**: Same principles as spherical soft lenses
2. **Choose Cylinder Power**: Match or closest to refractive cylinder
3. **Select Axis**: Match refractive axis
4. **Evaluate Rotation**: Wait 20-30 minutes for stabilization
5. **Assess Visual Acuity**: Distance and near
6. **Check Stability**: Lens should not rotate more than 5° with blink

**Rotation Compensation (LASA Rule)**
If lens rotates from intended position:
- **L**eft rotation: **A**dd to axis
- **S**table rotation: **A**djust proportionally

Example: 1.00D cylinder at 180°, lens rotates 10° clockwise (to the right)
- LARS: Add to axis
- Adjusted axis: 180° + 10° = 190° (or 10°)
- New lens ordered: 1.00D cylinder at 10°

**Acceptable Rotation**
- ≤5° rotation: Usually acceptable
- 5-15° rotation: May need compensation
- >15° rotation: Consider different lens design or brand

### 3.4 RGP Toric Lenses

**Back Surface Toric**
- Toric back surface matches corneal toricity
- Stabilizes through corneal shape conformity
- Used when corneal toricity ≥2.00D
- May require bitoric design for optimal optics

**Front Surface Toric**
- Spherical back surface with toric front surface
- Stabilized by prism ballast
- Used for residual astigmatism or low corneal toricity

**Bitoric Design**
- Both front and back surfaces toric
- Back surface toric for corneal astigmatism
- Front surface toric for residual astigmatism
- Provides optimal vision and stability

### 3.5 Toric Lens Power Calculation

**Optical Cross Method**
Place powers on optical cross and calculate cylinder:

Example: Rx: -3.00 -1.50 x 180
- Meridian 1 (180°): -3.00D
- Meridian 2 (90°): -3.00 + (-1.50) = -4.50D
- Cylinder power: -1.50D, Axis: 180°

**Vertex Distance Consideration**
For powers ≥4.00D, apply vertex conversion:
- Effective power = F / (1 - dF)
- Where F = spectacle power, d = vertex distance in meters

## 4. Multifocal Contact Lenses

### 4.1 Presbyopia and Contact Lens Correction

Presbyopia affects all individuals beginning around age 40, resulting from decreased accommodative ability. Contact lens options for presbyopia correction include:

- Multifocal contact lenses
- Monovision (one eye distance, one eye near)
- Modified monovision (multifocal in one eye, distance in other)

### 4.2 Multifocal Designs

**Simultaneous Vision Designs**

Multiple focal powers are present simultaneously on the lens, and the brain selects the appropriate image.

**Concentric (Annular) Design**
- Central distance zone surrounded by alternating near and distance rings
- Works well for moderate pupils
- May cause night vision disturbances
- Examples: Acuvue Oasys for Presbyopia, Biofinity multifocal

**Aspheric Design**
- Gradual power transition from center to periphery
- Center-distance or center-near designs available
- Provides intermediate vision
- Examples: Air Optix Multifocal, Ultra for Presbyopia

**Diffractive Design**
- Uses diffraction optics for near vision
- Provides good vision at all distances
- Less affected by pupil size
- Example: PureVision Multi-Focal

**Segmented (Translating) Designs**
- Similar to bifocal spectacles with distinct zones
- Requires inferior positioning and translation during downgaze
- Primarily available in RGP materials
- Excellent for high add powers

### 4.3 Soft Multifocal Fitting

**Fitting Approaches**

**Distance-Centered (Center-D)**
- Central zone for distance vision
- Surrounding near zone
- Preferred for distance-dominant visual tasks

**Near-Centered (Center-N)**
- Central zone for near vision
- Surrounding distance zone
- Preferred for near-dominant visual tasks

**Fitting Steps**

1. **Determine Dominant Eye**: Use sighting or blur suppression tests
2. **Select Initial Add Power**: Based on age and near requirements
3. **Fit Distance Power First**: Ensure adequate distance vision
4. **Assess Near Vision**: Add or adjust near power as needed
5. **Balance Distance and Near**: May require different lens designs per eye

**Fitting Tips**
- Start with manufacturer's recommended fitting guide
- Avoid over-minimizing distance power to improve near
- Consider modified monovision for difficult fits
- Manage patient expectations regarding compromises

### 4.4 RGP Multifocal Options

**Translating (Segmented) Bifocals**
- Flat-top or curved-top segment
- Requires lens positioning inferiorly
- Must translate upward during downgaze
- Prism ballast for stabilization
- Excellent for high adds and critical vision demands

**Aspheric Multifocals**
- Progressive addition from center to edge
- Simultaneous vision design
- Works well for active patients
- May require larger diameter for optimal function

**Diffractive Multifocals**
- Diffractive zone for near vision
- Works well with normal pupil sizes
- Good intermediate vision

### 4.5 Monovision Fitting

**Concept**
One eye corrected for distance, the other for near, with brain suppressing the blurred image based on viewing distance.

**Advantages**
- Simple fitting process
- No special lens designs required
- Good success rate (70-75%)
- Cost-effective

**Disadvantages**
- Reduced binocular vision and stereopsis
- May affect driving (especially night driving)
- Compromised intermediate vision
- Not suitable for all patients

**Modified Monovision**
- Distance lens in dominant eye
- Multifocal in non-dominant eye
- Improves near and intermediate vision
- Better success rate than standard monovision

### 4.6 Success Factors and Troubleshooting

**Patient Selection**
- Motivated patient with realistic expectations
- Good tear quality
- Regular corneal topography
- Willingness to adapt to visual compromises

**Common Problems and Solutions**

| Problem | Possible Cause | Solution |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Poor distance vision | Excess near power, misalignment | Adjust add power, refit with center-D design |
| Poor near vision | Insufficient add, pupil too small | Increase add power, try center-N design |
| Ghosting/double images | Simultaneous vision effect | Explain adaptation period, adjust zone sizes |
| Night vision issues | Pupil size larger than zones | Consider diffractive design or glasses for night |
| Dryness | Material incompatibility | Switch to different material, add lubricants |

## 5. Contact Lens Complications

### 5.1 Hypoxic Complications

**Corneal Edema**
- Result of inadequate oxygen supply
- Central corneal clouding visible with slit lamp
- Stromal thickening >5% indicates hypoxia
- Management: Increase Dk/t, reduce wearing time

**Microcysts**
- Small, irregular epithelial inclusions
- Indicate chronic epithelial hypoxia
- Reversed with improved oxygen supply
- May initially increase during recovery

**Neovascularization**
- Superficial: Blood vessel ingrowth from limbus <2mm
- Deep: Stromal vessels indicating severe hypoxia
- Grade 1: Vessels extending 1-2mm
- Grade 2: Vessels extending >2mm
- Management: Discontinue or refit with higher Dk lens

**Corneal Warpage**
- Change in corneal shape from lens wear
- More common with RGP and PMMA lenses
- Reversible with discontinuation
- May require weeks to months for resolution

### 5.2 Mechanical Complications

**Corneal Abrasion**
- Epithelial defect from lens trauma
- Pain, foreign body sensation, tearing
- Fluorescein staining reveals defect
- Management: Discontinue lens, antibiotic prophylaxis, follow-up

**Superior Epithelial Arcuate Lesion (SEAL)**
- Arcuate epithelial defect superiorly
- Caused by mechanical trauma from lens edge
- More common with thick lens edges
- Management: Refit with different edge design

**Corneal Erosion**
- Recurrent breakdown of epithelium
- May follow abrasion or occur spontaneously
- Morning pain, tearing, photophobia
- Management: Lubricants, bandage contact lens, prophylaxis

**Tight Lens Syndrome**
- Lens adheres to cornea with no movement
- Caused by improper fit or changes in corneal shape
- Pain, redness, decreased vision
- Management: Remove lens, evaluate fit, refit if necessary

### 5.3 Inflammatory Complications

**Contact Lens Acute Red Eye (CLARE)**
- Acute onset of redness, pain, photophobia
- Usually associated with extended wear
- Often occurs upon waking
- Caused by endotoxin release from Gram-negative bacteria
- Management: Discontinue lens wear, antibiotics if needed

**Contact Lens Papillary Conjunctivitis (CLPC)**
- Giant papillae on superior tarsus
- Itching, mucous discharge, lens discomfort
- Caused by mechanical irritation and antigenic response
- Grade 1: Mild papillae (0.3-0.5mm)
- Grade 2: Moderate papillae (0.5-1.0mm)
- Grade 3: Large papillae (>1.0mm)
- Management: Reduce wearing time, change lens type, mast cell stabilizers

**Infiltrative Keratitis**
- Focal white infiltrates in cornea
- Non-infectious inflammatory response
- Often associated with poor lens hygiene
- Management: Discontinue lens, consider prophylactic antibiotics

### 5.4 Infectious Complications

**Microbial Keratitis**
- Most serious contact lens complication
- Incidence: 1-4 per 10,000 daily wear; 20 per 10,000 extended wear
- Symptoms: Pain, redness, discharge, decreased vision
- Risk factors: Extended wear, poor hygiene, trauma, contamination

**Causative Organisms**
- **Pseudomonas aeruginosa**: Most common in contact lens wearers (60-70%)
- **Acanthamoeba**: Associated with water exposure, severe keratitis
- **Fusarium**: Associated with certain solutions and trauma
- **Staphylococcus**: Less common in contact lens wearers

**Clinical Signs**
- Corneal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect
- Stromal necrosis and melting
- Hypopyon in severe cases
- Anterior chamber reaction

**Management**
- Discontinue contact lens wear immediately
- Urgent referral to ophthalmologist
- Intensive topical antibiotics (fluoroquinolone)
- Frequent follow-up (daily initially)
- Never patch contact lens-related keratitis

### 5.5 Solution-Related Complications

**Solution-Induced Corneal Staining (SICS)**
- Diffuse punctate staining with certain solution-lens combinations
- Associated with polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) solutions
- More common with silicone hydrogel lenses
- Management: Change to different solution system

**Allergic Reactions**
- Redness, itching, swelling with solution use
- Thimerosal sensitivity (historical)
- Preservative sensitivity
- Management: Preservative-free solutions, hydrogen peroxide systems

**Solution Toxicity**
- Chemical burns from improper solution use
- Accidental use of hydrogen peroxide without neutralization
- Management: Rinse thoroughly, use appropriate solutions

### 5.6 Dry Eye and Contact Lenses

**Impact on Contact Lens Wear**
- Reduced wearing time
- Intermittent blurred vision
- Discomfort and foreign body sensation
- Increased deposit formation

**Management Strategies**
- Frequent replacement lenses
- Low water content lenses
- Daily disposable lenses
- Artificial tears (preservative-free)
- Punctal plugs
- LipiFlow or other meibomian gland treatments

### 5.7 Complication Prevention

**Patient Education**
- Proper lens handling and insertion/removal technique
- Hand washing before lens handling
- No water exposure (swimming, showering)
- No sleeping in lenses unless approved
- Solution usage per manufacturer instructions

**Follow-up Schedule**
- 1 week after initial fitting
- 1 month after fitting
- Every 6 months for established wearers
- Annual comprehensive examination

**Warning Signs to Report**
- Pain or discomfort
- Redness that persists
- Decreased vision
- Discharge or tearing
- Light sensitivity

## Conclusion

Contact lens practice for the DHA examination encompasses a broad range of knowledge areas including material science, fitting techniques, optical principles, and complication management. Successful practitioners must understand the interrelationships between lens properties, corneal physiology, and patient factors to achieve optimal outcomes while minimizing risks.

Key examination topics include:
1. Soft lens material properties and FDA classification
2. RGP fitting philosophies and fluorescein pattern interpretation
3. Toric lens stabilization mechanisms and rotation compensation
4. Multifocal design principles and fitting strategies
5. Complication recognition, grading, and management

Thorough preparation in these areas, combined with clinical reasoning skills, will enable success on the DHA Optometry examination and support safe contact lens practice.
