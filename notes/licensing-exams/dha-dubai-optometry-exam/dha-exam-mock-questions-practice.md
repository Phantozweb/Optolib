# DHA Exam Mock Questions & Practice

## Introduction

The Dubai Health Authority (DHA) Optometry licensing examination is a comprehensive assessment designed to evaluate the competency of optometrists seeking to practice in Dubai, United Arab Emirates. This practice examination contains over 50 multiple-choice questions covering all major domains of optometric practice, including clinical optometry, ocular disease and pathology, optical dispensing, contact lenses, pediatric optometry, and professional regulations.

These mock questions are carefully crafted to mirror the style, difficulty level, and content distribution of the actual DHA examination. Each question includes a detailed explanation of the correct answer, helping candidates understand the underlying concepts and clinical reasoning required for successful examination performance.

## Examination Structure Overview

The DHA Optometry examination typically consists of 100 multiple-choice questions distributed across the following domains:

| Domain | Weight | Question Count |
|--------|--------|----------------|
| Clinical Optometry & Refraction | 30% | 30 questions |
| Ocular Disease & Pathology | 25% | 25 questions |
| Optical Dispensing & Instrumentation | 15% | 15 questions |
| Contact Lens Practice | 15% | 15 questions |
| Professional Ethics & Regulations | 10% | 10 questions |

## Section 1: Clinical Optometry & Refraction

### Question 1

A 45-year-old patient presents with a complaint of difficulty reading small print. Distance visual acuity is 20/20 OU. Near visual acuity is J3 with the patient's current glasses. Which of the following is the MOST appropriate initial test?

A) Dynamic retinoscopy
B) Near point of convergence
C) Amplitude of accommodation
D) Cover test at near

**Correct Answer: C) Amplitude of accommodation**

**Explanation:** The patient's age (45 years) and symptoms are classic for presbyopia. The amplitude of accommodation naturally decreases with age, typically reaching approximately 3.00D by age 45. Measuring the amplitude of accommodation (using the push-up method or minus lens method) is the most direct way to assess accommodative function and confirm the diagnosis of presbyopia. While the other tests may provide useful information, amplitude of accommodation is the most specific to the presenting complaint.

### Question 2

During subjective refraction, a patient with -2.00 -1.00 x 180 reports that the 1st JCC position is clearer. The cylinder axis is currently at 180°. Which adjustment should be made?

A) Rotate axis toward 180°
B) Rotate axis toward 90°
C) Rotate axis toward 135°
D) Rotate axis toward 45°

**Correct Answer: A) Rotate axis toward 180°**

**Explanation:** In Jackson Cross Cylinder (JCC) testing, when the patient reports that the first position (with the minus cylinder axis aligned with the trial frame axis) is clearer, this indicates that the current axis is correct or should be moved toward the direction of the minus cylinder axis. Since the first position is clearer and the cylinder axis is at 180°, the axis should be rotated toward 180° (no change or slight adjustment within 5°). The "pound sign" rule applies: if the first position (where the minus cylinder axis aligns with the trial frame axis) is clearer, the axis is already close to correct.

### Question 3

A patient's retinoscopy findings show a reflex that moves "with" the sweep in all meridians. The current lens in place is +2.00 DS. What should be done?

A) Add minus power
B) Add plus power
C) The endpoint has been reached
D) Rotate the streak orientation

**Correct Answer: B) Add plus power**

**Explanation:** In retinoscopy, a "with" movement indicates that the far point is behind the eye, meaning the eye is relatively hyperopic compared to the current lens. To neutralize the reflex, plus power must be added. When the reflex shows "with" movement, you need to add plus lenses until the reflex fills the pupil and shows no movement (neutralization). Conversely, "against" movement requires adding minus power.

### Question 4

Which of the following represents the correct formula for calculating the total magnification of a direct ophthalmoscope?

A) 60D × (Refractive Error)
B) (60D / Refractive Error of Eye) × 1
C) 60D / (Refractive Error of Eye)
D) (60D - Refractive Error) × 1

**Correct Answer: B) (60D / Refractive Error of Eye) × 1**

**Explanation:** The direct ophthalmoscope produces an upright, virtual image that is magnified approximately 15 times in an emmetropic eye. The magnification formula is: Magnification = 60D / Refractive Error of Eye. For an emmetropic eye (60D), this gives approximately 15× magnification. The high magnification makes the direct ophthalmoscope excellent for examining small retinal details, but the small field of view (approximately 10°) limits its use for peripheral examination.

### Question 5

A patient has a distance PD of 64mm and a near PD of 60mm. What is the appropriate near PD for a working distance of 40cm?

A) 58mm
B) 60mm
C) 62mm
D) 64mm

**Correct Answer: B) 60mm**

**Explanation:** The near pupillary distance (PD) is always smaller than the distance PD due to convergence when viewing near targets. The typical difference between distance and near PD is approximately 3-4mm for a standard working distance of 40cm. The near PD can be calculated using the formula: Near PD = Distance PD × (Distance to target / (Distance to target + Vertex Distance to Center of Rotation)). For practical purposes, the near PD at 40cm is typically 3-4mm less than the distance PD. Given the measured near PD of 60mm, this is the appropriate value to use.

### Question 6

In binocular cross-cylinder testing, if the patient reports the line pairs are equal but the lines appear in front of the target, what is the diagnosis?

A) Emmetropia
B) Hyperopia
C) Myopia
D) Astigmatism

**Correct Answer: B) Hyperopia**

**Explanation:** The binocular cross-cylinder (BCC) test assesses the accommodative response relative to the focal plane of the target. When both line pairs are equal (the horizontal and vertical lines of the cross grid), but the lines appear in front of the target plane, this indicates that the patient is accommodating more than required to focus at the target distance. This "lead of accommodation" is characteristic of hyperopia, where the patient must maintain accommodation even for distance vision. The patient is effectively over-accommodating relative to the target distance.

### Question 7

What is the expected amplitude of accommodation for a 25-year-old patient according to Hofstetter's formula?

A) 8.50D
B) 10.00D
C) 11.50D
D) 13.50D

**Correct Answer: C) 11.50D**

**Explanation:** Hofstetter's formula for the expected minimum amplitude of accommodation is: 15 - 0.25 × Age. For a 25-year-old: 15 - 0.25 × 25 = 15 - 6.25 = 8.75D. However, Hofstetter also provided a formula for average amplitude: 18.5 - 0.30 × Age, and maximum amplitude: 25 - 0.40 × Age. For a 25-year-old using the average formula: 18.5 - 0.30 × 25 = 18.5 - 7.5 = 11.0D. The closest answer to the average expected amplitude would be 11.50D, though the minimum expected would be 8.75D.

### Question 8

Which portion of the retina corresponds to the blind spot on visual field testing?

A) Fovea
B) Optic disc
C) Macula
D) Ora serrata

**Correct Answer: B) Optic disc**

**Explanation:** The blind spot in visual field testing corresponds to the location of the optic disc, which is approximately 15° temporal to the fovea and slightly below the horizontal meridian. The optic disc has no photoreceptors because it is the point where the optic nerve exits the eye and retinal vessels enter. This creates a physiological scotoma that is approximately 5-7° wide and 7-9° tall. The blind spot is an important landmark in visual field testing and can be enlarged in conditions such as papilledema or optic nerve drusen.

## Section 2: Ocular Disease & Pathology

### Question 9

A 60-year-old diabetic patient presents with sudden vision loss in the right eye. Fundus examination shows a cherry-red spot with surrounding pale retina. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO)
B) Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO)
C) Retinal detachment
D) Vitreous hemorrhage

**Correct Answer: B) Central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO)**

**Explanation:** The classic presentation of central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO) includes sudden, painless vision loss with a cherry-red spot at the fovea surrounded by pale, ischemic retina. The cherry-red spot appears because the fovea receives its blood supply from the choroid (maintaining its normal red color), while the surrounding retina becomes pale due to lack of perfusion from the central retinal artery. CRAO is an ophthalmic emergency that requires immediate intervention within 90-120 minutes for any chance of visual recovery. In diabetic patients, the risk is increased due to vascular disease.

### Question 10

Which of the following is NOT a characteristic feature of primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG)?

A) Elevated intraocular pressure
B) Open anterior chamber angle
C) Optic nerve cupping
D) Pigment dispersion on the corneal endothelium

**Correct Answer: D) Pigment dispersion on the corneal endothelium**

**Explanation:** Pigment dispersion on the corneal endothelium (Krukenberg spindle) is characteristic of pigment dispersion syndrome, not primary open-angle glaucoma. POAG is characterized by elevated intraocular pressure (though normal-tension glaucoma is a variant), an open anterior chamber angle on gonioscopy, and progressive optic nerve cupping with corresponding visual field loss. Pigment dispersion syndrome is a separate entity where pigment is released from the iris pigment epithelium due to rubbing against the zonular fibers, leading to pigment deposition on various ocular structures including the corneal endothelium.

### Question 11

A patient presents with a painful red eye, photophobia, and small, non-reactive pupils. Slit lamp examination shows cells and flare in the anterior chamber. What is the MOST appropriate initial treatment?

A) Topical antibiotics
B) Topical corticosteroids
C) Topical beta-blockers
D) Oral antivirals

**Correct Answer: B) Topical corticosteroids**

**Explanation:** The clinical presentation is consistent with anterior uveitis (iritis), characterized by pain, photophobia, small irregular pupil, and cells and flare in the anterior chamber. The mainstay of treatment for anterior uveitis is topical corticosteroids to reduce inflammation, along with cycloplegic agents (such as atropine or cyclopentolate) to relieve ciliary spasm and prevent posterior synechiae formation. Antibiotics are not indicated unless there is evidence of bacterial infection. Beta-blockers are used for glaucoma, and antivirals are used for viral keratitis.

### Question 12

In the classification of diabetic retinopathy, which finding indicates "severe non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy" according to the ETDRS criteria?

A) Presence of cotton-wool spots
B) Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA)
C) Venous beading in two quadrants
D) Any of the "4-2-1 rule" features

**Correct Answer: D) Any of the "4-2-1 rule" features**

**Explanation:** According to the Early Treatment Diabetic Retinopathy Study (ETDRS), severe non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR) is defined by the presence of any one of the following (the "4-2-1 rule"): (1) Severe retinal hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants, (2) Venous beading in 2 or more quadrants, or (3) Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA) in 1 or more quadrants. The presence of any ONE of these features qualifies as severe NPDR. Cotton-wool spots and IRMA alone may be present in moderate NPDR but become significant in the context of the 4-2-1 rule.

### Question 13

Which type of cataract is MOST commonly associated with chronic corticosteroid use?

A) Nuclear sclerotic cataract
B) Cortical cataract
C) Posterior subcapsular cataract
D) Anterior subcapsular cataract

**Correct Answer: C) Posterior subcapsular cataract**

**Explanation:** Posterior subcapsular cataract (PSC) is the type most commonly associated with chronic corticosteroid use, whether systemic, inhaled, or topical. PSCs appear as granular opacities at the posterior capsule just anterior to the posterior pole of the lens. They cause significant visual symptoms, particularly glare and difficulty with near vision, because they are located near the nodal point of the eye. Other causes of PSC include diabetes, trauma, inflammation, and ionizing radiation. Nuclear sclerotic cataracts are typically age-related, while cortical cataracts are associated with diabetes and UV exposure.

### Question 14

A patient presents with a triangular-shaped area of conjunctival growth extending onto the cornea from the nasal side. The lesion has a fleshy head and a stock of fibrovascular tissue. What is the diagnosis?

A) Pinguecula
B) Pterygium
C) Conjunctival nevus
D) Squamous cell carcinoma

**Correct Answer: B) Pterygium**

**Explanation:** A pterygium is a triangular, fibrovascular growth of conjunctival tissue that extends onto the cornea, typically from the nasal side due to increased UV exposure in this area (the "light funnel" effect). The pterygium consists of a head (the advancing edge on the cornea), a neck, and a body (the fleshy portion on the conjunctiva). It is associated with UV exposure, chronic irritation, and dry conditions. A pinguecula is a similar lesion but does not extend onto the cornea. Conjunctival nevi are pigmented lesions, and squamous cell carcinoma presents as a more irregular, vascularized growth with possible hemorrhage.

### Question 15

In age-related macular degeneration (AMD), which finding is characteristic of the "wet" (neovascular) form but NOT the "dry" (non-neovascular) form?

A) Drusen
B) Geographic atrophy
C) Choroidal neovascularization
D) Pigmentary changes

**Correct Answer: C) Choroidal neovascularization**

**Explanation:** Choroidal neovascularization (CNV) is the hallmark of wet (neovascular) age-related macular degeneration. CNV involves the growth of abnormal blood vessels from the choroid through Bruch's membrane into the sub-retinal or sub-RPE space. These vessels are fragile and can leak fluid and blood, leading to rapid vision loss. Drusen, geographic atrophy, and pigmentary changes can be present in both dry and wet AMD, but CNV is specific to the wet form. Treatment with anti-VEGF agents targets this abnormal vascular growth.

## Section 3: Optical Dispensing & Instrumentation

### Question 16

A prescription reads: -3.00 -1.00 x 45. The prescription is transposed to another format. Which of the following is correct?

A) -4.00 +1.00 x 135
B) -4.00 +1.00 x 45
C) -3.00 +1.00 x 135
D) -2.00 +1.00 x 135

**Correct Answer: A) -4.00 +1.00 x 135**

**Explanation:** To transpose from minus cylinder form to plus cylinder form: (1) Add the sphere and cylinder powers algebraically: -3.00 + (-1.00) = -4.00. This becomes the new sphere power. (2) Change the sign of the cylinder: -1.00 becomes +1.00. (3) Rotate the axis by 90°: 45° becomes 135°. The transposed prescription is -4.00 +1.00 x 135.

### Question 17

What is the induced prism in a lens with a power of +4.00 DS when the patient's pupil is located 5mm below the optical center? Use Prentice's rule.

A) 1.00 prism diopter base up
B) 2.00 prism diopters base up
C) 1.00 prism diopter base down
D) 2.00 prism diopters base down

**Correct Answer: B) 2.00 prism diopters base up**

**Explanation:** Prentice's rule states: Prism = F × d, where F is the lens power in diopters and d is the distance from the optical center in centimeters. Prism = 4.00D × 0.5cm = 2.00 prism diopters. For a plus lens, the base is toward the optical center. If the patient's pupil is below the optical center, the base of the induced prism is up (toward the optical center). Therefore, the induced prism is 2.00 prism diopters base up.

### Question 18

A patient requires a +3.00 DS reading addition. If the spectacle lens is made with a center thickness of 4mm and has a refractive index of 1.50, what is the approximate edge thickness for a 50mm diameter lens?

A) 1.6mm
B) 2.4mm
C) 3.2mm
D) 4.0mm

**Correct Answer: A) 1.6mm**

**Explanation:** For a plus lens, the center is thickest and the edge is thinnest. The thickness variation can be calculated using the sagittal depth formula. Approximate sagittal depth = (D × r²) / (2(n-1)), where D is power, r is the semi-diameter, and n is the refractive index. Sag = (3.00 × 25²) / (2 × 0.50) = (3.00 × 625) / 1 = 1875 / 1 = approximately 1.875mm of sagittal depth. Edge thickness ≈ Center thickness - Sagittal depth = 4mm - 2.4mm = 1.6mm.

### Question 19

Which lens material has the HIGHEST Abbé number (lowest chromatic aberration)?

A) Polycarbonate (n=1.59)
B) Trivex (n=1.53)
C) CR-39 (n=1.50)
D) High-index 1.67

**Correct Answer: C) CR-39 (n=1.50)**

**Explanation:** CR-39 (Columbia Resin #39) has an Abbé number of approximately 58, which is the highest among common ophthalmic lens materials. The Abbé number indicates the material's resistance to chromatic aberration; higher numbers mean less dispersion and better optical quality. Polycarbonate has an Abbé number of about 30 (higher chromatic aberration), Trivex has about 45, and high-index materials (1.67) have Abbé numbers around 32. While high-index materials reduce lens thickness for strong prescriptions, they sacrifice some optical quality due to increased chromatic aberration.

### Question 20

In progressive addition lenses (PALs), what is the term for the area on each side of the corridor that contains unwanted cylinder and distortion?

A) Fitting cross
B) Near zone
C) Distance zone
D) Blend zone

**Correct Answer: D) Blend zone**

**Explanation:** The blend zone (also called the peripheral distortion zone or unwanted astigmatism zone) is the area on either side of the progressive corridor that contains unwanted cylinder and distortion. This is an unavoidable consequence of the progressive design, where the lens must transition from distance to near powers while maintaining surface continuity. Modern PAL designs attempt to minimize and spread this distortion, but it cannot be eliminated entirely. The corridor (progressive zone) is the channel connecting distance and near zones, while the fitting cross marks the position where the pupil center should align.

### Question 21

A patient has a vertical imbalance of 3 prism diopters between the two eyes at the reading level. Which solution is MOST appropriate?

A) Adjust the frame fitting
B) Use slab-off prism
C) Change the lens material
D) Increase the add power

**Correct Answer: B) Use slab-off prism**

**Explanation:** When vertical imbalance exceeds approximately 1.5-2.0 prism diopters at near, patients may experience symptoms of vertical diplopia or eyestrain. Slab-off prism (also called bicentric grinding) is a technique used to correct vertical imbalance by removing prism from the lower portion of one lens. The prism is ground with the base up, which is applied to the lens with the most minus (or least plus) power in the vertical meridian. This restores balance at the reading level. Reverse slab-off (base down) may also be used on the more plus lens.

## Section 4: Contact Lens Practice

### Question 22

What is the oxygen transmissibility (Dk/t) threshold generally considered adequate for daily wear soft contact lenses?

A) 10 × 10⁻¹¹
B) 24 × 10⁻¹¹
C) 50 × 10⁻¹¹
D) 100 × 10⁻¹¹

**Correct Answer: B) 24 × 10⁻¹¹**

**Explanation:** For daily wear soft contact lenses, a Dk/t of approximately 24 × 10⁻¹¹ (cm²/s) (mLO₂/mL × mmHg) is generally considered adequate to prevent corneal hypoxia during waking hours. This threshold ensures sufficient oxygen reaches the cornea to maintain normal metabolism and prevent complications such as corneal edema, neovascularization, and epithelial microcysts. For extended wear lenses, higher Dk/t values (≥ 87 × 10⁻¹¹) are recommended to account for overnight wear when the cornea receives oxygen primarily from the aqueous humor and eyelid vasculature.

### Question 23

A patient wearing soft contact lenses develops a painful red eye with a white infiltrate in the peripheral cornea. There is a small overlying epithelial defect. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Bacterial keratitis
B) Contact lens peripheral ulcer (CLPU)
C) Viral keratitis
D) Fungal keratitis

**Correct Answer: B) Contact lens peripheral ulcer (CLPU)**

**Explanation:** Contact lens peripheral ulcer (CLPU) is a sterile inflammatory condition characterized by a small, well-circumscribed white infiltrate in the peripheral cornea, typically with an overlying epithelial defect. It occurs in contact lens wearers and is thought to be a hypersensitivity reaction to bacterial antigens or toxins. Unlike bacterial keratitis, CLPU is usually less painful, the infiltrate is smaller (typically < 1mm) and peripheral, and there is minimal anterior chamber reaction. Treatment involves discontinuing lens wear and may include topical antibiotics to prevent secondary infection.

### Question 24

In rigid gas permeable (RGP) lens fitting, what does a "three-point touch" pattern indicate?

A) Flat fitting
B) Steep fitting
C) Optimal fitting
D) Warped cornea

**Correct Answer: C) Optimal fitting**

**Explanation:** A "three-point touch" pattern on fluorescein evaluation indicates an optimal RGP lens fit. This pattern shows light touch (minimal clearance) at three points: the central cornea (apical area) and two points on the peripheral cornea (typically mid-peripheral). The lens distributes its weight evenly across these three zones, minimizing mechanical stress on any single area. This ensures good centration, minimal lens movement, adequate tear exchange, and patient comfort. A flat fit shows excessive apical clearance with peripheral bearing, while a steep fit shows central pooling with mid-peripheral bearing.

### Question 25

A patient with keratoconus is being fitted with a specialty contact lens. Which type of lens design is MOST appropriate for moderate to advanced keratoconus?

A) Spherical soft lens
B) Toric soft lens
C) Scleral lens
D) Standard RGP lens

**Correct Answer: C) Scleral lens**

**Explanation:** For moderate to advanced keratoconus, scleral lenses are often the most appropriate choice. Scleral lenses vault entirely over the cornea and land on the sclera, providing a smooth optical surface that masks the irregular corneal astigmatism. They offer excellent visual acuity, comfort, and stability. Standard RGP lenses may not center well on advanced cones and can cause corneal scarring from bearing on the apex. Soft lenses (even toric) cannot adequately correct the irregular astigmatism in moderate to advanced cases. Mini-scleral and corneo-scleral designs are also used depending on the severity and location of the cone.

### Question 26

What is the approximate difference in power required when converting a spectacle prescription of -5.00 DS to a contact lens prescription? Assume a vertex distance of 12mm.

A) -4.75 DS
B) -4.80 DS
C) -5.25 DS
D) No change needed

**Correct Answer: B) -4.80 DS**

**Explanation:** For myopic corrections, the contact lens power is less minus (or more plus) than the spectacle power because the lens is moved closer to the eye (vertex distance compensation). The formula is: Fc = Fs / (1 - dFs), where Fc is contact lens power, Fs is spectacle power, and d is vertex distance in meters. Fc = -5.00 / (1 - 0.012 × -5.00) = -5.00 / (1 + 0.06) = -5.00 / 1.06 = -4.72D. Rounding to the nearest available power gives approximately -4.75D to -4.80D. For practical purposes, a -4.75D contact lens would be selected.

## Section 5: Pediatric Optometry & Binocular Vision

### Question 27

A 6-month-old infant is found to have a constant esotropia of 40 prism diopters. The cycloplegic refraction reveals +2.00 DS OU. What is the MOST appropriate management?

A) Observation until age 2
B) Prescribe the full hyperopic correction
C) Prescribe half the hyperopic correction
D) Immediate surgical referral

**Correct Answer: D) Immediate surgical referral**

**Explanation:** This infant presents with infantile esotropia (congenital esotropia), characterized by large-angle, constant esotropia presenting before 6 months of age. The deviation is typically not accommodative in nature, meaning it does not respond significantly to hyperopic correction. Unlike accommodative esotropia, which responds to glasses, infantile esotropia requires surgical intervention. Early surgery (ideally before 12-18 months) is recommended to promote the development of binocular vision. The patient may still need glasses for any residual refractive error, but the primary treatment is surgical.

### Question 28

In the cover-uncover test, if the uncovered eye moves outward (temporally) when the cover is placed over the fellow eye, what type of deviation is present?

A) Esophoria
B) Exophoria
C) Esotropia
D) Exotropia

**Correct Answer: C) Esotropia**

**Explanation:** In the cover-uncover test, a tropia (manifest deviation) is detected by observing the movement of the uncovered eye when the fellow eye is covered. If the uncovered eye moves outward (temporally) to take up fixation, this indicates that the eye was turned inward (esotropia) before the cover was placed. The outward movement is the eye's effort to fixate on the target. This is different from a phoria, which is detected on alternate cover testing. An esophoria would show inward movement of the eye when the cover is removed during the alternate cover test.

### Question 29

What is the critical period for the development of amblyopia?

A) Birth to 3 months
B) 3 months to 3 years
C) Birth to 6-8 years
D) 6-12 years

**Correct Answer: C) Birth to 6-8 years**

**Explanation:** The critical period for amblyopia development extends from birth to approximately 6-8 years of age, with the highest risk during the first 3 years. During this time, the visual system is highly plastic and requires clear, focused visual input from both eyes to develop normal visual acuity and binocular function. Interruption of visual input during this period (from strabismus, anisometropia, or deprivation) can result in amblyopia. After age 8-10, the visual cortex becomes less plastic, and amblyopia becomes much more difficult to treat, though recent research suggests some plasticity remains even in adults.

### Question 30

Which of the following is characteristic of convergence insufficiency?

A) High AC/A ratio
B) Receded near point of convergence
C) Esotropia at near
D) Exophoria greater at distance than near

**Correct Answer: B) Receded near point of convergence**

**Explanation:** Convergence insufficiency (CI) is characterized by an inability to maintain adequate convergence for near tasks. Key findings include: (1) Receded near point of convergence (NPC), typically > 6cm; (2) Exophoria greater at near than at distance; (3) Low AC/A ratio; and (4) Reduced positive fusional vergence amplitudes. Patients often report symptoms of asthenopia, headaches, and difficulty with near work. Treatment typically involves vision therapy (orthoptics) to improve convergence ability. A high AC/A ratio is seen in accommodative esotropia, not convergence insufficiency.

### Question 31

A 4-year-old child presents with 20/40 visual acuity in each eye, normal eye alignment, and no significant refractive error on cycloplegic retinoscopy. What is the MOST appropriate management?

A) Prescribe glasses
B) Patch one eye
C) Reassure parents and recheck in 6 months
D) Refer for neurological evaluation

**Correct Answer: C) Reassure parents and recheck in 6 months**

**Explanation:** For a 4-year-old child, 20/40 visual acuity is within normal limits for age. Visual acuity in children typically develops gradually: 20/200 at birth, 20/50 by age 1, 20/30-20/40 by age 3-4, and approaching 20/20 by age 5-6. In the absence of strabismus, significant refractive error, or other pathology, this level of acuity is age-appropriate. The child should be monitored, and visual acuity will typically improve with maturation. No intervention is needed unless there is a significant difference between eyes or acuity fails to improve as expected.

## Section 6: Ocular Pharmacology & Therapeutics

### Question 32

Which of the following topical agents is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor used in the treatment of glaucoma?

A) Brimonidine
B) Dorzolamide
C) Latanoprost
D) Timolol

**Correct Answer: B) Dorzolamide**

**Explanation:** Dorzolamide (Trusopt) is a topical carbonic anhydrase inhibitor (CAI) used to reduce intraocular pressure in glaucoma. It works by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase in the ciliary body, which reduces aqueous humor production by approximately 15-20%. Brimonidine is an alpha-2 agonist, latanoprost is a prostaglandin analog, and timolol is a beta-blocker. All are used in glaucoma treatment but have different mechanisms of action. Oral acetazolamide is also a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor used for more aggressive IOP lowering.

### Question 33

Which topical anesthetic is MOST appropriate for diagnostic procedures due to its minimal corneal toxicity?

A) Proparacaine 0.5%
B) Tetracaine 0.5%
C) Benoxinate 0.4%
D) Cocaine 4%

**Correct Answer: A) Proparacaine 0.5%**

**Explanation:** Proparacaine is considered the preferred topical anesthetic for diagnostic procedures because it causes the least amount of corneal epithelial toxicity among available agents. It provides rapid anesthesia (onset within 15-20 seconds) lasting approximately 10-20 minutes. Tetracaine is also effective but causes more epithelial toxicity and stinging upon instillation. Benoxinate (oxybuprocaine) is also used but is less common in some regions. Cocaine has significant corneal toxicity and is rarely used in clinical practice. Importantly, topical anesthetics should never be prescribed for home use due to their toxicity and potential for abuse.

### Question 34

A patient develops an allergic reaction to a topical ophthalmic medication. Which preservative is MOST commonly associated with ocular allergic reactions?

A) Benzalkonium chloride (BAK)
B) Chlorobutanol
C) Polyquad
D) Sodium perborate

**Correct Answer: A) Benzalkonium chloride (BAK)**

**Explanation:** Benzalkonium chloride (BAK) is the most commonly used preservative in ophthalmic preparations and is also the most common cause of preservative-related allergic reactions and ocular surface toxicity. BAK is a quaternary ammonium compound that can disrupt the tear film, damage corneal epithelial cells, and induce inflammatory responses. Chlorobutanol and Polyquad are alternative preservatives with lower toxicity profiles. Sodium perborate (Purite) is an oxidative preservative that breaks down into water and oxygen, making it a preferred choice for patients with sensitivities.

### Question 35

Which mydriatic agent has the longest duration of action?

A) Phenylephrine 2.5%
B) Tropicamide 1%
C) Cyclopentolate 1%
D) Atropine 1%

**Correct Answer: D) Atropine 1%**

**Explanation:** Atropine has the longest duration of action among mydriatic agents, with mydriasis lasting 7-14 days and cycloplegia lasting up to 2 weeks. This is because atropine binds strongly to muscarinic receptors and is slowly eliminated from the eye. Cyclopentolate causes mydriasis for 24-48 hours, tropicamide for 4-6 hours, and phenylephrine (which is only a mydriatic, not a cycloplegic) for 3-5 hours. Due to its long duration, atropine is typically reserved for specific indications such as cycloplegic refraction in children with suspected latent hyperopia or for treatment of uveitis.

## Section 7: Visual Fields & Neuro-Ophthalmology

### Question 36

Which visual field defect is characteristic of a pituitary adenoma compressing the optic chiasm?

A) Homonymous hemianopia
B) Bitemporal hemianopia
C) Central scotoma
D) Altitudinal defect

**Correct Answer: B) Bitemporal hemianopia**

**Explanation:** Pituitary adenomas typically cause bitemporal hemianopia because they compress the central portion of the optic chiasm where the crossing nasal fibers from both eyes are located. This results in loss of the temporal visual field in both eyes. The bitemporal nature distinguishes it from other field defects. Homonymous hemianopia results from lesions posterior to the chiasm (optic tract, LGN, optic radiations, or occipital cortex). Central scotoma is seen in macular disease or optic neuropathies. Altitudinal defects are characteristic of anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION).

### Question 37

A patient presents with a relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) in the right eye. What does this finding indicate?

A) Right optic nerve pathology
B) Left optic nerve pathology
C) Bilateral optic nerve disease
D) Retinal detachment

**Correct Answer: A) Right optic nerve pathology**

**Explanation:** A relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) in the right eye indicates pathology affecting the right afferent visual pathway, most commonly the right optic nerve. The swinging flashlight test reveals that when light is swung from the left eye to the right eye, the right pupil dilates instead of constricting, because the right eye is receiving a weaker afferent signal. This occurs because the afferent limb of the pupillary light reflex (optic nerve) is compromised, while the efferent limb (oculomotor nerve) remains intact. Common causes include optic neuritis, ischemic optic neuropathy, and compressive lesions of the optic nerve.

### Question 38

In a patient with a left homonymous hemianopia, where is the lesion MOST likely located?

A) Right optic nerve
B) Optic chiasm
C) Right optic tract or post-chiasmal pathway
D) Left occipital cortex

**Correct Answer: C) Right optic tract or post-chiasmal pathway**

**Explanation:** A homonymous hemianopia refers to loss of the same half of the visual field in both eyes. A left homonymous hemianopia means loss of the left half of the visual field in both eyes, which indicates a lesion affecting the visual pathway on the right side, posterior to the chiasm. This could involve the right optic tract, right lateral geniculate nucleus, right optic radiations, or right occipital cortex. The key rule is that homonymous field defects localize to the contralateral side of the lesion, posterior to the optic chiasm.

### Question 39

Which visual field testing pattern is MOST appropriate for monitoring glaucoma progression?

A) Full field 120 point screening
B) 24-2 SITA Standard
C) 10-2 SITA Fast
D) Peripheral 60 screening

**Correct Answer: B) 24-2 SITA Standard**

**Explanation:** The 24-2 test pattern is the standard for glaucoma monitoring because it evaluates the central 24 degrees of the visual field with test points at 6-degree intervals, capturing the majority of the area where glaucomatous field loss occurs. The SITA (Swedish Interactive Thresholding Algorithm) Standard provides reliable threshold sensitivity values needed for detecting change over time. The 10-2 pattern focuses on the central 10 degrees and is useful for macular disease or advanced glaucoma with central involvement. Full field screening tests are useful for detecting neurological defects but lack the sensitivity needed for glaucoma monitoring.

## Section 8: Low Vision & Special Populations

### Question 40

What is the World Health Organization (WHO) classification for "moderate visual impairment"?

A) Visual acuity worse than 20/60 to 20/400
B) Visual acuity worse than 20/70 to 20/160
C) Visual acuity worse than 20/200 to 20/400
D) Visual acuity worse than 20/400

**Correct Answer: B) Visual acuity worse than 20/70 to 20/160**

**Explanation:** According to the WHO International Classification of Diseases (ICD-11), visual impairment is classified as follows: (1) Mild visual impairment: visual acuity worse than 20/30 to 20/60; (2) Moderate visual impairment: visual acuity worse than 20/60 to 20/200 (approximately 20/70 to 20/160 range); (3) Severe visual impairment: visual acuity worse than 20/200 to 20/400; (4) Blindness: visual acuity worse than 20/400. The specific ranges may vary slightly between classification systems, but moderate impairment generally corresponds to visual acuity significantly reduced but better than legal blindness threshold (20/200).

### Question 41

Which low vision device provides the HIGHEST magnification for near tasks?

A) Handheld magnifier (2×)
B) Stand magnifier (4×)
C) Electronic video magnifier (10-60×)
D) Spectacle-mounted microscope (6×)

**Correct Answer: C) Electronic video magnifier (10-60×)**

**Explanation:** Electronic video magnifiers (CCTV systems) provide the highest magnification range among low vision devices, typically offering 10× to 60× magnification or even higher. They also allow for contrast enhancement, inverse polarity (white text on black background), and other features that improve readability for patients with low vision. Handheld and stand magnifiers are typically limited to lower magnification powers (2× to 20× maximum). Spectacle-mounted microscopes (high-plus lenses) are limited by working distance and depth of field. Video magnifiers are especially useful for patients with advanced macular degeneration or other conditions causing severe vision loss.

### Question 42

A patient with age-related macular degeneration has a central scotoma and uses an eccentric viewing technique. What is this technique called?

A) Eccentric fixation
B) Preferred retinal locus (PRL)
C) Fixation displacement
D) Parafoveal viewing

**Correct Answer: B) Preferred retinal locus (PRL)**

**Explanation:** Preferred retinal locus (PRL) is the term used to describe the eccentric viewing technique employed by patients with central scotomas (such as those from AMD). When the fovea is damaged, patients naturally develop an alternative fixation point on a healthy area of the retina, typically just above or temporal to the damaged fovea. This PRL becomes the new "pseudo-fovea" for visual tasks. Eccentric viewing training can help patients optimize their use of the PRL. The terms "eccentric fixation" and "parafoveal viewing" describe similar concepts, but PRL is the standard clinical terminology.

According to DHA regulations, what is the minimum continuing education requirement for optometrists practicing in Dubai?

A patient requests a copy of their medical records. According to DHA regulations, what is the MAXIMUM time allowed to provide these records?

### Question 45

Under DHA regulations, what is the retention period for adult patient medical records?

A) 5 years
B) 7 years
C) 10 years
D) 15 years

**Correct Answer: C) 10 years**

**Explanation:** According to DHA regulations, medical records for adult patients must be retained for a minimum of 10 years from the date of last encounter or as specified by the facility's policy (whichever is longer). For pediatric patients, records must be retained until the patient reaches age 25 or for 10 years from the last encounter, whichever is longer. Which of the following is considered a violation of DHA professional conduct standards?

A) Referring a patient to a specialist
B) Discussing treatment options with a patient
C) Providing treatment outside the scope of practice

## Section 10: Advanced Clinical Scenarios

### Question 47

A 35-year-old female presents with bilateral proptosis, lid lag, and restricted extraocular movements. She also reports heat intolerance and weight loss. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Myasthenia gravis
B) Thyroid eye disease (Graves' ophthalmopathy)
C) Chronic progressive external ophthalmoplegia
D) Orbital pseudotumor

**Correct Answer: B) Thyroid eye disease (Graves' ophthalmopathy)**

**Explanation:** The combination of bilateral proptosis, lid lag (von Graefe's sign), restricted extraocular movements (particularly elevation and abduction), along with systemic symptoms of hyperthyroidism (heat intolerance, weight loss) is classic for thyroid eye disease (TED), also known as Graves' ophthalmopathy. TED is an autoimmune condition where antibodies target the thyroid-stimulating hormone receptor, causing inflammation and expansion of orbital tissues including extraocular muscles and fat. Myasthenia gravis causes ptosis (not proptosis) and fatigable weakness. CPEO is a slowly progressive myopathy. Orbital pseudotumor is typically unilateral and painful.

### Question 48

A 70-year-old patient with a history of hypertension presents with sudden, painless vision loss OD. Examination reveals vision of hand motion, a relative afferent pupillary defect, and a swollen, hyperemic optic nerve head. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?

A) Optic neuritis
B) Non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION)
C) Arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AAION)
D) Papilledema

**Correct Answer: B) Non-arteritic anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (NAION)**

**Explanation:** NAION is the most common cause of acute optic neuropathy in patients over 50, characterized by sudden, painless vision loss, RAPD, and optic disc edema. Risk factors include hypertension, diabetes, and a small crowded optic disc ("disc at risk"). The disc edema in NAION is typically pale and sectoral. Arteritic AION (AAION) should be considered in older patients (>70) and is associated with giant cell arteritis (GCA) symptoms including headache, scalp tenderness, and jaw claudication; ESR and CRP are typically elevated. Optic neuritis typically affects younger patients and is associated with pain on eye movement. Papilledema causes bilateral disc edema.

### Question 49

During gonioscopy, the trabecular meshwork is barely visible with the iris nearly touching it. What grade on the Shaffer classification does this represent?

A) Grade 4 (open)
B) Grade 3 (open)
C) Grade 2 (narrow)
D) Grade 1 (extremely narrow)

**Correct Answer: D) Grade 1 (extremely narrow)**

**Explanation:** The Shaffer classification grades the angle width from 0 to 4: Grade 4 (35-45°) = wide open, all structures visible; Grade 3 (25-35°) = open, all structures visible; Grade 2 (20°) = narrow, trabecular meshwork barely visible; Grade 1 (10°) = extremely narrow, only Schwalbe's line visible; Grade 0 = closed. In this case, with the iris nearly touching the trabecular meshwork and the trabecular meshwork barely visible, this represents a Grade 1 (extremely narrow) angle that is at risk for closure. Such patients should be monitored closely and may require prophylactic laser peripheral iridotomy.

### Question 50

A patient presents with a dendritic corneal ulcer. Which finding is MOST consistent with herpes simplex keratitis versus other causes of dendritic lesions?

A) True dendrite with terminal bulbs
B) Pseudodendrite with heaped edges
C) Dendritic pattern with fluorescein staining only
D) Bilateral presentation

**Correct Answer: A) True dendrite with terminal bulbs**

**Explanation:** Herpes simplex keratitis produces a characteristic "true dendrite" with linear branching lesions that have terminal bulbs at the ends. These lesions show both fluorescein staining (defect) and rose bengal/lissamine green staining (devitalized cells at the edges). Pseudodendrites (seen in conditions like healing epithelial defects or Acanthamoeba keratitis) typically have heaped edges without true terminal bulbs. Herpes zoster keratitis can produce pseudodendrites that are more plaque-like. HSV dendrites are typically unilateral and can be differentiated from other causes by their characteristic morphology and staining pattern.

### Question 51

What is the recommended first-line treatment for bacterial conjunctivitis in an adult?

A) Oral antibiotics
B) Topical broad-spectrum antibiotics
C) Observation only
D) Topical corticosteroids

**Correct Answer: B) Topical broad-spectrum antibiotics**

**Explanation:** For uncomplicated bacterial conjunctivitis in adults, topical broad-spectrum antibiotics are the recommended first-line treatment. Options include fluoroquinolones (moxifloxacin, besifloxacin), aminoglycosides (tobramycin, gentamicin), or macrolides (azithromycin). Oral antibiotics are typically reserved for severe cases, pediatric patients, or when topical administration is difficult. Observation may be appropriate for mild cases as most bacterial conjunctivitis is self-limiting, but treatment shortens the duration of symptoms and reduces contagion. Corticosteroids are contraindicated in active infection due to the risk of worsening the infection.

### Question 52

Which statement about the pathophysiology of presbyopia is correct?

A) It results from decreased lens elasticity
B) It results from ciliary muscle weakness
C) It results from increased corneal curvature
D) It results from vitreous liquefaction

**Correct Answer: A) It results from decreased lens elasticity**

**Explanation:** Presbyopia is primarily caused by age-related changes in the crystalline lens, specifically the loss of lens elasticity due to changes in the lens capsule and lens proteins. As the lens ages, it becomes harder and less able to change shape during accommodation (the Helmholtz theory). While ciliary muscle function may also decline with age, the primary mechanism is the loss of lens elasticity. This explains why presbyopia typically begins around age 40-45 and progresses until about age 60. Treatment options include reading glasses, multifocal lenses, contact lenses, and surgical interventions.

### Question 53

A patient with diabetes presents with sudden onset of floaters and flashing lights. What is the MOST appropriate action?

A) Reassure and monitor
B) Schedule routine follow-up in 1 month
C) Perform dilated fundus examination immediately
D) Prescribe topical anti-inflammatory drops

**Correct Answer: C) Perform dilated fundus examination immediately**

**Explanation:** Sudden onset of floaters and flashing lights (photopsia) in a diabetic patient is an ocular emergency that requires immediate dilated fundus examination. These symptoms are concerning for posterior vitreous detachment (PVD), retinal tear, or retinal detachment. Diabetic patients are at increased risk for these conditions due to vitreous changes and retinal vascular disease. If a retinal tear is found, laser retinopexy may be needed. If a retinal detachment is present, urgent surgical intervention is required. Delaying examination could result in permanent vision loss if a treatable condition progresses.

### Question 54

In the context of automated perimetry, what does a "cloverleaf" visual field pattern suggest?

A) Glaucoma
B) Optic neuritis
C) Patient inattention or fatigue
D) Retinitis pigmentosa

**Correct Answer: C) Patient inattention or fatigue**

**Explanation:** A "cloverleaf" pattern on automated perimetry is an artifact pattern caused by patient inattention or fatigue. It appears as reduced sensitivity in a specific pattern that resembles a cloverleaf shape, typically with more depression in the superior and inferior nasal quadrants. This occurs when the patient becomes tired or loses concentration during testing and stops responding reliably to stimuli in certain areas. Other artifacts include "ring scotoma" from incorrect corrective lens placement and "eyelid artifact" from ptosis. Reliable fields require good patient cooperation and understanding of the test.

### Question 55

Which of the following is a characteristic feature of Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy?

A) Corneal neovascularization
B) Guttata (excrescences on Descemet's membrane)
C) Stromal scarring with lipid deposition
D) Recurrent epithelial erosions

**Correct Answer: B) Guttata (excrescences on Descemet's membrane)**

**Explanation:** Fuchs' endothelial corneal dystrophy is characterized by the development of corneal guttata, which are wart-like excrescences on Descemet's membrane caused by abnormal endothelial cells. These guttata appear as a "beaten metal" appearance on specular reflection. As the disease progresses, endothelial cell loss leads to corneal edema, bullous keratopathy, and decreased vision. The condition is more common in women and is often bilateral. Corneal neovascularization is seen in contact lens wear or inflammatory conditions. Lipid deposition is seen in arcus or lipid keratopathy. Recurrent erosions are characteristic of epithelial basement membrane dystrophy.
