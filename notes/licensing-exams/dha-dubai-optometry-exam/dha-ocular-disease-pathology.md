# Ocular Disease & Pathology for DHA Exam

## Introduction

Ocular disease and pathology represent one of the most critical domains in the Dubai Health Authority (DHA) Optometry licensing examination. This comprehensive guide covers the essential pathological conditions affecting the eye, their clinical presentations, diagnostic approaches, and management strategies. Optometrists must demonstrate thorough understanding of these conditions to ensure patient safety and appropriate referral decisions in clinical practice.

The DHA examination allocates approximately 25% of total marks to ocular disease and pathology questions, making this section crucial for achieving a passing score. Questions assess both theoretical knowledge and clinical decision-making skills through case-based scenarios.

## 1. Anterior Segment Disorders

### 1.1 Conjunctival Disorders

#### Conjunctivitis

**Bacterial Conjunctivitis**
Bacterial conjunctivitis is one of the most common ocular infections encountered in optometric practice. It presents with mucopurulent discharge, conjunctival injection, and matting of eyelids, particularly upon waking. The most common causative organisms include Staphylococcus aureus, Streptococcus pneumoniae, and Haemophilus influenzae.

**Clinical Features:**
- Unilateral or bilateral conjunctival injection
- Mucopurulent discharge (yellow-green)
- Morning crusting and eyelash matting
- Foreign body sensation
- Pre-auricular lymphadenopathy (in some cases)

**Management:**
- Broad-spectrum topical antibiotics (fluoroquinolones, aminoglycosides)
- Hygiene measures to prevent spread
- Usually self-limiting in 7-14 days without treatment
- Treatment reduces contagious period and symptom duration

**Viral Conjunctivitis**
Viral conjunctivitis, most commonly caused by adenovirus, is highly contagious and presents with watery discharge, follicular conjunctival reaction, and pre-auricular lymphadenopathy.

**Clinical Features:**
- Watery, serous discharge
- Follicular reaction on palpebral conjunctiva
- Pre-auricular lymphadenopathy (tender)
- Recent upper respiratory infection
- Petechial hemorrhages possible

**Pharyngoconjunctival Fever:**
Caused by adenovirus serotypes 3 and 7, characterized by:
- Fever
- Pharyngitis
- Conjunctivitis
- More common in children

**Epidemic Keratoconjunctivitis (EKC):**
Caused by adenovirus serotypes 8, 19, and 37, featuring:
- More severe conjunctivitis
- Pseudomembrane formation
- Subepithelial corneal infiltrates (after 2 weeks)
- May affect visual acuity

**Management:**
- Supportive care (cold compresses, artificial tears)
- Strict hygiene to prevent transmission
- No specific antiviral treatment available
- Pseudomembrane removal if present
- Topical steroids for severe corneal infiltrates (with caution)

**Allergic Conjunctivitis**
Allergic conjunctivitis represents a Type I hypersensitivity reaction to environmental allergens. It presents with bilateral itching, watery discharge, and chemosis.

**Types:**

1. **Seasonal Allergic Conjunctivitis (SAC)**
   - Most common form
   - Associated with seasonal allergens (pollen)
   - Bilateral itching and redness
   - Watery discharge

2. **Perennial Allergic Conjunctivitis (PAC)**
   - Year-round symptoms
   - Triggered by dust mites, pet dander
   - Similar presentation to SAC

3. **Vernal Keratoconjunctivitis (VKC)**
   - More severe, chronic form
   - Predominantly affects young males
   - Giant papillae on upper tarsus ("cobblestones")
   - Horner-Trantas dots (eosinophil deposits at limbus)
   - Shield ulcer may develop

4. **Atopic Keratoconjunctivitis (AKC)**
   - Associated with atopic dermatitis
   - Chronic, potentially sight-threatening
   - Eyelid involvement common
   - Increased risk of corneal complications

**Management:**
- Allergen avoidance when possible
- Cold compresses
- Topical antihistamines (olopatadine, ketotifen)
- Mast cell stabilizers
- Dual-action agents (antihistamine + mast cell stabilizer)
- Topical steroids for severe cases
- Systemic antihistamines for associated symptoms

#### Pinguecula and Pterygium

**Pinguecula:**
A pinguecula is a yellowish, elevated conjunctival lesion located nasally or temporally on the interpalpebral bulbar conjunctiva. It represents elastotic degeneration of conjunctival tissue due to ultraviolet exposure.

**Clinical Features:**
- Yellow-white, slightly elevated lesion
- Located at limbus, usually nasal
- May cause irritation or dry eye symptoms
- Does not extend onto cornea

**Pterygium:**
A pterygium is a wing-shaped, fibrovascular growth extending from the conjunctiva onto the cornea. It represents progression of pinguecula with corneal invasion.

**Clinical Features:**
- Triangular, wing-shaped growth
- Head on cornea, body on conjunctiva
- Stocker's line (iron deposition at advancing edge)
- May cause irregular astigmatism
- Can obstruct visual axis if progressive

**Management:**
- UV protection (sunglasses)
- Lubricating drops for irritation
- Surgical excision for:
  - Visual axis involvement
  - Significant astigmatism
  - Persistent inflammation
  - Cosmetic concerns
- Conjunctival autograft reduces recurrence rate
- Recurrence common (5-15% with modern techniques)

### 1.2 Corneal Disorders

#### Keratitis

**Bacterial Keratitis**
Bacterial keratitis is a sight-threatening corneal infection requiring urgent intervention. Risk factors include contact lens wear, corneal trauma, and pre-existing ocular surface disease.

**Common Organisms:**
- Pseudomonas aeruginosa (most common in contact lens wearers)
- Staphylococcus species
- Streptococcus pneumoniae
- Moraxella species

**Clinical Features:**
- Severe pain and photophobia
- Corneal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect
- Hypopyon (sterile or infectious)
- Conjunctival injection
- Mucopurulent discharge
- Lid edema

**Management:**
- Corneal scraping for culture and sensitivity
- Intensive topical antibiotics (hourly initially)
- Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, moxifloxacin, ofloxacin)
- Fortified antibiotics (cefazolin + tobramycin/gentamicin)
- Taper as clinical improvement observed
- Consider hospital admission for severe cases

**Viral Keratitis (Herpes Simplex)**
Herpes simplex keratitis (HSK) is a leading cause of infectious blindness in developed countries. It is caused by HSV-1 in most cases.

**Types of HSV Keratitis:**

1. **Epithelial Keratitis**
   - Dendritic ulcer (classic appearance)
   - Branching pattern with terminal bulbs
   - Fluorescein staining of defect
   - Rose Bengal staining of virus-infected cells at edge

2. **Stromal Keratitis**
   - Disciform keratitis (round stromal edema)
   - Immune-mediated stromal inflammation
   - May have underlying endotheliitis
   - Keratic precipitates possible

3. **Endotheliitis**
   - Focal or diffuse endothelial dysfunction
   - Associated keratic precipitates
   - Corneal edema
   - May have secondary glaucoma

**Management:**
- Topical antiviral (aciclovir ointment, ganciclovir gel)
- Oral antiviral (acyclovir 400mg 5x daily, valacyclovir)
- Debridement of dendritic ulcer
- Topical steroids for stromal disease (only with antiviral cover)
- Long-term suppressive therapy for recurrent disease

**Fungal Keratitis**
Fungal keratitis is more common in tropical climates and in patients with trauma involving vegetable matter.

**Risk Factors:**
- Agricultural work
- Trauma with vegetable matter
- Contact lens wear
- Chronic topical steroid use
- Immunocompromised state

**Clinical Features:**
- Indolent course (slower than bacterial)
- Gray-white infiltrate with feathery margins
- Satellite lesions
- Hypopyon with irregular margins
- Ring infiltrate possible
- Endothelial plaque

**Management:**
- Corneal scraping for microscopy and culture
- Topical natamycin 5% (first-line for filamentous fungi)
- Topical amphotericin B (for yeasts)
- Voriconazole (topical or systemic)
- Prolonged treatment course (weeks to months)
- Surgical intervention for non-responsive cases

**Acanthamoeba Keratitis**
Acanthamoeba keratitis is a rare but severe infection, most commonly associated with contact lens wear and exposure to contaminated water.

**Risk Factors:**
- Contact lens wear (especially soft lenses)
- Swimming or showering with contact lenses
- Use of tap water for lens cleaning
- Corneal trauma

**Clinical Features:**
- Severe pain disproportionate to findings
- Ring infiltrate (late finding)
- Perineuritis (radial keratoneuritis) - early sign
- Epithelial irregularity
- Progressive stromal involvement
- Refractory to antibiotics

**Diagnosis:**
- Confocal microscopy (cyst visualization)
- Culture on non-nutrient agar with E. coli overlay
- PCR testing
- Corneal biopsy in uncertain cases

**Management:**
- Combination therapy with biguanides:
  - Polyhexamethylene biguanide (PHMB) 0.02%
  - Chlorhexidine 0.02%
- Aromatic diamidines (propamidine, hexamidine)
- Intensive dosing (hourly initially)
- Prolonged treatment (months)
- Corneal transplantation may be required

#### Corneal Dystrophies

**Epithelial and Subepithelial Dystrophies**

*Epithelial Basement Membrane Dystrophy (EBMD)*
- Most common corneal dystrophy
- Maps, dots, and fingerprints appearance
- Recurrent corneal erosions
- Irregular astigmatism possible
- Treatment: Lubricants, hypertonic saline, debridement, PTK

*Reis-Bücklers Corneal Dystrophy*
- Subepithelial honeycomb opacities
- Recurrent erosions common
- Progressive visual loss
- Autosomal dominant inheritance

**Stromal Dystrophies**

*Lattice Dystrophy*
- Amyloid deposits in stroma
- Branching lattice lines
- Recurrent erosions
- Visual impairment with progression
- TGFBI gene mutation

*Granular Dystrophy*
- Hyaline deposits in stroma
- Well-defined, bread-crumb-like opacities
- Clear stroma between deposits
- Visual impact when deposits encroach on visual axis

*Macular Dystrophy*
- Mucopolysaccharide deposits
- Diffuse, ill-defined opacities
- Affects entire stroma
- Most visually significant stromal dystrophy
- Autosomal recessive inheritance

**Endothelial Dystrophies**

*Fuchs' Endothelial Corneal Dystrophy (FECD)*
- Progressive endothelial cell loss
- Cornea guttata (excrescences on Descemet's membrane)
- Stromal edema, bullous keratopathy
- Worse in the morning
- Visual impairment progressive
- Treatment: Hypertonic saline, DSAEK/DMEK/PKP

## 2. Cataract

### 2.1 Classification and Types

Cataract, defined as any opacity of the crystalline lens, is the leading cause of reversible blindness worldwide. Understanding cataract types, grading, and indications for surgery is essential for DHA optometrists.

#### Age-Related Cataracts

**Nuclear Sclerosis Cataract**
Nuclear sclerosis is the most common form of age-related cataract, involving gradual hardening and yellowing of the lens nucleus.

**Clinical Features:**
- Progressive yellowing to brunescence
- Increased optical density
- May cause "second sight" phenomenon (improved near vision)
- Progressive myopia due to increased refractive index
- Color perception changes (blue-yellow deficiency)
- Glare symptoms

**Grading (LOCS III):**
- NO1-NO6: Nuclear opalescence
- Grades based on comparison with photographic standards

**Cortical Cataract**
Cortical cataracts involve opacification of the lens cortex, appearing as wedge-shaped or radial spoke-like opacities.

**Clinical Features:**
- Radial spoke-like opacities
- Wedge-shaped cortical spokes
- Often begins inferonasally
- Glare and vision fluctuation
- Monocular diplopia possible
- Affects visual acuity when extending to visual axis

**Posterior Subcapsular Cataract (PSC)**
PSC involves opacification of the posterior cortical layer, often causing disproportionate visual symptoms for its size.

**Clinical Features:**
- Granular opacities under posterior capsule
- Located axially
- Disproportionate visual symptoms
- Severe glare disability
- Difficulty with near vision and bright lights
- Rapid progression possible

**Risk Factors:**
- Steroid use (topical or systemic)
- Diabetes mellitus
- High myopia
- Retinitis pigmentosa
- Trauma
- Younger patients

#### Congenital Cataracts

Congenital cataracts may be inherited or associated with systemic conditions. They require urgent evaluation due to risk of amblyopia.

**Types:**
- Anterior polar cataract
- Posterior polar cataract
- Nuclear cataract
- Lamellar (zonular) cataract
- Sutural cataract
- Cerulean (blue-dot) cataract

**Associated Conditions:**
- Intrauterine infections (TORCH)
- Metabolic disorders (galactosemia)
- Chromosomal abnormalities
- Ocular associations (persistent fetal vasculature, aniridia)

**Management:**
- Urgent ophthalmology referral
- Surgery typically within 6 weeks for bilateral dense cataracts
- Critical period for visual development
- Patching and visual rehabilitation post-surgery

### 2.2 Secondary Cataracts

**Traumatic Cataract**
- Blunt or penetrating trauma
- Vossius ring (imprint of iris pigment on anterior capsule)
- Rosette-shaped opacity
- May be progressive or stable

**Diabetic Cataract**
- "Snowflake" cataract in uncontrolled diabetes
- Rapid onset in young diabetics
- Accelerated age-related changes in older patients

**Drug-Induced Cataract**
- Corticosteroids: PSC
- Phenothiazines: Anterior subcapsular star-shaped deposits
- Amiodarone: Anterior subcapsular deposits
- Statins: Variable patterns

### 2.3 Cataract Grading Systems

**LOCS III (Lens Opacities Classification System III)**
Standardized grading system using slit-lamp examination compared to photographic standards:

| Type | Grades | Description |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Nuclear Opalescence | NO1-NO6 | Yellowing/hardening of nucleus |
| Nuclear Color | NC1-NC6 | Color change from yellow to brown |
| Cortical | C1-C5 | Amount of cortex involved |
| Posterior Subcapsular | P1-P5 | Area of posterior capsule involved |

### 2.4 Indications for Surgery

**Visual Function Indications:**
- Best corrected visual acuity affecting daily activities
- Glare disability affecting function
- Anisometropia from cataract-induced refractive change
- Monocular diplopia or polyopia

**Ocular Health Indications:**
- Phacomorphic glaucoma (intumescent lens)
- Phacolytic glaucoma (lens protein leakage)
- Phacoantigenic uveitis
- Hindrance to retinal examination or treatment

### 2.5 Pre-operative Assessment

**Essential Evaluations:**
- Visual acuity (distance and near)
- Refraction
- Pupil examination
- Intraocular pressure
- Slit-lamp examination
- Fundus examination (if possible)
- Biometry (axial length, keratometry)
- Calculation of IOL power

**IOL Power Calculation Formulas:**
- SRK/T (Sanders-Retzlaff-Kraff/Theoretical)
- Hoffer Q
- Holladay 1 and 2
- Haigis
- Barrett Universal II (preferred for most eyes)

### 2.6 Post-operative Complications

**Early Complications:**
- Corneal edema
- Wound leak
- Iritis
- Endophthalmitis (rare but serious)
- Toxic anterior segment syndrome (TASS)
- IOL dislocation
- Retained lens material

**Late Complications:**
- Posterior capsule opacification (PCO) - most common
- Cystoid macular edema (Irvine-Gass syndrome)
- Retinal detachment
- Corneal decompensation
- IOL dislocation/subluxation
- Refractive surprise

**Posterior Capsule Opacification (PCO)**
- Occurs in 10-50% of patients within 5 years
- Lens epithelial cell proliferation
- Visual symptoms similar to cataract
- Treatment: Nd:YAG laser capsulotomy

## 3. Glaucoma

### 3.1 Overview and Classification

Glaucoma represents a group of progressive optic neuropathies characterized by optic nerve damage and visual field loss. It is the second leading cause of blindness globally and a significant component of the DHA examination.

**Classification:**
- Primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG)
- Primary angle-closure glaucoma (PACG)
- Secondary glaucomas
- Developmental glaucomas

### 3.2 Primary Open-Angle Glaucoma (POAG)

POAG is characterized by open anterior chamber angles and characteristic optic nerve damage, typically associated with elevated intraocular pressure.

**Risk Factors:**
- Elevated intraocular pressure (>21 mmHg)
- Age > 40 years
- Family history of glaucoma
- African, Hispanic, or Asian descent
- Myopia
- Diabetes mellitus
- Systemic hypertension
- Corticosteroid use

**Clinical Features:**

*Intraocular Pressure:*
- Typically >21 mmHg
- May be normal in normal-tension glaucoma
- Diurnal variation (higher in morning)

*Optic Nerve Changes:*
- Increased cup-to-disc ratio (>0.6 or asymmetry >0.2)
- Vertical cup elongation
- Notching of neuroretinal rim
- Splinter hemorrhages
- Peripapillary atrophy
- Nerve fiber layer defects

*Visual Field Loss:*
- Early: Paracentral scotomas, nasal step
- Moderate: Arcuate scotomas (Bjerrum's area)
- Advanced: Ring scotoma, temporal island
- End-stage: Central island remaining

**Diagnosis:**
- Tonometry (Goldmann applanation tonometry - gold standard)
- Gonioscopy (open angles in POAG)
- Optic nerve head assessment
- Visual field testing (Humphrey, Octopus)
- Optical coherence tomography (OCT) of RNFL and GCC
- Pachymetry (central corneal thickness)

**Treatment Targets:**
- Initial target IOP: 20-30% reduction from baseline
- More aggressive targets for advanced disease
- Individualize based on risk factors and progression

**Medical Treatment:**

| Class | Mechanism | Examples |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| Prostaglandin analogs | Increased uveoscleral outflow | Latanoprost, Travoprost, Bimatoprost, Tafluprost |
| Beta-blockers | Decreased aqueous production | Timolol, Betaxolol |
| Alpha-2 agonists | Decreased aqueous production, increased outflow | Brimonidine, Apraclonidine |
| Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors | Decreased aqueous production | Dorzolamide, Brinzolamide |
| Rho kinase inhibitors | Increased trabecular outflow | Netarsudil, Ripasudil |
| Miotics | Increased trabecular outflow | Pilocarpine |

**Laser and Surgical Options:**
- Laser trabeculoplasty (SLT, ALT)
- Trabeculectomy
- Aqueous shunts (Ahmed, Baerveldt)
- Minimally invasive glaucoma surgery (MIGS)

### 3.3 Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)

PACG results from apposition of the peripheral iris to the trabecular meshwork, obstructing aqueous outflow.

**Risk Factors:**
- Hyperopia
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Short axial length
- Older age
- Female gender
- Asian or Inuit descent
- Thick crystalline lens
- Plateau iris configuration

**Classification (ISGEO):**

1. **Primary Angle-Closure Suspect (PACS)**
   - Narrow angles (≥180° trabecular meshwork not visible)
   - No PAS, normal IOP, no optic nerve damage

2. **Primary Angle-Closure (PAC)**
   - Narrow angles with PAS and/or elevated IOP
   - No optic nerve damage

3. **Primary Angle-Closure Glaucoma (PACG)**
   - Narrow angles with optic nerve damage and visual field loss

**Acute Angle-Closure Crisis (AACC)**

*Clinical Features:*
- Sudden onset severe pain
- Blurred vision with halos around lights
- Nausea and vomiting
- Conjunctival injection
- Corneal edema (microcystic)
- Shallow anterior chamber
- Mid-dilated, non-reactive pupil
- IOP typically >40 mmHg (may exceed 60-70 mmHg)

*Immediate Management:*
- Topical beta-blocker
- Topical alpha-2 agonist
- Topical or systemic carbonic anhydrase inhibitor
- Topical steroid (to reduce inflammation)
- Pilocarpine (after IOP reduction; ineffective at high IOP)
- Systemic osmotic agents (mannitol IV, glycerol oral)
- Laser peripheral iridotomy (definitive treatment)

*Chronic Management:*
- Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI)
- Prophylactic LPI for fellow eye
- Consider lens extraction

### 3.4 Secondary Glaucomas

**Pigmentary Glaucoma**
- Pigment dispersion syndrome with elevated IOP
- Krukenberg spindle (pigment on corneal endothelium)
- Iris transillumination defects
- Pigment deposition on trabecular meshwork
- More common in young myopic males

**Pseudoexfoliation Glaucoma**
- Associated with pseudoexfoliation syndrome
- White, fluffy deposits on lens and pupillary margin
- "Target" appearance on lens
- Pigment accumulation in angle
- Pupil dilation difficulties
- Higher risk of complications during cataract surgery

**Neovascular Glaucoma**
- New blood vessels on iris and angle (rubeosis iridis)
- Associated with retinal ischemia
- Causes: Diabetic retinopathy, CRVO, CAR
- Treatment: Panretinal photocoagulation, anti-VEGF, glaucoma surgery

**Uveitic Glaucoma**
- Secondary to intraocular inflammation
- Mechanisms: Trabecular meshwork obstruction, PAS, steroid response
- Treatment: Control inflammation, manage IOP

**Steroid-Induced Glaucoma**
- IOP elevation from topical or systemic steroids
- Risk factors: POAG, POAG family history, myopia
- IOP rise typically within 2-4 weeks
- Usually reversible with discontinuation
- "Steroid responders" require monitoring

## 4. Retinal Disorders

### 4.1 Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy is a leading cause of blindness in working-age adults. Understanding its classification and management is essential for optometrists.

**Classification:**

*Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR)*

**Mild NPDR:**
- Microaneurysms only

**Moderate NPDR:**
- Microaneurysms
- Retinal hemorrhages (dot and blot)
- Hard exudates
- Cotton wool spots (few)

**Severe NPDR (4-2-1 Rule):**
- Diffuse hemorrhages in all 4 quadrants
- Venous beading in 2 quadrants
- Intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA) in 1 quadrant
- No PDR features

*Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)*

**Features:**
- Neovascularization (NVD - disc, NVE - elsewhere)
- Vitreous hemorrhage
- Tractional retinal detachment
- High risk of severe visual loss

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)**
- Retinal thickening within 1 disc diameter of fovea
- May occur at any stage of DR
- Leading cause of visual impairment in diabetics

**Classification:**
- Center-involved DME: Edema involving fovea
- Non-center-involved DME: Edema near but not involving fovea

**Management:**
- Glycemic control (HbA1c target <7%)
- Blood pressure control
- Lipid management
- Regular screening
- Anti-VEGF therapy for DME
- Laser photocoagulation for PDR
- Vitrectomy for non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage

**Screening Recommendations:**
- Type 1 diabetes: 5 years after diagnosis, then annually
- Type 2 diabetes: At diagnosis, then annually
- Pregnancy: Before conception, each trimester

### 4.2 Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

AMD is the leading cause of irreversible central vision loss in elderly populations in developed countries.

**Classification:**

*Early AMD:*
- Drusen (small to medium)
- RPE pigmentary changes
- Usually asymptomatic

*Intermediate AMD:*
- Large drusen (>125 μm)
- Geographic atrophy not involving fovea
- May have mild visual symptoms

*Late AMD:*
- Neovascular (wet) AMD
- Geographic atrophy involving fovea

**Dry AMD (Non-Neovascular)**
- Drusen accumulation
- RPE atrophy and mottling
- Geographic atrophy (well-demarcated areas)
- Slowly progressive
- No specific treatment
- AREDS2 supplements for intermediate AMD

**Wet AMD (Neovascular)**
- Choroidal neovascularization (CNV)
- Sudden vision loss
- Metamorphopsia (distortion)
- Subretinal fluid and hemorrhage
- Rapid progression without treatment

**Clinical Features of Wet AMD:**
- Sudden visual loss
- Central scotoma
- Metamorphopsia
- Subretinal hemorrhage
- Subretinal fluid
- Pigment epithelial detachment

**CNV Types:**
- Type 1: Occult (below RPE)
- Type 2: Classic (above RPE)
- Type 3: Retinal angiomatous proliferation (RAP)

**Management:**
- Anti-VEGF therapy (ranibizumab, aflibercept, bevacizumab)
- Regular injections (monthly or PRN)
- OCT monitoring
- Self-monitoring with Amsler grid

**AREDS2 Supplements:**
- Vitamin C 500 mg
- Vitamin E 400 IU
- Lutein 10 mg
- Zeaxanthin 2 mg
- Zinc 80 mg (or 25 mg)
- Copper 2 mg
- Indicated for intermediate AMD or unilateral late AMD

### 4.3 Retinal Vascular Diseases

**Central Retinal Vein Occlusion (CRVO)**

*Ischemic CRVO:*
- More severe vision loss
- Extensive hemorrhages ("blood and thunder" appearance)
- Cotton wool spots
- Relative afferent pupillary defect
- Risk of neovascular glaucoma (90-day glaucoma)
- Poor prognosis

*Non-ischemic CRVO:*
- Milder presentation
- Better visual prognosis
- May convert to ischemic type

*Management:*
- Investigate underlying causes
- Anti-VEGF for macular edema
- Dexamethasone implant
- Panretinal photocoagulation for neovascularization

**Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion (BRVO)**
- Localized hemorrhages in affected sector
- Macular edema common
- Better prognosis than CRVO
- Grid laser for macular edema (historically)
- Anti-VEGF therapy (current standard)

**Central Retinal Artery Occlusion (CRAO)**
- Sudden, profound vision loss
- "Cherry-red spot" at fovea
- Retinal whitening
- Attenuated arteries
- Boxcar segmentation of blood column

*Management (Time-Sensitive):*
- Ocular massage
- Anterior chamber paracentesis
- Intravenous acetazolamide
- Hyperbaric oxygen
- Thrombolytic therapy (within hours)
- Generally poor visual prognosis

**Retinal Detachment**

*Rhegmatogenous Retinal Detachment:*
- Due to retinal break (tear or hole)
- Associated with PVD, lattice degeneration, myopia
- Symptoms: Flashes, floaters, curtain/veil
- Urgent surgical repair required

*Signs:*
- Elevated, mobile retina
- Corrugated appearance
- Retinal break visible
- Pigment in vitreous (tobacco dust)

*Tractional Retinal Detachment:*
- Fibrovascular membranes pull retina
- Associated with PDR, proliferative vitreoretinopathy
- Concave surface, limited mobility
- May be stable or progressive

*Exudative Retinal Detachment:*
- Subretinal fluid without break
- Causes: Inflammation, tumor, vascular disease
- Shifting fluid with position changes

*Management:*
- Pneumatic retinopexy (selected cases)
- Scleral buckle
- Pars plana vitrectomy
- Combination procedures

## 5. Uveitis

### 5.1 Classification

Uveitis refers to inflammation of the uveal tract (iris, ciliary body, choroid). Classification is anatomical:

**Anatomical Classification:**

| Type | Primary Site | Includes |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Anterior uveitis | Iris, ciliary body | Iritis, iridocyclitis |
| Intermediate uveitis | Vitreous, pars plana | Pars planitis |
| Posterior uveitis | Choroid, retina | Choroiditis, retinitis, retinochoroiditis |
| Panuveitis | All structures | Diffuse inflammation |

**Clinical Classification:**
- Acute (< 3 months duration)
- Chronic (> 3 months duration)
- Recurrent

### 5.2 Anterior Uveitis

Anterior uveitis is the most common form of uveitis, accounting for approximately 75% of cases.

**Clinical Features:**
- Eye pain
- Photophobia
- Red eye (perilimbal injection)
- Tearing
- Decreased vision (variable)

**Signs:**
- Ciliary flush (perilimbal injection)
- Keratic precipitates (KPs)
  - Fine KPs: Non-granulomatous
  - Mutton-fat KPs: Granulomatous
- Anterior chamber cells (grading 0.5+ to 4+)
- Anterior chamber flare (protein leakage)
- Hypopyon (severe cases)
- Iris changes:
  - Posterior synechiae (adhesions to lens)
  - Iris nodules (Koeppe, Busacca)
- Pupil:
  - Miosis
  - Irregular due to synechiae

**Etiology:**

*Idiopathic (most common)*

*HLA-B27 Associated:*
- Ankylosing spondylitis
- Reactive arthritis (Reiter's syndrome)
- Inflammatory bowel disease
- Psoriatic arthritis

*Infectious:*
- Herpes simplex virus
- Varicella zoster virus
- Syphilis
- Tuberculosis
- Lyme disease

*Other:*
- Sarcoidosis
- Juvenile idiopathic arthritis
- Fuchs' heterochromic iridocyclitis

**Management:**
- Topical corticosteroids (prednisolone acetate, dexamethasone)
- Cycloplegics (atropine, cyclopentolate, homatropine)
- Steroid-sparing agents for chronic cases
- Treat underlying cause

### 5.3 Intermediate Uveitis

Intermediate uveitis primarily involves the vitreous and pars plana region.

**Clinical Features:**
- Floaters (common presenting symptom)
- Mild to moderate vision loss
- Minimal pain and redness
- Vitreous cells
- Snowball opacities (vitreous)
- Snowbanking (pars plana exudate)

**Pars Planitis:**
- Idiopathic intermediate uveitis
- Most common form in children and young adults
- Bilateral in 80% of cases
- May cause cystoid macular edema

**Associated Conditions:**
- Multiple sclerosis
- Sarcoidosis
- Lyme disease
- Tuberculosis
- Syphilis

**Management:**
- Observation for mild cases
- Periocular steroids (triamcinolone)
- Systemic steroids
- Immunosuppressants (methotrexate, cyclosporine)
- Vitrectomy for complications

### 5.4 Posterior Uveitis

Posterior uveitis involves the choroid and/or retina.

**Infectious Causes:**

*Toxoplasmosis:*
- Most common cause of infectious posterior uveitis
- Congenital or acquired
- Focal necrotizing retinitis
- "Headlight in fog" appearance
- Adjacent chorioretinal scar (reactivation)
- Vitritis common
- Treatment: Pyrimethamine + sulfadiazine + steroids

*Cytomegalovirus (CMV) Retinitis:*
- Immunocompromised patients (HIV/AIDS)
- "Pizza pie" appearance (hemorrhages + exudates)
- Granular or brushfire patterns
- Treatment: Ganciclovir, foscarnet, cidofovir

*Acute Retinal Necrosis (ARN):*
- Caused by HSV or VZV
- Rapidly progressive peripheral necrosis
- Retinal detachment common
- Treatment: IV acyclovir, anticoagulation

*Tuberculosis:*
- Multifocal choroiditis
- Serpiginous-like choroiditis
- Requires systemic anti-tubercular therapy

*Syphilis:*
- "Great mimicker"
- Variable presentations
- Positive FTA-ABS, RPR/VDRL
- Treatment: IV penicillin

**Non-Infectious Causes:**

*Sarcoidosis:*
- Multisystem granulomatous disease
- Candle-wax drippings (periphlebitis)
- Dalen-Fuchs nodules
- Chronic, recurrent course

*Behçet's Disease:*
- Recurrent oral and genital ulcers
- Uveitis with hypopyon
- Retinal vasculitis
- Branch retinal vein occlusions

*Vogt-Koyanagi-Harada (VKH) Syndrome:*
- Bilateral granulomatous panuveitis
- Poliosis and vitiligo
- Exudative retinal detachment
- Hearing disturbances
- More common in darker-skinned individuals

*Sympathetic Ophthalmia:*
- Bilateral granulomatous uveitis
- Follows penetrating trauma or surgery
- Fellow eye involvement (sympathizing eye)
- Occurs within 3 months to years

### 5.5 Complications of Uveitis

- Cataract
- Glaucoma (open or angle-closure)
- Cystoid macular edema (CME)
- Band keratopathy
- Vitreous opacification
- Retinal detachment
- Choroidal neovascularization
- Hypotony
- Phthisis bulbi

## Conclusion

Ocular disease and pathology form a cornerstone of DHA Optometry examination preparation. Optometrists must demonstrate comprehensive knowledge of anterior segment conditions, cataract grading and management, glaucoma diagnosis and treatment, retinal diseases including diabetic retinopathy and AMD, and the spectrum of uveitic conditions. Understanding when to refer patients for specialist care is as important as managing conditions within the optometric scope of practice.

Key clinical pearls for the DHA examination include recognizing red flag signs that require urgent referral, understanding the interplay between systemic and ocular disease, and demonstrating competence in diagnostic reasoning through case-based questions.

---

## Key Takeaways for DHA Examination

1. **Anterior Segment:** Know distinguishing features of conjunctivitis types, recognize corneal dystrophies, and understand keratitis management

2. **Cataract:** Master LOCS III grading, know indications for surgery, and understand IOL calculations

3. **Glaucoma:** Differentiate POAG from PACG, recognize risk factors, understand treatment algorithms, and know when to suspect secondary glaucomas

4. **Retina:** Classify diabetic retinopathy, recognize AMD types, and understand retinal vascular occlusion management

5. **Uveitis:** Classify anatomically, recognize granulomatous vs non-granulomatous features, and know infectious vs non-infectious causes
