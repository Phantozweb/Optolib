# Ocular Pharmacology for DHA Exam

## Introduction

Ocular pharmacology is a fundamental component of the DHA Optometry examination, comprising approximately 15-20% of the clinical competency questions. Understanding the mechanisms, indications, contraindications, and clinical applications of various ophthalmic medications is essential for safe and effective patient care. This comprehensive guide covers all major drug classes tested in the DHA examination, including topical medications, antibiotics, anti-inflammatory agents, and glaucoma therapeutics.

## 1. Principles of Ocular Drug Delivery

### Ocular Anatomy and Drug Absorption

The eye presents unique challenges for drug delivery due to its anatomical barriers. Understanding these barriers is crucial for optimizing therapeutic outcomes.

**Corneal Penetration Pathways:**
- **Epithelial barrier**: Lipophilic layer that preferentially absorbs lipophilic drugs
- **Stroma**: Hydrophilic layer that favors water-soluble compounds
- **Endothelium**: Single cell layer with minimal barrier function

**Factors Affecting Drug Absorption:**

| Factor | Effect on Absorption |
|--------|---------------------|
| Lipophilicity | Higher lipophilicity improves corneal penetration |
| Molecular weight | Smaller molecules penetrate more readily |
| Protein binding | Higher binding reduces free drug concentration |
| pH | Optimal absorption at physiological pH (7.4) |
| Concentration | Higher concentration gradient increases absorption |

### Vehicle Selection

The choice of vehicle significantly impacts drug bioavailability:

**Solutions:**
- Rapid clearance (5-10 minutes)
- Limited contact time
- Suitable for acute conditions

**Suspensions:**
- Prolonged contact time
- Particle size affects comfort and absorption
- Require shaking before use

**Ointments:**
- Extended contact time (4-6 hours)
- Blurred vision after application
- Ideal for bedtime use

**Gels:**
- Intermediate contact time
- Less blurring than ointments
- Better patient compliance

**Preservatives:**
- Benzalkonium chloride (BAK): Most common, disrupts tear film
- Chlorobenzalkonium chloride: Less toxic alternative
- Polyquad: Newer, less irritating preservative
- Preservative-free formulations: Essential for chronic use

## 2. Topical Antibiotics

### Classification and Mechanisms

Ophthalmic antibiotics are classified by their mechanism of action and spectrum of coverage.

**Cell Wall Synthesis Inhibitors:**

*Fluoroquinolones (DNA Gyrase Inhibitors)*

Fluoroquinolones are the most commonly prescribed ophthalmic antibiotics due to their broad-spectrum coverage and excellent corneal penetration.

| Generation | Drugs | Coverage |
|------------|-------|----------|
| Second | Ciprofloxacin 0.3% | Gram-negative, some Gram-positive |
| Third | Ofloxacin 0.3% | Improved Gram-positive coverage |
| Fourth | Moxifloxacin 0.5% | Broad-spectrum, including atypicals |
| Fourth | Gatifloxacin 0.3% | Broad-spectrum, good penetration |
| Fifth | Besifloxacin 0.6% | Enhanced coverage, reduced resistance |

**Clinical Applications:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis: 1-2 drops QID for 7 days
- Corneal ulcers: Hourly initially, then taper
- Post-surgical prophylaxis: TID-QID for 1-2 weeks

**Side Effects:**
- Local irritation and burning
- Allergic reactions (rare)
- Corneal deposits (ciprofloxacin)
- Potential for resistance with overuse

*Aminoglycosides (Protein Synthesis Inhibitors)*

Gentamicin and tobramycin remain important options for specific infections.

| Drug | Concentration | Primary Coverage |
|------|---------------|------------------|
| Gentamicin | 0.3% | Gram-negative, some Gram-positive |
| Tobramycin | 0.3% | Gram-negative, Pseudomonas |

**Clinical Pearls:**
- Preferred for Pseudomonas infections
- Synergistic with beta-lactams
- Monitor for ototoxicity in neonates
- Can cause punctate keratopathy with prolonged use

*Macrolides (Protein Synthesis Inhibitors)*

Erythromycin and azithromycin provide coverage against Gram-positive organisms and atypical bacteria.

**Erythromycin 0.5% Ointment:**
- First-line for neonatal prophylaxis
- Treatment of chlamydial conjunctivitis
- Adjunct in blepharitis management
- Apply QID or at bedtime

**Azithromycin 1% Solution:**
- Extended dosing (BID for 2 days, then daily)
- Excellent tissue penetration
- Effective against atypicals
- Useful in blepharitis and meibomian gland dysfunction

*Polymyxin B Combinations*

Polymyxin B/Trimethoprim offers broad-spectrum coverage with low toxicity profile.

**Indications:**
- Acute bacterial conjunctivitis
- Prophylaxis for corneal abrasions
- Alternative to fluoroquinolones

### Antibiotic Resistance Considerations

The DHA examination increasingly tests knowledge of antimicrobial stewardship:

**Mechanisms of Resistance:**
- Efflux pumps (fluoroquinolones)
- Target modification (all classes)
- Enzymatic inactivation (beta-lactamase)

**Strategies to Minimize Resistance:**
1. Use narrow-spectrum agents when pathogen identified
2. Complete prescribed course
3. Avoid prophylactic use unless indicated
4. Culture and sensitivity for severe infections

## 3. Topical Anti-Inflammatory Agents

### Corticosteroids

Corticosteroids remain the most potent anti-inflammatory agents in ophthalmology, but their use requires careful monitoring.

**Classification by Potency:**

| Potency | Drug | Concentration | Relative Strength |
|---------|------|---------------|-------------------|
| High | Dexamethasone | 0.1% | 6x prednisolone |
| High | Prednisolone acetate | 1% | High |
| Medium | Fluorometholone | 0.1% | 0.1x dexamethasone |
| Low | Loteprednol etabonate | 0.5% | 0.5x prednisolone |
| Low | Rimexolone | 1% | Low |

**Clinical Applications:**

*Anterior Uveitis:*
- Prednisolone acetate 1%: QID to hourly initially
- Dexamethasone 0.1%: Alternative option
- Taper slowly over weeks to prevent rebound

*Post-operative Inflammation:*
- Prednisolone acetate: Starting 4-6 times daily
- Loteprednol: Lower IOP risk, good for sensitive patients
- Difluprednate: High potency, twice daily dosing

*Allergic Conjunctivitis (Severe):*
- Loteprednol: Preferred due to lower side effect profile
- Short course (1-2 weeks) usually sufficient

*Corneal Inflammation:*
- Use with caution in epithelial defects
- Risk of delayed healing and infection
- Consider NSAIDs as alternative

**Side Effects and Monitoring:**

*Intraocular Pressure Elevation:*
- Risk varies with potency and duration
- Prednisolone > fluorometholone > loteprednol
- Check IOP at 1-2 weeks, then monthly
- "Steroid responders": 5-6% of general population

*Cataract Formation:*
- Posterior subcapsular cataracts
- Risk increases with duration and dose
- Document baseline lens status

*Infection Risk:*
- Herpes simplex reactivation
- Fungal infections with prolonged use
- Delayed wound healing

*Contraindications:*
- Active viral keratitis (HSV, VZV)
- Fungal infections
- Active bacterial infection (without antibiotic coverage)
- Corneal epithelial defects (relative)

### Non-Steroidal Anti-Inflammatory Drugs (NSAIDs)

Ophthalmic NSAIDs provide effective anti-inflammatory action without steroid-related complications.

**Available Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing Frequency |
|------|---------------|------------------|
| Ketorolac | 0.5% | QID |
| Diclofenac | 0.1% | QID |
| Bromfenac | 0.09% | BID |
| Nepafenac | 0.1% | TID |
| Flurbiprofen | 0.03% | QID |

**Clinical Applications:**

*Post-operative Pain and Inflammation:*
- Begin 1 day before surgery
- Continue 2-4 weeks post-operatively
- Reduces need for steroids

*Cystoid Macular Edema Prophylaxis:*
- After cataract surgery
- Especially important in diabetic patients
- Continue 4-6 weeks

*Pain Management:*
- Corneal abrasions (controversial)
- Post-photorefractive keratectomy (PRK)
- Reduced risk of corneal melt vs. steroids

*Allergic Conjunctivitis:*
- Ketorolac approved for allergic conjunctivitis
- Symptom relief without rebound

**Side Effects:**
- Corneal melting (rare, with prolonged use)
- Burning and stinging
- Delayed corneal healing
- Allergic reactions

### Immunomodulators

For steroid-sparing and steroid-resistant inflammation:

**Cyclosporine:**
- Available as 0.05% and 0.09% emulsions
- Indicated for dry eye disease and vernal keratoconjunctivitis
- Mechanism: Inhibits T-cell activation
- Onset: 4-6 weeks for full effect
- Side effects: Burning, stinging (common initially)

**Tacrolimus:**
- Available as 0.1% ointment
- More potent than cyclosporine
- Used for severe atopic and vernal keratoconjunctivitis
- Requires compounding in some regions

**Lifitegrast:**
- 5% solution, BID dosing
- LFA-1 antagonist
- Approved for dry eye disease
- Side effects: Dysgeusia, irritation

## 4. Glaucoma Medications

### Classification Overview

Glaucoma medications represent one of the most heavily tested areas in the DHA examination. Understanding mechanisms, side effects, and clinical decision-making is essential.

**Drug Classes:**
1. Prostaglandin analogues (first-line)
2. Beta-blockers (first-line alternative)
3. Alpha-2 agonists (second-line)
4. Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (second-line)
5. Miotic agents (limited use)
6. Fixed combination agents

### Prostaglandin Analogues

Prostaglandin analogues are the most commonly prescribed first-line agents due to their efficacy, once-daily dosing, and favorable safety profile.

**Available Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | IOP Reduction |
|------|---------------|--------|---------------|
| Latanoprost | 0.005% | QHS | 25-35% |
| Travoprost | 0.004% | QHS | 25-35% |
| Bimatoprost | 0.01%/0.03% | QHS | 25-35% |
| Tafluprost | 0.0015% | QHS | 25-30% |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Increases uveoscleral outflow
- Possibly increases trabecular outflow
- Independent of aqueous production

**Clinical Considerations:**
- Evening dosing optimal (circadian rhythm)
- Effects seen within 2-4 hours, maximum at 8-12 hours
- May require 4-6 weeks for full effect

**Side Effects:**

*Ocular:*
- Iris pigmentation changes (permanent)
- Eyelash growth (reversible)
- Periocular skin darkening
- Conjunctival hyperemia (common)
- Cystoid macular edema (in pseudophakic patients)
- Anterior uveitis (rare)

*Systemic:*
- Minimal systemic absorption
- Generally well-tolerated

**Contraindications:**
- Active intraocular inflammation
- Caution in aphakic/pseudophakic patients with torn capsule

### Beta-Blockers

Beta-blockers reduce aqueous humor production and remain important therapeutic options.

**Available Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | Selectivity |
|------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| Timolol | 0.25%/0.5% | BID | Non-selective |
| Betaxolol | 0.25%/0.5% | BID | Beta-1 selective |
| Levobunolol | 0.25%/0.5% | QD-BID | Non-selective |
| Carteolol | 1% | BID | Non-selective, ISA |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Blocks beta-adrenergic receptors
- Reduces aqueous production by 20-50%
- Acts primarily on ciliary body

**Clinical Considerations:**
- 20-25% IOP reduction
- Timolol 0.5% is standard initial dose
- BID dosing (QD for gel-forming solutions)
- Less effective during sleep

**Side Effects:**

*Ocular:*
- Generally well-tolerated
- May cause dry eye symptoms
- Decreased corneal sensation (rare)

*Systemic (Significant Concerns):*
- Bradycardia
- Bronchospasm
- Fatigue and depression
- Sexual dysfunction
- Masked hypoglycemia symptoms

**Contraindications:**
- Asthma and COPD
- Bradycardia and heart block
- Severe heart failure
- Caution in diabetics

**Clinical Pearls:**
- Betaxolol preferred in pulmonary disease (beta-1 selective)
- Carteolol has intrinsic sympathomimetic activity (less bradycardia)
- Nasolacrimal occlusion reduces systemic absorption
- Check pulse before initiating therapy

### Alpha-2 Agonists

Alpha-2 agonists reduce aqueous production and increase uveoscleral outflow.

**Available Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | Duration |
|------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Brimonidine | 0.1%/0.15%/0.2% | TID | 12 hours |
| Apraclonidine | 0.5%/1% | TID | Short-term |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Stimulates alpha-2 receptors
- Decreases aqueous production
- Increases uveoscleral outflow

**Clinical Applications:**
- Second-line therapy
- Useful as adjunctive therapy
- Apraclonidine for post-laser IOP spikes
- Neuroprotective properties (investigational)

**Side Effects:**

*Ocular:*
- Allergic conjunctivitis (10-15%)
- Follicular conjunctivitis
- Conjunctival blanching
- Miosis
- Mydriasis (rare)

*Systemic:*
- Dry mouth
- Fatigue and drowsiness
- Hypotension
- Headache

**Contraindications:**
- MAO inhibitor use
- Severe cardiovascular disease
- Depression (relative)
- Children under 2 years (risk of CNS depression)

**Clinical Pearls:**
- Alphagan P (purite preservative) reduces allergy risk
- Allergy rate increases with duration of use
- Cross-allergy with apraclonidine
- Contraindicated in infants

### Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors

CAIs reduce aqueous humor production by inhibiting carbonic anhydrase enzyme.

**Topical Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | IOP Reduction |
|------|---------------|--------|---------------|
| Dorzolamide | 2% | TID | 15-20% |
| Brinzolamide | 1% | TID | 15-20% |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Inhibits carbonic anhydrase II
- Reduces bicarbonate production
- Decreases aqueous humor formation

**Clinical Considerations:**
- BID to TID dosing
- Often used as adjunctive therapy
- Less effective than systemic CAIs
- May be used in combination with beta-blockers

**Side Effects:**

*Ocular:*
- Burning and stinging (dorzolamide)
- Blurred vision (brinzolamide suspension)
- Taste perversion (bitter taste)
- Superficial punctate keratitis

*Systemic:*
- Generally minimal with topical use
- Less than oral CAIs

**Oral CAIs:**

| Drug | Dosage | Indications |
|------|--------|-------------|
| Acetazolamide | 125-500 mg BID-QID | Acute angle closure, resistant cases |
| Methazolamide | 25-50 mg TID | Alternative, longer acting |

**Oral CAI Side Effects:**
- Paresthesias (tingling)
- Fatigue
- GI upset
- Renal stones
- Metabolic acidosis
- Hypokalemia
- Aplastic anemia (rare but serious)

**Contraindications:**
- Sulfa allergy
- Renal failure
- Hepatic failure
- Hypokalemia
- Severe COPD

### Miotic Agents (Cholinergics)

Pilocarpine remains the primary miotic agent, though its use has diminished with newer medications.

**Available Agents:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | Duration |
|------|---------------|--------|----------|
| Pilocarpine | 0.5%-8% | QID | 4-8 hours |

**Mechanism of Action:**
- Stimulates muscarinic receptors
- Causes miosis and ciliary muscle contraction
- Increases trabecular outflow

**Clinical Applications:**
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (emergency)
- Pupillary dilation in certain conditions
- Testing for Adie's pupil
- Historical: primary open-angle glaucoma

**Side Effects:**
- Brow ache (very common)
- Miosis (reduced night vision)
- Induced myopia
- Headache
- Retinal detachment risk (in predisposed individuals)
- Angle closure in predisposed eyes

### Fixed Combination Agents

Combination therapy improves compliance and efficacy while reducing preservative exposure.

**Available Combinations:**

| Combination | Dosing | Components |
|-------------|--------|------------|
| Dorzolamide/Timolol | BID | CAI + Beta-blocker |
| Brimonidine/Timolol | BID | Alpha-2 agonist + Beta-blocker |
| Latanoprost/Timolol | QD | PGA + Beta-blocker |
| Brinzolamide/Brimonidine | TID | CAI + Alpha-2 agonist |

**Advantages:**
- Improved compliance
- Reduced preservative load
- Often more effective than either agent alone
- May be more cost-effective

**Disadvantages:**
- Less flexibility in dosing
- Both components must be tolerated
- May mask individual drug effects

## 5. Antiviral Medications

### Herpes Simplex Keratitis Treatment

**Topical Antivirals:**

| Drug | Concentration | Dosing | Availability |
|------|---------------|--------|--------------|
| Ganciclovir | 0.15% gel | 5x daily | Available |
| Acyclovir | 3% ointment | 5x daily | Limited availability |

**Oral Antivirals:**
- Acyclovir: 400 mg TID for epithelial, 400 mg BID for stromal
- Valacyclovir: 500 mg BID
- Famciclovir: 250 mg BID

**Treatment Protocols:**
- Epithelial keratitis: Topical or oral antiviral
- Stromal keratitis: Oral antiviral + topical steroid (cautiously)
- Disciform keratitis: Topical steroid + oral antiviral

## 9. Mydriatics and Cycloplegics

### Anticholinergic Agents

| Drug | Concentration | Mydriasis Duration | Cycloplegia Duration |
|------|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Tropicamide | 0.5-1% | 4-6 hours | 2-6 hours |
| Cyclopentolate | 0.5-2% | 24 hours | 24-48 hours |
| Homatropine | 2-5% | 1-3 days | 1-3 days |
| Atropine | 0.5-3% | 7-14 days | 7-14 days |
| Scopolamine | 0.25% | 7-14 days | 7-14 days |

**Clinical Applications:**
- Tropicamide: Routine dilation, cycloplegic refraction (brief)
- Cyclopentolate: Cycloplegic refraction, uveitis
- Atropine: Severe uveitis, amblyopia treatment

**Side Effects:**
- Photophobia
- Blurred vision
- Angle-closure risk (predisposed eyes)
- Systemic: Tachycardia, dry mouth, flushing (especially in children)

### Sympathomimetic Agents

| Drug | Concentration | Effect |
|------|---------------|--------|
| Phenylephrine | 2.5%/10% | Pure mydriasis, no cycloplegia |

**Clinical Uses:**
- Combined with anticholinergics for maximal dilation
- Diagnostic purposes
- Intraoperative use

**Precautions:**
- 10% solution: Risk of systemic effects (hypertension, tachycardia)
- 2.5% preferred for routine use
- Caution in cardiovascular disease
- Risk of rebound miosis

## 11. Tear Substitutes and Lubricants

### Classification

**Aqueous Deficiency:**
- Artificial tears (carboxymethylcellulose, hydroxypropyl methylcellulose)
- Polyethylene glycol solutions
- Sodium hyaluronate

**Evaporative/MGD:**
- Lipid-based emulsions
- Castor oil preparations
- Nanoparticle formulations

### Preservative Considerations

**Preserved Formulations:**
- Suitable for intermittent use
- BAK most common preservative
- Can worsen dry eye with frequent use

**Preservative-Free Options:**
- Essential for chronic use (>4x daily)
- Unit-dose vials
- Novel preserved formulations (polyquad, purite, sodium chlorite)

### Viscosity Grading

| Viscosity | Examples | Use |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| Low | Normal saline, Refresh Tears | Mild symptoms |
| Medium | Systane, Optive | Moderate symptoms |
| High | Gels, Celluvisc | Severe symptoms |

## 12. Clinical Decision-Making for DHA Examination

### Key Examination Points

**When selecting glaucoma medications, consider:**
1. Patient age and systemic conditions
2. Current medications and interactions
3. Compliance factors (dosing frequency)
4. Cost and availability
5. Side effect profile

**Common Examination Scenarios:**

*Scenario 1: Young patient with asthma requiring glaucoma treatment*
- Avoid beta-blockers
- First-line: Prostaglandin analogue
- Alternative: Alpha-2 agonist or CAI

*Scenario 2: Patient on oral beta-blockers*
- May have reduced efficacy from topical beta-blocker
- Consider PGA as first-line

*Scenario 3: Elderly patient with bradycardia*
- Avoid beta-blockers
- PGA first-line
- Monitor for alpha-2 agonist hypotension

*Scenario 4: Post-operative inflammation after cataract surgery*
- Prednisolone acetate or difluprednate
- Consider NSAID for pain control
- Monitor IOP

*Scenario 5: Contact lens wearer with bacterial conjunctivitis*
- Avoid ointments
- Consider preservative-free drops
- Discontinue contact lens wear during treatment

## Conclusion

Mastery of ocular pharmacology is essential for the DHA Optometry examination. Key areas to focus on include:

1. **Mechanisms of action** for each drug class
2. **Clinical indications** and contraindications
3. **Side effects**, both ocular and systemic
4. **Drug interactions** and special considerations
5. **Dosing regimens** and compliance factors
6. **Cost-effectiveness** in clinical decision-making

Understanding these concepts will not only help you pass the DHA examination but also enable you to provide safe and effective patient care in your practice.
