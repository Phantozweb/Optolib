# Neuro-Ophthalmology for DHA Exam

## Introduction

Neuro-ophthalmology represents one of the most challenging and clinically significant subspecialties bridging ophthalmic practice and neurological medicine. For candidates preparing for the Dubai Health Authority (DHA) Optometry licensing examination, a thorough understanding of neuro-ophthalmic conditions is essential, as these conditions often present with subtle signs that can have profound implications for patient health and visual function. This comprehensive guide covers the core topics of pupil disorders, optic nerve pathology, cranial nerve anatomy and dysfunction, and visual field analysis—all critical areas that appear frequently on the DHA examination.

## 1. Pupil Disorders

### 1.1 Normal Pupil Anatomy and Physiology

The pupil serves as a dynamic aperture that regulates the amount of light entering the eye, with a normal diameter ranging from 2-4mm in bright light to 4-8mm in darkness. Pupil size and reactivity are controlled by two antagonistic muscle groups within the iris: the sphincter pupillae (constrictor) and the dilator pupillae (dilator).

**Sphincter Pupillae:**
- Located in the pupillary margin of the iris
- Innervated by parasympathetic fibers via cranial nerve III (oculomotor nerve)
- Receives input from the Edinger-Westphal nucleus in the midbrain
- Primary function is pupillary constriction (miosis)
- Composed of smooth muscle arranged in a circular pattern

**Dilator Pupillae:**
- Radially arranged myoepithelial cells in the iris
- Innervated by sympathetic fibers originating from the superior cervical ganglion
- Three-neuron pathway: hypothalamus → ciliospinal center → superior cervical ganglion
- Primary function is pupillary dilation (mydriasis)
- Active during stress, fear, and low-light conditions

### 1.2 Pupillary Light Reflex Pathway

Understanding the pupillary light reflex is fundamental to diagnosing afferent and efferent pupillary defects. The pathway consists of both afferent and efferent components:

**Afferent Pathway:**
1. Light stimulates retinal photoreceptors (rods, cones, and intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells)
2. Signals travel via optic nerve (CN II) axons
3. Partial decussation at the optic chiasm (approximately 53% of fibers cross)
4. Fibers exit the optic tract before the lateral geniculate nucleus
5. Synapse in the pretectal nucleus of the midbrain
6. Bilateral connections to both Edinger-Westphal nuclei

**Efferent Pathway:**
1. Edinger-Westphal nuclei send preganglionic parasympathetic fibers
2. Fibers travel within the oculomotor nerve (CN III)
3. Synapse in the ciliary ganglion within the orbit
4. Post-ganglionic fibers travel via short ciliary nerves
5. Innervate the sphincter pupillae muscle causing constriction

**Clinical Significance:** The bilateral connections at the pretectal nucleus explain why shining light in one eye causes both pupils to constrict (direct and consensual response).

### 1.3 Relative Afferent Pupillary Defect (RAPD)

RAPD, also known as the Marcus Gunn pupil, is one of the most important clinical signs in neuro-ophthalmology. It indicates an asymmetric lesion of the afferent visual pathway anterior to the chiasm.

**Testing Technique (Swinging Flashlight Test):**
1. Patient fixates at distance to prevent accommodative miosis
2. Room lighting should be dim to allow pupil dilation
3. Light is shone in one eye for 2-3 seconds
4. Quickly moved to the other eye for 2-3 seconds
5. Repeated 3-4 times while observing both pupils

**Interpretation:**
- Normal response: Both pupils constrict equally regardless of which eye is illuminated
- RAPD present: When light moves to the affected eye, the pupil appears to dilate instead of maintaining constriction
- The "dilation" is actually relative—the pupil constricts less than it should

**Grading RAPD:**
- Grade 1+: Mild initial dilation after 2-3 seconds of illumination
- Grade 2+: Immediate dilation upon illumination of affected eye
- Grade 3+: No initial constriction, immediate dilation
- Grade 4+: No constriction at all, marked dilation

**Causes of RAPD:**
- Optic neuritis (most common cause)
- Ischemic optic neuropathy (AION)
- Severe retinal disease (central retinal vein occlusion, retinal detachment)
- Optic nerve compression (tumors, thyroid ophthalmopathy)
- Traumatic optic neuropathy
- Advanced glaucoma (unilateral or asymmetric)

### 1.4 Anisocoria

Anisocoria refers to unequal pupil sizes and requires systematic evaluation to determine whether the abnormal pupil is the larger or smaller one.

**Physiologic Anisocoria:**
- Affects approximately 20% of the population
- Difference typically ≤1mm
- Same degree of anisocoria in both bright and dim lighting
- Normal light reactivity in both pupils
- Long-standing history (check old photographs)

**Pathologic Anisocoria - Evaluation Approach:**

**Step 1: Determine which pupil is abnormal**
- Compare pupil sizes in bright light and dim light
- If anisocoria is greater in bright light → the larger pupil is abnormal (parasympathetic defect or pharmacologic mydriasis)
- If anisocoria is greater in darkness → the smaller pupil is abnormal (sympathetic defect, Horner's syndrome)

**Step 2: Evaluate pupillary reactivity**
- Test direct and consensual light reflexes
- Test near reflex (accommodation)
- Check for light-near dissociation

**Step 3: Consider pharmacologic testing**
- Pilocarpine testing for third nerve palsy vs. pharmacologic mydriasis
- Apraclonidine or cocaine testing for Horner's syndrome

### 1.5 Third Nerve Palsy (Oculomotor Palsy)

Third nerve palsy can cause significant pupillary dysfunction along with other ocular motor deficits.

**Features of Complete Third Nerve Palsy:**
- Complete ptosis (levator palpebrae involvement)
- Eye positioned "down and out" due to unopposed CN IV (superior oblique) and CN VI (lateral rectus)
- Mydriasis (dilated pupil from parasympathetic involvement)
- Loss of accommodation (ciliary muscle paralysis)
- Limited adduction, elevation, and depression of the eye

**Pupil-Involving vs Pupil-Sparing:**

**Pupil-Involving Third Nerve Palsy:**
- Mydriasis present with sluggish or absent light response
- Suggests compressive lesion (posterior communicating artery aneurysm, tumor)
- Parasympathetic fibers travel on the superficial surface of the nerve and are affected first by compression
- Neurosurgical emergency requiring immediate imaging

**Pupil-Sparing Third Nerve Palsy:**
- Pupil size and reactivity are normal
- Typically ischemic (microvascular) from diabetes or hypertension
- Central core of nerve affected while superficial fibers are spared
- Usually painful but resolves over 8-12 weeks

**Emergency Considerations:**
- Painful pupil-involving third nerve palsy requires immediate neuroimaging (CTA or MRA) to rule out posterior communicating artery aneurysm
- Gradual onset with pupillary involvement suggests tumor
- Isolated pupil-sparing palsy in a diabetic patient can be managed conservatively

### 1.6 Horner's Syndrome

Horner's syndrome results from disruption of the sympathetic pathway and presents with a characteristic triad of signs.

**Classic Triad:**
1. Miosis (small pupil) - more noticeable in darkness
2. Ptosis (mild, 1-2mm, due to Muller's muscle weakness)
3. Anhidrosis (absence of sweating on the affected side of the face)

**Additional Signs:**
- Heterochromia iridis (different colored iris) in congenital Horner's
- Apparent enophthalmos (eyes appear sunken due to ptosis)
- Dilatation lag (slow dilation of affected pupil in darkness, taking 15-20 seconds)
- Red conjunctiva (loss of sympathetic vasoconstriction)

**Three-Neuron Pathway:**
- First-order neuron: Hypothalamus → ciliospinal center of Budge (C8-T2)
- Second-order neuron: Preganglionic fibers from T1-T2 → superior cervical ganglion
- Third-order neuron: Postganglionic fibers from superior cervical ganglion → eye

**Pharmacologic Testing:**

**Apraclonidine Test (diagnostic):**
- 0.5% or 1% apraclonidine drops in both eyes
- Weak α1 and strong α2 agonist
- Normal pupil: slight constriction
- Horner's pupil: dilates due to denervation hypersensitivity
- Reversal of anisocoria is diagnostic
- Positive in any neuron order lesion

**Cocaine Test (historical, confirms Horner's):**
- 4-10% cocaine drops in both eyes
- Cocaine blocks norepinephrine reuptake
- Normal pupil: dilates
- Horner's pupil: fails to dilate (no norepinephrine to accumulate)
- Positive if anisocoria increases by ≥0.8mm after 45 minutes

**Hydroxyamphetamine Test (localizes lesion):**
- 1% hydroxyamphetamine drops
- Distinguishes first/second-order from third-order neuron
- First/second-order lesion: pupil dilates (postganglionic neuron intact, can release norepinephrine)
- Third-order lesion: pupil fails to dilate (postganglionic neuron damaged)

**Causes by Location:**

**First-Order Neuron (Central):**
- Stroke (lateral medullary/Wallenberg syndrome)
- Brainstem tumor
- Demyelination (multiple sclerosis)
- Trauma, syringomyelia

**Second-Order Neuron (Preganglionic):**
- Pancoast tumor (lung apex)
- Thyroid carcinoma
- Neck trauma or surgery
- Carotid artery dissection

**Third-Order Neuron (Postganglionic):**
- Carotid artery dissection (painful Horner's)
- Cavernous sinus lesion
- Cluster headache
- Nasopharyngeal carcinoma

### 1.7 Adie's Tonic Pupil

Adie's pupil is a benign condition caused by damage to the ciliary ganglion or short ciliary nerves, typically idiopathic.

**Clinical Features:**
- Unilateral dilated pupil (initially; may become bilateral over time)
- Minimal or absent light reaction (light-near dissociation)
- Slow, tonic constriction to near target (takes seconds)
- Vermiform (worm-like) movements of iris sphincter on slit lamp examination
- Deep tendon hyporeflexia (Adie's syndrome when reflexes involved)

**Pharmacologic Testing:**
- Dilute pilocarpine (0.1%) in both eyes
- Tonic pupil: constricts due to denervation supersensitivity
- Normal pupil: no constriction (concentration too low)
- Confirms the diagnosis

**Management:**
- Reassurance (benign condition)
- Reading glasses for accommodative paresis
- Pilocarpine 0.1% drops if symptomatic from glare or photophobia
- Condition often stabilizes; pupil may become smaller over years

### 1.8 Argyll Robertson Pupil

Argyll Robertson pupil is a classic neuro-ophthalmic sign historically associated with neurosyphilis.

**Characteristics:**
- Small, irregular pupils (miosis)
- Light-near dissociation (no reaction to light, constriction to near)
- Bilateral but may be asymmetric
- Poor dilation in darkness
- Usually associated with other signs of neurosyphilis (tabes dorsalis)

**Causes:**
- Neurosyphilis (classic and most common cause)
- Diabetes mellitus
- Chronic alcoholism
- Multiple sclerosis
- Midbrain lesions (dorsal midbrain syndrome)
- Lyme disease

**Testing for Syphilis:**
- RPR or VDRL screening
- FTA-ABS or TP-PA confirmatory testing
- Lumbar puncture for CSF analysis if neurosyphilis suspected

## 2. Optic Nerve Disorders

### 2.1 Optic Nerve Anatomy

The optic nerve is a white matter tract of the central nervous system, approximately 50mm in length from the globe to the chiasm. It is myelinated by oligodendrocytes and surrounded by meninges.

**Four Anatomical Segments:**

**Intraocular Segment (1mm):**
- Optic nerve head visible on fundoscopy
- Contains unmyelinated nerve fibers
- Lamina cribrosa marks transition to myelinated fibers
- Vulnerable to elevated intraocular pressure

**Intraorbital Segment (25mm):**
- Longest segment with redundant length (S-shaped)
- Allows for eye movement without tension
- Surrounded by meninges, CSF, and orbital fat
- Central retinal artery enters here

**Intracanalicular Segment (4-10mm):**
- Within the bony optic canal
- Fixed to surrounding bone by dura
- Vulnerable to compression and traumatic shear injury
- No redundancy—fixed position

**Intracranial Segment (10mm):**
- From optic canal to chiasm
- Above the cavernous sinus
- Near the internal carotid artery and anterior cerebral artery
- Vulnerable to aneurysm compression

### 2.2 Optic Disc Evaluation

Systematic evaluation of the optic disc is essential for detecting optic neuropathies.

**Normal Optic Disc Features:**
- Color: Pink-orange with well-defined margins
- Cup-to-disc ratio: 0.3 (average), range 0.0-0.7
- Normal vessels emerging from central cup
- No disc swelling, pallor, or hemorrhages
- Physiologic cup located centrally

**Signs of Optic Neuropathy:**
- Disc swelling (edema): Elevated, blurred margins
- Disc pallor (atrophy): White or gray appearance
- Cupping: Enlarged cup (glaucomatous or non-glaucomatous)
- Vascular changes: Venous congestion, hemorrhages, cotton wool spots

### 2.3 Optic Neuritis

Optic neuritis is an inflammatory demyelinating condition of the optic nerve, commonly associated with multiple sclerosis.

**Clinical Presentation:**
- Acute unilateral vision loss (progresses over days, peaks at 1-2 weeks)
- Pain with eye movement (present in 90% of cases)
- Visual acuity: Variable, from 20/20 to light perception
- Color vision: Desaturation, especially red (dyschromatopsia)
- Visual field: Central scotoma or diffuse depression
- RAPD: Present (key diagnostic feature)

**Fundus Findings:**
- Two-thirds have normal disc appearance (retrobulbar neuritis)
- One-third have disc swelling (papillitis)
- May have mild vitreous cells

**Workup:**
- MRI brain and orbits with gadolinium (assesses MS risk)
- Consider lumbar puncture if MRI inconclusive
- OCT for baseline nerve fiber layer thickness
- Visual field testing

**Treatment (ONTT Protocol):**
- IV methylprednisolone 1g/day for 3 days, followed by oral prednisone taper
- Accelerates visual recovery but does not improve final visual outcome
- Oral prednisone alone is contraindicated (increases recurrence risk)
- Consider disease-modifying therapy if high-risk MS

**Prognosis:**
- Visual recovery begins within 2-3 weeks
- Most patients recover to 20/40 or better
- Risk of developing MS: 50% at 15 years
- Higher risk if white matter lesions on MRI

### 2.4 Ischemic Optic Neuropathy

Ischemic optic neuropathy is the most common cause of acute optic nerve-related vision loss in older adults.

**Anterior Ischemic Optic Neuropathy (AION):**

**Arteritic AION (Giant Cell Arteritis - GCA):**
- Ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate treatment
- Age typically >55 years
- Associated symptoms: headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, polymyalgia rheumatica, fatigue, weight loss
- Vision loss: Severe, often complete, may be bilateral if untreated
- Disc appearance: Chalky white swelling, often with hemorrhages

**Diagnostic Tests for GCA:**
- ESR: Usually >50mm/hr (but can be normal)
- CRP: Elevated, more specific than ESR
- Platelet count: Often elevated (thrombocytosis)
- Temporal artery biopsy: Gold standard (skip lesions possible)
- Fluorescein angiography: Choroidal filling delay

**Treatment of GCA:**
- Immediate high-dose steroids (IV methylprednisolone or high-dose oral)
- Do NOT wait for biopsy results to treat
- Biopsy should be performed within 1-2 weeks of starting steroids
- Steroids do not improve vision but prevent fellow eye involvement

**Non-Arteritic AION (NAION):**
- More common than arteritic form
- Age typically >50 years
- Predisposing factors: Hypertension, diabetes, sleep apnea, hyperlipidemia
- "Disc at risk": Small cup-to-disc ratio (<0.2), crowded disc
- Vision loss: Variable severity, often altitudinal (typically inferior)
- Disc appearance: Segmental swelling, peripapillary hemorrhages

**Treatment of NAION:**
- No proven effective treatment to improve vision
- Address vascular risk factors
- Avoid nocturnal hypotension
- 15-20% risk to fellow eye within 5 years

### 2.5 Optic Disc Edema

Optic disc edema indicates swelling of the optic nerve head with multiple potential etiologies.

**Causes (Mnemonic: "PIE-IN-TIME"):**
- Papilledema (increased intracranial pressure)
- Inflammation (optic neuritis, uveitis)
- Ischemia (AION)
- Infiltration (lymphoma, leukemia, sarcoidosis)
- Neoplasm (optic nerve glioma, meningioma)
- Toxic/metabolic (methanol, ethambutol, vitamin B12 deficiency)
- Infection (Lyme disease, syphilis, cat-scratch disease)
- Medication-induced (amiodarone, tetracyclines)
- Elevated IOP (acute glaucoma)

**Papilledema (Bilateral Disc Edema from Increased ICP):**
- Early signs: Hyperemia, blurred disc margins, venous congestion
- Fully developed: Marked elevation, hemorrhages, exudates, cotton wool spots
- Late: Optic atrophy, gliosis
- Associated symptoms: Headache, nausea, vomiting, transient visual obscurations
- Visual acuity: Usually normal early (key differentiator from optic neuritis)

### 2.6 Optic Atrophy

Optic atrophy represents the end stage of optic nerve damage, characterized by loss of nerve fibers and glial proliferation.

**Types:**

**Primary Optic Atrophy:**
- White disc with sharp, well-defined margins
- No gliosis or excavation
- Causes: Compressive lesions, toxic (methanol), hereditary (Leber's), nutritional

**Secondary Optic Atrophy:**
- Gray-white disc with indistinct margins
- Gliosis present from prior edema
- Follows long-standing disc edema or papilledema

**Consecutive Optic Atrophy:**
- Follows retinal disease (retinitis pigmentosa, CRAO, extensive laser)
- Waxy pallor appearance
- Attenuated retinal vessels

**Glaucomatous Optic Atrophy:**
- Progressive cupping with thinning of neuroretinal rim
- Notching, vertical elongation of cup
- Nerve fiber layer defects
- Disc hemorrhages (splinter)

## 3. Cranial Nerves

### 3.1 Overview of Cranial Nerves in Ocular Function

Six cranial nerves are directly involved in ocular function:

| Nerve | Type | Function | Clinical Sign of Dysfunction |
|-------|------|----------|------------------------------|
| CN II | Sensory | Vision, afferent pupillary pathway | Vision loss, RAPD, optic atrophy |
| CN III | Motor | Eye movement, pupil constriction, lid elevation | Ptosis, mydriasis, diplopia, eye down and out |
| CN IV | Motor | Superior oblique (intorsion, depression) | Vertical diplopia, head tilt |
| CN V | Mixed | Facial sensation, corneal reflex | Facial numbness, corneal anesthesia |
| CN VI | Motor | Lateral rectus (abduction) | Esotropia, horizontal diplopia |
| CN VII | Motor | Facial movement, lid closure | Lagophthalmos, exposure keratopathy |

### 3.2 Oculomotor Nerve (CN III)

**Anatomy:**
- Originates in midbrain (oculomotor nucleus)
- Nucleus has subnuclei for each muscle
- Passes between posterior cerebral and superior cerebellar arteries
- Travels along the cavernous sinus lateral wall
- Divides into superior and inferior branches in the orbit

**Functions:**
- Superior division: Levator palpebrae (lid elevation), superior rectus (elevation)
- Inferior division: Medial rectus (adduction), inferior rectus (depression), inferior oblique (extorsion, elevation), parasympathetics to pupil and ciliary muscle

**Third Nerve Palsy Causes:**
- Microvascular (diabetes, hypertension) - most common, pupil-sparing
- Aneurysm (posterior communicating artery) - pupil-involving, emergency
- Tumor (cavernous sinus, orbital)
- Trauma
- Inflammation (Tolosa-Hunt syndrome)

### 3.3 Trochlear Nerve (CN IV)

**Anatomy:**
- Longest intracranial course of any cranial nerve
- Only cranial nerve to exit dorsally from the brainstem
- Crosses to innervate the contralateral superior oblique muscle
- Most commonly injured cranial nerve in head trauma

**Superior Oblique Functions:**
- Primary action in adduction: Intorsion
- Primary action in abduction: Depression
- Secondary action: Abduction

**Fourth Nerve Palsy Features:**
- Vertical diplopia (worse looking down, such as reading or walking downstairs)
- Hypertropia of the affected eye
- Head tilt to the opposite side (to minimize diplopia)
- Diplopia worse on gaze to the opposite side

**Bielschowsky Three-Step Test:**
1. Which eye is hypertropic? (Right or left hypertropia)
2. Is hypertropia worse on right or left gaze?
3. Is hypertropia worse on right or left head tilt?

**Causes:**
- Trauma (most common cause, often bilateral)
- Congenital (decompensated in adulthood)
- Microvascular (diabetes, hypertension)
- Tumor (rare)

### 3.4 Abducens Nerve (CN VI)

**Anatomy:**
- Nucleus located in pons (near facial nerve)
- Longest subarachnoid course
- Ascends along the clivus
- Passes through Dorello's canal (petrous apex)
- Travels within the cavernous sinus

**Sixth Nerve Palsy Features:**
- Esotropia (eye turned inward, toward nose)
- Horizontal diplopia (worse at distance than near)
- Limited abduction on the affected side
- May have abduction nystagmus on attempted lateral gaze

**Localization:**
- Nuclear: Gaze palsy (affects both CN VI and internuclear neurons)
- Fascicular: Foville syndrome (facial weakness, hearing loss, Horner's)
- Subarachnoid: Increased ICP (false localizing sign), meningitis, clivus tumor
- Petrous apex: Gradenigo syndrome (otitis media, facial pain, CN VI palsy)
- Cavernous sinus: Multiple cranial nerves involved

**Causes:**
- Microvascular (most common in adults over 50)
- Trauma
- Tumor
- Increased ICP (false localizing sign due to downward brainstem shift)
- Multiple sclerosis
- Infection (Gradenigo syndrome)

### 3.5 Multiple Ocular Motor Nerve Palsies

When multiple cranial nerves are involved simultaneously, consider specific anatomical locations:

**Cavernous Sinus Syndrome:**
- Cranial nerves affected: CN III, IV, V1, V2, VI
- Also affects oculosympathetic fibers
- Signs: Complete ophthalmoplegia, ptosis, mydriasis, facial numbness
- Causes: Tumor, thrombosis, aneurysm, inflammation (Tolosa-Hunt)

**Orbital Apex Syndrome:**
- Cranial nerves affected: CN II, III, IV, V1, VI
- Signs: Vision loss (CN II), ophthalmoplegia, proptosis
- Causes: Tumor, infection, inflammation, trauma

**Brainstem Syndromes:**
- Millard-Gubler: CN VI palsy + contralateral hemiplegia (pons)
- Foville: CN VI palsy + facial weakness + contralateral hemiplegia (pons)
- Weber: CN III palsy + contralateral hemiplegia (midbrain)

## 4. Visual Fields

### 4.1 Visual Pathway Anatomy and Field Correlation

Understanding visual field defects requires knowledge of the visual pathway anatomy and the relationship between retinal locations and visual field areas.

**Retina to Visual Field:**
- Nasal retina → Temporal visual field
- Temporal retina → Nasal visual field
- Superior retina → Inferior visual field
- Inferior retina → Superior visual field

**Visual Pathway Segments:**

**Optic Nerve:**
- Carries all fibers from one eye
- Papillomacular bundle: Central vision (most fibers)
- Peripheral fibers: Located peripherally in nerve

**Optic Chiasm:**
- Nasal retinal fibers cross (approximately 53%)
- Temporal retinal fibers remain uncrossed
- Macular fibers cross posteriorly in chiasm

**Optic Tract:**
- Contains fibers from corresponding visual fields of both eyes
- Right tract: Left visual field of both eyes
- Left tract: Right visual field of both eyes

**Optic Radiations:**
- Upper fibers: Travel through parietal lobe → Inferior visual field
- Lower fibers (Meyer's loop): Travel through temporal lobe → Superior visual field ("pie in the sky")

**Visual Cortex:**
- Located in occipital lobe (Brodmann area 17)
- Retinotopic organization preserved
- Macular representation: Posterior occipital lobe (dual blood supply)

### 4.2 Visual Field Defects by Location

**Prechiasmal Lesions (Optic Nerve):**
- Unilateral visual field loss
- Central scotoma (optic neuritis)
- Altitudinal defect (AION)
- Enlarged blind spot (papilledema)
- Arcuate scotoma (glaucoma)
- Generalized depression

**Chiasmal Lesions:**
- Junctional scotoma: Central scotoma in one eye + superior temporal defect in other eye (anterior chiasm)
- Bitemporal hemianopia: Classic chiasmal syndrome (compressive lesion)
- Bitemporal hemianopic scotoma: Central chiasm involvement

**Postchiasmal Lesions:**
- All produce homonymous defects (same side of visual field in both eyes)
- Optic tract: Incongruous homonymous hemianopia
- LGN: Incongruous homonymous hemianopia, possible sectoral
- Temporal lobe: Superior homonymous quadrantanopia ("pie in the sky")
- Parietal lobe: Inferior homonymous quadrantanopia
- Occipital lobe: Congruous homonymous hemianopia
- Macular sparing: Occipital cortex (dual blood supply from MCA and PCA)

### 4.3 Visual Field Testing Methods

**Confrontation Testing:**
- Quick screening at bedside
- Finger counting in four quadrants
- Compare to examiner's visual field
- Detects moderate to severe defects
- Can localize major lesions

**Automated Perimetry (Humphrey, Octopus):**
- Gold standard for quantification
- Static perimetry (stimuli at fixed locations)
- Sensitivity mapping across the visual field
- Statistical analysis comparing to age-matched normals
- Useful for monitoring progression

**Interpretation Parameters:**
- Fixation losses: Should be <20%
- False positives: Should be <15% (trigger-happy)
- False negatives: Should be <25% (inattentive)
- Mean deviation: Overall sensitivity loss
- Pattern deviation: Localized defects

### 4.4 Common Visual Field Patterns

**Glaucoma:**
- Nasal step (early)
- Paracentral scotoma
- Arcuate scotoma (Bjerrum's area)
- Ring scotoma (advanced)
- Temporal island remaining (end-stage)

**Neurologic Conditions:**
- Central scotoma: Optic neuritis, macular disease
- Altitudinal defect: AION, traumatic optic neuropathy
- Bitemporal hemianopia: Pituitary adenoma, craniopharyngioma
- Homonymous hemianopia: Stroke, tumor (postchiasmal)
- Junctional scotoma: Anterior chiasmal lesion

## 5. Clinical Examination Approach for DHA Exam

### 5.1 Systematic Neuro-Ophthalmic Examination

**History:**
- Onset, duration, and progression of symptoms
- Associated symptoms (pain, headache, neurologic deficits)
- Past medical history (diabetes, hypertension, MS)
- Current medications
- Family history

**Visual Acuity and Color Vision:**
- Distance and near acuity (best corrected)
- Color vision testing (Ishihara plates, Farnsworth D-15)
- Red desaturation test

**Pupil Examination:**
- Size in bright and dim light
- Direct and consensual light reflex
- Swinging flashlight test for RAPD
- Near response testing

**Ocular Motility:**
- Ductions and versions
- Cover test for alignment
- Smooth pursuit and saccades
- Convergence

**Visual Fields:**
- Confrontation testing
- Automated perimetry if indicated

**Fundoscopy:**
- Optic disc appearance (color, cup, margins)
- Macula
- Peripheral retina
- Blood vessels

### 5.2 Red Flags Requiring Urgent Referral

- Sudden severe vision loss with pain (giant cell arteritis, optic neuritis)
- Bilateral disc edema (increased intracranial pressure)
- Pupil-involving third nerve palsy (aneurysm)
- Proptosis with vision loss (orbital apex syndrome)
- Headache with neurologic signs
- Painful Horner's syndrome (carotid dissection)

## 6. Key Points for DHA Examination

1. **RAPD** indicates an afferent pathway defect; always test with the swinging flashlight method
2. **Horner's syndrome** presents with the classic triad: miosis, ptosis, and anhidrosis
3. **Third nerve palsy** with pupil involvement is a neurosurgical emergency (rule out aneurysm)
4. **Optic neuritis** typically presents with pain on eye movement and central scotoma
5. **AION** is the most common optic neuropathy in older adults
6. **Giant cell arteritis** requires immediate steroids to prevent bilateral blindness
7. **Bitemporal hemianopia** localizes to the optic chiasm
8. **Congruous homonymous defects** localize to the occipital lobe
9. **CN IV** is most commonly injured in head trauma
10. **CN VI** palsy can be a false localizing sign of increased intracranial pressure

## Conclusion

Neuro-ophthalmology encompasses a wide range of conditions that can present to the optometrist. A systematic approach to history and examination, combined with knowledge of neuroanatomy, allows for accurate localization and appropriate management. For the DHA Optometry examination, candidates should be comfortable with pupillary assessment, optic disc evaluation, cranial nerve testing, and visual field interpretation. Recognition of sight-threatening and life-threatening conditions is essential for patient safety and successful clinical practice in Dubai.
