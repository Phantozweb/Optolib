# Systemic Conditions & Ocular Manifestations for DHA

## Introduction

Understanding the relationship between systemic diseases and ocular manifestations is a critical competency for optometrists preparing for the Dubai Health Authority (DHA) licensing examination. The eye serves as a unique window into systemic health, with many systemic conditions presenting characteristic ocular findings that can aid in diagnosis, monitoring, and management. This comprehensive guide covers the major systemic conditions that appear frequently on the DHA examination, with particular emphasis on diabetes mellitus, hypertension, thyroid disorders, and autoimmune diseases.

## Diabetes Mellitus

### Overview and Classification

Diabetes mellitus (DM) represents one of the most significant systemic conditions affecting ocular health. The DHA examination frequently tests knowledge of diabetic eye disease, as optometrists in Dubai play a crucial role in screening and monitoring diabetic patients. Diabetes is classified into Type 1 (insulin-dependent), Type 2 (non-insulin-dependent), and gestational diabetes, with each type carrying distinct risks for ocular complications.

### Diabetic Retinopathy

Diabetic retinopathy (DR) remains the leading cause of preventable blindness in working-age adults worldwide. The DHA examination tests both the classification and management of DR.

**Non-Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (NPDR)** presents with microaneurysms, dot-and-blot hemorrhages, hard exudates, cotton-wool spots, and venous abnormalities. NPDR is further classified as mild, moderate, or severe based on the extent of these findings. The 4-2-1 rule helps classify severe NPDR: hemorrhages in all four quadrants, venous beading in two or more quadrants, or prominent intraretinal microvascular abnormalities (IRMA) in one or more quadrants.

**Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy (PDR)** is characterized by neovascularization of the disc (NVD), neovascularization elsewhere (NVE), or neovascularization of the iris (NVI). The presence of these new vessels indicates severe ischemia and carries a high risk of vitreous hemorrhage and tractional retinal detachment. Preretinal or vitreous hemorrhage may occur as fragile new vessels bleed.

**Diabetic Macular Edema (DME)** can occur at any stage of DR and represents a leading cause of visual impairment in diabetic patients. Clinically significant macular edema (CSME) is defined by retinal thickening within 500 micrometers of the foveal center, hard exudates within 500 micrometers of the fovea with adjacent thickening, or retinal thickening of one disc diameter or larger, any part of which is within one disc diameter of the foveal center.

### Other Ocular Manifestations of Diabetes

Beyond diabetic retinopathy, diabetes affects multiple ocular structures:

**Corneal Complications** include recurrent corneal erosions, delayed wound healing, and increased susceptibility to infection. Diabetic patients often exhibit decreased corneal sensitivity due to neuropathy, making them prone to neurotrophic ulcers. The corneal epithelium in diabetic patients shows altered basement membrane adhesion, contributing to recurrent erosion syndrome.

**Lens Changes** include transient refractive changes during periods of poor glycemic control, with hyperglycemia typically causing a myopic shift due to lens swelling. Cataracts develop earlier and progress more rapidly in diabetic patients. True diabetic cataracts (snowflake cataracts) appear as white, snowflake-like opacities in the anterior and posterior cortex, though these are relatively rare.

**Glaucoma Association** demonstrates increased risk of primary open-angle glaucoma in diabetic patients. Neovascular glaucoma is a severe complication of PDR, where new vessels on the iris and anterior chamber angle lead to angle closure and elevated intraocular pressure.

**Cranial Neuropathies** most commonly affect the third cranial nerve (oculomotor), causing ptosis and ophthalmoplegia. Diabetic third nerve palsy typically spares the pupil due to the peripheral location of pupillary fibers, distinguishing it from compressive lesions. The sixth cranial nerve is also commonly affected.

### Screening and Management

The DHA examination tests knowledge of appropriate screening intervals and referral criteria. Type 1 diabetics should be screened beginning five years after diagnosis, while Type 2 diabetics should be screened at diagnosis due to the possibility of prolonged undiagnosed disease. Pregnant diabetic patients require more frequent monitoring. Management includes optimal glycemic control, blood pressure management, and lipid control. Treatment options for DR include intravitreal anti-VEGF injections, focal and panretinal laser photocoagulation, and vitrectomy for non-clearing vitreous hemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment.

## Hypertension

### Overview and Classification

Hypertension affects approximately one billion people worldwide and has significant ocular manifestations. The DHA examination tests knowledge of hypertensive retinopathy grading and associated findings. Hypertension is classified as essential (primary) or secondary, with ocular findings generally correlating with the severity and duration of elevated blood pressure.

### Hypertensive Retinopathy

The Keith-Wagener-Barker classification system remains the standard for grading hypertensive retinopathy:

**Grade 1** presents with generalized arteriolar narrowing, where the arteriolar-to-venous ratio decreases below the normal 2:3 ratio. The light reflex becomes more prominent.

**Grade 2** shows more pronounced arteriolar narrowing with arteriovenous (AV) nicking. At AV crossings, the arteriole appears to compress the underlying venule, causing venous constriction and dilation distal to the crossing. "Silver wiring" may be observed where the arteriolar wall becomes thickened and hyperreflective.

**Grade 3** demonstrates cotton-wool spots (nerve fiber layer infarcts), flame-shaped hemorrhages, and hard exudates. Cotton-wool spots appear as fluffy white lesions with feathery edges in the nerve fiber layer. Flame-shaped hemorrhages track along nerve fiber layer anatomy.

**Grade 4** represents malignant hypertension and includes all Grade 3 findings plus optic disc edema (papilledema). This stage indicates a medical emergency requiring immediate blood pressure control to prevent permanent visual loss and systemic complications.

### Hypertensive Choroidopathy

Severe acute hypertension can affect the choroidal circulation, producing characteristic findings:

**Elschnig Spots** appear as focal areas of retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) infarction, visible as pale lesions that later become pigmented scars. These represent focal choroidal infarction.

**Siegrist Streaks** are linear pigmented lesions along choroidal vessels, representing fibrinoid necrosis of choroidal arterioles.

**Serous Retinal Detachment** may occur in severe hypertension, particularly in preeclampsia and eclampsia. The detachment typically resolves with blood pressure control.

### Hypertension and Other Ocular Conditions

Hypertension is a risk factor for retinal vein occlusions, particularly central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) and branch retinal vein occlusion (BRVO). Arteriosclerotic changes in arteries compress veins at AV crossings, predisposing to thrombosis. Hypertension also increases the risk of retinal artery occlusions and anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION).

### Clinical Significance

The DHA examination emphasizes that hypertensive retinopathy findings may indicate systemic vascular disease affecting other organs. Optometrists should communicate findings to primary care physicians, as moderate to severe hypertensive retinopathy is associated with increased risk of stroke, myocardial infarction, and cardiovascular mortality.

## Thyroid Disorders

### Overview

Thyroid dysfunction affects multiple organ systems, with the orbit and ocular adnexa being primary targets. Thyroid eye disease (TED), also known as Graves' ophthalmopathy or thyroid-associated ophthalmopathy (TAO), is the most common extrathyroidal manifestation of Graves' disease. The DHA examination tests knowledge of TED presentation, classification, and management.

### Thyroid Eye Disease Pathophysiology

TED results from autoimmune activation of orbital fibroblasts by thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins. These fibroblasts produce glycosaminoglycans (primarily hyaluronic acid) that accumulate in extraocular muscles and orbital fat, causing expansion of orbital tissues. Inflammatory cell infiltration, adipogenesis, and ultimately fibrosis contribute to the clinical manifestations.

### Clinical Features

**Eyelid Signs** are often the earliest manifestations of TED. Lid retraction (Dalrymple's sign) presents as widening of the palpebral fissure, giving a staring appearance. Upper lid retraction more than 2mm above the superior limbus is significant. Lid lag (von Graefe's sign) occurs when the upper lid moves slower than the globe during downward gaze. Infrequent blinking (Stellwag's sign) and difficulty everting the upper lid (Gifford's sign) are additional findings.

**Proptosis** (exophthalmos) results from increased orbital volume pushing the globe forward. Proptosis is measured using an exophthalmometer, with normal values ranging from 12-21mm. Asymmetric proptosis or measurements greater than 21mm warrant investigation. Proptosis may be compressive and can lead to exposure keratopathy.

**Extraocular Muscle Involvement** typically follows a characteristic pattern, with the inferior rectus being most commonly affected, followed by the medial rectus, superior rectus, and lateral rectus. Muscle enlargement spares the tendons, distinguishing TED from orbital inflammatory pseudotumor. Restricted elevation due to inferior rectus fibrosis produces hypotropia and diplopia.

**Optic Nerve Compression** is a sight-threatening complication occurring in approximately 5% of TED patients. Compressive optic neuropathy presents with decreased visual acuity, relative afferent pupillary defect, visual field defects, and color vision deficits. The optic nerve is compressed at the orbital apex by enlarged extraocular muscles.

**Corneal Exposure** results from proptosis and lid retraction leading to incomplete eyelid closure (lagophthalmos). Exposure keratopathy presents with punctate epithelial erosions, particularly in the inferior cornea. Severe cases may progress to corneal ulceration and perforation.

### Classification Systems

**The NOSPECS Classification** provides a mnemonic for TED staging:
- N: No signs or symptoms
- O: Only signs (lid retraction)
- S: Soft tissue involvement (conjunctival injection, edema)
- P: Proptosis
- E: Extraocular muscle involvement
- C: Corneal involvement
- S: Sight loss (optic nerve compression)

**Clinical Activity Score (CAS)** assesses disease activity to guide immunosuppressive therapy. Points are assigned for spontaneous retrobulbar pain, pain on eye movement, eyelid erythema, eyelid edema, conjunctival injection, conjunctival chemosis, and swelling of the caruncle. A score of 3 or more suggests active disease.

### Management

Management of TED includes smoking cessation, as smoking is a major risk factor for disease severity. Selenium supplementation has shown benefit in mild TED. Artificial tears and lubricating ointments manage exposure keratopathy. Active moderate-to-severe disease may require intravenous methylprednisolone or oral steroids. Sight-threatening optic nerve compression requires urgent decompression surgery. Rehabilitative surgery (strabismus surgery, eyelid surgery, orbital decompression) is performed after disease stabilization.

## Autoimmune Diseases

### Overview

Autoimmune diseases represent a diverse group of conditions where the immune system mistakenly attacks the body's own tissues. The DHA examination tests knowledge of ocular manifestations in rheumatoid arthritis, systemic lupus erythematosus, Sjogren's syndrome, sarcoidosis, and vasculitic disorders.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis (RA)

RA is a chronic systemic inflammatory disease primarily affecting synovial joints. Ocular involvement is common and affects multiple structures.

**Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca** is the most common ocular manifestation, occurring in 15-25% of RA patients. Secondary Sjogren's syndrome develops in some patients, with lymphocytic infiltration of lacrimal glands causing decreased tear production. Symptoms include dryness, burning, foreign body sensation, and fluctuating vision.

**Scleritis** presents with severe, boring ocular pain that may radiate to the brow and jaw. The sclera shows deep violaceous injection that does not blanch with topical phenylephrine. Anterior scleritis may be diffuse, nodular, or necrotizing. Necrotizing scleritis with inflammation is the most severe form and carries significant risk of scleral thinning, staphyloma formation, and perforation. Scleritis in RA may correlate with systemic vasculitis and requires systemic immunosuppression.

**Peripheral Ulcerative Keratitis (PUK)** is a sight-threatening condition characterized by corneal epithelial breakdown and stromal thinning at the limbus. PUK often occurs adjacent to scleritis and may rapidly progress to perforation. Underlying collagen vascular disease must be excluded in patients presenting with PUK.

**Other Findings** include episcleritis (less common than scleritis and typically benign), corneal melt syndrome, and drug-induced ocular effects from RA medications (hydroxychloroquine retinopathy, steroid-induced cataracts and glaucoma).

### Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

SLE is a multisystem autoimmune disease with characteristic ocular findings affecting the eyelids, anterior segment, and posterior segment.

**Eyelid Manifestations** include the classic malar "butterfly rash" that may extend to the eyelids. Discoid lupus may cause characteristic erythematous, scaly plaques with follicular plugging and scarring. Madarosis (loss of eyelashes) may occur.

**Anterior Segment Findings** include keratoconjunctivitis sicca, which affects up to one-third of SLE patients. Secondary Sjogren's syndrome is common. Corneal involvement may include peripheral keratitis and corneal melt.

**Posterior Segment Findings** are more distinctive and include:

**Lupus Retinopathy** presents with cotton-wool spots, hemorrhages, retinal vascular tortuosity, and vascular occlusions. Cotton-wool spots are the most common finding and reflect microvascular damage. Severe retinopathy with widespread cotton-wool spots and hemorrhages correlates with active systemic disease and central nervous system involvement.

**Retinal Vascular Occlusions** including central retinal vein occlusion and branch retinal vein occlusion occur with increased frequency in SLE patients. Antiphospholipid antibody syndrome, often associated with SLE, increases thrombotic risk.

**Choroidal Disease** may present as serous retinal detachment or choroidal infarction. Lupus choroidopathy typically occurs in patients with severe systemic disease.

**Neuro-ophthalmic Manifestations** include optic neuritis, ischemic optic neuropathy, and pseudotumor cerebri (idiopathic intracranial hypertension) associated with lupus medications or the disease itself.

### Sjogren's Syndrome

Sjogren's syndrome is an autoimmune disorder characterized by lymphocytic infiltration of exocrine glands, leading to sicca symptoms. Primary Sjogren's occurs alone, while secondary Sjogren's is associated with other autoimmune diseases, most commonly rheumatoid arthritis.

**Ocular Manifestations** primarily involve keratoconjunctivitis sicca, with symptoms of dryness, grittiness, burning, and photophobia. Tear film instability, reduced tear meniscus height, and punctate epithelial erosions are found on examination. Schirmer's test shows reduced wetting (less than 5mm in 5 minutes is significant). Rose Bengal or lissamine green staining highlights devitalized epithelial cells in a characteristic interpalpebral pattern.

**Management** includes artificial tears, punctal plugs, secretagogues (pilocarpine, cevimeline), and anti-inflammatory agents (cyclosporine ophthalmic emulsion). Severe cases may require autologous serum tears or scleral lenses.

### Sarcoidosis

Sarcoidosis is a multisystem granulomatous disorder of unknown etiology. Ocular involvement occurs in 25-50% of patients and may be the presenting manifestation.

**Anterior Uveitis** is the most common ocular manifestation, presenting as acute or chronic granulomatous inflammation. Granulomatous keratic precipitates (mutton-fat KPs) appear as large, greasy-appearing deposits on the corneal endothelium. Iris nodules (Koeppe nodules at the pupillary margin, Busacca nodules in the iris stroma) are characteristic. Posterior synechiae may form, leading to iris bombe and angle-closure glaucoma.

**Posterior Segment Findings** include vitritis, retinal periphlebitis, and choroidal granulomas. Retinal periphlebitis produces "candle-wax drippings" (taches de bougie) along retinal veins. Cystoid macular edema may develop secondary to uveitis.

**Orbital and Adnexal Involvement** includes lacrimal gland enlargement, orbital mass lesions, and eyelid granulomas. Bilateral lacrimal gland enlargement is suggestive of sarcoidosis.

### Vasculitic Disorders

**Giant Cell Arteritis (GCA)**, also known as temporal arteritis, is a systemic vasculitis affecting medium and large arteries, particularly branches of the external carotid artery. GCA is an ophthalmic emergency due to the risk of irreversible vision loss.

Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION) is the most common ocular manifestation, presenting with sudden, painless vision loss, visual field defects (typically altitudinal), relative afferent pupillary defect, and optic disc edema. The optic disc typically shows pallid edema. Up to one-third of untreated patients develop involvement of the fellow eye.

Other ocular manifestations include central retinal artery occlusion, cilioretinal artery occlusion, ocular motor cranial nerve palsies, and choroidal infarction.

Diagnosis requires high clinical suspicion. Symptoms include headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication, polymyalgia rheumatica, and constitutional symptoms. Erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) and C-reactive protein (CRP) are typically elevated. Temporal artery biopsy showing granulomatous inflammation with giant cells confirms the diagnosis.

Treatment with high-dose corticosteroids should begin immediately upon clinical suspicion, without waiting for biopsy confirmation, to prevent further vision loss. Biopsy should be performed within 1-2 weeks of starting steroids.

**Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (Wegener's)** is a necrotizing vasculitis affecting small and medium vessels. Ocular involvement occurs in 50-60% of patients and includes orbital inflammatory disease, scleritis, peripheral ulcerative keratitis, and nasolacrimal duct obstruction. The classic triad involves the upper airways, lungs, and kidneys. Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA) testing is positive in most patients.

## Clinical Pearls for the DHA Examination

Understanding the interplay between systemic diseases and ocular manifestations is essential for the DHA examination. Key points to remember include:

1. **Diabetic retinopathy screening intervals** differ between Type 1 and Type 2 diabetes
2. **Hypertensive retinopathy grading** uses the Keith-Wagener-Barker classification
3. **Thyroid eye disease** spares the extraocular muscle tendons, distinguishing it from orbital pseudotumor
4. **Scleritis in rheumatoid arthritis** may indicate systemic vasculitis requiring immunosuppression
5. **Giant cell arteritis** requires immediate treatment to prevent bilateral vision loss

## Conclusion

The DHA examination tests comprehensive knowledge of systemic conditions and their ocular manifestations. Optometrists in Dubai serve as frontline healthcare providers in detecting, monitoring, and managing these conditions. Understanding the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, and management of diabetic eye disease, hypertensive retinopathy, thyroid eye disease, and autoimmune ocular manifestations is essential for providing optimal patient care and passing the DHA licensing examination.

Regular screening, appropriate referral, and collaboration with primary care physicians and specialists ensure that patients with systemic diseases receive comprehensive care that addresses both their ocular and systemic health needs.
