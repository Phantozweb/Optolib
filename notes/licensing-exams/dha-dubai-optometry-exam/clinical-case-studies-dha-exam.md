# Clinical Case Studies for DHA Exam

## Introduction

Clinical case studies form a critical component of the Dubai Health Authority (DHA) Optometry licensing examination. These scenarios test the candidate's ability to apply theoretical knowledge to real-world patient presentations, demonstrate clinical reasoning, and make appropriate management decisions. This comprehensive collection presents 15 detailed case studies across five major categories: red eye presentations, vision loss cases, refraction challenges, contact lens complications, and pediatric optometry scenarios. Each case includes patient presentation, clinical findings, differential diagnosis, management approach, and key learning points essential for DHA examination success.

## SECTION 1: RED EYE CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 1: Acute Bacterial Conjunctivitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 32-year-old male presents with a 2-day history of redness, irritation, and discharge in the right eye. He reports waking up with his eyelids "stuck together" and notices a yellowish discharge throughout the day. He works as a laboratory technician and denies any recent trauma or contact with chemicals. His past ocular history is unremarkable, and he has no known allergies.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/6 OS (unaffected)
- External examination: Diffuse conjunctival injection OD, mild lid edema
- Discharge: Mucopurulent, yellow-green in color
- Preauricular lymph node: Not palpable
- Slit lamp examination: Mild papillary reaction on upper tarsal conjunctiva, clear cornea, quiet anterior chamber
- Intraocular pressure: 14 mmHg OD, 15 mmHg OS

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Acute bacterial conjunctivitis (most likely)
2. Viral conjunctivitis
3. Allergic conjunctivitis
4. Toxic conjunctivitis

**Diagnosis:**
Acute bacterial conjunctivitis, likely caused by Staphylococcus aureus or Streptococcus pneumoniae based on the mucopurulent discharge and absence of preauricular lymphadenopathy.

**Management Plan:**
1. Broad-spectrum topical antibiotic (e.g., moxifloxacin 0.5% qid for 7 days)
2. Lid hygiene with warm compresses
3. Patient education on infection control measures
4. Work restriction until discharge resolves (24-48 hours after starting antibiotics)
5. Follow-up in 5-7 days or sooner if symptoms worsen

**Key Learning Points:**
- Bacterial conjunctivitis typically presents with mucopurulent discharge and lid matting
- Visual acuity should be unaffected; decreased vision suggests more serious pathology
- Culture is not routinely required unless treatment fails or in neonatal cases
- Patient education on hand hygiene prevents spread to contacts

### Case Study 2: Herpes Simplex Keratitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 45-year-old female presents with a 3-day history of right eye redness, pain, photophobia, and blurred vision. She describes the pain as a "deep ache" and reports previous similar episodes, the last occurring approximately 1 year ago. She has a history of "cold sores" on her lips but is otherwise healthy.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/18 OD, 6/6 OS
- External examination: Mild right eyelid edema, conjunctival injection greatest around the limbus
- Corneal sensation: Reduced OD (compared to OS)
- Slit lamp examination: Dendritic epithelial defect with terminal bulbs visible on fluorescein staining, mild underlying stromal edema
- Anterior chamber: +1 cells, mild flare
- Intraocular pressure: 18 mmHg OD, 16 mmHg OS

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Herpes simplex virus (HSV) keratitis (most likely given dendritic pattern)
2. Herpes zoster ophthalmicus
3. Acanthamoeba keratitis
4. Bacterial keratitis

**Diagnosis:**
Recurrent HSV epithelial keratitis with mild stromal involvement.

**Management Plan:**
1. Topical antiviral therapy: Acyclovir ointment 3% five times daily OR Ganciclovir gel 0.15% five times daily
2. Consider oral antiviral (acyclovir 400mg 5 times daily) for severe cases
3. Topical steroid CONTRAINDICATED at this stage (may worsen epithelial disease)
4. Cycloplegic for comfort (cyclopentolate 1% bid)
5. Urgent ophthalmology referral for management and monitoring
6. Patient education on prodromal symptoms and importance of early treatment

**Key Learning Points:**
- Dendritic ulcers with terminal bulbs are pathognomonic for HSV keratitis
- Reduced corneal sensation is a classic finding supporting HSV diagnosis
- Steroids are contraindicated in active epithelial disease
- Recurrence is common; consider prophylactic oral antivirals for frequent recurrences
- Always document visual acuity and refer urgently to ophthalmology

### Case Study 3: Acute Angle-Closure Glaucoma

**Patient Presentation:**
A 62-year-old Asian female presents at 9 PM with sudden onset of severe right eye pain, blurred vision, and seeing "halos around lights" that began approximately 4 hours ago. She reports associated nausea and a frontal headache. She has hyperopia and had a recent dilated eye examination 2 days prior. No significant medical history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/60 OD (pinhole no improvement), 6/9 OS
- External examination: Right eye: severely injected, lid edema, conjunctival chemosis
- Cornea: Steamy, edematous with microcystic changes
- Anterior chamber: Extremely shallow, +2 flare
- Iris: Mid-dilated, fixed pupil, sectoral atrophy visible
- Intraocular pressure: 58 mmHg OD, 18 mmHg OS
- Gonioscopy (OS only): Narrow angle, appositional closure possible

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (classic presentation)
2. Secondary angle-closure (phacomorphic, pupil block from other causes)
3. Uveitic glaucoma
4. Neovascular glaucoma

**Diagnosis:**
Acute primary angle-closure glaucoma (APACG) - ophthalmic emergency.

**Management Plan:**
1. IMMEDIATE ophthalmology referral (sight-threatening emergency)
2. Initial medical therapy (while awaiting transfer):
   - IV acetazolamide 500mg (if no contraindications)
   - Topical pilocarpine 2% (once IOP reduced to <40mmHg)
   - Topical beta-blocker (timolol 0.5%)
   - Topical alpha-agonist (apraclonidine 1%)
   - Oral hyperosmotic agent (mannitol or glycerol)
3. Definitive treatment: Laser peripheral iridotomy (LPI) once cornea clears
4. Prophylactic LPI in fellow eye (within days to weeks)
5. Long-term: Consider lens extraction if significant cataract

**Key Learning Points:**
- APACG is an ophthalmic emergency requiring immediate intervention
- Classic triad: ocular pain, blurred vision with halos, elevated IOP
- Risk factors: hyperopia, Asian ethnicity, shallow anterior chamber, advanced age
- Pilocarpine ineffective at very high IOP (iris ischemia); use after IOP reduction
- Fellow eye is at risk and needs prophylactic treatment

## SECTION 2: VISION LOSS CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 4: Central Retinal Vein Occlusion

**Patient Presentation:**
A 58-year-old male with a history of hypertension and type 2 diabetes presents with sudden, painless vision loss in the left eye that he noticed upon waking 3 days ago. He describes it as a "dark curtain" affecting most of his vision. His diabetes is "moderately controlled" with oral medications, and his last HbA1c was 8.2%.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/60 OS
- Pupils: Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) present OS
- Intraocular pressure: 16 mmHg OU
- Fundus examination OS: Dilated and tortuous retinal veins in all quadrants, diffuse retinal hemorrhages ("blood and thunder" appearance), cotton wool spots, optic disc edema, macular edema
- Fundus examination OD: Within normal limits

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Central retinal vein occlusion (CRVO) - most likely given diffuse hemorrhages
2. Ocular ischemic syndrome
3. Diabetic retinopathy (severe proliferative)
4. Papillophlebitis

**Diagnosis:**
Non-ischemic CRVO (pending fluorescein angiography confirmation) with macular edema.

**Management Plan:**
1. Urgent ophthalmology referral (within 24-48 hours)
2. Blood pressure measurement: 168/95 mmHg - requires optimization
3. Systemic evaluation: HbA1c, lipid profile, cardiac evaluation
4. Collaborate with primary care physician for vascular risk factor management
5. Ophthalmology management may include:
   - Intravitreal anti-VEGF injections for macular edema
   - Fluorescein angiography to determine ischemic vs non-ischemic
   - Monitor for neovascularization (iris, angle, retina)
   - Consider panretinal photocoagulation if neovascularization develops

**Key Learning Points:**
- CRVO presents with sudden, painless vision loss
- "Blood and thunder" fundus appearance is classic
- Systemic conditions (hypertension, diabetes) are major risk factors
- Distinguish ischemic from non-ischemic CRVO (prognosis differs significantly)
- Monitor for neovascular complications (neovascular glaucoma risk)

### Case Study 5: Optic Neuritis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 28-year-old female presents with a 4-day history of progressive vision loss in her right eye accompanied by pain that worsens with eye movement. She reports colors appearing "washed out" and describes a "central blur" in her vision. She had a similar episode of "numbness in her legs" 6 months ago that resolved spontaneously.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/30 OD, 6/6 OS
- Color vision: Markedly reduced OD (Ishihara: 3/21 plates)
- Pupils: Relative afferent pupillary defect (RAPD) present OD
- Visual fields: Central scotoma OD
- Fundus examination: Mild optic disc hyperemia OD, otherwise normal; no disc edema (retrobulbar optic neuritis)
- Extraocular movements: Pain on right eye movement

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Optic neuritis (highly likely - typical presentation)
2. Anterior ischemic optic neuropathy (AION)
3. Compressive optic neuropathy
4. Infiltrative optic neuropathy

**Diagnosis:**
Typical optic neuritis, highly suspicious for multiple sclerosis (MS) given history of previous neurological symptoms.

**Management Plan:**
1. Urgent ophthalmology and neurology referral
2. MRI brain and orbits with gadolinium (to assess for MS and rule out compression)
3. High-dose IV methylprednisolone (1g daily for 3 days) followed by oral taper - speeds recovery but does not affect final visual outcome
4. Lumbar puncture may be considered if MRI inconclusive
5. Patient education: Visual recovery expected over weeks to months
6. Long-term: Disease-modifying therapy if MS confirmed

**Key Learning Points:**
- Optic neuritis typically affects young women (20-50 years)
- Pain with eye movement is a hallmark symptom
- Retrobulbar optic neuritis has normal fundus appearance initially
- Strong association with multiple sclerosis (50% develop MS within 15 years)
- Visual prognosis is generally good, but color vision may remain impaired

### Case Study 6: Retinal Detachment

**Patient Presentation:**
A 55-year-old myopic male presents with a 1-week history of new floaters and flashing lights in his left eye, followed by a "curtain-like shadow" progressing from the superior temporal aspect over the past 2 days. He had cataract surgery 6 months ago with uneventful recovery. He reports no trauma.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/12 OS (with current correction)
- Intraocular pressure: 16 mmHg OD, 10 mmHg OS (slightly reduced)
- Anterior segment: Posterior chamber IOL well-positioned OS
- Fundus examination OS: Bullous superior temporal retinal detachment extending to the macula, visible tear at 10 o'clock, subretinal fluid extending to fovea
- Fundus examination OD: Peripheral lattice degeneration at 2 o'clock

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment (confirmed)
2. Tractional retinal detachment
3. Exudative retinal detachment
4. Retinoschisis

**Diagnosis:**
Rhegmatogenous retinal detachment, macula-involving, secondary to retinal tear.

**Management Plan:**
1. URGENT ophthalmology referral (within 24 hours)
2. Surgical repair required - options include:
   - Pars plana vitrectomy (preferred for macula-involving RD)
   - Scleral buckle
   - Pneumatic retinopexy (for specific cases)
3. Preoperative: Limited eye movements, maintain position as directed
4. Postoperative: Positioning requirements, activity restrictions
5. Prophylactic laser to peripheral lattice OD (prophylactic treatment)
6. Patient education regarding symptoms of recurrent detachment

**Key Learning Points:**
- Myopia and previous intraocular surgery are risk factors for RD
- "Curriculum progression" of symptoms: floaters → photopsia → visual field defect
- Lower IOP may suggest subretinal fluid (Schwartz-Matsuo syndrome)
- Macula-involving RD has worse visual prognosis than macula-sparing
- Fellow eye requires thorough peripheral examination and prophylaxis if needed

## SECTION 3: REFRACTION CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 7: High Myopia with Amblyopia

**Patient Presentation:**
A 24-year-old female presents for her first eye examination in 5 years. She reports struggling with distance vision since childhood but was told she had "lazy eye" and "nothing could be done." She works as an accountant and experiences eye strain and headaches after prolonged computer work. No significant medical or family history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (unaided): 6/120 OD, 6/15 OS
- Visual acuity (pinhole): 6/30 OD, 6/6 OS
- Cover test: Exotropia 10 prism diopters at distance, comitant
- Refraction:
  - OD: -8.00/-2.00 x 180 → 6/24 (no improvement with further correction)
  - OS: -2.00/-0.50 x 90 → 6/6
- Binocular vision: Worth 4-dot shows suppression OD at distance
- Fundus examination: Myopic fundus changes OD > OS (tilted disc, peripapillary atrophy, lacquer cracks)

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Anisometropic amblyopia (high myopia OD)
2. Strabismic amblyopia (concurrent exotropia)
3. Mixed mechanism amblyopia
4. Organic pathology (rule out macular pathology)

**Diagnosis:**
Anisometropic and strabismic amblyopia (mixed mechanism) in right eye, with high myopia.

**Management Plan:**
1. Full optical correction with high-index lenses (reduce weight and thickness)
2. Discuss anti-reflective coating for computer work
3. Vision therapy referral (discuss potential for improvement at this age)
4. Discuss contact lens option (better cosmesis and peripheral vision)
5. Patient education: Amblyopia treatment most effective before age 7-8, but some improvement possible in adults with compliance
6. Monitor for myopic complications: Annual dilated fundus examination
7. Discuss refractive surgery options in future (may not correct amblyopia)

**Key Learning Points:**
- Anisometropia >2D can cause amblyopia
- Mixed amblyopia (anisometropic + strabismic) is common
- Adult amblyopia treatment has limited efficacy but some improvement possible
- High myopia requires regular monitoring for complications (retinal detachment, myopic macular degeneration)
- Contact lenses may provide better vision than spectacles in high anisometropia

### Case Study 8: Presbyopia with Accommodative Insufficiency

**Patient Presentation:**
A 48-year-old female teacher presents with increasing difficulty reading, especially in the evenings after work. She has worn distance glasses since age 12 and has been using over-the-counter reading glasses (+1.50) for the past year, but finds them increasingly inadequate. She reports eye fatigue, headaches, and blurred near vision after 20-30 minutes of reading.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (with current glasses):
  - Distance: 6/6 OD, 6/6 OS
  - Near: N8 at 40cm (comfortable initially, blurs after testing)
- Current prescription: -3.00 DS OU
- Cover test: Orthophoric at distance and near
- Amplitude of accommodation: 3.00D OU (expected for age: 4.00-5.00D)
- Near point of convergence: 8cm (normal)
- Cycloplegic refraction: -2.75 DS OU
- Intraocular pressure: 16 mmHg OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Presbyopia with concurrent accommodative insufficiency
2. Uncorrected hyperopia (ruled out by cycloplegic refraction)
3. Convergence insufficiency (ruled out by normal NPC)
4. Systemic causes (diabetes, medications, thyroid dysfunction)

**Diagnosis:**
Presbyopia with accommodative insufficiency, myopic correction required.

**Management Plan:**
1. Discuss options:
   - Bifocal spectacles: Distance -3.00 DS with +1.75 add
   - Progressive addition lenses: Distance -3.00 DS with +1.75 add (preferred for teacher)
   - Contact lenses: Multifocal or monovision option
2. Near vision assessment at working distance (consider +2.00 add if extended reading needed)
3. Recommend 20-20-20 rule for digital eye strain
4. Good lighting for reading
5. Follow-up in 6-12 months (presbyopia progresses in early stages)
6. Consider computer glasses with intermediate add

**Key Learning Points:**
- Presbyopia typically begins around age 40-45
- Accommodative amplitude can be measured and compared to age-expected values
- Progressive lenses preferred for active presbyopes (teachers, professionals)
- Consider working distance and intermediate vision needs
- Presbyopic adds typically increase by +0.50D every 2-3 years until plateau

### Case Study 9: Astigmatism with Irregular Cornea

**Patient Presentation:**
A 35-year-old male presents with complaints of "distorted vision" that has progressively worsened over 2 years. He reports frequent changes in glasses prescription and has tried three different prescriptions in the past 18 months without satisfaction. He experiences eye rubbing, especially after removing his contact lenses at night. No significant medical or family history.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (unaided): 6/30 OD, 6/36 OS
- Visual acuity (with best spectacle correction): 6/12 OD, 6/15 OS
- Refraction:
  - OD: -2.00/-3.50 x 120 → 6/12
  - OS: -1.50/-4.00 x 70 → 6/15
- Keratometry:
  - OD: 45.00 @ 30° / 48.50 @ 120° (3.50D irregular)
  - OS: 45.50 @ 160° / 49.50 @ 70° (4.00D irregular)
- Corneal topography: Inferior steepening consistent with keratoconus
- Slit lamp examination: Thinning and cone formation at inferior cornea, Vogt's striae visible OS
- Munson's sign: Present OS on downgaze

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Keratoconus (confirmed)
2. Pellucid marginal degeneration
3. Post-refractive surgery ectasia (ruled out by history)
4. Contact lens-induced corneal warpage

**Diagnosis:**
Moderate keratoconus, bilateral, more advanced OS.

**Management Plan:**
1. Discontinue soft contact lens use (contributing to eye rubbing)
2. Rigid gas permeable (RGP) contact lens fitting - best visual correction
3. Discuss corneal cross-linking (CXL) to halt progression
4. Scleral lenses if RGP intolerance develops
5. Avoid eye rubbing (instruct patient)
6. Surgical options in future if progression:
   - Deep anterior lamellar keratoplasty (DALK)
   - Penetrating keratoplasty (PK)
7. Genetic counseling (family screening recommended)
8. Regular monitoring with topography every 6 months

**Key Learning Points:**
- Irregular astigmatism that cannot be corrected with spectacles suggests corneal pathology
- Keratoconus presents in young adults with progressive myopic astigmatism
- Eye rubbing is a risk factor and should be avoided
- RGP lenses provide best visual acuity by creating regular refractive surface
- Corneal cross-linking can halt progression - early diagnosis crucial

## SECTION 4: CONTACT LENS CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 10: Contact Lens-Related Microbial Keratitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 26-year-old female soft contact lens wearer presents with a 2-day history of left eye pain, redness, and light sensitivity. She admits to sleeping in her monthly replacement lenses "occasionally" and sometimes extending the replacement schedule. She last replaced her lenses 2 months ago. She tried using a friend's contact lens solution when hers ran out 3 days ago.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OD, 6/24 OS (with correction)
- External examination: Severe conjunctival injection OS, mild lid edema
- Slit lamp examination OS:
  - Corneal epithelial defect: 2.5mm diameter, central
  - Infiltrate: Dense, underlying stromal infiltrate with overlying epithelial defect
  - Anterior chamber: +2 cells, hypopyon present (1mm)
  - No satellite lesions
- Corneal sensation: Intact

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Bacterial keratitis (most likely - contact lens related)
2. Fungal keratitis (consider if culture negative or not responding)
3. Acanthamoeba keratitis (pain out of proportion to findings)
4. Herpes simplex keratitis (dendritic pattern typically seen)

**Diagnosis:**
Severe contact lens-related microbial keratitis (suspected bacterial) with hypopyon.

**Management Plan:**
1. IMMEDIATE ophthalmology referral (sight-threatening emergency)
2. Discontinue contact lens wear immediately
3. Corneal scraping for culture and sensitivity (Gram stain, culture on blood, chocolate, and Sabouraud agar)
4. Intensive topical antibiotics (after scraping):
   - Fortified cefazolin 5% + gentamicin 1.4% (hourly, around the clock for 24-48 hours)
   - OR fluoroquinolone monotherapy (moxifloxacin 0.5% hourly)
5. Cycloplegic for comfort
6. Close follow-up (daily initially)
7. Patient education on proper lens care and replacement schedule
8. Consider delaying re-fitting until complete resolution

**Key Learning Points:**
- Contact lens-related keratitis is an ophthalmic emergency
- Risk factors: extended wear, poor hygiene, topping off solution, swimming in lenses
- Hypopyon indicates severe inflammation/infection
- Culture-guided therapy preferred; empirical therapy while awaiting results
- Patient education crucial for prevention of recurrence

### Case Study 11: Giant Papillary Conjunctivitis

**Patient Presentation:**
A 29-year-old male soft contact lens wearer presents with a 3-week history of increasing discomfort and itching in both eyes. He reports that his lenses feel "uncomfortable" and move excessively. He has been wearing monthly silicone hydrogel lenses for 5 years, typically 14 hours per day. He uses hydrogen peroxide solution and replaces lenses monthly. He has a history of seasonal allergies.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity: 6/6 OU (with current lenses)
- External examination: Mild bilateral conjunctival injection, mild lid edema
- Slit lamp examination:
  - Everting upper lids: Giant papillae (cobblestone appearance) on tarsal conjunctiva, >1mm diameter
  - Conjunctiva: Mild chemosis, papillary reaction
  - Cornea: Clear, no staining
- Lens fit: Excessive movement, decentration noted

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Giant papillary conjunctivitis (GPC) - contact lens related (confirmed)
2. Vernal keratoconjunctivitis
3. Atopic keratoconjunctivitis
4. Bacterial conjunctivitis

**Diagnosis:**
Giant papillary conjunctivitis secondary to contact lens wear.

**Management Plan:**
1. Temporary discontinuation of contact lenses (2-4 weeks)
2. Switch to daily disposable lenses upon return (reduce protein deposits and allergens)
3. Topical mast cell stabilizer/antihistamine combination (olopatadine 0.1% bid)
4. Topical steroid (fluorometholone 0.1%) for severe cases (short course)
5. Cold compresses for symptomatic relief
6. Limit wearing time to 8-10 hours upon resumption
7. Follow-up in 2-4 weeks to assess improvement
8. Consider rigid gas permeable lenses if GPC recurs

**Key Learning Points:**
- GPC is a common contact lens complication (mechanical + immune response)
- Cobblestone papillae on upper tarsal conjunctiva are diagnostic
- Risk factors: extended wear time, deposits on lenses, history of atopy
- Daily disposables reduce risk by eliminating deposit buildup
- Mast cell stabilizers help prevent recurrence
- Lens fit may change as papillae resolve

### Case Study 12: Tight Contact Lens Syndrome

**Patient Presentation:**
A 34-year-old female presents with a 2-day history of right eye discomfort and blurred vision that began after wearing her new RGP lenses for the first time. She was recently refitted from soft lenses to RGPs due to astigmatism correction needs. She reports the lens feels "stuck" to her eye and removal is difficult.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (with RGP): 6/9 OD, 6/6 OS
- External examination: Moderate conjunctival injection OD
- Slit lamp examination OD:
  - Cornea: Central superficial punctate keratitis, mild edema
  - Lens fit assessment:
    - Absence of post-lens tear film movement on blink
    - Lens centered but immobile on blink
    - Impression of lens edge visible
  - After lens removal: Corneal indentation ring visible
- Fluorescein pattern: Central pooling, no movement observed

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Tight lens syndrome (confirmed)
2. Incorrect lens base curve
3. Lens material incompatibility
4. Corneal edema from other causes

**Diagnosis:**
Tight contact lens syndrome secondary to improperly fitted RGP lens.

**Management Plan:**
1. Remove lens immediately
2. Assess corneal integrity after removal
3. Temporarily discontinue RGP wear OD (2-3 days for corneal recovery)
4. Refit with flatter base curve (increase by 0.05-0.10mm)
5. Verify lens parameters with manufacturer
6. Follow-up fitting to ensure proper movement (0.5-1.0mm on blink)
7. Patient education on signs of tight lens and when to remove
8. Consider training session for lens insertion and removal

**Key Learning Points:**
- Tight lens syndrome results from steep base curve fitting
- Signs: no lens movement, corneal indentation, discomfort
- RGP lenses should move 0.5-1.0mm on blink
- Corneal hypoxia can occur with prolonged tight lens wear
- Refitting with flatter base curve is corrective action
- Patient education on proper lens fit assessment is essential

## SECTION 5: PEDIATRIC CASE STUDIES

### Case Study 13: Pediatric Amblyopia

**Patient Presentation:**
A 5-year-old boy is brought by his parents for a routine eye examination before starting school. They have noticed he occasionally squints when watching television but have no major concerns. He was born full-term with no perinatal complications. Developmental milestones are normal. There is a family history of "lazy eye" in the father.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Visual acuity (Allen pictures):
  - OD: 6/30 (single optotype), no improvement with pinhole
  - OS: 6/6 (single optotype)
- Cover test: Esotropia 25 prism diopters at distance and near (comitant)
- Cycloplegic refraction (cyclopentolate 1%):
  - OD: +5.00 DS → 6/30
  - OS: +1.00 DS → 6/6
- Stereopsis: None (suppression on Worth 4-dot)
- Fundus examination: Within normal limits OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Accommodative esotropia with anisometropic amblyopia (most likely)
2. Congenital esotropia
3. Partial accommodative esotropia
4. Sensory esotropia (due to organic pathology)

**Diagnosis:**
Accommodative esotropia with anisometropic amblyopia in the right eye.

**Management Plan:**
1. Full-time spectacle correction with full hyperopic correction
2. Consider bifocals if near esotropia remains after distance correction
3. Occlusion therapy: 2 hours daily patching of OS (better eye)
4. Atropine penalization as alternative if patching fails
5. Monitor visual acuity every 4-6 weeks initially
6. Vision therapy for binocular function after acuity equalizes
7. Family education on importance of compliance
8. Long-term: May reduce hyperopia over years; monitor for deterioration

**Key Learning Points:**
- Amblyopia treatment most effective before age 7 (critical period)
- Anisometropia >1.5D in children requires monitoring
- Esotropia with hyperopia suggests accommodative component
- Full cycloplegic correction essential for accommodative esotropia
- Compliance with patching is key to successful treatment
- Regular follow-up essential to monitor improvement

### Case Study 14: Congenital Nasolacrimal Duct Obstruction

**Patient Presentation:**
A 9-month-old infant is brought by parents with a 6-month history of persistent tearing and discharge from the right eye. The eye has been "watery" since birth and more recently has developed "sticky" discharge, especially in the mornings. There is no redness or swelling. The parents have tried massaging the area as advised by their pediatrician with minimal improvement.

**Clinical Findings:**
- External examination: 
  - Right eye: Increased tear lake, mucoid discharge, no conjunctival injection
  - Left eye: Normal
- Lids: Normal position, no lid abnormalities, patent puncta
- Pressure over lacrimal sac: Reflux of mucopurulent material from right punctum
- Visual behavior: Fixation and following age-appropriate OU
- Red reflex: Present and equal OU

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (CNLDO) (confirmed)
2. Congenital glaucoma (ruled out - no photophobia, corneal clarity, normal eye size)
3. Dacryocystocele
4. Lid abnormalities (ectropion, entropion - ruled out)

**Diagnosis:**
Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction (membranous), right eye.

**Management Plan:**
1. Continue conservative management with Crigler massage (2-3 times daily)
2. Topical antibiotic if mucopurulent discharge (fluoroquinolone tid for 7 days)
3. Parents educated on proper massage technique (proximal to distal along lacrimal sac)
4. Expected spontaneous resolution: 90% by 12 months
5. If no resolution by 12-13 months: Refer for probing under general anesthesia
6. Alternative: Balloon dacryoplasty for persistent cases
7. Monitor for signs of dacryocystitis (fever, erythema, swelling - requires urgent referral)

**Key Learning Points:**
- CNLDO affects ~6% of newborns (most common cause of infant epiphora)
- Membranous obstruction at valve of Hasner is typical
- 90% resolve spontaneously by 12 months
- Massage technique: 10 strokes, 2-3 times daily
- Distinguish from congenital glaucoma (photophobia, corneal edema, enlarged eye)
- Probing success rate >90% when performed at appropriate age

### Case Study 15: Retinopathy of Prematurity Screening

**Patient Presentation:**
A 4-week-old male infant, born at 27 weeks gestational age with birth weight of 850 grams, is due for retinopathy of prematurity (ROP) screening. He has been receiving oxygen therapy in the NICU for respiratory distress syndrome. The neonatology team requests an ROP examination.

**Clinical Findings:**
- Birth history: 27 weeks GA, 850g birth weight, oxygen therapy for 3 weeks
- Current postmenstrual age: 31 weeks
- Binocular indirect ophthalmoscopy with 28D lens:
  - Zone I: Vessels extend to near nasal rim
  - Stage: Flat neovascularization at ridge, visible in temporal retina (Stage 3)
  - Plus disease: Dilated and tortuous vessels in posterior pole present
  - Extent: 5 clock hours involvement
  - Left eye: Similar findings with less extensive neovascularization

**Classification:**
Right eye: Zone I, Stage 3 ROP with plus disease
Left eye: Zone I, Stage 2 ROP with plus disease

**Differential Diagnosis:**
1. ROP (confirmed) - requires treatment decision
2. Familial exudative vitreoretinopathy (FEVR) - family history needed
3. Persistent fetal vasculature (PFV)

**Diagnosis:**
Retinopathy of prematurity, bilateral, Type 1 (treatment-requiring) in right eye.

**Management Plan:**
1. URGENT ophthalmology referral for treatment (within 72 hours)
2. Treatment indication: Type 1 ROP (Zone I, any stage with plus disease, or Zone I Stage 3)
3. Treatment options:
   - Laser photocoagulation (gold standard)
   - Intravitreal anti-VEGF injection (alternative, especially for Zone I)
4. Close monitoring of left eye (may progress)
5. Follow-up examination every 1-2 weeks until retina fully vascularized
6. Long-term monitoring for:
   - Strabismus
   - Refractive errors (high myopia common)
   - Retinal detachment (late complication)
7. Parent counseling regarding prognosis and need for follow-up

**Key Learning Points:**
- ROP screening criteria: <30 weeks GA OR <1500g birth weight
- Classification: Zone (location), Stage (severity), Plus disease (vascular activity)
- Type 1 ROP requires treatment within 72 hours
- Plus disease is critical determinant for treatment
- Laser therapy reduces unfavorable outcomes by 50%
- Long-term follow-up essential due to risk of late complications
- Oxygen management is key in prevention

## Summary and Key Examination Points

### Red Eye Evaluation Framework
1. Always measure visual acuity first
2. Determine if condition is sight-threatening
3. Check pupil responses and intraocular pressure
4. Slit lamp examination for accurate diagnosis
5. Know when to refer urgently

### Vision Loss Assessment Approach
1. Distinguish between sudden and gradual onset
2. Determine if unilateral or bilateral
3. Assess for pain (painful vs painless vision loss)
4. Check for relative afferent pupillary defect
5. Urgent referral for retinal detachment, CRVO, optic neuritis

### Refraction Problem-Solving
1. Always perform cycloplegic refraction in children and young hyperopes
2. Consider irregular astigmatism - suspect keratoconus if spectacles fail
3. Address anisometropia to prevent amblyopia
4. Consider working distance and task requirements for presbyopia

### Contact Lens Complications Management
1. Know warning signs requiring immediate lens removal
2. Microbial keratitis is an emergency - urgent referral
3. GPC is managed with lens modification and pharmacotherapy
4. Patient education is crucial for prevention

### Pediatric Considerations
1. Early detection of amblyopia is critical
2. Use cycloplegic refraction routinely
3. Congenital nasolacrimal duct obstruction often resolves spontaneously
4. ROP screening and treatment protocols must be followed
